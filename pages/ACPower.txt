     <p><br> <b><a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">Power</a></b> in an electric circuit is the rate of flow of energy past a given point of the circuit. In <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> circuits, energy storage elements such as <a href="/wiki/Inductor" title="Inductor">inductors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a> may result in periodic reversals of the direction of energy flow. The portion of power that, averaged over a complete cycle of the <a href="/wiki/AC_waveform" title="AC waveform" class="mw-redirect">AC waveform</a>, results in net transfer of energy in one direction is known as <b>active power</b> (sometimes also called real power). The portion of power due to stored energy, which returns to the source in each cycle, is known as <b>reactive power</b>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Active.2C_reactive.2C_and_apparent_power"><span id="Active_power"></span><span id="Reactive_power"></span><span id="Apparent_power"></span><span id="Complex_power"></span>Active, reactive, and apparent power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Active, reactive, and apparent power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In a simple <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) circuit consisting of a source and a linear load, both the current and voltage are <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoidal</a>. If the load is purely <a href="/wiki/Resistive" title="Resistive" class="mw-redirect">resistive</a>, the two quantities reverse their polarity at the same time. At every instant the product of voltage and current is positive or zero, with the result that the direction of energy flow does not reverse. In this case, only active power is transferred.</p> 
     <p>If the loads are purely <a href="/wiki/Electrical_reactance" title="Electrical reactance"><i>reactive</i></a>, then the voltage and current are 90 degrees out of phase. For half of each cycle, the product of voltage and current is positive, but on the other half of the cycle, the product is negative, indicating that on average, exactly as much energy flows toward the load as flows back. There is no net energy flow over one cycle. In this case, only reactive power flows—there is no net transfer of energy to the load.</p> 
     <p>Practical loads have resistance, inductance, and capacitance, so both active and reactive power will flow to real loads. Power engineers measure apparent power as the magnitude of the vector sum of active and reactive power. Apparent power is the product of the <a href="/wiki/Root-mean-square" title="Root-mean-square" class="mw-redirect">root-mean-square</a> of voltage and current.</p> 
     <p>Electrical engineers take apparent power into account when designing and operating power systems, because though the current associated with reactive power does no work at the load, it heats the conductors and wastes energy. Conductors, transformers and generators must be sized to carry the total current, not just the current that does useful work. Failure to provide for the supply of sufficient reactive power in electrical grids can lead to lowered voltage levels and under certain operating conditions to the complete collapse of the network or <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">blackout</a>.</p> 
     <p>Another consequence is that adding the apparent power for two loads will not accurately give the total apparent power unless they have the same displacement between current and voltage (the same <a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">power factor</a>).</p> 
     <p>Conventionally, capacitors are considered to generate reactive power and inductors to consume it. If a capacitor and an inductor are placed in parallel, then the currents flowing through the inductor and the capacitor tend to cancel rather than add. This is the fundamental mechanism for controlling the power factor in electric power transmission; capacitors (or inductors) are inserted in a circuit to partially compensate reactive power 'consumed' by the load. Purely capacitive circuits supply reactive power with the current waveform leading the voltage waveform by 90 degrees, while purely inductive circuits absorb reactive power with the current waveform lagging the voltage waveform by 90 degrees. The result of this is that capacitive and inductive circuit elements tend to cancel each other out.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Engineers use the following terms to describe energy flow in a system (and assign each of them a different unit to differentiate between them):</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Active power</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P</i>, or <b>real power</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> (W)</li> 
      <li><b>Reactive power</b>, <i>Q</i>: <a href="/wiki/Volt-ampere_reactive" title="Volt-ampere reactive">volt-ampere reactive</a> (var)</li> 
      <li><b>Complex power</b>, <i>S</i>: <a href="/wiki/Volt-ampere" title="Volt-ampere">volt-ampere</a> (VA)</li> 
      <li><b>Apparent power</b>, |<i>S</i>|: the <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(vector)" title="Magnitude (vector)" class="mw-redirect">magnitude</a> of complex power <i>S</i>: volt-ampere (VA)</li> 
      <li><b>Phase of voltage relative to current</b>, <i>φ</i>: the angle of difference (in degrees) between current and voltage; current lagging voltage (<a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry)" title="Quadrant (plane geometry)">quadrant</a> I vector), current leading voltage (quadrant IV vector)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In the diagram, <i>P</i> is the active power, <i>Q</i> is the reactive power (in this case positive), <i>S</i> is the complex power and the length of <i>S</i> is the apparent power. Reactive power does not do any work, so it is represented as the <b>imaginary axis</b> of the vector diagram. Active power does do work, so it is the real axis.</p> 
     <p>The unit for all forms of power is the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> (symbol: W), but this unit is generally reserved for active power. Apparent power is conventionally expressed in <a href="/wiki/Volt-ampere" title="Volt-ampere">volt-amperes</a> (VA) since it is the product of <a href="/wiki/Root_mean_square" title="Root mean square">rms</a> <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> and rms <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a>. The unit for reactive power is expressed as var, which stands for <a href="/wiki/Volt-ampere_reactive" title="Volt-ampere reactive">volt-ampere reactive</a>. Since reactive power transfers no net energy to the load, it is sometimes called "wattless" power. It does, however, serve an important function in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grids</a> and its lack has been cited as a significant factor in the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Blackout_of_2003" title="Northeast Blackout of 2003" class="mw-redirect">Northeast Blackout of 2003</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Understanding the relationship among these three quantities lies at the heart of understanding power engineering. The mathematical relationship among them can be represented by vectors or expressed using complex numbers, <i>S</i> = <i>P</i> + j<i>Q</i> (where j is the <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_unit" title="Imaginary unit">imaginary unit</a>).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_factor">Power factor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Power factor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">Power factor</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The ratio of active power to apparent power in a circuit is called the <a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">power factor</a>. For two systems transmitting the same amount of active power, the system with the lower power factor will have higher circulating currents due to energy that returns to the source from energy storage in the load. These higher currents produce higher losses and reduce overall transmission efficiency. A lower power factor circuit will have a higher apparent power and higher losses for the same amount of active power.</p> 
     <p>The power factor is one when the voltage and current are in <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>. It is zero when the current leads or lags the voltage by 90 degrees. Power factors are usually stated as "leading" or "lagging" to show the sign of the phase angle of current with respect to voltage. Voltage is designated as the base to which current angle is compared, meaning that we think of current as either "leading" or "lagging" voltage.</p> 
     <p>Where the waveforms are purely sinusoidal, the power factor is the cosine of the phase angle (φ) between the current and voltage sinusoid waveforms. Equipment data sheets and nameplates will often abbreviate power factor as "<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\cos \phi" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/3/1/b312dfe89761f508d18753997ed06a67.png">" for this reason.</p> 
     <p>Example: The active power is <span class="nowrap">700 W</span> and the phase angle between voltage and current is 45.6°. The power factor is <span class="nowrap">cos(45.6°) = 0.700</span>. The apparent power is then: <span class="nowrap">700 W / cos(45.6°) = 1000 VA</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-utm_my-AC_CircuitAnalysis2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utm_my-AC_CircuitAnalysis2-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For instance, a power factor of 68 percent (0.68) means that only 68 percent of the total current supplied is actually doing work; the remaining 32 percent is reactive and has to be made up by the utility. Usually, utilities do not charge consumers for the reactive power losses as they do no real work for the consumer. However, if there are inefficiencies at the customer’s load source that causes the power factor to fall below a certain level, utilities may charge customers in order to cover an increase in their power plant fuel use and their worse line and plant capacity.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactive_power">Reactive power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactive power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>In a direct current circuit, the power flowing to the load is proportional to the product of the current through the load and the potential drop across the load. Energy flows in one direction from the source to the load.</p> 
     <p>In AC power, the voltage and current both vary approximately sinusoidally. When there is inductance or capacitance in the circuit, the voltage and current waveforms do not line up perfectly. The power flow has two components - one component flows from source to load and can perform work at the load, the other portion, known as "reactive power" is due to the delay between voltage and current, known as phase angle, and cannot do useful work at the load. It can be thought of as current that is arriving at the wrong time (too late or too early). To distinguish reactive power from active power, it is measured in units of "volt-amperes reactive" or var. These units can simplify to Watts, but are left as var to denote that they represent no actual work output.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Energy stored in capacitive or inductive elements of the network give rise to reactive power flow. Reactive power flow strongly influences the voltage levels across the network. Voltage levels and reactive power flow must be carefully controlled to allow a power system to be operated within acceptable limits.</p> 
     <p>A technique known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reactive_compensation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reactive compensation (page does not exist)">reactive compensation</a> is used to reduce apparent power flow to a load by reducing reactive power supplied from transmission lines and providing it locally. For example, to compensate an inductive load a shunt capacitor is installed close to the load itself. This allows all reactive power needed by the load to be supplied by the capacitor and not have to be transferred over the transmission lines. This practice alone does not save energy because reactive power does no work, but instead reduces total current flow on the transmission lines allowing them to have smaller current ratings.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacitive_vs._Inductive_Loads">Capacitive vs. Inductive Loads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Capacitive vs. Inductive Loads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Stored energy in the magnetic or electric field of a load device, such as a motor or capacitor, causes an offset between the current and the voltage waveforms.</p> 
     <p>A capacitor is an AC device that stores energy in the form of an electric field. As current is driven through the capacitor, charge build-up causes an opposing voltage to develop across the capacitor. This voltage increases until some maximum dictated by the capacitor structure. In an AC network, the voltage across a capacitor is constantly changing. The capacitor opposes this change, causing the current to lead the voltage in phase. Capacitors are said to "source" reactive power, and thus to cause a leading power factor.</p> 
     <p>Induction machines are some of the most common types of loads in the electric power system today. These machines use <a href="/wiki/Inductors" title="Inductors" class="mw-redirect">inductors</a>, or large coils of wire to store energy in the form of a magnetic field. When a voltage is initially placed across the coil, the inductor strongly resists this change in current and magnetic field, which causes a time delay for the current to reach its maximum value. This causes the current to lag behind the voltage in phase. Inductors are said to "sink" reactive power, and thus to cause a lagging power factor. <a href="/wiki/Induction_generator" title="Induction generator">Induction generators</a> can source or sink reactive power, and provide a measure of control to system operators over reactive power flow and thus voltage.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because these devices have opposite effects on the phase angle between voltage and current, they can be used to "cancel out" each other's effects. This usually takes the form of capacitor banks being used to counteract the lagging power factor caused by induction motors.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactive_power_control">Reactive power control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactive power control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Transmission connected generators are generally required to support reactive power flow. For example, on the United Kingdom transmission system generators are required by the Grid Code Requirements to supply their rated power between the limits of 0.85 power factor lagging and 0.90 power factor leading at the designated terminals. The system operator will perform switching actions to maintain a secure and economical voltage profile while maintaining a reactive power balance equation:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       Generator_MVARs + System_gain + Shunt_capacitors = MVAR_Demand + Reactive_losses + Shunt_reactors
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The ‘System gain’ is an important source of reactive power in the above power balance equation, which is generated by the capacitive nature of the transmission network itself. By making decisive switching actions in the early morning before the demand increases, the system gain can be maximized early on, helping to secure the system for the whole day.</p> 
     <p>To balance the equation some pre-fault reactive generator use will be required. Other sources of reactive power that will also be used include shunt capacitors, shunt reactors, Static VAR Compensators and voltage control circuits.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unbalanced_polyphase_systems">Unbalanced polyphase systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unbalanced polyphase systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>While active power and reactive power are well defined in any system, the definition of apparent power for unbalanced polyphase systems is considered to be one of the most controversial topics in power engineering. Originally, apparent power arose merely as a figure of merit. Major delineations of the concept are attributed to <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley,_Jr." title="William Stanley, Jr.">Stanley</a>'s <i>Phenomena of Retardation in the Induction Coil</i> (1888) and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Proteus_Steinmetz" title="Charles Proteus Steinmetz">Steinmetz</a>'s <i>Theoretical Elements of Engineering</i> (1915). However, with the development of <a href="/wiki/Three_phase" title="Three phase" class="mw-redirect">three phase</a> power distribution, it became clear that the definition of apparent power and the power factor could not be applied to unbalanced <a href="/wiki/Polyphase_systems" title="Polyphase systems" class="mw-redirect">polyphase systems</a>. In 1920, a "Special Joint Committee of the AIEE and the National Electric Light Association" met to resolve the issue. They considered two definitions:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="pf = {P_a + P_b + P_c \over |S_a| + |S_b| + |S_c|}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/0/3/5034a75b9f600ea78fd4fdb8cbd40924.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>that is, the quotient of the sums of the active powers for each phase over the sum of the apparent power for each phase.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="pf = {P_a + P_b + P_c \over |P_a + P_b + P_c + j(Q_a + Q_b + Q_c)|}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/0/7/307f07424728fbbc14bcd6b38083a9cb.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>that is, the quotient of the sums of the active powers for each phase over the magnitude of the sum of the complex powers for each phase.</p> 
     <p>The 1920 committee found no consensus and the topic continued to dominate discussions. In 1930 another committee formed and once again failed to resolve the question. The transcripts of their discussions are the lengthiest and most controversial ever published by the AIEE (Emanuel, 1993). Further resolution of this debate did not come until the late 1990s.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_number_formulas">Real number formulas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Real number formulas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A perfect resistor stores no energy, so current and voltage are in phase. Therefore, there is no reactive power and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P=S" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/7/ee7982ca64867c9d10f07d832a58070a.png">. Therefore, for a perfect resistor</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P = S = V_\mathrm{RMS} I_\mathrm{RMS} = I_\mathrm{RMS}^2 R = \frac{V_\mathrm{RMS}^2} {R}\,\!" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/6/3/363fc15671600783fdb622ec61ebdcc5.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>For a perfect capacitor or inductor there is no net power transfer, so all power is reactive. Therefore, for a perfect capacitor or inductor:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\begin{align}
  P &amp;= 0 \\
  Q &amp;= |S| = V_\mathrm{RMS} I_\mathrm{RMS} = I_\mathrm{RMS}^2 |X| = \frac{V_\mathrm{RMS}^2}{|X|}
\end{align}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/d/7/dd727623b38533f741bd7aa793713ac7.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Where <i>X</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Reactance_(electronics)" title="Reactance (electronics)" class="mw-redirect">reactance</a> of the capacitor or inductor.</p> 
     <p>If X is defined as being positive for an inductor and negative for a capacitor then we can remove the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_value" title="Absolute value">modulus</a> signs from S and X and get</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q = I_\mathrm{RMS}^2 X = \frac{V_\mathrm{RMS}^2} {X}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/d/7/fd782c2fcfffa6f81bda5b9523c9f6b3.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Instantaneous power is defined as:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P_\text{inst}(t) = V(t) I(t)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/0/8/e086666bdc4506e1a1646489ca64f637.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where v(t) and i(t) are the time varying voltage and current waveforms.</p> 
     <p>This definition is useful because it applies to all waveforms, whether they are sinusoidal or not. This is particularly useful in power electronics, where nonsinusoidal waveforms are common.</p> 
     <p>In general, we are interested in the active power averaged over a period of time, whether it is a low frequency line cycle or a high frequency power converter switching period. The simplest way to get that result is to take the integral of the instantaneous calculation over the desired period.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P_\text{avg} = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1}\int_{t_1}^{t_2} V(t)I(t)\,\operatorname{d}t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/2/9/b29de8ae7f5cdc3ec7b5bd5f95cc9aed.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>This method of calculating the average power gives the active power regardless of harmonic content of the waveform. In practical applications, this would be done in the digital domain, where the calculation becomes trivial when compared to the use of rms and phase to determine active power.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" P_\text{avg} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=1}^n  V[k]I[k] " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/b/6/2b6b7bb2b16dd339b7528d35684b5662.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_frequency_systems">Multiple frequency systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AC_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Multiple frequency systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Since an RMS value can be calculated for any waveform, apparent power can be calculated from this.</p> 
     <p>For active power it would at first appear that we would have to calculate many product terms and average all of them. However, if we look at one of these product terms in more detail we come to a very interesting result.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\begin{align}
       &amp;A\cos(\omega_1t+k_1)\cos(\omega_2t + k_2) \\
  = {} &amp;\frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\left(\omega_1t + k_1\right) + \left(\omega_2t + k_2\right)\right] + \frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\left(\omega_1t + k_1\right) - \left(\omega_2t + k_2\right)\right] \\
  = {} &amp;\frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\left(\omega_1 + \omega_2\right)t + k_1 + k_2\right] + \frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\left(\omega_1 - \omega_2\right)t + k_1 - k_2\right]
\end{align}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/5/2/6528a0cc5049b769022584966c58165b.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>However, the time average of a function of the form cos(ω<i>t</i>+<i>k</i>) is zero provided that ω is nonzero. Therefore, the only product terms that have a nonzero average are those where the frequency of voltage and current match. In other words, it is possible to calculate active (average) power by simply treating each frequency separately and adding up the answers.</p> 
     <p>Furthermore, if we assume the voltage of the mains supply is a single frequency (which it usually is), this shows that <a href="/wiki/Harmonics_(electrical_power)" title="Harmonics (electrical power)">harmonic currents</a> are a bad thing. They will increase the rms current (since there will be non-zero terms added) and therefore apparent power, but they will have no effect on the active power transferred. Hence, harmonic currents will reduce the power factor.</p> 
     <p>Harmonic currents can be reduced by a filter placed at the input of the device. Typically this will consist of either just a capacitor (relying on parasitic resistance and inductance in the supply) or a capacitor-inductor network. An active <a href="/wiki/Power_factor_correction" title="Power factor correction" class="mw-redirect">power factor correction</a> circuit at the input would generally reduce the harmonic currents further and maintain the power factor closer to unity.</p>
