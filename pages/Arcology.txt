     <p><b>Arcology</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-MatterSpirit_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MatterSpirit-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is a vision of architectural design principles for very densely populated habitats. The concept has been primarily popularized, and the term itself coined, by architect <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Soleri" title="Paolo Soleri">Paolo Soleri</a>. It also appears in <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> These structures have been largely hypothetical insofar as no 'arcology' envisioned by Soleri himself has yet been completed, but he posited that a completed arcology would provide space for a variety of residential, commercial, and agricultural facilities while minimizing individual human <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact" class="mw-redirect">environmental impact</a>. Arcologies are often portrayed in sci-fi as self-contained or <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">economically self-sufficient</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>An arcology is distinguished from a merely large building in that it is designed to lessen the <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">impact of human habitation</a> on any given <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. It could be self-sustainable, employing all or most of its own available resources for a comfortable life: power; climate control; food production; air and water conservation and purification; sewage treatment; etc. An arcology is designed to make it possible to supply those items for a large population. An arcology would supply and maintain its own municipal or urban infrastructures in order to operate and connect with other urban environments apart from its own.</p> 
     <p>Arcology was proposed to reduce human impact on natural resources. Arcology designs might apply conventional building and civil engineering techniques in very large but practical projects in order to achieve pedestrian economies of scale that have proven, post-automobile, to be difficult to achieve in other ways.</p> 
     <p>Frank Lloyd Wright proposed an early version<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> called <a href="/wiki/Broadacre_City" title="Broadacre City">Broadacre City</a> although, in contrast to an arcology, Wright's idea is comparatively two-dimensional and depends on a road network. Wright's plan described transportation, agriculture, and commerce systems that would support an economy. Critics said that Wright's solution failed to account for population growth, and assumed a more rigid democracy than the U.S.A. actually has.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a> proposed the <a href="/wiki/Old_Man_River%27s_City_project" title="Old Man River's City project">Old Man River's City project</a>, a domed city with a capacity of 125,000, as a solution to the housing problems in <a href="/wiki/East_St._Louis,_Illinois" title="East St. Louis, Illinois">East St. Louis, Illinois</a>.</p> 
     <p>Paolo Soleri proposed later solutions, and coined the term 'arcology'.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Soleri describes ways of compacting city structures in three dimensions to combat two-dimensional urban sprawl, to economize on transportation and other energy uses. Like Wright, Soleri proposed changes in transportation, agriculture, and commerce. Soleri explored reductions in resource consumption and duplication, land reclamation; he also proposed to eliminate most private transportation. He advocated for greater "frugality" and favored greater use of shared social resources, including public transit (and public libraries).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_real-world_projects">Similar real-world projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Similar real-world projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Arcosanti" title="Arcosanti">Arcosanti</a> is an experimental "arcology prototype" - a demonstration project under construction in central <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. Designed by Paolo Soleri, its primary purpose is to demonstrate Soleri's personal designs, his application of principles of arcology to create a pedestrian-friendly urban form.</p> 
     <p>Many cities in the world have proposed projects adhering to the design principles of the arcology concept, like <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dongtan" title="Dongtan">Dongtan</a> near <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The Dongtan project may have collapsed, and it failed to open for the <a href="/wiki/Expo_2010" title="Expo 2010">Shanghai World Expo</a> in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Certain urban projects reflect arcology principles. Pedestrian connection systems often provide a wide range of goods and services in a single structure. Some examples include the <a href="/wiki/%2B15" title="+15">+15</a> system in downtown <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, Montréal’s <a href="/wiki/R%C3%89SO" title="RÉSO" class="mw-redirect">RÉSO</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Skyway_System" title="Minneapolis Skyway System">Minneapolis Skyway System</a> and The Windscreen in <a href="/wiki/Fermont" title="Fermont">Fermont, Quebec</a>. They include supermarkets, malls and entertainment complexes. The +15 is the world's most extensive skywalk, at 16&nbsp;km (9.9&nbsp;mi) in total length. Minneapolis has the longest single path, at 13&nbsp;km (8&nbsp;mi). <a href="/wiki/Seward%27s_Success,_Alaska" title="Seward's Success, Alaska">Seward's Success, Alaska</a> was never built, but would have been a small city just outside <a href="/wiki/Anchorage" title="Anchorage" class="mw-redirect">Anchorage</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> has a sizeable tunnel system known as the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Pedway" title="Chicago Pedway">Chicago Pedway</a> connecting a portion of the buildings in the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Loop" title="Chicago Loop">Chicago Loop</a>.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas Strip</a> has many arcology features to protect people from the 45&nbsp;°C (113&nbsp;°F) heat. Many major casinos are connected by tunnels, footbridges, and monorails. It is possible to travel from <a href="/wiki/Mandalay_Bay" title="Mandalay Bay">Mandalay Bay</a> at the south end of the Strip to the <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Convention_Center" title="Las Vegas Convention Center">Las Vegas Convention Center</a>, three miles (5&nbsp;km) to the north, without using streets. In many cases, it is possible to travel between several different casinos without ever going outdoors. It is possible to live in this complex without need to venture outside, except the Strip has not generally been considered self-sustainable. Soleri did not advocate for enclosed cities, although he did sketch a design and build a model of an 'arcology' for outer space.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> downtown area features an underground pedestrian network, <a href="/wiki/PATH_(Toronto)" title="PATH (Toronto)">PATH</a>. Multiple high-rises are connected by a series of underground tunnels. It is possible to live in this complex without needing to venture outside, but the PATH network is not self-sustaining, nor is it presently self-sustainable. The total network spans 28-kilometres (17&nbsp;mi).</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">McMurdo Station</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Antarctic_Program" title="United States Antarctic Program">United States Antarctic Program</a> and other scientific research stations on the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> resemble the popular conception of an arcology as a technologically advanced, relatively self-sufficient human community. The Antarctic research base provides living and entertainment amenities for roughly 3,000 staff who visit each year. Its remoteness and the measures needed to protect its population from the harsh environment give it an insular character. The station is not self-sufficient — the U.S. military delivers 30,000 cubic metres (800,000 US gal) of fuel and 5 kilotonnes (11&nbsp;million pounds) of supplies and equipment yearly through its <a href="/wiki/Operation_Deep_Freeze" title="Operation Deep Freeze">Operation Deep Freeze</a> resupply effort<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> — but it is isolated from conventional support networks. The base generates electricity with its own power plant, and grows fruits and vegetables in a hydroponic green house when resupply is non-existent. Under <a href="/wiki/Protocol_on_Environmental_Protection_to_the_Antarctic_Treaty" title="Protocol on Environmental Protection to the Antarctic Treaty">international treaty</a>, it must avoid damage to the surrounding ecosystem.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Crystal_Island" title="Crystal Island">Crystal Island</a> is a proposed arcology in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. In 2009, construction was postponed indefinitely due to the <a href="/wiki/Global_financial_crisis_of_2008%E2%80%932009" title="Global financial crisis of 2008–2009" class="mw-redirect">global economic crisis</a>.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Begich_Towers" title="Begich Towers">Begich Towers</a> operates like a small-scale arcology encompassing nearly all of the population of <a href="/wiki/Whittier,_Alaska" title="Whittier, Alaska">Whittier, Alaska</a>. The pair of buildings contains residential housing as well as a school, grocery, and municipal offices Whittier once boasted a second structure known as the Buckner Building. The Buckner Building still stands but was deemed unfit for habitation after the 1969 earthquake. <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Most proposals to build real arcologies have failed due to financial, structural or conceptual shortcomings. Arcologies are therefore found primarily in fictional works.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> One significant example is the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Fealty_(novel)" title="Oath of Fealty (novel)">Oath of Fealty</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">Larry Niven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Pournelle" title="Jerry Pournelle">Jerry Pournelle</a>. In the novel, a segment of the Los Angeles population has moved into an arcology. The plot examines the social changes that result, both inside and outside the arcology. Thus the arcology is presented not just as a plot device but as a subject of critique.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels_and_comics">Novels and comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Novels and comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>1899</b> – <a href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells" class="mw-redirect">H.G. Wells</a>'s story "<a href="/wiki/When_the_Sleeper_Wakes" title="When the Sleeper Wakes" class="mw-redirect">When the Sleeper Wakes</a>" describes a rudimentary version of pre-Soleri arcology, having developed from the evolution of transportation. These are hotel-like and dominate the surrounding landscape, having replaced all towns and cities, albeit preserving their names.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>1912</b> – <a href="/wiki/William_Hope_Hodgson" title="William Hope Hodgson">William Hope Hodgson</a>'s 1912 story "The Last Redoubt" from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Night_Land" title="The Night Land">The Night Land</a></i> features the first example of what we now would call an arcology, albeit with full artificial ecology, agriculture, public transport by mobile roadways. The future Earthlings depicted – millions of years into the future – have different reasons for building their metallic pyramid.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>1940–85</b> – In <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Robot_series_(Asimov)" title="Robot series (Asimov)"><i>Robot</i> series</a>, Earth's population lives in large hyperstructures simply called Cities. In Asimov's <i><a href="/wiki/Galactic_Empire_(series)" title="Galactic Empire (series)">Empire</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Foundation_series" title="Foundation series">Foundation</a></i> series, the capital planet <a href="/wiki/Trantor" title="Trantor">Trantor</a> of the galactic empire is a completely built-up planet (<a href="/wiki/Ecumenopolis" title="Ecumenopolis">ecumenopolis</a>), covered in its entirety with tall buildings and subterranean structures.</li> 
      <li><b>1967–68</b> – In <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Youd" title="Samuel Youd" class="mw-redirect">John Christopher</a>'s trilogy of novels <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tripods" title="The Tripods">The Tripods</a></i>, an alien race known as "the Masters" live in three huge, domed arcologies built on Earth to use as a base from which to colonise the planet. The structures are made from a golden material, and are capped with a crystal that replicates the atmospheric conditions of the Masters' home planet.</li> 
      <li><b>1971</b> – In the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_Inside" title="The World Inside">The World Inside</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Silverberg" title="Robert Silverberg">Robert Silverberg</a>, the characters all live in "Urban Monads", self-contained three-kilometer-high hyperstructures of 1000-storeys each, providing everything necessary to the society.</li> 
      <li><b>1974</b> – In the <a href="/wiki/Joe_Haldeman" title="Joe Haldeman">Joe Haldeman</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Forever_War" title="The Forever War">The Forever War</a></i> much of the action between William Mandella's first and second deployments occurs in an arcology.</li> 
      <li><b>1975</b> – <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a>'s novel <i>High Rise</i> features a luxury arcology in which disparity between social classes among the residents eventually leads to widespread anarchy and a reversion to primitive archetypes.</li> 
      <li><b>1977</b> – <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dosadi_Experiment" title="The Dosadi Experiment">The Dosadi Experiment</a></i> focuses on the creation of a super race through the control of another race, that forces them to live in an arcological situation.</li> 
      <li><b>1977–96</b> – In <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Benford" title="Gregory Benford">Gregory Benford</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center_Saga" title="Galactic Center Saga">Galactic Center Saga</a></i>, several thousand years in the future descendants of humanity have migrated deep towards the galactic core, to directly compete with the machine based civilizations. To survive, humanity is forced to transform itself, adding biological and cybernetic enhancements, to compete with the machines. Over 5000 years, as humanity falls further behind, culture decays from the Chandelier Age where humanity lived in great citadels between the stars, to the High Arcology Era and finally the Citadel Age.</li> 
      <li><b>1977–present</b> – In the <a href="/wiki/Judge_Dredd" title="Judge Dredd">Judge Dredd</a> comic stories, originally published in <i><a href="/wiki/2000_AD_(comic)" title="2000 AD (comic)" class="mw-redirect">2000 AD</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Megalopolis_(city_type)" title="Megalopolis (city type)">megalopolis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mega-City_One" title="Mega-City One">Mega-City One</a> consists of many hundreds, if not thousands, of <a href="/wiki/City_Block_(Judge_Dredd)" title="City Block (Judge Dredd)" class="mw-redirect">City Blocks</a>, in which a citizen can be born, grow, live, and die without ever leaving.</li> 
      <li><b>1982</b> – In <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">Larry Niven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Pournelle" title="Jerry Pournelle">Jerry Pournelle</a>'s collaboration <i><a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Fealty_(novel)" title="Oath of Fealty (novel)">Oath of Fealty</a></i>, much of the action is set in and around Todos Santos, an arcology built in a burnt-out section of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> that has evolved a separate culture from the city around it.</li> 
      <li><b>1984–88</b> – <a href="/wiki/William_Gibson" title="William Gibson">William Gibson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sprawl_trilogy" title="Sprawl trilogy">Sprawl trilogy</a> features various arcologies, including the "projects" (a megastructure built to provide heat, water, power, and food to its residents) and those built by global corporations to house their top research scientists. They are also featured in the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_Trilogy" title="Bridge Trilogy" class="mw-redirect">Bridge Trilogy</a>.</li> 
      <li><b>1986–present</b> – In <a href="/wiki/Lois_McMaster_Bujold" title="Lois McMaster Bujold">Lois McMaster Bujold</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Vorkosigan_Saga" title="Vorkosigan Saga">Vorkosigan Saga</a> novels, the inhabitants of the planet <a href="/wiki/Komarr_(fictional_planet)" title="Komarr (fictional planet)">Komarr</a> live in arcologies, because the surface of the planet is inhospitable.</li> 
      <li><b>1989–99</b> – <a href="/wiki/David_Wingrove" title="David Wingrove">David Wingrove</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chung_Kuo_(novel_series)" title="Chung Kuo (novel series)">Chung Kuo</a> series depicts a dystopian future Earth in which almost the entire population lives within several hyperstructures that are thousands of feet tall and span entire continents.</li> 
      <li><b>1996–99</b> – All the remaining cities of the Earth are hyperstructures in <a href="/wiki/Peter_F._Hamilton" title="Peter F. Hamilton">Peter F. Hamilton</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Night%27s_Dawn_Trilogy" title="The Night's Dawn Trilogy">The Night's Dawn Trilogy</a></i>. Arcology structures are located on worlds in <a href="/wiki/Peter_F._Hamilton" title="Peter F. Hamilton">Peter F. Hamilton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Dreaming_Void" title="The Dreaming Void">The Dreaming Void</a> (<a href="/wiki/Void_Trilogy" title="Void Trilogy">Void Trilogy</a>).</li> 
      <li><b>2015</b> - Arcologies are featured prominently in the plot of <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Bacigalupi" title="Paolo Bacigalupi">Paolo Bacigalupi</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Water_Knife" title="The Water Knife">The Water Knife</a></i>, which takes place in a drought-stricken near-future.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films_and_television">Films and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Films and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>1976</b> – The city in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Logan%27s_Run_(film)" title="Logan's Run (film)">Logan's Run</a></i> is entirely self-contained for all resources, except power, which is said to be supplied by water moved by the tides.</li> 
      <li><b>1982</b> – In the film <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>, the main offices of the fictional Tyrell Corporation (a <a href="/wiki/Megacorp" title="Megacorp" class="mw-redirect">Megacorp</a>) resemble a hyperstructure.</li> 
      <li><b>1985–89</b> – In the <a href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic" title="Post-apocalyptic" class="mw-redirect">post-apocalyptic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Appleseed_(manga)" title="Appleseed (manga)">Appleseed</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Masamune_Shirow" title="Masamune Shirow">Masamune Shirow</a>, in which hyperstructures dominate the skyline of the city Olympus.</li> 
      <li><b>1999</b> – In the science fiction movie series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i>, the last human city, known as <a href="/wiki/Zion_(The_Matrix)" title="Zion (The Matrix)">Zion</a>, is a hyperstructure. Due to nuclear scarring of the Earth's surface and atmosphere, the hyperstructure is buried deep underground. While ecologically sparse, the habitat's climate is controlled by complex machinery in the lower levels. The population is in the realm of 200,000. Due to the nature of the aggression from the machines, Zion is an example of a heavily fortified hyperstructure.</li> 
      <li><b>1999–2007</b> – The anime <i><a href="/wiki/GetBackers" title="GetBackers">GetBackers</a></i> has overarching plots involving a semi-constructed megabuilding called the Limitless Fortress. However, because its construction was abandoned, it has few inhabitants, and those it has may not all be (entirely) real.</li> 
      <li><b>2002</b> – In the film <i><a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_(film)" title="Equilibrium (film)">Equilibrium</a></i>, an arcology named Libria is the last human civilization, a society in which peace is kept by the forced administration of an injected liquid drug designed to completely suppress emotions.</li> 
      <li><b>2002</b> – In the anime of <i><a href="/wiki/Shangri-La_(novel)" title="Shangri-La (novel)">Shangri-La</a></i>, the arcology called Atlas stands behind a Tokyo abandoned after a devastating earthquake in the 2060s. It was intended to hold about three million people, and only those with connections or those who were chosen by lottery are allowed to live there.</li> 
      <li><b>2005</b> – In the film <i><a href="/wiki/Aeon_Flux_(film)" title="Aeon Flux (film)" class="mw-redirect">Aeon Flux</a></i>, Earth's surviving humans live in Bregna, an enclosed and self-sufficient city-state.</li> 
      <li><b>2005</b> – In the fourth-season finale of the science fiction show <i><a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(TV_series)" title="Andromeda (TV series)">Andromeda</a></i>, a large battle takes place in space around an antiquated space hyperstructure known simply as 'Arcology'.</li> 
      <li><b>2006–07</b> – In the anime <i><a href="/wiki/Code_Geass" title="Code Geass">Code Geass</a></i>, the Holy Britannian Empire constructs an arcology over the ruins of Tokyo called the Tokyo Settlement.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Will_Wright_(game_designer)" title="Will Wright (game designer)">Will Wright's</a> computer game <i><a href="/wiki/SimCity_2000" title="SimCity 2000">SimCity 2000</a></i> allows the construction of four different types of arcologies. Arcologies reappeared in <i><a href="/wiki/SimCity_(2013_video_game)" title="SimCity (2013 video game)">SimCity</a></i> as a "Great Work."</li> 
      <li>Another Wright game, <i><a href="/wiki/Spore_(2008_video_game)" title="Spore (2008 video game)">Spore</a></i>, features bubbled cities that serve the same function. In Wright's 1990 <i><a href="/wiki/SimEarth" title="SimEarth">SimEarth</a></i>, "Nanotech Age" cities eventually advance to an exodus of the entire sentient species of the planet.</li> 
      <li>Two levels of the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex:_Invisible_War" title="Deus Ex: Invisible War">Deus Ex: Invisible War</a></i> posits (circa 2072) a futuristic arcology, simply called the Arcology, on the edge of an ancient <a href="/wiki/Medina_quarter" title="Medina quarter">medina</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Chrono_Trigger" title="Chrono Trigger">Chrono Trigger</a></i>, the structure known as the Black Omen that appears across timelines after Queen Zeal summons Lavos to the Ocean Palace is defined as an arcology. The city of Chronopolis seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Chrono_Cross" title="Chrono Cross">Chrono Cross</a></i> can also be considered as an arcology.</li> 
      <li>In the computer game <i><a href="/wiki/Afterlife_(computer_game)" title="Afterlife (computer game)" class="mw-redirect">Afterlife</a></i>, the player controlling <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">Hell</a> can eventually purchase <i>Love Domes</i> or <i>Omnibolges</i>. Functioning similarly to arcologies, these structures are the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(philosophy)" title="Transcendence (philosophy)">transcended</a>/destroyed Heaven/Hells that are able to hold billions of souls.</li> 
      <li>In the computer game <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization:_Call_to_Power" title="Civilization: Call to Power">Civilization: Call to Power</a></i>, the "Arcology Advance," found in a near future part of the technology list, grants access to the Arcology building, which reduces overcrowding effects in its host city. This is also available in <i><a href="/wiki/Call_to_Power_II" title="Call to Power II">Call to Power II</a></i>.</li> 
      <li>The tutorial in the computer game <i><a href="/wiki/Dystopia_(computer_game)" title="Dystopia (computer game)" class="mw-redirect">Dystopia</a></i> takes place in Yggdrasil's first arcology.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Mass_Effect" title="Mass Effect">Mass Effect</a></i> the Codex (an in-game encyclopedia) explains that Earth is composed mainly of Arcology buildings.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_VII" title="Final Fantasy VII">Final Fantasy VII</a></i> the city of <i><a href="/wiki/Midgar" title="Midgar" class="mw-redirect">Midgar</a></i> and the military base of <i><a href="/wiki/Gaia_(Final_Fantasy_VII)#Geography" title="Gaia (Final Fantasy VII)" class="mw-redirect">Junon</a></i> (both are built by the Shinra Company) are examples of arcology.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_VIII" title="Final Fantasy VIII">Final Fantasy VIII</a></i> the city of <i><a href="/wiki/Esthar" title="Esthar" class="mw-redirect">Esthar</a></i> and the flying universities of Galbadia and Balamb are arcologies.</li> 
      <li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Outpost_(video_game)" title="Outpost (video game)">Outpost</a></i> computer game and its sequel both focus on building arcologies (called 'colonies' in the game) on various planets to contain what remains of Humanity after Earth is obliterated by an asteroid.</li> 
      <li>The game <a href="/wiki/Brink_(video_game)" title="Brink (video game)">Brink</a> is set on a futuristic arcology to preserve humanity after a natural flooding disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_XIII" title="Final Fantasy XIII">Final Fantasy XIII</a></i>, Cocoon is an example of an arcology.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Xenogears" title="Xenogears">Xenogears</a></i>, the city of Nortune is the remains of a very ancient (4000 + years) military base that is a self-contained city.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p><b>Role-playing and table-top games</b></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>In the table-top strategy game <i><a href="/wiki/Warhammer_40,000" title="Warhammer 40,000">Warhammer 40,000</a></i>, hyperstructures, called "hives," are extremely common and are the main method of housing large populations in the billions. Arcologies are so widespread that some planets, dubbed 'hive worlds', are constructed entirely of hyperstructures. <i><a href="/wiki/Necromunda" title="Necromunda">Necromunda</a></i>, an offshoot game set in the Warhammer 40,000 universe, involves conflict between rival gangs on the hive world of Necromunda.</li> 
      <li>In the RPG <i><a href="/wiki/Shadowrun" title="Shadowrun">Shadowrun</a></i>, a number of hyperstructures such as the "Renraku Arcology" exist by 2050, most of which are mega-corporate controlled. A major theme to these is the desire of a large corporation to control every aspect of its employees' lives. A major meta-plot element was the sealing off of the aforementioned Renraku Arcology in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> when the advanced computer control system <i>awakened</i> into a self-aware <a href="/wiki/Artificial_Intelligence" title="Artificial Intelligence" class="mw-redirect">AI</a> named Deus.</li> 
      <li>In the RPG <i><a href="/wiki/Trinity_(role-playing_game)" title="Trinity (role-playing game)">Trinity</a></i>, a number of hyperstructures exist, with the largest being that of the New New York Arcology run by the Psi-Order Orgotek.</li> 
      <li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Rifts_(role-playing_game)" title="Rifts (role-playing game)">Rifts</a></i> RPG, the capital of the Coalition States is the city of Chi-Town. Along with some of the other major Coalition cities, Chi-Town is considered a "Mega-City" in that its entire population is housed inside one giant structure consisting of more than thirty levels, each of which are several stories high and contain a number of sub-levels.</li> 
      <li>In WildFire's <i><a href="/wiki/CthulhuTech" title="CthulhuTech">CthulhuTech</a></i> RPG, humanity has been forced to live in fortified arcologies due to attacks from the Old Ones and the Migou.</li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Mind_Storm_Labs" title="Mind Storm Labs">Mind Storm Labs</a>'s <i>Alpha Omega</i> RPG, the world's populations have retreated into arcology city-states to protect themselves from the war-torn decimation of the Earth's surface</li> 
      <li>The <a href="/wiki/Fallout_(series)" title="Fallout (series)"><i>Fallout</i> franchise</a>, created in 1997 by Interplay, contains several self-sustaining vaults that are cities. Each vault can hold around 1000 people, has its own self-sustaining power supply, water treatment center and built in food creators. Most vaults however, are known to have failed in less than 50 years or so in the series timeline, from attacks by enclave or mutants, destruction, or accidents. The only known vaults to be completely running 200 years after the bombs dropped are Vault 101, located in Washington DC, and Vault 21, Located in Las Vegas, Nevada.</li> 
      <li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Android:_Netrunner" title="Android: Netrunner">Android: Netrunner</a></i>, players controlling a corporation can construct arcologies or control resources associated with them.</li> 
     </ul>
