   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Low-energy house</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p>A <b>low-energy house</b> is any type of house that from design, technologies and building products uses less energy, from any source, than a traditional or average contemporary house. In the practice of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_design" title="Sustainable design">sustainable design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low-energy_building" title="Low-energy building" class="mw-redirect">low-energy building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy-efficient_landscaping" title="Energy-efficient landscaping">energy-efficient landscaping</a> low-energy houses often use <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">active solar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passive solar building design</a> techniques and components to reduce their energy expenditure.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_usage">General usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The meaning of the term 'low-energy house' has changed over time, but in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> it generally refers to a house that uses around half of the German or Swiss low-energy standards referred to below for <a href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating" class="mw-redirect">space heating</a>, typically in the range from 30 kWh/m²a to 20 kWh/m²a (9,500 Btu/ft²/yr to 6,300 Btu/ft²/yr). Below this the term 'Ultra-low-energy building' is often used.</p> 
     <p>The term can also refer to any dwelling whose energy use is below the standards demanded by current <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">building codes</a>. Because national standards vary considerably around the world, 'low-energy' developments in one country may not meet 'normal practice' in another.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_standards">National standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: National standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In some countries the term relates to a specific building standard.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In particular, these seek to limit the energy used for space heating, since in many <a href="/wiki/Climate_zone" title="Climate zone" class="mw-redirect">climate zones</a> it represents the largest energy use. Other energy use may also be regulated. The <a href="/wiki/History_of_passive_solar_building_design" title="History of passive solar building design">history of passive solar building design</a> gives an international look at one form of low-energy building development and standards.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> a low-energy house <i>(Niedrigenergiehaus)</i> has a limit equivalent to 7 litres of heating oil for each square metre of room <i>for space heating</i> annually (50 kWh/m²a or 15,850 Btu/ft²/yr). In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, the term is used in connection with the <i>MINERGIE</i> standard (42 kWh/m²a or 13,300&nbsp;Btu/ft²/yr) or the Minergie-P (equivalent to the Passivhaus).</p> 
     <p>In comparison, the German <i><a href="/wiki/Passivhaus" title="Passivhaus" class="mw-redirect">Passivhaus</a></i> ultra-low-energy standard, currently undergoing adoption in some other <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries, has a maximum space heating requirement of 15 kWh/m²a or 4,755&nbsp;Btu/ft²/yr.</p> 
     <p>A "sub-10 passive house" is under construction in Ireland that has an independently evaluated PHPP (Passive House) rating of 9.5&nbsp;kW/m<sup>2</sup>/year. Its form of construction also tackles the issue of embodied energy, which can significantly distort the lifecycle CO<sub>2</sub> emissions associated with even low energy use houses.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">ENERGY STAR</a> program is the largest program defining low-energy homes and <a href="/wiki/Consumer_product" title="Consumer product" class="mw-redirect">consumer products</a>. Homes earning ENERGY STAR certification use at least 15% less energy than standard new homes built to the International Residential Code, although homes typically achieve 20%–30% savings.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In addition, the US Department of Energy launched a program in 2008 with the goal of spreading zero-energy housing over the US. Currently, participating builders commit to constructing new homes that achieve 30% savings on a <a href="/wiki/Home_energy_rating" title="Home energy rating">home energy rating</a> scale.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zero-energy_and_energy-plus_buildings">Zero-energy and energy-plus buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zero-energy and energy-plus buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Beyond ultra-low-energy buildings are those that use, on average over the course of a year, no imported energy - <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_building" title="Zero-energy building">zero-energy buildings</a> – or even those that generate a surplus - <a href="/wiki/Energy-plus_house" title="Energy-plus house" class="mw-redirect">energy-plus houses</a> – both of which have been and are being successfully built.</p> 
     <p>This can be achieved by a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> technologies and the use of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources. However, in the absence of recognized standards, the mix between these – and consequently the energy-use profile and environmental impact of the building – can vary significantly.</p> 
     <p>At one end of the spectrum are buildings with an ultra-low space heating requirement that therefore require low levels of imported energy, even in winter, approaching the concept of an <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_building" title="Autonomous building">autonomous building</a>.</p> 
     <p>At the opposite end of the spectrum are buildings where few attempts are made to reduce the space heating requirement and which therefore use high levels of imported energy in winter. While this can be balanced by high levels of renewable energy generation throughout the year, it imposes greater demands on the traditional national energy infrastructure during the peak winter season.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-energy_technology">Low-energy technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Low-energy technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_low-energy_building_techniques" title="List of low-energy building techniques">List of low-energy building techniques</a>
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      For history and further detail, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_energy_building" title="Zero energy building" class="mw-redirect">Zero energy building</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Passivhaus" title="Passivhaus" class="mw-redirect">Passivhaus</a>.
     </div> 
     <p>Low-energy buildings typically use high levels of <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulation</a>, energy efficient <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">windows</a>, low levels of air infiltration and <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">heat recovery ventilation</a> to lower heating and cooling energy. They may also use <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passive solar building design</a> techniques or <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">active solar</a> technologies. These homes may use <a href="/wiki/Hot_water_heat_recycling" title="Hot water heat recycling" class="mw-redirect">hot water heat recycling</a> technologies to recover heat from showers and dishwashers. Lighting and <a href="/wiki/Miscellaneous_electric_load" title="Miscellaneous electric load">miscellaneous</a> energy use is allieviated with <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lighting</a> and efficient appliances. <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">Weatherization</a> provides more information on increasing building energy efficiency.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Passive_House" title="Passive House" class="mw-redirect">Passive Houses</a> are required to achieve a whole building <a href="/wiki/Air_changes_per_hour" title="Air changes per hour">air change rate</a> of no more than 0.6 ac/hr <sup id="cite_ref-Passivhaus_ac_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Passivhaus_ac-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-ac.2Fhr_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ac.2Fhr-5"><span>[</span>footnote 1<span>]</span></a></sup> under forced pressurisation and depressurisation testing at 50Pa minimum. On site blower door testing by certified testers is used to prove compliance.</p> 
     <p>A significant feature of ultra-low-energy buildings is the increasing importance of heat loss through linear thermal bridging within the construction. Failure to eliminate thermal pathways from warm to cold surfaces ("bridges") creates the conditions for <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_condensation" title="Interstitial condensation">interstitial condensation</a> forming deep within the construction and lead to potentially serious issues of mould growth and rot. With near zero filtration losses through the fabric of the dwelling, air movement cannot be relied upon to dry out the construction and a comprehensive condensation risk analysis of every abutment detail is recommended.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_design_and_landscape">Passive solar design and landscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Passive solar design and landscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">Passive solar building design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy-efficient_landscaping" title="Energy-efficient landscaping">energy-efficient landscaping</a> support the low-energy house in conservation and can integrate them into a <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood" title="Neighborhood" class="mw-redirect">neighborhood</a> and environment. Following <a href="/wiki/List_of_low-energy_building_techniques" title="List of low-energy building techniques">passive solar building techniques</a>, where possible buildings are compact in shape to reduce their surface area, with principal windows oriented towards the equator - south in the northern hemisphere and north in the southern hemisphere - to maximize passive <a href="/wiki/Solar_gain" title="Solar gain">solar gain</a>. However, the use of solar gain, especially in <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> regions, is secondary to minimizing the overall house energy requirements. In climates and regions needing to reduce excessive summer passive solar heat gain, whether from the direct or reflected sources, can be done with a <a href="/wiki/Brise_soleil" title="Brise soleil">Brise soleil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, attached <a href="/wiki/Pergola" title="Pergola">pergolas</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vertical_garden" title="Vertical garden" class="mw-redirect">vertical gardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a>, and other techniques.</p> 
     <p>Low-energy houses can be constructed from dense or lightweight materials, but some internal <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">thermal mass</a> is normally incorporated to reduce summer peak temperatures, maintain stable winter temperatures, and prevent possible overheating in spring or autumn before the higher <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate" class="mw-redirect">sun angle</a> "shades" mid-day wall exposure and window penetration. Exterior wall color, when the surface allows choice, for reflection or absorption <a href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation" class="mw-redirect">insolation</a> qualities depends on the predominant year-round ambient outdoor temperature. The use of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> trees and wall <a href="/wiki/Trellis_(architecture)" title="Trellis (architecture)">trellised</a> or self attaching vines can assist in climates not at the temperature extremes.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting_and_electrical_appliances">Lighting and electrical appliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-energy_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lighting and electrical appliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">Daylighting</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_daylighting" title="Passive daylighting">Passive daylighting</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Active_daylighting" title="Active daylighting">Active daylighting</a>
     </div> 
     <p>To minimize the total primary energy consumption, the many <a href="/wiki/Passive_daylighting" title="Passive daylighting">passive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_daylighting" title="Active daylighting">active</a> <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">daylighting</a> techniques are the first daytime solution to employ. For low light level days, non-daylighted spaces, and nighttime; the use of creative-sustainable <a href="/wiki/Lighting_design" title="Lighting design" class="mw-redirect">lighting design</a> using low-energy sources such as 'standard voltage' <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">compact fluorescent lamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_lighting" title="Solid-state lighting">solid-state lighting</a> with <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">Light-emitting diode</a>-<a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lamps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_light-emitting_diode" title="Organic light-emitting diode" class="mw-redirect">organic light-emitting diodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_lighting#Polymer_light-emitting_diodes" title="Solid-state lighting">PLED - polymer light-emitting diodes</a>; and 'low voltage' <a href="/wiki/Electrical_filament" title="Electrical filament" class="mw-redirect">electrical filament</a>-<a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">Incandescent light bulbs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_discharge_metal_halide_lamp" title="Ceramic discharge metal halide lamp" class="mw-redirect">compact Metal halide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenon_arc_lamps" title="Xenon arc lamps" class="mw-redirect">Xenon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halogen_lamps" title="Halogen lamps" class="mw-redirect">Halogen lamps</a>, can be used.</p> 
     <p>Solar powered exterior circulation, security, and <a href="/wiki/Landscape_lighting" title="Landscape lighting">landscape lighting</a> - with <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_cell" title="Photovoltaic cell" class="mw-redirect">photovoltaic cells</a> on each fixture or connecting to a central <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">Solar panel</a> system, are available for <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> and outdoor needs. Low voltage systems can be used for more controlled or independent illumination, while still using less electricity than conventional fixtures and lamps. Timers, <a href="/wiki/Motion_detection" title="Motion detection">motion detection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_lighting" title="Natural lighting" class="mw-redirect">natural light</a> operation sensors reduce energy consumption, and <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> even further for a Low-energy house setting.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">Appliance</a> <a href="/wiki/Consumer_product" title="Consumer product" class="mw-redirect">consumer products</a> meeting independent energy efficiency testing and receiving <a href="/wiki/Ecolabel" title="Ecolabel">Ecolabel</a> <a href="/wiki/Certification_mark" title="Certification mark">certification marks</a> for reduced electrical-'natural-gas' consumption and product manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission_label" title="Carbon emission label">carbon emission labels</a> are preferred for use in Low-energy houses. The ecolabel certification marks of <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> and <a href="/wiki/EKOenergy" title="EKOenergy">EKOenergy</a> are examples.</p>
