     <p>A <b>green roof</b> or <b>living roof</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roof</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> that is partially or completely covered with vegetation and a growing medium, planted over a <a href="/wiki/Waterproofing#Construction_waterproofing" title="Waterproofing">waterproofing membrane</a>. It may also include additional layers such as a <a href="/wiki/Root_barrier" title="Root barrier">root barrier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> systems. <a href="/wiki/Container_garden" title="Container garden">Container gardens</a> on roofs, where plants are maintained in pots, are not generally considered to be true green roofs, although this is debated. Rooftop ponds are another form of green roofs which are used to treat <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a>.</p> 
     <p>Green roofs serve several purposes for a building, such as absorbing <a href="/wiki/Rainwater" title="Rainwater" class="mw-redirect">rainwater</a>, providing <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulation</a>, creating a habitat for wildlife, increasing benevolence and decreasing stress of the people around the roof by providing a more aesthetically pleasing landscape, and helping to lower urban air temperatures and mitigate the <a href="/wiki/Heat_island_effect" title="Heat island effect" class="mw-redirect">heat island effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They effectively utilize the natural functions of plants to filter water and treat air in urban and suburban landscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are two types of green roof: intensive roofs, which are thicker, with a minimum depth of 12.8&nbsp;cm (5.0&nbsp;in), and can support a wider variety of plants but are heavier and require more maintenance, and extensive roofs, which are shallow, ranging in depth from 2&nbsp;cm (0.79&nbsp;in) to 12.7&nbsp;cm (5.0&nbsp;in), lighter than intensive green roofs, and require minimal maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The term <i>green roof</i> may also be used to indicate roofs that use some form of green technology, such as a <a href="/wiki/Cool_roof" title="Cool roof" class="mw-redirect">cool roof</a>, a roof with <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar thermal collectors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_panels" title="Photovoltaic panels" class="mw-redirect">photovoltaic panels</a>. Green roofs are also referred to as <i>eco-roofs</i>, <i>oikosteges</i>, <i>vegetated roofs</i>, <i>living roofs</i>, <i>greenroofs</i> and <i>VCP<sub>H</sub><sup id="cite_ref-VCP_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCP-4">[4]</a></sup> (Horizontal Vegetated Complex Partitions)</i></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_benefits">Environmental benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Environmental benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG/200px-CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG" width="200" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG/300px-CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG/400px-CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3888"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption">

         <a href="/wiki/File:CalifAcadSciRoof_0820.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A modern green roof (

        <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>

     <p>Green roofs are used to:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Reduce <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">heating</a> (by adding <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and thermal resistance value)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>A 2005 study by Brad Bass of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> showed that green roofs can also reduce heat loss and energy consumption in winter conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Reduce cooling (by <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling" class="mw-redirect">evaporative cooling</a>) loads on a building by fifty to ninety percent,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> especially if it is glassed in so as to act as a <a href="/wiki/Terrarium" title="Terrarium">terrarium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passive solar</a> heat reservoir – a concentration of green roofs in an urban area can even reduce the city's average temperatures during the summer</li> 
      <li>Reduce <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> run off<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> — see <a href="/wiki/Water-wise_gardening" title="Water-wise gardening" class="mw-redirect">water-wise gardening</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>—A study presented at the Green Roofs for Healthy Cities Conference in June 2004, cited by the EPA, found water runoff was reduced by over 75% during rainstorms. See the PDF at <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> for more information.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Natural Habitat Creation<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> — see <a href="/wiki/Urban_wilderness" title="Urban wilderness">urban wilderness</a></li> 
      <li>Filter <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> out of the air which helps lower disease rates such as asthma<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>— see <a href="/wiki/Green_wall" title="Green wall">green wall</a></li> 
      <li>Filter pollutants and heavy metals out of rainwater</li> 
      <li>Help to insulate a building for sound; the soil helps to block lower frequencies and the plants block higher frequencies<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>If installed correctly many living roofs can contribute to <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> points</li> 
      <li>Increase agricultural space</li> 
      <li>With green roofs, water is stored by the substrate and then taken up by the plants from where it is returned to the atmosphere through transpiration and evaporation.</li> 
      <li>Green roofs not only retain rainwater, but also moderate the temperature of the water and act as natural filters for any of the water that happens to run off.</li> 
     </ul>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

        <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_New_York_College_of_Environmental_Science_and_Forestry" title="State University of New York College of Environmental Science and Forestry">SUNY-ESF</a>, Syracuse, NY

     <p>Many green roofs are installed to comply with local regulations and government fees, often regarding <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> runoff management.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In areas with <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer" title="Combined sewer">combined sewer-stormwater systems</a>, heavy storms can <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">overload</a> the <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">wastewater system</a> and cause it to flood, dumping raw sewage into the local waterways. Green roofs decrease the total amount of runoff and slow the rate of runoff from the roof. It has been found that they can retain up to 75% of rainwater, gradually releasing it back into the atmosphere via <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>, while retaining pollutants in their soil.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Often, phosphorus and nitrogen are in this category of environmentally harmful substances even though they are stimulating to the growth of plant life and agriculture. When these substances are added to a system, it can create mass biological activity since they are considered limiting factors of plant growth and by adding more of them to a system, it allows for more plant growth.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.momirents.com/Elevation314/elevationMain.html">Elevation 314</a>, a new development in Washington, D.C. uses green roofs to filter and store some of its storm water on site, avoiding the need for expensive underground sand filters to meet D.C. Department of Health storm-water regulations.</p> 
     <p>Combating the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island_effect" title="Urban heat island effect" class="mw-redirect">urban heat island effect</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> is another reason for creating a green roof. Traditional building materials soak up the sun's <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation" class="mw-redirect">radiation</a> and re-emit it as heat, making cities at least 4&nbsp;°C (7.2&nbsp;°F) hotter than surrounding areas. On Chicago's City Hall, by contrast, which features a green roof, roof temperatures on a hot day are typically 1.4–4.4&nbsp;°C (2.5–7.9&nbsp;°F) cooler than they are on traditionally roofed buildings nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Green roofs are becoming common in Chicago, as well as in Atlanta, Portland, and other United States cities, where their use is encouraged by regulations to combat the urban heat-island effect. Green roofs are a type of <a href="/wiki/Low-impact_development_(UK)" title="Low-impact development (UK)">low impact development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the case of Chicago, the city has passed codes offering incentives to builders who put green roofs on their buildings. The Chicago City Hall green roof is one of the earliest and most well-known examples of green roofs in the United States; it was planted as an experiment to determine the effects a green roof would have on the microclimate of the roof. Following this and other studies, it has now been estimated that if all the roofs in a major city were greened, urban temperatures could be reduced by as much as 7 degrees Celsius.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Green roofs also provide <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> for plants, insects, and animals that otherwise have limited natural space in cities. Even in high-rise urban settings as tall as 19 stories, it has been found that green roofs can attract beneficial insects, birds, bees and butterflies. Rooftop greenery complements wild areas by providing stepping stones for songbirds, migratory birds and other wildlife facing <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">shortages of natural habitat</a>.</p> 
     <p>An additional environmental benefit of greens roofs is the ability to sequester carbon. Carbon is the main component of plant matter and is naturally absorbed by plant tissue. The carbon is stored in the plant tissue and the soil substrate through plant litter and root exudates.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowe_2100.E2.80.932110_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowe_2100.E2.80.932110-20">[20]</a></sup> A study on green roofs in Michigan and Maryland found the above ground biomass and below ground substrate stored on average between 168 g C m<sup>−2</sup> and 107 g C m<sup>−2</sup> . Variations occurred among the different species of plant used. Substrate carbon content averaged 913 g C m<sup>−2</sup> and after the subtraction of the original carbon content the total sequestration was 378 g C m<sup>−2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Getter_7564.E2.80.937570_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Getter_7564.E2.80.937570-21">[21]</a></sup> The sequestration can be improved by changing plant species, increasing substrate depth, substrate composition, and management practices. In a study done in Michigan above ground sequestration ranged from 64 g C m<sup>−2</sup> to 239 g C m<sup>−2</sup> for S. acre and S album.<sup id="cite_ref-Getter_7564.E2.80.937570_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Getter_7564.E2.80.937570-21">[21]</a></sup> Also, by increasing the substrate depth would allow for more area of carbon storage and diversify the types of plants with greater potential of carbon storage. The direct carbon sequestration techniques and methods can be measured and accounted for. Green roofs also indirectly reduce CO<sub>2</sub> given off by power plants through their ability to insulate buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowe_2100.E2.80.932110_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowe_2100.E2.80.932110-20">[20]</a></sup> Buildings in the US account for 38% of the total carbon dioxide emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A model supported by the U.S. Department of Energy found a 2 percent reduction in electricity consumption and 9-11% reduction in natural gas when implementing green roofs.</p>

     <p>A properly designed and installed extensive green-roof system can cost $108–248/m2 ($10–23/ft2) while an intensive green roof costs $355–2368/m2 ($33–220/ft2) however, since most of the materials used to build the green roof can be salvaged it is estimated that the cost of replacing a green roof is generally one third of the initial installation costs.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>With the initial cost of installing a green roof in mind, there are many financial benefits that accompany green roofing.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Green roofing can extend the lifespan of a roof by over 200% by covering the waterproofing membrane with growing medium and vegetation, this shields the membrane from ultra-violet radiation and physical damage.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Further, Penn State University’s Green Roof Research Center expects the lifespan of a roof to increase by as much as three times after greening the roof.<sup id="cite_ref-hortweb1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hortweb1-25">[25]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>It is estimated that the installation of a green roof could increase the real estate value of an average house by about 7%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Reduction in energy use is an important property of green roofing. By improving the thermal performance of a roof, green roofing allows buildings to better retain their heat during the cooler winter months while reflecting and absorbing solar radiation during the hotter summer months, allowing buildings to remain cooler. A study conducted by Environment Canada found a 26% reduction in summer cooling needs and a 26% reduction in winter heat losses when a green roof is used.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> With respect to hotter summer weather, green roofing is able to reduce the solar heating of a building by reflecting 27% of solar radiation, absorbing 60% by the vegetation through photosynthesis and evapotranspiration, and absorbing the remaining 13% into the growing medium. Such mitigation of solar radiation has been found to reduce building temperatures by up to 20&nbsp;°C and reduce energy needs for air-conditioning by 25% to 80%. This reduction in energy required to cool a building in the summer is accompanied by a reduction in energy required to heat a building in the winter, thus reducing the energy requirements of the building year-round which allows the building temperature to be controlled at a lower cost.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Depending on the region in which a green roof is installed, incentives may be available in the form of stormwater tax reduction, grants, or rebates. The regions where these incentives will most likely be found are areas where failing storm water management infrastructure is in place, urban heat island effect has significantly increased the local air temperature, or areas where environmental contaminants in the storm water runoff is of great concern.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> An example of such an incentive is a one-year property tax credit is available in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, since 2009, for property owners who green at least 50% of their roof area.<sup id="cite_ref-EL_NYC_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EL_NYC-30">[30]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul>

     <p>The main disadvantage of green roofs is that the initial cost of installing a green roof can be double that of a normal roof.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The additional mass of the soil substrate and retained water places a large strain on the structural support of a building. This makes it unlikely for intensive green roofs to become widely implemented due to a lack of buildings that are able to support such a large amount of added weight as well as the added cost of reinforcing buildings to be able to support such weight.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some types of green roofs do have more demanding structural standards especially in seismic regions of the world. Some existing buildings cannot be retrofitted with certain kinds of green roof because of the weight load of the substrate and vegetation exceeds permitted <a href="/wiki/Static_load" title="Static load" class="mw-redirect">static loading</a>. Depending on what kind of green roof it is, the maintenance costs could be higher, but some types of green roof have little or no ongoing cost. Some kinds of green roofs also place higher demands on the <a href="/wiki/Waterproofing" title="Waterproofing">waterproofing</a> system of the structure, both because water is retained on the roof and due to the possibility of roots penetrating the waterproof membrane. Another disadvantage is that the wildlife they attract may include <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a> insects which could easily infiltrate a residential building through open windows.</p> 
     <p>Green roofs require significantly more maintenance and maintenance energy compared to a standard roof. Standard maintenance include removing debris, controlling weeds, deadhead trimming, checking moisture levels, and fertilizing.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The maintenance energy use for green roofs has many variables including: climate, intensity of rainfall, type of building, type of vegetation, and external coatings.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> The most significant effect comes from scarce rainfall which will increase the maintenance energy due to the watering required. During a 10-year roof maintenance cycle a house with a green roof requires more retrofit embodied energy than a house with a white roof. The individual components of a green have CO<sub>2</sub> implications during the manufacturing process have additional implications compared to a conventional roof.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The embodied energy for green roof components are 23.6&nbsp;kg (52&nbsp;lb) CO<sub>2</sub> per square meter of green roof. This value is equivalent to 6448 g C m<sup>−2</sup> which is significantly greater than 378 g C m<sup>-2.</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-:0_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Green roofs can be categorized as intensive, semi-intensive, or extensive, depending on the depth of planting medium and the amount of maintenance they need. Extensive green roofs traditionally support 10-25 pounds of vegetation per square foot (50–120&nbsp;kg/m<sup>2</sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> while intensive roofs support 80-150 pounds of vegetation per square foot (390–730&nbsp;kg/m<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Traditional <a href="/wiki/Roof_garden" title="Roof garden">roof gardens</a>, which require a reasonable depth of soil to grow large plants or conventional lawns, are considered intensive because they are labour-intensive, requiring irrigation, feeding, and other maintenance. Intensive roofs are more park-like with easy access and may include anything from kitchen herbs to shrubs and small trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Seattle.2C_2007_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle.2C_2007-38">[38]</a></sup> Extensive green roofs, by contrast, are designed to be virtually self-sustaining and should require only a minimum of maintenance, perhaps a once-yearly weeding or an application of slow-release fertiliser to boost growth. Extensive roofs are usually only accessed for maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-Seattle.2C_2007_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle.2C_2007-38">[38]</a></sup> They can be established on a very thin layer of soil (most use specially formulated composts): even a thin layer of <a href="/wiki/Mineral_wool" title="Mineral wool">rockwool</a> laid directly onto a watertight roof can support a planting of <i><a href="/wiki/Sedum" title="Sedum">Sedum</a></i> species and <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a>. Some green roof designs incorporate both intensive and extensive elements. To protect the roof, a waterproofing membrane is often used, which is manufactured to remain watertight in extreme conditions including constant dampness, ponding water, high and low alkaline conditions and exposure to plant roots, fungi and bacterial organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_.28Planted.29_Roofs_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_.28Planted.29_Roofs-39">[39]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Advances in green roof technology have led to the development of new systems that do not fit into the traditional classification of green roof types. Comprehensive green roofs bring the most advantageous qualities of extensive and intensive green roofs together. Comprehensive green roofs support plant varieties typically seen in intensive green roofs at the depth and weight of an extensive green roof system.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Another important distinction is between pitched green roofs and flat green roofs. Pitched <a href="/wiki/Sod_roof" title="Sod roof">sod roofs</a>, a traditional feature of many Scandinavian buildings, tend to be of a simpler design than flat green roofs. This is because the pitch of the roof reduces the risk of water penetrating through the roof structure, allowing the use of fewer waterproofing and drainage layers.</p>

     <p>Modern green roofs, which are made of a system of manufactured layers deliberately placed over roofs to support growing medium and vegetation, are a relatively new phenomenon. However, green roofs or <a href="/wiki/Sod_roof" title="Sod roof">sod roofs</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> have been around for centuries. The modern trend started when green roofs were developed in Germany in the 1960s, and has since spread to many countries. Today, it is estimated that about 10% of all German roofs have been “greened”.<sup id="cite_ref-hortweb1_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hortweb1-25">[25]</a></sup> Green roofs are also becoming increasingly popular in the United States, although they are not as common as in some parts of Europe.</p> 
     <p>A number of European Countries have very active associations promoting green roofs, including Germany, Switzerland, the Netherlands, Norway, Italy, Austria, Hungary, Sweden, the UK, and Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The City of Linz in Austria has been paying developers to install green roofs since 1983, and in Switzerland it has been a federal law since the late 1990s. In the UK, their uptake has been slow, but a number of cities have developed policies to encourage their use, notably London and Sheffield.</p> 
     <p>Rooftop <a href="/wiki/Greywater_treatment" title="Greywater treatment" class="mw-redirect">water purification</a> is also being implemented in green roofs. These forms of green roofs are actually <a href="/wiki/Treatment_pond#Rooftop_treatment_ponds" title="Treatment pond">treatment ponds</a> built into the rooftops. They are built either from a simple substrate (as being done in <a href="/wiki/Dongtan" title="Dongtan">Dongtan</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>) or with plant-based ponds (as being done by WaterWorks UK Grow System<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and Waterzuiveren.be<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Plants used include <a href="/wiki/Calamus_(palm)" title="Calamus (palm)">calamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menyanthes_trifoliata" title="Menyanthes trifoliata" class="mw-redirect">Menyanthes trifoliata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mentha_aquatica" title="Mentha aquatica">Mentha aquatica</a>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>)</p> 
     <p>Several studies have been carried out in Germany since the 1970s. Berlin is one of the most important centers of green roof research in Germany. Particularly in the last 10 years, much more research has begun. About ten green roof research centers exist in the US and activities exist in about 40 countries. In a recent study on the impacts of green infrastructure, in particular green roofs in the Greater Manchester area, researchers found that adding green roofs can help keep temperatures down, particularly in urban areas: “adding green roofs to all buildings can have a dramatic effect on maximum surface temperatures, keeping temperatures below the 1961–1990 current form case for all time periods and emissions scenarios. Roof greening makes the biggest difference…where the building proportion is high and the evaporative fraction is low. Thus, the largest difference was made in the town centers.”<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brown_roofs">Brown roofs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brown roofs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Industrial <a href="/wiki/Brownfield" title="Brownfield" class="mw-redirect">brownfield</a> sites can be valuable ecosystems, supporting rare species of plants, animals and invertebrates. Increasingly in demand for redevelopment, these habitats are under threat. "Brown roofs", also known as "biodiverse roofs",<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> can partly mitigate this loss of habitat by covering the flat roofs of new developments with a layer of locally sourced material. Construction techniques for brown roofs are typically similar to those used to create flat green roofs, the main difference being the choice of growing medium (usually locally sourced rubble, gravel, soil, etc...) to meet a specific biodiversity objective.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In Switzerland, it is common to use alluvial gravels from the foundations; in London, a mix of brick rubble and some concrete has been used. The original idea was to allow the roofs to self-colonise with plants, but they are sometimes seeded to increase their biodiversity potential in the short term. Such practices are derided by purists.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The roofs are colonised by spiders and insects (many of which are becoming extremely rare in the UK as such sites are developed) and provide a feeding site for insectivorous birds. <a href="/wiki/Laban_Dance_Centre" title="Laban Dance Centre" class="mw-redirect">Laban</a>, a centre for contemporary dance in London, has a brown roof specifically designed to encourage the nationally rare <a href="/wiki/Black_redstart" title="Black redstart">black redstart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> A green roof, 160m above ground level, and claimed to be the highest in the UK and Europe "and probably in the world" to act as nature reserve, is on the Barclays Bank HQ in <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf" title="Canary Wharf">Canary Wharf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Designed combining the principles of green and brown roofs, it is already home to a range of rare invertebrates.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_by_country">Examples by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Examples by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Green roofs have been increasing in popularity in Australia over the past 10 years. Some of the early examples include the Freshwater Place residential tower in Melbourne (2002) with its Level 10 rooftop Half Acre Garden, CH2 building housing the Melbourne City Council (2006) – Australia’s first 6-star Green Star Design commercial office building as certified by the <a href="/wiki/Green_Building_Council_of_Australia" title="Green Building Council of Australia">Green Building Council of Australia</a>, and Condor Tower (2005) with a 75-square-metre lawn on the 4th floor.</p> 
     <p>In 2010, the largest Australian green roof project was announced. The Victorian Desalination Project <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> will have a “living tapestry” of 98,000 Australian indigenous plants over a roof area spanning more than 26,000 square metres. The roof will form part of the desalination plant’s sophisticated roof system, designed to blend the building into the landscape, and provide acoustic protection, corrosion resistance, thermal control, and reduced maintenance. The green roof was designed by ASPECT Studios, ARM / pecvkvonhartel architecture, and will be installed by Fytogreen Australia <sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since 2008, City Councils and influential business groups in Australia have become active promoting the benefits of green roofs. “The Blueprint to Green Roof Melbourne” is one program being run by the Committee for Melbourne.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In June 2014 ecological artist Lloyd Godman, with Stuart Jones and Grant Harris in a ground breaking experiment installed Tillandsia plants at level 92, 91, 65 and 56 on Eureka Tower. The air plants, are extremely light, grow with no soil or watering system and have survived for nearly a year.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">Costa Rica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Living green roofs have been built and grown at Saint Michael's Sustainable community since 2012. Native plants, mostly flowers chosen for the environment, maximum shade and mass provide a colorful and functional living roof.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, soil-less agriculture is used to grow plants on the roofs of buildings. No soil is placed directly on the roof itself, thus eliminating the need for an insulating layer; instead, plants are grown on wooden tables. Vegetables and fruit are the most popular candidates, providing a fresh, healthy source of food that is free from pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A more advanced method, (<a href="/wiki/Aquaponics" title="Aquaponics">aquaponics</a>), being used experimentally in Egypt, is farming fish next to plants in a closed cycle. This allows the plants to benefit from the ammonia excreted by the fish, helping the plants to grow better and at the same time eliminating the need for changing the water for the fish, because the plants help to keep it clean by absorbing the ammonia. The fish also get some nutrients from the roots of the plants.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Long-held green roof traditions started in the early industrialization period more than 100 years ago exist in Germany. In the 1970s, green roof technology was elevated to the next level. Serious storm-water issues made cities think about innovative solutions, preferably with living plants. Modern green roof technology with high performance, lightweight materials were utilized to grow hardy vegetation even on roofs that can hardly support any additional load. In the 1980s modern green roof technology was common knowledge in Germany while it was practically unknown in any other country in the world. In <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.stuttgart-tourist.de/ENG/leisure/gruenes-u.htm">Stuttgart</a>, with one of the most innovative Department of Parks and Recreation and with the worlds oldest horticultural <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.uni-hohenheim.de/startseite.html?&amp;L=1">Universities</a>, modern green roof technology was perfected and implemented on a large scale.</p> 
     <p>With the first green roof industry boom in Germany there were quality issues recorded. The FLL formed a committee that is focused on modern green roof technology. FLL stands for Forschungsgesellschaft Landschaftsentwicklung Landschaftsbau e.V. (FLL)or in English: The German Landscape Research, Development and Construction Society. The FLL is an independent non-profit organization. It was founded in 1975 by eight professional organizations for “the improvement of environmental conditions through the advancement and dissemination of plant research and its planned applications”. The FLL green roof working group is only one of 40 committees which have published a long list of guidelines and labor instructions. Some of these guidelines also available in English including <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fll.de/shop/index.php?cat=c4_Englische-Publikationen.html">the German FLL-Guideline for the Planning, Execution and Upkeep of Green-Roof Sites</a>. The results of the research and synthesis done by FLL members are constantly updated and promulgated utilizing the same principles which govern the compilation of DIN standards and are published as either guiding principles or labor instructions.</p> 
     <p>The current Green Roof Guideline was published in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Today most elements of the German FLL are part of standards and guidelines around the world (FM Global, ASTM, NRCA, SPRI etc.).</p> 
     <p>Fachvereinigung Bauwerksbegrünung (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fbb.de/">FBB</a>) was founded in 1990 as the second green roof association after DDV (Deutscher Dachgaertner Verband) in 1985. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fbb.de/">FBB</a> was founded as an open forum for manufacturers and planners, merchants and operators in 1990. The organization was born from the then-visionary idea of understanding the relationship between nature and constructions not as oppositional, but as an opportunity. Both the green roofing and conventional roofing industries are equally represented. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fbb.de/">FBB</a> has developed to become an innovative lobbying group with a strong market presence, internationally known through its cooperation with other European associations. Today, approximately 100 member companies use the multifaceted services offered by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fbb.de/">FBB</a>, which offers a greater degree of market expertise and competitiveness. "Kompetenz im Markt".</p> 
     <p>Today, about 10,000,000m<sup>2</sup> (or 100,000,000 square feet) of new green roofs are being constructed each year. According latest studies about ¾ of these are extensive; the last ¼ are roof gardens. The cities with the most green roofs in Germany are Berlin and <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>. Surveys about the status of regulation are done by the FBB. Nearly one third of all German cities have regulations to support green-roof and rain-water technology. Green-roof research institutions are located in several cities as including <a href="/wiki/Hannover" title="Hannover" class="mw-redirect">Hannover</a>, Berlin, <a href="/wiki/Geisenheim" title="Geisenheim">Geisenheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neubrandenburg" title="Neubrandenburg">Neubrandenburg</a>.</p> 
     <p>Germany is the country with the most green roofs in the world and it is the country with the most advanced knowledge in modern green roof technology. Green roofs in Germany are part of the 2 –3 years apprentice educations system of landscaping professionals. Since green roof are common knowledge and common sense it is interesting to take a look at the <a href="//de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dachbegr%C3%BCnung" class="extiw" title="de:Dachbegrünung">German Wikipedia page for green roofs</a> – there is no need to describe projects, case studies or related research. The green roof technology was implemented before the internet age.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greece">Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The Greek Ministry of Finance has now installed a green roof on the Treasury in Constitution Square in Athens.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The so-called "oikostegi" (Greek – <i>oiko</i>, pronounced <i>eeko</i>, meaning building-ecological, and <i>stegi</i>, pronounced <i>staygee</i>, meaning roof-abode-shelter) was inaugurated in September, 2008. Studies of the thermodynamics of the roof in September 2008 concluded that the thermal performance of the building was significantly affected by the installation.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In further studies, in August 2009, energy savings of 50% were observed for air conditioning in the floor directly below the installation. The ten-floor building has a total floor space of 1.4 hectares. The oikostegi covers 650&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>, equalling 52% of the roof space and 8% of the total floor space. Despite this, energy savings totalling €5,630 per annum were recorded, which translates to a 9% saving in air conditioning and a 4% saving in heating bills for the whole building.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> An additional observation and conclusion of the study was that the thermodynamic performance of the oikostegi had improved as biomass was added over the 12 months between the first and second study. This suggests that further improvements will be observed as the biomass increases still further. The study also stated that while measurements were being made by thermal cameras, a plethora of beneficial insects were observed on the roof, such as butterflies, honey bees and ladybirds. Obviously this was not the case before installation. Finally, the study suggested that both the micro-climate and biodiversity of Constitution Square, in Athens, Greece had been improved by the oikostegi.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Switzerland has one of Europe's oldest green roofs, created in 1914 at the Moos lake water-treatment plant, <a href="/wiki/Wollishofen" title="Wollishofen">Wollishofen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>. Its filter tanks have 30,000 square metres (320,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;ft) of flat concrete roofs. To keep the interior cool and prevent bacterial growth in the filtration beds, a drainage layer of gravel and a 15-cm (6-in) layer of soil was spread over the roofs, which had been waterproofed with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>. A meadow developed from seeds already present in the soil; it is now a haven for many plant species, some of which are now otherwise extinct in the district, most notably 6,000 <i>Orchis morio</i> (<a href="/wiki/Green-winged_orchid" title="Green-winged orchid" class="mw-redirect">green-winged orchid</a>). More recent Swiss examples can be found at Klinikum 1 and Klinikum 2, the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">Cantonal</a> Hospitals of <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sihlpost&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sihlpost (page does not exist)">Sihlpost</a> platform at Zürich's main railway station.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>What is claimed<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> to be the world's first green roof <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> was set up in <a href="/wiki/Augustenborg,_Malm%C3%B6" title="Augustenborg, Malmö">Augustenborg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malmö">Malmö</a> in May 1999. The International Green Roof Institute (IGRI) opened to the public in April 2001 as a research station and educational facility. (It has since been renamed the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.greenroof.se/">Scandinavian Green Roof Institute (SGRI)</a>, in view of the increasing number of similar organisations around the world.) Green roofs are well-established in Malmö: the Augustenborg housing development near the SGRI botanical garden incorporates green roofs and extensive landscaping of streams, ponds, and soak-ways between the buildings to deal with storm water run-off.</p> 
     <p>The new <a href="/wiki/Bo01" title="Bo01">Bo01</a> urban residential development (in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=V%C3%A4stra_Hamnen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Västra Hamnen (page does not exist)">Västra Hamnen</a> (Western Harbour) close to the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Turning_Torso" title="Turning Torso">Turning Torso</a> office and apartment block, designed by <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a>) is built on the site of old shipyards and industrial areas, and incorporates many green roofs.</p> 
     <p>In 2012, the shopping mall <a href="/wiki/Emporia_(shopping_mall)" title="Emporia (shopping mall)">Emporia</a> with its 27,000 square metre roof garden, was opened. The size of the roof garden is approximately equivalent to 4 soccer fields, which makes it one of the biggest green roof parks in Europe that is accessible to the public.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_roof&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>In 2003 English Nature concluded that 'in the UK policy makers have largely ignored green roofs'.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> However, British examples can be found with increasing frequency. A notable early roof garden was built above the Derry &amp; Toms Department Store in Kensington, London in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> More recent examples can be found at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University of Nottingham</a> <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_Campus" title="Jubilee Campus" class="mw-redirect">Jubilee Campus</a>, and in London at Sainsbury's Millennium Store in Greenwich, the <a href="/wiki/Horniman_Museum" title="Horniman Museum">Horniman Museum</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Canary_Wharf" title="Canary Wharf">Canary Wharf</a>. The Ethelred Estate, close to the River <a href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames" class="mw-redirect">Thames</a> in central London, is the British capital's largest roof-greening project to date. <a href="/wiki/Toxteth" title="Toxteth">Toxteth</a> in Liverpool is also a candidate for a major roof-greening project.</p> 
     <p>In the United Kingdom, intensive green roofs are sometimes used in built-up city areas where residents and workers often do not have access to gardens or local parks. Extensive green roofs are sometimes used to blend buildings into rural surroundings, for example by <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Motor_Cars" title="Rolls-Royce Motor Cars">Rolls-Royce Motor Cars</a>, who has one of the biggest green roofs in Europe (covering more than 32,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup> on their factory at Goodwood, West Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The University of Sheffield has created a Green Roof Centre of Excellence and conducted research, particularly in a UK context, into green roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Dr Nigel Dunnett of Sheffield University published a UK-centric book about green roofing in 2004 (updated 2008).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Fort_Dunlop" title="Fort Dunlop">Fort Dunlop</a> has the largest green roof in the UK since its redevelopment between 2004 and 2006.</p> 
     <p>The UK also boasts one of the most innovative food preparation facilities in Europe, the Kanes salad factory in <a href="/wiki/Evesham" title="Evesham">Evesham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> It is topped with a wildflower roof featuring nearly 90 species of wildflower and natural grasses. The seed mix was prepared in consultation with leading ecologists to try and minimise the impact on the local environment.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The pre grown wildflower blanket sits on top of a standing seam roof and is combined with solar panels to create an eco friendly finish to the entire factory.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The development also won the 2013 National Federation of Roofing Contractors Sustainable Roof Award for Green Roofing.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>

     <p>One of the largest expanses of extensive green roof is to be found in the US, at <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>'s <a href="/wiki/River_Rouge_Plant" title="River Rouge Plant" class="mw-redirect">River Rouge Plant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dearborn,_Michigan" title="Dearborn, Michigan">Dearborn</a>, Michigan, where 42,000 square metres (450,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;ft) of assembly plant roofs are covered with <a href="/wiki/Sedum" title="Sedum">sedum</a> and other plants, designed by <a href="/wiki/William_McDonough" title="William McDonough">William McDonough</a>; the $18 million assembly avoids the need of what otherwise would be $50 million worth of mechanical treatment facilities on site. Built over Millennium Park Garage, Chicago's 24.5-acre (99,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Park" title="Millennium Park">Millennium Park</a> is considered one of the largest intensive green roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Other well-known American examples include Chicago’s City Hall and the former Gap headquarters, now the headquarters of YouTube, in San Bruno, CA. Recently, the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Landscape_Architects" title="American Society of Landscape Architects">American Society of Landscape Architects</a> retrofitted their existing headquarters building in Washington, D.C. with a green roof designed by landscape architect <a href="/wiki/Michael_Van_Valkenburgh" title="Michael Van Valkenburgh">Michael Van Valkenburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Another example of a green roof in the United States is the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.spl.org/default.asp?pageID=branch_open_other&amp;branchID=3">Ballard Library</a> in Seattle. This green roof has over 18,000 plants to help with insulation and reduce runoff. The plants used on the roof include <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Achillea_tomentosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Achillea tomentosa (page does not exist)">Achillea tomentosa</a></i> (woolly yarrow), <i><a href="/wiki/Armeria_maritima" title="Armeria maritima">Armeria maritima</a></i> (sea pink, sea thrift), <i><a href="/wiki/Carex_inops" title="Carex inops">Carex inops</a> pensylvanica</i> (long-stoloned sedge), <i><a href="/wiki/Eriophyllum_lanatum" title="Eriophyllum lanatum">Eriophyllum lanatum</a></i> (Oregon sunshine), <i><a href="/wiki/Festuca_rubra" title="Festuca rubra">Festuca rubra</a></i> (red creeping fescue), <i><a href="/wiki/Festuca_idahoensis" title="Festuca idahoensis">Festuca idahoensis</a></i> (Idaho fescue), <i><a href="/wiki/Phlox_subulata" title="Phlox subulata">Phlox subulata</a></i> (creeping phlox), <i><a href="/wiki/Saxifraga_caespitosa" title="Saxifraga caespitosa" class="mw-redirect">Saxifraga caespitosa</a></i> (tufted saxifrage), <i><a href="/wiki/Sedum_oreganum" title="Sedum oreganum">Sedum oreganum</a></i> (Oregon stonecrop), <i><a href="/wiki/Sedum_album" title="Sedum album">Sedum album</a></i> (white stonecrop), <i><a href="/wiki/Sedum_spurium" title="Sedum spurium">Sedum spurium</a></i> (two-row stonecrop), <i><a href="/wiki/Sisyrinchium_idahoense" title="Sisyrinchium idahoense">Sisyrinchium idahoense</a></i> (blue-eyed grass), <i><a href="/wiki/Thymus_serpyllum" title="Thymus serpyllum">Thymus serpyllum</a></i> (wild thyme), <i><a href="/wiki/Triteleia_hyacinthina" title="Triteleia hyacinthina">Triteleia hyacinthina</a></i> (fool's onion).</p> 
     <p>The new <a href="/wiki/California_Academy_of_Sciences" title="California Academy of Sciences">California Academy of Sciences</a> building in San Francisco's <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park" title="Golden Gate Park">Golden Gate Park</a> has a green roof that provides 2.5 acres (10,000&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) of native vegetation designed as a habitat for indigenous species, including the threatened <a href="/wiki/Bay_checkerspot_butterfly" title="Bay checkerspot butterfly">Bay checkerspot butterfly</a>. According to the Academy's fact sheet on the building,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> the building consumes 30–35% less energy than required by code.</p> 
     <p>An early green-roofed building (completed in 1971) is the 358,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;ft (33,300&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) Weyerhaeuser Corporate Headquarters building in Federal Way, Washington. Its 5-story office roof system comprises a series of stepped terraces covered in greenery. From the air, the building blends into the landscape.</p> 
     <p>The largest green roof in New York City was installed in midtown <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> atop the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a>'s Morgan Processing and Distribution Center. Construction on the 109,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;ft (10,100&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) project began in September 2008, and was finished and dedicated in July 2009. Covered in native vegetation and having an expected lifetime of fifty years, this green roof will not only save the USPS approximately $30,000 a year in heating and cooling costs, but will also significantly reduce the amount of storm water contaminants entering the municipal water system.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Atop the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> is a modern sculptural garden spanning over 16,750 square feet (7,500 + 9,250). This rooftop is physically inaccessible to the public but has some of the most visible rooftop space in New York. <a href="/wiki/Ken_Smith_(architect)" title="Ken Smith (architect)">Ken Smith</a> designed MoMA rooftop as a Landscape Architect by using 185 plastic rocks, 560 artificial boxwoods, 300 pounds of clear crushed glass, 4 tons of recycled rubber mulch. His design was awarded the <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Society_of_Landscape_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Society of Landscape Architecture (page does not exist)">American Society of Landscape Architecture</a> for design in 2009.</p>

     <p>In 2001, atop <a href="/wiki/Chicago_City_Hall" title="Chicago City Hall">Chicago City Hall</a>, the 38,800 square foot roof gardens were completed serving as a pilot project to assess the impact green roofs would have on the <a href="/wiki/Heat_island" title="Heat island" class="mw-redirect">heat island</a> effect in urban areas, rainwater runoff, and the effectiveness of differing types of green roofs and plant species for Chicago's climate. Although the rooftop is not normally accessible to the public, it is visually accessible from 33 taller buildings in the area. The garden consists of 20,000 plants of more than 150 species, including shrubs, vines and two trees. The green roof design team was headed by the Chicago area firm <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cdfinc.com/Project?project_id=24">Conservation Design Forum</a> in conjunction with noted "green" architect <a href="/wiki/William_McDonough" title="William McDonough">William McDonough</a>. With an abundance of flowering plants on the rooftop, beekeepers harvest approximately 200 pounds of honey each year from hives installed on the rooftop. Tours of the green roof are by special arrangement only. Chicago City Hall Green Roof won merit design award of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Society_of_Landscape_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Society of Landscape Architecture (page does not exist)">American Society of Landscape Architecture</a> (ASLA) competition in 2002.</p> 
     <p>The 14,000 square feet of outdoor space on the seventh floor of <a href="/wiki/Zeckendorf_Towers" title="Zeckendorf Towers">Zeckendorf Towers</a>, formerly an undistinguished rooftop filled with potted plants, make up the largest residential green roof in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-zeckendorftowers_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zeckendorftowers-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The roof was transformed in 2010 as part of Mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a>'s NYC Green Infrastructure campaign, and supposedly serves to capture some of the rain that falls on it rather than letting it run off and contribute to flooding in the adjacent <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_%E2%80%93_Union_Square_(New_York_City_Subway)" title="14th Street – Union Square (New York City Subway)">Union Square subway station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zeckendorftowers_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zeckendorftowers-85">[85]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some cost can also be attributed to maintenance. Extensive green roofs have low maintenance requirements but they are generally not maintenance free. German research has quantified the need to remove unwanted seedlings to approximately 6 seconds/m<sup>2</sup>/year.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Maintenance of green roofs often includes fertilization to increase flowering and succulent plant cover. If aesthetics are not an issue, fertilization and maintenance are generally not needed. Extensive green roofs should only be fertilized with controlled-release fertilizers in order to avoid pollution of the storm water. Conventional fertilizers should never be used on extensive vegetated roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> German studies have approximated the nutrient requirement of vegetated roofs to 5&nbsp;gN/m<sup>2</sup>. It is also important to use a substrate that does not contain too many available nutrients. The FLL guidelines specify maximum-allowable nutrient content of substrates.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>One of the oldest American green roofs in existence is atop the Rockefeller Center in Manhattan, built in 1936. This roof was primarily an aesthetic undertaking for the enjoyment of the center's workers, and remains to this day, having been refurbished in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
