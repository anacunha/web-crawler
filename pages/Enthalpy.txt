     <p><b>Enthalpy</b> is a measure of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">thermodynamic system</a>. It includes the <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a>, which is the energy required to create a system, and the amount of energy required to make room for it by displacing its <a href="/wiki/Environment_(systems)" title="Environment (systems)">environment</a> and establishing its volume and pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Enthalpy is defined as a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state function</a> that depends only on the prevailing equilibrium state identified by the variables internal energy, pressure, and volume. It is an <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">extensive</a> quantity. The unit of measurement for enthalpy in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI) is the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>, but other historical, conventional units are still in use, such as the <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">British thermal unit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calorie</a>.</p> 
     <p>The enthalpy is the preferred expression of system energy changes in many chemical, biological, and physical measurements at constant pressure, because it simplifies the description of <a href="/wiki/Energy_transfer" title="Energy transfer" class="mw-redirect">energy transfer</a>. At constant pressure, the enthalpy change equals the energy transferred from the environment through heating or work other than expansion work.</p> 
     <p>The total enthalpy, <i>H</i>, of a system cannot be measured directly. The same situation exists in classical mechanics: only a change or difference in energy carries physical meaning. Enthalpy itself is a thermodynamic potential, so in order to measure the enthalpy of a system, we must refer to a defined reference point; therefore what we measure is the change in enthalpy, Δ<i>H</i>. The Δ<i>H</i> is a positive change in <a href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic" class="mw-redirect">endothermic</a> reactions, and negative in heat-releasing <a href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic" class="mw-redirect">exothermic</a> processes.</p> 
     <p>For processes under constant pressure, Δ<i>H</i> is equal to the change in the internal energy of the system, plus the <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)#Pressure-volume_work" title="Work (thermodynamics)">pressure-volume work</a> that the system has done on its surroundings.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This means that the change in enthalpy under such conditions is the heat absorbed (or released) by the material through a chemical reaction or by external heat transfer. Enthalpies for chemical substances at constant pressure assume <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a>: most commonly 1 bar pressure. Standard state does not, strictly speaking, specify a temperature (see <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a>), but expressions for enthalpy generally reference the standard heat of formation at 25&nbsp;°C.</p> 
     <p>Enthalpy of ideal gases and incompressible solids and liquids does not depend on pressure, unlike <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs energy</a>. Real materials at common temperatures and pressures usually closely approximate this behavior, which greatly simplifies enthalpy calculation and use in practical designs and analyses.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The word <i>enthalpy</i> is based on the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>enthalpein</i> (ἐνθάλπειν), which means "to warm in".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It comes from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greek_language" title="Classical Greek language" class="mw-redirect">Classical Greek</a> prefix <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%90%CE%BD" class="extiw" title="wikt:ἐν">ἐν-</a> <i>en-</i>, meaning "to put into", and the verb <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B8%CE%AC%CE%BB%CF%80%CF%89" class="extiw" title="wikt:θάλπω">θάλπειν</a> <i>thalpein</i>, meaning "to heat". The word <i>enthalpy</i> is often incorrectly attributed to <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Paul_%C3%89mile_Clapeyron" title="Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron">Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius" title="Rudolf Clausius">Rudolf Clausius</a> through the 1850 publication of their <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron relation">Clausius–Clapeyron relation</a>. This misconception was popularized by the 1927 publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy%E2%80%93entropy_chart" title="Enthalpy–entropy chart">The Mollier Steam Tables and Diagrams</a></i>. However, neither the concept, the word, nor the symbol for enthalpy existed until well after Clapeyron's death.</p> 
     <p>The earliest writings to contain the concept of enthalpy did not appear until 1875,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">Josiah Willard Gibbs</a> introduced "a heat function for constant pressure". However, Gibbs did not use the word "enthalpy" in his writings.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[note 1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The actual word first appears in the scientific literature in a 1909 publication by J. P. Dalton. According to that publication, <a href="/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes" title="Heike Kamerlingh Onnes">Heike Kamerlingh Onnes</a> (1853–1926) actually coined the word.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Over the years, many different symbols were used to denote enthalpy. It was not until 1922 that Alfred W. Porter proposed the symbol "<i>H</i>" as the accepted standard,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> thus finalizing the terminology still in use today.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_definition">Formal definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formal definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The enthalpy of a homogeneous system is defined as<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H = U + p V," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/d/1/ad16146d17c069951d655745283879a4.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <i>H</i> is the enthalpy of the system,
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <i>U</i> is the 
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> of the system,
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <i>p</i> is the 
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of the system,
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <i>V</i> is the 
       <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">volume</a> of the system.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The enthalpy is an <a href="/wiki/Extensive_property" title="Extensive property" class="mw-redirect">extensive property</a>. This means that, for homogeneous systems, the enthalpy is proportional to the size of the system. It is convenient to introduce the specific enthalpy <i>h</i> = <i>H</i>/<i>m</i>, where <i>m</i> is the mass of the system, or the molar enthalpy <i>H</i><sub>m</sub> = <i>H</i>/<i>n</i>, where <i>n</i> is the number of moles (<i>h</i> and <i>H</i><sub>m</sub> are <a href="/wiki/Intensive_properties" title="Intensive properties" class="mw-redirect">intensive properties</a>). For inhomogeneous systems the enthalpy is the sum of the enthalpies of the composing subsystems:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H = \sum_k H_k," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/d/c/2dc688c3622503091d7e85d23a91ac43.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where the label <i>k</i> refers to the various subsystems. In case of continuously varying <i>p</i>, <i>T</i>, and/or composition, the summation becomes an integral:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H = \int \rho h \, dV," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/7/d/77da6917eff893a989177df7215d07d8.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <i>ρ</i> is the density.</p> 
     <p>The enthalpy of homogeneous systems can be viewed as function <i>H</i>(<i>S</i>,<i>p</i>) of the entropy <i>S</i> and the pressure <i>p</i>, and a differential relation for it can be derived as follows. We start from the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a> for closed systems for an infinitesimal process:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU = \delta Q - \delta W." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/d/2/6d2134aca66d58a214b070abb42f7232.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Here, δ<i>Q</i> is a small amount of heat added to the system, and δ<i>W</i> a small amount of work performed by the system. In a homogeneous system only reversible processes can take place, so the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> gives δ<i>Q</i> = <i>T dS</i>, with <i>T</i> the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature" class="mw-redirect">absolute temperature</a> of the system. Furthermore, if only <i>pV</i> work is done, δ<i>W</i> = <i>p dV</i>. As a result,</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU = T\,dS - p\,dV." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/7/8/a783a78b5d3e2a652c6e376eeb71941f.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Adding <i>d</i>(<i>pV</i>) to both sides of this expression gives</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU + d(pV) = T\,dS - p\,dV + d(pV)," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/b/b/cbb5bcb36e9609a188374fb350c5ed07.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>or</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="d(U + pV) = T\,dS + V\,dp." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/9/f/89f6798e20f3fff5482d457abd17c0da.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>So</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH(S,p) = T\,dS + V\,dp." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/0/a/a0a3712bcb0539a3691c363c16e40271.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_expressions">Other expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The above expression of <i>dH</i> in terms of entropy and pressure may be unfamiliar to some readers. However, there are expressions in terms of more familiar variables such as temperature and pressure:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH = C_p\,dT + V(1 - \alpha T)\,dp." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/e/3/8e3f437c02e83289e6d3e32c431f4934.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Here <i>C</i><sub><i>p</i></sub> is the heat capacity at constant pressure and <i>α</i> is the coefficient of (cubic) thermal expansion:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha = \frac{1}{V}\left(\frac{\part V}{\part T}\right)_p." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/7/5/5753db37e21ff31c3029dd704c5678f1.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>With this expression one can, in principle, determine the enthalpy if <i>C</i><sub>p</sub> and <i>V</i> are known as functions of <i>p</i> and <i>T</i>.</p> 
     <p>Notice that for an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha T = 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/5/6/75600679723d93bd75495de6ae7f0c0b.png">,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[note 2]</a></sup> so that</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH = C_p\,dT." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/c/c/fcc0978239ecd0f41ac0eeb19fb7ebd0.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>In a more general form, the first law describes the internal energy with additional terms involving the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_potential" title="Chemical potential">chemical potential</a> and the number of particles of various types. The differential statement for <i>dH</i> then becomes</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH = T\,dS + V\,dp + \sum_i \mu_i\,dN_i," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/9/689a27cf079de1a4352798fddd5c7ecf.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <i>μ</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> is the chemical potential per particle for an <i>i</i>-type particle, and <i>N</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> is the number of such particles. The last term can also be written as <i>μ</i><sub>i</sub> <i>dn</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> (with <i>dn</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> the number of moles of component <i>i</i> added to the system and, in this case, <i>μ</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> the molar chemical potential) or as <i>μ</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> <i>dm</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> (with <i>dm</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> the mass of component <i>i</i> added to the system and, in this case, <i>μ</i><sub><i>i</i></sub> the specific chemical potential).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_interpretation">Physical interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The <i>U</i> term can be interpreted as the energy required to create the system, and the <i>pV</i> term as the energy that would be required to "make room" for the system if the pressure of the environment remained constant. When a system, for example, <i>n</i> <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a> of a gas of <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">volume</a> <i>V</i> at <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> <i>p</i> and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> <i>T</i>, is created or brought to its present state from <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>, energy must be supplied equal to its internal energy <i>U</i> plus <i>pV</i>, where <i>pV</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> done in pushing against the ambient (atmospheric) pressure.</p> 
     <p>In basic <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> it may be more interesting to study the internal properties of the system and therefore the internal energy is used.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> In basic <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, experiments are often conducted at constant <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, and the pressure-volume work represents an energy exchange with the atmosphere that cannot be accessed or controlled, so that Δ<i>H</i> is the expression chosen for the <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_reaction" title="Heat of reaction" class="mw-redirect">heat of reaction</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_heat">Relationship to heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship to heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In order to discuss the relation between the enthalpy increase and heat supply, we return to the first law for closed systems: <i>dU</i> = δ<i>Q</i> − δ<i>W</i>. We apply it to the special case with a uniform pressure at the surface. In this case the work term can be split into two contributions, the so-called <i>pV</i> work, given by <i>p dV</i> (where here <i>p</i> is the pressure at the surface, <i>dV</i> is the increase of the volume of the system) and all other types of work δ<i>W</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>, such as by a shaft or by electromagnetic interaction. So we write δ<i>W</i> = <i>p dV</i> + δ<i>W</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>. In this case the first law reads:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU = \delta Q - p\,dV - \delta W'," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/d/5/ad5c5627407271059b866043039e01fe.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>or</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH = \delta Q + V\,dp - \delta W'." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/2/d/d2d4893cc8bfedd7899d46439effc4d9.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>From this relation we see that the increase in enthalpy of a system is equal to the added <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dH = \delta Q," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/b/5/bb5eac5ae00e639e01364645feb98712.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>provided that the system is under <a href="/wiki/Isobaric_system" title="Isobaric system" class="mw-redirect">constant pressure</a> (<i>dp</i> = 0) and that the only work done by the system is expansion work (δ<i>W</i>&nbsp;=&nbsp;0).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In thermodynamics, one can calculate enthalpy by determining the requirements for creating a system from "nothingness"; the mechanical work required, <i>pV</i>, differs based upon the conditions that obtain during the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">thermodynamic system</a>.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> must be supplied to remove particles from the surroundings to make space for the creation of the system, assuming that the pressure <i>p</i> remains constant; this is the <i>pV</i> term. The supplied energy must also provide the change in internal energy, <i>U</i>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energies</a>, ionization energies, mixing energies, vaporization energies, chemical bond energies, and so forth. Together, these constitute the change in the enthalpy <i>U</i> + <i>pV</i>. For systems at constant pressure, with no external work done other than the <i>pV</i> work, the change in enthalpy is the heat received by the system.</p> 
     <p>For a simple system, with a constant number of particles, the difference in enthalpy is the maximum amount of thermal energy derivable from a thermodynamic process in which the pressure is held constant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE" title="Wikipedia:MOSQUOTE" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The text near this tag needs citation. (September 2014)">this quote needs a citation</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_of_reaction">Heat of reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heat of reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_of_reaction" title="Standard enthalpy of reaction">Standard enthalpy of reaction</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The total enthalpy of a system cannot be measured directly, the <i>enthalpy change</i> of a <a href="/wiki/System_(thermodynamics)" title="System (thermodynamics)" class="mw-redirect">system</a> is measured instead. Enthalpy change is defined by the following equation:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Delta H = H_f - H_i," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/5/a/25aac35e75610900a1374b605a9f0d27.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Delta H" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/9/e/19e2c87dfcd499d96746ef27b99584a5.png"> is the "enthalpy change",
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H_f" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/a/c/cac238970fab52e5307e43ac90125c31.png"> is the final enthalpy of the system (in a chemical reaction, the enthalpy of the products),
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/5/d/45d4bf87d928aada05c4b8df8e5f057e.png"> is the initial enthalpy of the system (in a chemical reaction, the enthalpy of the reactants).
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>For an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a> at constant <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, the system's change in enthalpy equals the energy released in the reaction, including the energy retained in the system and lost through expansion against its surroundings. In a similar manner, for an <a href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic" class="mw-redirect">endothermic</a> reaction, the system's change in enthalpy is equal to the energy <i>absorbed</i> in the reaction, including the energy <i>lost by</i> the system and <i>gained</i> from compression from its surroundings. A relatively easy way to determine whether or not a reaction is exothermic or endothermic is to determine the sign of Δ<i>H</i>. If Δ<i>H</i> is positive, the reaction is endothermic, that is heat is absorbed by the system due to the products of the reaction having a greater enthalpy than the reactants. On the other hand if Δ<i>H</i> is negative, the reaction is exothermic, that is the overall decrease in enthalpy is achieved by the generation of heat.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_enthalpy">Specific enthalpy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Specific enthalpy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The specific enthalpy of a uniform system is defined as <i>h</i> = <i>H</i>/<i>m</i> where <i>m</i> is the mass of the system. The <a href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit" class="mw-redirect">SI unit</a> for specific enthalpy is joule per kilogram. It can be expressed in other specific quantities by <i>h</i> = <i>u</i> + <i>pv</i>, where <i>u</i> is the specific <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a>, <i>p</i> is the pressure, and <i>v</i> is specific volume, which is equal to 1/<i>ρ</i>, where <i>ρ</i> is the density.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enthalpy_changes">Enthalpy changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Enthalpy changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>An enthalpy change describes the change in enthalpy observed in the constituents of a thermodynamic system when undergoing a transformation or chemical reaction. It is the difference between the enthalpy after the process has completed, i.e. the enthalpy of the <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a>, and the initial enthalpy of the system, i.e. the reactants. These processes are reversible<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="(March 2015)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> and the enthalpy for the reverse process is the negative value of the forward change.</p> 
     <p>A common standard enthalpy change is the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_formation" title="Enthalpy of formation" class="mw-redirect">enthalpy of formation</a>, which has been determined for a large number of substances. Enthalpy changes are routinely measured and compiled in chemical and physical reference works, such as the <a href="/wiki/CRC_Handbook_of_Chemistry_and_Physics" title="CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics">CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics</a>. The following is a selection of enthalpy changes commonly recognized in thermodynamics.</p> 
     <p>When used in these recognized terms the qualifier <i>change</i> is usually dropped and the property is simply termed <i>enthalpy of 'process'</i>. Since these properties are often used as reference values it is very common to quote them for a standardized set of environmental parameters, or <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions" title="Standard conditions" class="mw-redirect">standard conditions</a>, including:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of 25&nbsp;°C or 298 K,</li> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of one atmosphere (1 atm or 101.325 kPa),</li> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of 1.0 M when the element or compound is present in solution,</li> 
      <li>Elements or compounds in their normal physical states, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a>.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>For such standardized values the name of the enthalpy is commonly prefixed with the term <i>standard</i>, e.g. <i>standard enthalpy of formation</i>.</p> 
     <p>Chemical properties:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_reaction" title="Enthalpy of reaction" class="mw-redirect">Enthalpy of reaction</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of substance reacts completely.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_formation" title="Enthalpy of formation" class="mw-redirect">Enthalpy of formation</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a compound is formed from its elementary antecedents.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_combustion" title="Enthalpy of combustion" class="mw-redirect">Enthalpy of combustion</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a substance burns completely with oxygen.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_hydrogenation" title="Enthalpy of hydrogenation" class="mw-redirect">Enthalpy of hydrogenation</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of an unsaturated compound reacts completely with an excess of hydrogen to form a saturated compound.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_atomization" title="Enthalpy of atomization">Enthalpy of atomization</a>, defined as the enthalpy change required to atomize one mole of compound completely.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_neutralization" title="Enthalpy of neutralization">Enthalpy of neutralization</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of water is formed when an acid and a base react.</li> 
      <li>Standard <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_solution" title="Enthalpy of solution" class="mw-redirect">Enthalpy of solution</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a solute is dissolved completely in an excess of solvent, so that the solution is at infinite dilution.</li> 
      <li>Standard enthalpy of <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">Denaturation (biochemistry)</a>, defined as the enthalpy change required to denature one mole of compound.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Hydration_energy" title="Hydration energy">Enthalpy of hydration</a>, defined as the enthalpy change observed when one mole of gaseous ions are completely dissolved in water forming one mole of aqueous ions.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Physical properties:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_fusion" title="Enthalpy of fusion">Enthalpy of fusion</a>, defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between solid and liquid states.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">Enthalpy of vaporization</a>, defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between liquid and gaseous states.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_sublimation" title="Enthalpy of sublimation">Enthalpy of sublimation</a>, defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between solid and gaseous states.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Lattice_enthalpy" title="Lattice enthalpy" class="mw-redirect">Lattice enthalpy</a>, defined as the energy required to separate one mole of an ionic compound into separated gaseous ions to an infinite distance apart (meaning no force of attraction).</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_mixing" title="Enthalpy of mixing">Enthalpy of mixing</a>, defined as the enthalpy change upon mixing of two (non-reacting) chemical substances.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_systems">Open systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Open systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" title="Thermodynamic" class="mw-redirect">thermodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open systems</a>, matter may flow in and out of the system boundaries. The first law of thermodynamics for open systems states: The increase in the internal energy of a system is equal to the amount of energy added to the system by matter flowing in and by heating, minus the amount lost by matter flowing out and in the form of work done by the system:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU = \delta Q + dU_\text{in} - dU_\text{out} - \delta W," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/8/4/484221baa0d97d2020d4632f93e29f6e.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_\text{in}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/0/6/d06f5e8132dbf5602970e617b82e7a7f.png"> is the average internal energy entering the system, and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_\text{out}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/d/f/5df28cbc2968391589802e96166f7d8f.png"> is the average internal energy leaving the system.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:First_law_open_system.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_law_open_system.svg/250px-First_law_open_system.svg.png" width="250" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_law_open_system.svg/375px-First_law_open_system.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_law_open_system.svg/500px-First_law_open_system.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="374" data-file-height="257"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:First_law_open_system.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> During 
        <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_(chemical_engineering)" title="Steady-state (chemical engineering)" class="mw-redirect">steady, continuous</a> operation, an energy balance applied to an open system equates shaft work performed by the system to heat added plus net enthalpy added
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The region of space enclosed by the boundaries if the open system is usually called a <a href="/wiki/Control_volume" title="Control volume">control volume</a>, and it may or may not correspond to physical walls. If we choose the shape of the control volume such that all flow in or out occurs perpendicular to its surface, then the flow of matter into the system performs work as if it were a piston of fluid pushing mass into the system, and the system performs work on the flow of matter out as if it were driving a piston of fluid. There are then two types of work performed: <i>flow work</i> described above, which is performed on the fluid (this is also often called <i>pV work</i>), and <i>shaft work</i>, which may be performed on some mechanical device.</p> 
     <p>These two types of work are expressed in the equation</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\delta W = d(p_\text{out}V_\text{out}) - d(p_\text{in}V_\text{in}) + \delta W_\text{shaft}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/3/c/c3ce4cad07e607ab12c4d10bfbc98f9f.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Substitution into the equation above for the control volume cv yields:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU_\text{cv} = \delta Q + dU_\text{in} + d(p_\text{in}V_\text{in}) - dU_\text{out} - d(p_\text{out}V_\text{out}) - \delta W_\text{shaft}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/3/3/633f5829cfe1f345810aa0cbe6d10bbc.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The definition of enthalpy, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="H" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/1/d/c1d9f50f86825a1a2302ec2449c17196.png">, permits us to use this <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_potential" title="Thermodynamic potential">thermodynamic potential</a> to account for both internal energy and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="pV" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/d/0/0d0e7b94e4fd1fbb404e609f2d6427a9.png"> work in fluids for open systems:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="dU_\text{cv} = \delta Q + dH_\text{in} - dH_\text{out} - \delta W_\text{shaft}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/b/d/ebd7a8674b324469faed28b1961f90ab.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>If we allow also the system boundary to move (e.g. due to moving pistons), we get a rather general form of the first law for open systems.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> In terms of time derivatives it reads:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{dU}{dt} = \sum_k \dot Q_k + \sum_k \dot H_k - \sum_k p_k\frac{dV_k}{dt} - P," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/8/a/78a5c19a1f36a2e15d2d791dbafc9bda.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>with sums over the various places <i>k</i> where heat is supplied, matter flows into the system, and boundaries are moving. The <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot H_k" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/f/7/df7278f2b9ba93faff82377da611c806.png"> terms represent enthalpy flows, which can be written as</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot H_k = h_k\dot m_k = H_m\dot n_k," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/a/d/9adc4abc114bac2aeb6357707a0ea09c.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>with <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot m_k" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/1/e/91e1d5e3da2676b979c8f813bb7dea66.png"> the mass flow and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot n_k" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/c/0/ec0ba0575cf796e904e41fd82b87b009.png"> the molar flow at position <i>k</i> respectively. The term <i>dV</i><sub><i>k</i></sub>/<i>dt</i> represents the rate of change of the system volume at position <i>k</i> that results in <i>pV</i> power done by the system. The parameter <i>P</i> represents all other forms of power done by the system such as shaft power, but it can also be e.g. electric power produced by an electrical power plant.</p> 
     <p>Note that the previous expression holds true only if the kinetic energy flow rate is conserved between system inlet and outlet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This new type of energy, kinetic energy, was not mentioned before. Is it part of U? Does it need to be conserved, or just the net flow across boundary be zero? (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Otherwise, it has to be included in the enthalpy balance. During <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_(chemical_engineering)" title="Steady-state (chemical engineering)" class="mw-redirect">steady-state</a> operation of a device (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pump</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a></i>), the average <i>dU</i>/<i>dt</i> may be set equal to zero. This yields a useful expression for the average <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> generation for these devices in the absence of chemical reactions:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" P = \sum_k \left\langle \dot Q_k \right\rangle
+ \sum_k \left\langle \dot H_k \right\rangle
- \sum_k \left\langle p_k\frac{\mathrm{d}V_k}{\mathrm{d}t} \right\rangle," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/f/3/bf3f410f15f2f0ab95378b8890da5643.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where the <a href="/wiki/Angle_bracket" title="Angle bracket" class="mw-redirect">angle brackets</a> denote time averages. The technical importance of the enthalpy is directly related to its presence in the first law for open systems, as formulated above.</p>

     <p>Nowadays the enthalpy values of important substances can be obtained using commercial software. Practically all relevant material properties can be obtained either in tabular or in graphical form.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Throttling">Throttling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Throttling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">Joule–Thomson effect</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Schematic_of_throttling.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Schematic_of_throttling.png/220px-Schematic_of_throttling.png" width="220" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Schematic_of_throttling.png/330px-Schematic_of_throttling.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Schematic_of_throttling.png/440px-Schematic_of_throttling.png 2x" data-file-width="588" data-file-height="260"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Schematic_of_throttling.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Schematic diagram of a throttling in the steady state. Fluid enters the system (dotted rectangle) at point 1 and leaves it at point 2. The mass flow is 
        <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot m" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/8/6/a863aba97eca7ee0949b944cc7946aef.png">.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>One of the simple applications of the concept of enthalpy is the so-called throttling process, also known as <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">Joule-Thomson expansion</a>. It concerns a steady adiabatic flow of a fluid through a flow resistance (valve, porous plug, or any other type of flow resistance) as shown in the figure. This process is very important, since it is at the heart of domestic refrigerators, where it is responsible for the temperature drop between ambient temperature and the interior of the refrigerator. It is also the final stage in many types of liquefiers.</p> 
     <p>In the first law for open systems (see above) applied to the system, all terms are zero, except the terms for the enthalpy flow. Hence</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="0 = \dot m h_1 - \dot m h_2." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/3/9/639c53f5d29e1a865ce95ee253078575.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Since the mass flow is constant, the specific enthalpies at the two sides of the flow resistance are the same:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="h_1 = h_2," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/1/d/91d2531b56bb908cedd999f620c37b00.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>that is, the enthalpy per unit mass does not change during the throttling. The consequences of this relation can be demonstrated using the <i>T</i>–<i>s</i> diagram above. Point <i>c</i> is at 200 bar and room temperature (300 K). A Joule–Thomson expansion from 200 bar to 1 bar follows a curve of constant enthalpy of roughly 425 kJ/kg (not shown in the diagram) lying between the 400 and 450 kJ/kg isenthalps and ends in point <i>d</i>, which is at a temperature of about 270 K. Hence the expansion from 200 bar to 1 bar cools nitrogen from 300 K to 270 K. In the valve, there is a lot of friction, and a lot of entropy is produced, but still the final temperature is below the starting value!</p> 
     <p>Point <i>e</i> is chosen so that it is on the saturated liquid line with <i>h</i> = 100 kJ/kg. It corresponds roughly with <i>p</i> = 13 bar and <i>T</i> = 108 K. Throttling from this point to a pressure of 1 bar ends in the two-phase region (point <i>f</i>). This means that a mixture of gas and liquid leaves the throttling valve. Since the enthalpy is an extensive parameter, the enthalpy in <i>f</i> (<i>h</i><sub><i>f</i></sub>) is equal to the enthalpy in <i>g</i> (<i>h</i><sub><i>g</i></sub>) multiplied with the liquid fraction in <i>f</i> (<i>x</i><sub><i>f</i></sub>) plus the enthalpy in <i>h</i> (<i>h</i><sub><i>h</i></sub>) multiplied with the gas fraction in <i>f</i> (1 − <i>x</i><sub><i>f</i></sub>). So</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" h_f = x_f h_g + (1 - x_f)h_h." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/f/3/bf3671ba779f6e443a4aaf0be58222ed.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>With numbers: 100 = <i>x</i><sub><i>f</i></sub> 28 + (1 − <i>x</i><sub><i>f</i></sub>)230, so <i>x</i><sub><i>f</i></sub> = 0.64. This means that the mass fraction of the liquid in the liquid–gas mixture that leaves the throttling valve is 64%.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressors">Compressors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Compressors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">Gas compressor</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Schematic_of_compressor.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Schematic_of_compressor.png/220px-Schematic_of_compressor.png" width="220" height="109" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Schematic_of_compressor.png/330px-Schematic_of_compressor.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Schematic_of_compressor.png/440px-Schematic_of_compressor.png 2x" data-file-width="566" data-file-height="280"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Schematic_of_compressor.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Schematic diagram of a compressor in the steady state. Fluid enters the system (dotted rectangle) at point 1 and leaves it at point 2. The mass flow is 
        <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot m" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/8/6/a863aba97eca7ee0949b944cc7946aef.png">. A power 
        <i>P</i> is applied and a heat flow 
        <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot Q" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/0/2/6021f97c262fc5548a7741c06f07e2fe.png"> is released to the surroundings at ambient temperature 
        <i>T</i>
        <sub>a</sub>.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>A power <i>P</i> is applied e.g. as electrical power. If the compression is adiabatic, the gas temperature goes up. In the reversible case it would be at constant entropy, which corresponds with a vertical line in the <i>T</i>–<i>s</i> diagram. For example, compressing nitrogen from 1 bar (point <i>a</i>) to 2 bar (point <i>b</i>) would result in a temperature increase from 300 K to 380 K. In order to let the compressed gas exit at ambient temperature <i>T</i><sub><i>a</i></sub>, heat exchange, e.g. by cooling water, is necessary. In the ideal case the compression is isothermal. The average heat flow to the surroundings is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot Q" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/0/2/6021f97c262fc5548a7741c06f07e2fe.png">. Since the system is in the steady state the first law gives</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="0 = -\dot Q + \dot m h_1 - \dot m h_2 + P." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/3/8d39ea7e1e36ef25e5b18f99111c1e0b.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The minimal power needed for the compression is realized if the compression is reversible. In that case the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> for open systems gives</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="0 = -\frac{\dot Q}{T_a} + \dot m s_1 - \dot m s_2." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/e/1/2e1559488125daf56e3ce737c86cb20a.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Eliminating <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\dot Q" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/0/2/6021f97c262fc5548a7741c06f07e2fe.png"> gives for the minimal power</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{P_\text{min}}{\dot m} = h_2 - h_1 - T_a(s_2 - s_1)." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/a/2/8a223454892347e28662e0d58967f62f.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>For example, compressing 1&nbsp;kg of nitrogen from 1 bar to 200 bar costs at least (<i>h</i><sub><i>c</i></sub> − <i>h</i><sub><i>a</i></sub>) − <i>T</i><sub><i>a</i></sub>(<i>s</i><sub><i>c</i></sub> − <i>s</i><sub><i>a</i></sub>). With the data, obtained with the <i>T</i>–<i>s</i> diagram, we find a value of (430 − 461) − 300 (5.16 − 6.85) = 476 kJ/kg.</p> 
     <p>The relation for the power can be further simplified by writing it as</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{P_\text{min}}{\dot m} = \int_1^2(dh - T_a\,ds)." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/6/f/16f3756434566118a4b96309218bab18.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>With <i>dh</i> = <i>T</i> <i>ds</i> + <i>v</i> <i>dp</i>, this results in the final relation</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{P_\text{min}}{\dot m} = \int_1^2 v\,dp." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/7/3/573f7c3180528b54d02e9c76faa53687.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl>
