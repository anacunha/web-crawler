     <p>The <b>watt</b> (symbol: <b>W</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">derived unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI), named after the Scottish engineer <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> (1736–1819). The unit is defined as <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a> per <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and can be used to express the rate of <a href="/wiki/Energy_transformation" title="Energy transformation">energy conversion</a> or transfer with respect to time. It has dimensions of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">L</a><sup>2</sup><a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">M</a><a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">T</a><sup>−3</sup>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>When an object's <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> is held constant at one <a href="/wiki/Meter_per_second" title="Meter per second" class="mw-redirect">meter per second</a> against constant opposing force of one <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> the rate at which <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> is done is 1 watt.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{W = \frac{J}{s} = \frac{N\cdot m}{s} = \frac{kg\cdot m^2}{s^3}}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/9/8/f98c838ae0ccac27a9478498943ca68a.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>In terms of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, one watt is the rate at which work is done when one <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a> (A) of current flows through an electrical <a href="/wiki/Potential_difference" title="Potential difference" class="mw-redirect">potential difference</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a> (V).</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{W = V \cdot A}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/5/7/657b30706d502e4316e72050a93182ee.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Two additional <a href="/wiki/Conversion_of_units" title="Conversion of units">unit conversions</a> for watt can be found using the above equation and <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_Law" title="Ohm's Law" class="mw-redirect">Ohm's Law</a>.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{W = \frac{V^2}{\Omega} = A^2\cdot\Omega}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/9/709912018901cd11eb107295528b8371.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Where <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">ohm</a> (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Omega" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/9/d/b9d99db8626de63193c7fe96273a6cae.png">) is the <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance" class="mw-redirect">electrical resistance</a>.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A person having a mass of 100 kilograms who climbs a 3-meter-high ladder in 5 seconds is doing work at a rate of about 600 watts. Mass times acceleration due to gravity times height divided by the time it takes to lift the object to the given height gives the <i>rate of doing work</i> or <i>power</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[notes 1]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>A laborer over the course of an 8-hour day can sustain an average output of about 75 watts; higher power levels can be achieved for short intervals and by athletes.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_adoption_as_an_SI_unit">Origin and adoption as an SI unit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and adoption as an SI unit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The watt is named after the Scottish scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> for his contributions to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>. The measurement unit was recognized by the Second Congress of the <a href="/wiki/British_Science_Association" title="British Science Association">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a> in 1882, concurrent with the start of commercial power production from both <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_plant" title="Thermal power plant" class="mw-redirect">steam</a>. In 1960 the 11th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> adopted it for the measurement of power into the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI).</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Femtowatt">Femtowatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Femtowatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The femtowatt is equal to one quadrillionth (10<sup>−15</sup>) of a watt. Technologically important powers that are measured in femtowatts are typically found in reference(s) to <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> receivers. For example, meaningful FM tuner performance figures for sensitivity, quieting and signal-to-noise require that the RF energy applied to the antenna input be specified. These input levels are often stated in dBf (<a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">decibels</a> referenced to 1 femtowatt). This is 0.2739 microvolt across a 75-ohm load or 0.5477 microvolt across a 300-ohm load; the specification takes into account the RF input impedance of the tuner.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Picowatt">Picowatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Picowatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The picowatt is equal to one trillionth (10<sup>−12</sup>) of a watt. Technologically important powers that are measured in picowatts are typically used in reference to radio and radar receivers, acoustics and in the science of <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanowatt">Nanowatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nanowatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The nanowatt is equal to one billionth (10<sup>−9</sup>) of a watt. Important powers that are measured in nanowatts are also typically used in reference to radio and radar receivers.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwatt">Microwatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microwatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The microwatt is equal to one millionth (10<sup>−6</sup>) of a watt. Important powers that are measured in microwatts are typically stated in <a href="/wiki/Medical_instrument" title="Medical instrument" class="mw-redirect">medical instrumentation</a> systems such as the <a href="/wiki/EEG" title="EEG" class="mw-redirect">EEG</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiography" title="Electrocardiography">ECG</a>, in a wide variety of scientific and engineering instruments and also in reference to radio and radar receivers. Compact <a href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells" class="mw-redirect">solar cells</a> for devices such as calculators and watches are typically measured in microwatts.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milliwatt">Milliwatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Milliwatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The milliwatt is equal to one thousandth (10<sup>−3</sup>) of a watt. A typical <a href="/wiki/Laser_pointer" title="Laser pointer">laser pointer</a> outputs about five milliwatts of light power, whereas a typical <a href="/wiki/Hearing_aid" title="Hearing aid">hearing aid</a> for people uses less than one milliwatt.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">Audio signals</a> and other electronic signal levels are often measured in <a href="/wiki/DBm" title="DBm">dBm</a>, referenced to one milliwatt.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kilowatt">Kilowatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kilowatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      "Kilowatt" and "Kilowatts" redirect here. For the place in the United States, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt,_California" title="Kilowatt, California">Kilowatt, California</a>. For the musician James Watts, see 
      <a href="/wiki/KiloWatts_(musician)" title="KiloWatts (musician)">KiloWatts (musician)</a>.
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      "KW" redirects here. For other uses, see 
      <a href="/wiki/KW_(disambiguation)" title="KW (disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig">KW (disambiguation)</a>.
     </div> 
     <p>The kilowatt is equal to one thousand (10<sup>3</sup>) watts, or one <a href="/wiki/Sth%C3%A8ne" title="Sthène">sthène</a>-<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> per <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>. This unit is typically used to express the output power of <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engines</a> and the power of electric motors, tools, machines, and heaters. It is also a common unit used to express the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic</a> power output of broadcast radio and television <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a>.</p> 
     <p>One kilowatt is approximately equal to 1.34 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a>. A small electric heater with one heating element can use 1.0 kilowatt. The average electric power consumption of a household in the United States is about one kilowatt.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[notes 2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Also, kilowatts of <a href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light" class="mw-redirect">light</a> power can be measured in the output pulses of some <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>.</p> 
     <p>A surface area of one square meter on Earth receives typically about one kilowatt of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> from the sun (the <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">solar irradiance</a>) (on a clear day at mid day, close to the equator).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megawatt">Megawatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Megawatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The megawatt is equal to one million (10<sup>6</sup>) watts. Many events or machines produce or sustain the conversion of energy on this scale, including large electric motors; large <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warships</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruisers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>; large <a href="/wiki/Server_farm" title="Server farm">server farms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Data_center#Energy_use" title="Data center">data centers</a>; and some scientific research equipment, such as <a href="/wiki/Supercollider" title="Supercollider" class="mw-redirect">supercolliders</a>, and the output pulses of very large lasers. A large residential or commercial building may use several megawatts in electric power and heat. On railways, modern high-powered <a href="/wiki/Electric_locomotives" title="Electric locomotives" class="mw-redirect">electric locomotives</a> typically have a peak power output of 5 or 6&nbsp;MW, although some produce much more. The <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_373" title="British Rail Class 373">Eurostar</a>, for example, uses more than 12&nbsp;MW, while heavy <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_transmission" title="Diesel-electric transmission">diesel-electric</a> <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotives</a> typically produce/use 3 to 5&nbsp;MW. U.S. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a> have net summer capacities between about 500 and 1300&nbsp;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The earliest citing of the megawatt in the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> (OED) is a reference in the 1900 <a href="/wiki/Webster%27s_Dictionary" title="Webster's Dictionary"><i>Webster's International Dictionary of English Language</i></a>. The OED also states that megawatt appeared in a 28 November 1947 article in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> (506:2).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gigawatt">Gigawatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gigawatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The gigawatt is equal to one billion (10<sup>9</sup>) watts or 1 gigawatt = 1000 megawatts. This unit is often used for large power plants or power grids. For example, by the end of 2010 power shortages in China's Shanxi province were expected to increase to 5–6 GW<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> and the installed capacity of wind power in Germany was 25.8 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> The largest unit (out of four) of the Belgian <a href="/wiki/Doel_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Doel Nuclear Power Station">Doel Nuclear Power Station</a> has a peak output of 1.04 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HVDC_converter" title="HVDC converter">HVDC converters</a> have been built with power ratings of up to 2&nbsp;GW.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terawatt">Terawatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Terawatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The terawatt is equal to one trillion (10<sup>12</sup>) watts. The total power used by humans worldwide is commonly measured in terawatts (see <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption#Primary_energy" title="World energy consumption">primary energy</a>). The most powerful <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> from the mid-1960s to the mid-1990s produced power in terawatts, but only for <a href="/wiki/Nanosecond" title="Nanosecond">nanosecond</a> time frames. The average <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> strike peaks at 1 terawatt, but these strikes only last for 30 <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">microseconds</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petawatt">Petawatt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Petawatt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The petawatt is equal to one quadrillion (10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup>) watts and can be produced by the current generation of lasers for time-scales on the order of picoseconds (10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">12</span></sup>&nbsp;s). One such laser is the Lawrence Livermore's <a href="/wiki/Nova_(laser)" title="Nova (laser)">Nova laser</a>, which achieved a power output of 1.25&nbsp;PW (<span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7015125000000000000♠</span>1.25<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em">×</span>10<sup>15</sup></span>&nbsp;W) by a process called <a href="/wiki/Chirped_pulse_amplification" title="Chirped pulse amplification">chirped pulse amplification</a>. The duration of the pulse was roughly 0.5&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Picosecond" title="Picosecond">ps</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6987500000000000000♠</span>5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em">×</span>10<sup>−13</sup></span>&nbsp;s), giving a total energy of 600&nbsp;J, or enough energy to power a 100&nbsp;W light bulb for six seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> Another example is the Laser for Fast Ignition Experiments (LFEX) at the Institute of Laser Engineering (ILE), <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a>, which achieved a power output of 2&nbsp;PW (<span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7015200000000000000♠</span>2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em">×</span>10<sup>15</sup></span>&nbsp;W) for a duration of approximately 1&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Picosecond" title="Picosecond">ps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Based on the average <a href="/wiki/Total_solar_irradiance" title="Total solar irradiance" class="mw-redirect">total solar irradiance</a><sup id="cite_ref-TSI_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSI-17">[15]</a></sup> of 1.366&nbsp;kW/m<sup>2</sup>, the total power of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> striking Earth's atmosphere is estimated at 174&nbsp;PW (see: <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_and_thermal_watts">Electrical and thermal watts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Electrical and thermal watts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In the electric power industry, <i>megawatt electrical</i> (<b>MWe</b><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> or MW<sub>e</sub><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup>) is <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>, while <i>megawatt thermal</i> or <i>thermal megawatt</i><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> (<b>MWt</b>, MW<sub>t</sub>, or <b>MWth</b>, MW<sub>th</sub>) refers to thermal power produced. Other <a href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix" class="mw-redirect">SI prefixes</a> are sometimes used, for example <i>gigawatt electrical</i> (<b>GW<sub>e</sub></b>). "watt electrical" and "watt thermal" are not SI units,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> states that further information about a quantity should not be attached to the unit symbol but instead to the quantity symbol (i.e., <i>P</i><sub>thermal</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;270&nbsp;W rather than <i>P</i>&nbsp;=&nbsp;270&nbsp;W<sub>th</sub>) and regards these symbols as incorrect.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>For example, the <a href="/wiki/Embalse_nuclear_power_plant" title="Embalse nuclear power plant" class="mw-redirect">Embalse nuclear power plant</a> in Argentina uses a <a href="/wiki/Fission_reactor" title="Fission reactor" class="mw-redirect">fission reactor</a> to generate 2109&nbsp;MW<sub>t</sub> of heat, which creates steam to drive a turbine, which generates 648&nbsp;MW<sub>e</sub> of electricity (a numerical energy conversion efficiency of 648/2109 = 0.307, or 30.7%). The difference is due to the inefficiency of steam-turbine generators and the limitations of the theoretical <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confusion_of_watts.2C_watt-hours_and_watts_per_hour">Confusion of watts, watt-hours and watts per hour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Confusion of watts, watt-hours and watts per hour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The terms <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> are frequently confused. Power is the rate at which energy is generated or consumed and hence is measured in units (e.g. watts) that represent 'energy per unit time'.</p> 
     <p>For example, when a light bulb with a power rating of <span style="white-space:nowrap">100<span style="margin-left:0.2em">W</span></span> is turned on for one hour, the energy used is 100&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Watt_hour" title="Watt hour" class="mw-redirect">watt hours</a> (W·h), 0.1&nbsp;kilowatt hour, or 360 <a href="/wiki/Joule#Kilojoule" title="Joule">kJ</a>. This same amount of energy would light a 40-watt bulb for 2.5&nbsp;hours, or a 50-watt bulb for 2&nbsp;hours. A power station would be rated in multiples of watts, but its annual energy sales would be in multiples of watt hours. A <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hour</a> is the amount of energy equivalent to a steady power of 1 kilowatt running for 1 hour, or 3.6&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule" class="mw-redirect">MJ</a> (1000&nbsp;watts × 3600&nbsp;seconds (i.e., 60&nbsp;seconds per minute × 60&nbsp;minutes per hour) = 3,600,000&nbsp;joules = 3.6&nbsp;MJ).</p> 
     <p>Terms such as <i>watts per hour</i> are often misused when <i>watts</i> would be correct.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> Watts per hour properly refers to the <i>change</i> of power per hour. Watts per hour (W/h) might be useful to characterize the ramp-up behavior of <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant" class="mw-redirect">power plants</a>. For example, a power plant that reaches a power output of 1&nbsp;MW from 0&nbsp;MW in 15 minutes has a ramp-up rate of 4&nbsp;MW/h. <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectric</a> power plants have a very high ramp-up rate, which makes them particularly useful in peak load and emergency situations.</p> 
     <p>Major energy production or consumption is often expressed as <a href="/wiki/Terawatt_hour" title="Terawatt hour" class="mw-redirect">terawatt hours</a> for a given period that is often a calendar year or financial year. One terawatt hour is equal to a sustained power of approximately 114 megawatts for a period of one year.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Watt_second" title="Watt second">watt second</a> is a unit of energy, equal to the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>. One kilowatt hour is 3,600,000 watt seconds. The watt second is used, for example, to rate the energy storage of <a href="/wiki/Flash_(photography)" title="Flash (photography)">flash lamps</a> used in photography, although the term <i>joule</i> is generally employed.</p>
