     <p>An <b>oven</b> is a thermally insulated chamber used for the <a href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating" class="mw-redirect">heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drying" title="Drying">drying</a> of a substance,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and most commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">Kilns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a> are special-purpose ovens, used in <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a>, respectively.</p>

     <p>The earliest ovens were found in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, and dated to 29,000 BC. They were roasting and boiling pits inside <a href="/wiki/Yurt" title="Yurt">yurts</a> used to cook <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> from 20,000 BC they used pits with hot coals covered in ashes. The food was wrapped in leaves and set on top, then covered with earth.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In camps found in <a href="/wiki/Mezhirich" title="Mezhirich" class="mw-redirect">Mezhirich</a>, each mammoth bone house had a <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">hearth</a> used for heating and cooking.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Ovens were used by <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Indus Valley Civilization" class="mw-redirect">cultures who lived in the Indus Valley</a> and in pre-dynastic Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-Indus_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indus-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> By 3200 BC, each mud-brick house had an oven in settlements across the Indus Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-Indus_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indus-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Indus_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indus-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dales_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dales-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Ovens were used to cook food and to make bricks.<sup id="cite_ref-Indus_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indus-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Pre-dynastic civilizations in Egypt used kilns around 5000–4000 BC to make pottery.<sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Culinary historians credit the <a href="/wiki/Greek_people" title="Greek people" class="mw-redirect">Greeks</a> for developing <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> baking significantly.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffman2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman2004-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Front-loaded bread ovens were developed in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Castella2010_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castella2010-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The Greeks created a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">doughs</a>, loaf shapes, and styles of serving bread with other foods. Baking developed as a trade and profession as bread increasingly was prepared outside of the family home by specially trained workers to be sold to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffman2004_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman2004-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>During the Middle Ages, instead of earth and ceramic ovens, Europeans used fireplaces in conjunction with large cauldrons. These were similar to the Dutch oven. Following the Middle-Ages, ovens underwent many changes over time from wood, iron, coal, gas, and even electric. Each design had its own motivation and purpose. The wood burning stoves saw improvement through the addition of fire chambers that allowed better containment and release of smoke. Another recognizable oven would be the cast-iron stove. These were first used around the early 1700s when they themselves underwent several variations including the Stewart Oberlin iron stove that was smaller and had its own chimney.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellis_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellis-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the early part of the 19th century, coal ovens were developed. Its shape was cylindrical and was made of heavy cast-iron. The gas oven saw its first use as early as the beginning of the 19th century as well. Gas stoves became very common household ovens once gas lines were available to most houses and neighborhoods. James Sharp patented one of the first gas stoves in 1826. Other various improvements to the gas stove included the <a href="/wiki/AGA_cooker" title="AGA cooker">AGA cooker</a> invented in 1922 by <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Dal%C3%A9n" title="Gustaf Dalén">Gustaf Dalén</a>. The first electric ovens were invented in the very late 19th century, however, like many electrical inventions destined for commercial use, mass ownership of electrical ovens could not be a reality until better and more efficient use of electricity was available.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellis_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellis-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>More recently, ovens have become slightly more high-tech in terms of cooking strategy. The microwave as a cooking tool was discovered by Percy Spencer in 1946, and with the help from engineers, the microwave oven was patented.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellis_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellis-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The microwave oven uses microwave radiation to excite the molecules in food causing friction, thus producing heat.<sup id="cite_ref-Gallawa_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gallawa-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <ul> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Earth_oven" title="Earth oven">Earth oven</a></b>: An earth oven is a pit dug into the ground and then heated, usually by rocks or smoldering debris. Historically these have been used by many cultures for cooking. Cooking times are usually long, and the process is usually cooking by slow roasting the food. Earth ovens are among the most common things archaeologists look for at an anthropological dig, as they are one of the key indicators of human civilization and static society.<sup id="cite_ref-Derling_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Derling-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Ceramic oven</b>: The ceramic oven is an oven constructed of clay or any other ceramic material and takes different forms depending on the culture. The Indians refer to it as a <a href="/wiki/Tandoor" title="Tandoor">tandoor</a>, and use it for cooking. They can be dated back as far as 3,000 BC, and they have been argued to have their origins in the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley" title="Indus Valley" class="mw-redirect">Indus Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Brick ovens are also another ceramic type oven. A culture most notable for the use of brick ovens is Italy and its intimate history with pizza. However, its history also dates further back to Roman times, wherein the brick oven was used not only for commercial use but household use as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Forno_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forno-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Gas oven</b>: One of the first recorded uses of a gas stove and oven referenced a dinner party in 1802 hosted by Zachaus Winzler, where all the food was prepared either on a gas stove or in its oven compartment. In 1834, British inventor James Sharp began to commercially produce gas ovens after installing one in his own house. In 1851, the Bower's Registered Gas Stove was displayed at the Great Exhibition. This stove would set the standard and basis for the modern gas oven. Notable improvements to the gas stove since include the addition of the thermostat which assisted in temperature regulation; also an enamel coating was added to the production of gas stoves and ovens in order to help with easier cleaning.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Masonry_oven" title="Masonry oven">Masonry oven</a></b>: Masonry ovens consist of a baking chamber made of fireproof <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>. Though traditionally <a href="/wiki/Wood-fired_oven" title="Wood-fired oven">wood-fired</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>-fired ovens were common in the 19th century. Modern masonry ovens are often fired with <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, and are closely associated with artisanal <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pizza" title="Pizza">pizza</a>. In the past, however, they were also used for any cooking task that required baking.</li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">Microwave oven</a></b>: An oven that uses micro radiation waves as a source of heat in order to cook food as opposed to a fire source. Conceptualized in 1946, Dr. Perry Spencer allegedly discovered the heating properties of microwaves while studying the <a href="/wiki/Magnetron" title="Magnetron" class="mw-redirect">magnetron</a>. By 1947, the first commercial microwave was in use in Boston, Mass.<sup id="cite_ref-Microtech_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Microtech-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Wall oven</b>: Wall ovens make it easier to work with large roasting pans and Dutch ovens. A width is typically 24, 27, or 30 inches. Mounted at waist or eye level, a wall oven eliminates bending. However, it can be nested under a countertop to save space. A separate wall oven is expensive compared with a range.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cooking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Oven.agr.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oven.agr.jpg/220px-Oven.agr.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oven.agr.jpg/330px-Oven.agr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Oven.agr.jpg/440px-Oven.agr.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1840" data-file-height="1292"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Oven.agr.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Interior of a modern oven
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In cooking, the conventional oven is a <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_appliance" title="Kitchen appliance" class="mw-redirect">kitchen appliance</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Roasting" title="Roasting">roasting</a> and heating. Foods normally cooked in this manner include <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casserole" title="Casserole">casseroles</a> and baked goods such as <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cake</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">desserts</a>. In modern times, the oven is used to cook and heat food in many households across the globe.</p> 
     <p>Modern ovens are typically fueled by either <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">bottle gas</a> models available but not common. When an oven is contained in a complete stove, the fuel used for the oven may be the same as or different from the fuel used for the burners on top of the stove.</p> 
     <p>Ovens usually can use a variety of methods to cook. The most common may be to heat the oven from below. This is commonly used for baking and roasting. The oven may also be able to heat from the top to provide <a href="/wiki/Broiling" title="Broiling" class="mw-redirect">broiling</a> (US) or <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilling</a> (UK/Commonwealth). In order to provide faster, more-even cooking, a fan oven, which has a fan with a heating element around, that provides the heat. Or a fan-assisted oven that use a small fan to circulate the air in the cooking chamber, can be used.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Both also known as <a href="/wiki/Convection_oven" title="Convection oven">convection ovens</a>. An oven may also provide an integrated <a href="/wiki/Rotisserie" title="Rotisserie">rotisserie</a>.</p> 
     <p>Ovens also vary in the way that they are controlled. The simplest ovens (for example, the <a href="/wiki/AGA_cooker" title="AGA cooker">AGA cooker</a>) may not have any controls at all; the ovens simply run continuously at various temperatures. More conventional ovens have a simple <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">thermostat</a> which turns the oven on and off and selects the temperature at which it will operate. Set to the highest setting, this may also enable the broiler element. A <a href="/wiki/Timer" title="Timer">timer</a> may allow the oven to be turned on and off automatically at pre-set times. More sophisticated ovens may have complex, <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a>-based controls allowing a wide variety of operating modes and special features including the use of a <a href="/wiki/Thermistor" title="Thermistor">temperature probe</a> to automatically shut the oven off when the food is completely cooked to the desired degree.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning">Cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Some ovens provide various aids to cleaning. <i>Continuous cleaning</i> ovens have the oven chamber coated with a <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalytic</a> surface that helps break down (oxidize) food splatters and spills over time. <a href="/wiki/Self-cleaning_oven" title="Self-cleaning oven"><i>Self-cleaning</i> ovens</a> use <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolytic decomposition</a> (extreme heat) to oxidize dirt. Steam ovens may provide a wet-soak cycle to loosen dirt, allowing easier manual removal. In the absence of any special methods, chemical <i>oven cleaners</i> are sometimes used or just scrubbing.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial.2C_scientific.2C_and_artisanal_use">Industrial, scientific, and artisanal use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial, scientific, and artisanal use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Pec_na_chleba.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pec_na_chleba.JPG/220px-Pec_na_chleba.JPG" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pec_na_chleba.JPG/330px-Pec_na_chleba.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pec_na_chleba.JPG/440px-Pec_na_chleba.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3707" data-file-height="2442"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Pec_na_chleba.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Example of an industrial bakery oven
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Outside the culinary world, ovens are used for a number of purposes.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> can be used either to provide heat to a building or used to melt substances such as glass or metal for further processing. A <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> is a particular type of furnace generally associated with metal <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> manufacture) using refined <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> or similar hot-burning substance as a fuel, with air pumped in under pressure to increase the temperature of the fire. A <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a> uses a temporarily blown furnace, the smith's heart to heat iron to a glowing red to yellow temperature.</li> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a> is a high-temperature oven used in <a href="/wiki/Wood_drying" title="Wood drying">wood drying</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> manufacturing to <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">convert</a> mineral feedstock (in the form of clay or calcium or aluminum rocks) into a <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glassier</a>, more solid form. In the case of ceramic kilns, a shaped clay object is the final result, while cement kilns produce a substance called <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> that is crushed to make the final cement product. (Certain types of drying ovens used in food manufacture, especially those used in <a href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting" class="mw-redirect">malting</a>, are also referred to as kilns.)</li> 
      <li>An <a href="/wiki/Autoclave_(industrial)" title="Autoclave (industrial)">autoclave</a> is an oven-like device with features similar to a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_cooker" title="Pressure cooker" class="mw-redirect">pressure cooker</a> that allows the heating of aqueous solutions to higher temperatures than water's boiling point in order to sterilize the contents of the autoclave.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Industrial_oven" title="Industrial oven">Industrial ovens</a> are similar to their culinary equivalents and are used for a number of different applications that do not require the high temperatures of a kiln or furnace.</li> 
     </ul>
