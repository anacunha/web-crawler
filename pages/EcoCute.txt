   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">EcoCute</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p>The <b>EcoCute</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficient</a> electric <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">water heating</a> and supply system that uses heat extracted from the air to heat water for domestic, industrial and <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a> use. Instead of the more conventional <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">haloalkane</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>, EcoCute uses <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a>. The technology offers a means of <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> and reduces the emission of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EcoCute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The name of the EcoCute comes from the Japanese phrase <i>Shizen Reibai Hīto Ponpu Kyūtō-ki</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">自然冷媒ヒートポンプ給湯機</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which literally means "natural refrigerant heat pump water heater".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Eco</i> is a contraction of either <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economical</a> and <i>Cute</i> is a near homonym to <i>kyūtō</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">給湯</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>; literally “supply hot water.”<sup id="cite_ref-CRIEPI-1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRIEPI-1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EcoCute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Modern chemical refrigeration techniques developed after the proposal of the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a> in 1824. <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Perkins" title="Jacob Perkins">Jacob Perkins</a> invented an <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>-making machine that used <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> in 1843, and <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmond_Carr%C3%A9" class="extiw" title="fr:Edmond Carré">Edmond Carré</a></span></i> built a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerator</a> that used <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> in 1850. In Japan, <a href="/wiki/Fusanosuke_Kuhara" title="Fusanosuke Kuhara">Fusanosuke Kuhara</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Hitachi,_Ltd." title="Hitachi, Ltd." class="mw-redirect">Hitachi, Ltd.</a>, made an <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioner" title="Air conditioner" class="mw-redirect">air conditioner</a> for his own home use using compressed CO<sub>2</sub> as a refrigerant.</p> 
     <p>In 1930 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Midgley,_Jr." title="Thomas Midgley, Jr.">Thomas Midgley, Jr.</a> discovered dichlorodifluoromethane, a chlorinated fluorocarbon (<a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFC</a>) known as <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">freon</a>. CFCs rapidly replaced traditional refrigerant substances, including CO<sub>2</sub> (which proved hard to compress for domestic use<sup id="cite_ref-discoverCO2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discoverCO2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>), for use in <a href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" title="Heat pumps" class="mw-redirect">heat pumps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refrigerators" title="Refrigerators" class="mw-redirect">refrigerators</a>. But from the 1980s CFCs began to lose favor as refrigerant when their damaging effects on the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> were discovered. Two alternative types of refrigerant, hydrofluorocarbons (<a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbon" title="Hydrofluorocarbon" class="mw-redirect">HFCs</a>) and hydrochlorofluorocarbons (<a href="/wiki/Hydrochlorofluorocarbons#Development_of_alternatives_for_CFCs" title="Hydrochlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect">HCFCs</a>), also lost favour when they were identified as <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases" class="mw-redirect">greenhouse gases</a> (additionally, HCFCs were found to be more damaging to the ozone layer than originally thought). The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> call for the complete abandonment of such refrigerants by 2030.</p> 
     <p>In 1989, amid international concern about the effects of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect">chlorofluorocarbons</a> and hydrochlorofluorocarbons on the ozone layer, scientist <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Lorentzen_(scientist)" title="Gustav Lorentzen (scientist)">Gustav Lorentzen</a> and <a href="/wiki/SINTEF" title="SINTEF">SINTEF</a> patented a method for using CO<sub>2</sub> as a refrigerant in heating and cooling. Further research into CO<sub>2</sub> refrigeration was then conducted at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shecco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shecco (page does not exist)">Shecco</a> (Sustainable Heating and Cooling with CO<sub>2</sub>) in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, leading to increasing use of CO<sub>2</sub> refrigerant technology in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-discoverCO2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discoverCO2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1993 the Japanese company <a href="/wiki/Denso" title="Denso">Denso</a>, in collaboration with Gustav Lorentzen, developed an <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile" class="mw-redirect">automobile</a> air conditioner using CO<sub>2</sub> as a refrigerant. They demonstrated the invention at the June 1998 International Institute of Refrigeration/Gustav Lorentzen Conference.<sup id="cite_ref-bloom_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloom-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> After the conference, CRIEPI (<a href="/wiki/Central_Research_Institute_of_Electric_Power_Industry" title="Central Research Institute of Electric Power Industry">Central Research Institute of Electric Power Industry</a>) and TEPCO (<a href="/wiki/The_Tokyo_Electric_Power_Company" title="The Tokyo Electric Power Company" class="mw-redirect">The Tokyo Electric Power Company</a>) approached Denso about developing a prototype air conditioner using natural refrigerant materials instead of freon. Together they produced 30 <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototype</a> EcoCute units for a year-long experimental installation at locations throughout <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, from the cold climate of <a href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaidō" class="mw-redirect">Hokkaidō</a> to hotter <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa</a>. After this successful <a href="/wiki/Feasibility_study" title="Feasibility study">feasibility study</a>, Denso obtained a patent to compress CO<sub>2</sub> refrigerant for use in a heat pump from SINTEF in September 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-J-Pat_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J-Pat-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The first commercial domestic EcoCute was marketed in Japan by Corona Corporation in May 2001, and several manufacturers sold 1.5 million units there by October, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-bloom_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloom-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Electric_Power_Companies" title="Federation of Electric Power Companies">FEPC</a> reported 2 million EcoCute units had been delivered by end of October 2009, with the equivalent CO<sub>2</sub> absorption of 9400&nbsp;km² of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features_and_demand">Features and demand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EcoCute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Features and demand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In Japan in 1998, water heating (Kyuto <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">給湯</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kyūtō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>) accounted for 33.8% of typical domestic energy consumption, with air conditioner and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_heater" title="Kerosene heater">kerosene heater</a> heating accounting for another 26.9% and cooling by air conditioner another 2.3%. Most of the remaining 37% was spent on electrical home appliances, a field where 21st century innovations in energy conservation began to make considerable energy savings.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TEPCO-1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TEPCO-1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> This left hot water supply as the most difficult area for energy conservation, leaving a gap in the market for the EcoCute. By January 2005, 26 Japanese companies were producing more than 450 models of EcoCute machines, and sales of domestic units increased 130-150% each year between 2001 and 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-J-Pat_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J-Pat-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Denso first introduced the EcoCute outside Japan at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#2003_.E2.80.93_COP_9.2C_Milan.2C_Italy" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">COP9</a> <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> on December 9, 2003. From 2007, Denso began concentrating on marketing the EcoCute in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discoverCO2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discoverCO2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In Japan, the Japanese government incorporated the EcoCute into its CO<sub>2</sub> reduction program under the Kyoto Protocol, mandating the installation of 5.2 million units in commercial and domestic properties by 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-CRIEPI-1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRIEPI-1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The cost of EcoCute is approximately 500 thousand <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">Japanese yen</a> as of February - March 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EcoCute_machine_basics">EcoCute machine basics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EcoCute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EcoCute machine basics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>An EcoCute machine or system consists of a heat pump and <a href="/wiki/Hot_water" title="Hot water" class="mw-redirect">hot water</a> storage unit. The components are serially concatenated with <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">sealing</a> refrigerant CO<sub>2</sub> gas in <a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">circulation</a>.</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>At the first stage, a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> collects heat from the air outside to use as energy for the refrigerant. Air flow is usually obtained using a <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_fan" title="Centrifugal fan">centrifugal fan</a>; in cold areas with ambient temperatures around -20 to -25°C an <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/auxiliary" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:auxiliary">auxiliary</a> <a href="/wiki/Fan_heater" title="Fan heater">fan heater</a> is attached.</li> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">gas compressor</a> is used to heat the gas CO<sub>2</sub> refrigerant to around 100°C under pressure of 10<a href="/wiki/Mega-" title="Mega-">M</a><a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> via <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> compression. The carbon dioxide becomes a supercritical fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Several types of compressors can be used, including dual layer cylindrical compressors, <a href="/wiki/Scroll_compressor" title="Scroll compressor">scroll compressors</a>, and dual stage <a href="/wiki/Rotary_screw_compressor" title="Rotary screw compressor" class="mw-redirect">rotary compressors</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>At the second stage a heat exchanger transfers energy from the hot refrigerant into water to produce hot water. Water temperatures around 5°C and up are suitable at this stage.</li> 
      <li>Finally, ejector<sup id="cite_ref-J-Pat_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-J-Pat-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> or expansion <a href="/wiki/Valve#Types_of_valves" title="Valve">valves</a> reduce pressure on the refrigerant, letting it cool via <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_expansion" title="Adiabatic expansion" class="mw-redirect">adiabatic expansion</a> and revert to CO<sub>2</sub> gas.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>The EcoCute can derive over two units of heat energy from ambient air for every unit of input electrical energy. In combination it can produce more than three units of hot water energy, resulting in reduced CO<sub>2</sub> emissions compared to water heating via electricity or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. To produce 90°C hot water, an EcoCute consumes 66% less energy than an electric water heater, and costs 80% less than heating water via natural gas in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Also, by reducing use of fossil fuels, the EcoCute results in more than 50% reductions in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Not considering upstream losses of input source energy, the EcoCute's <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">COP</a> is 3.8 in industrial use, while electric power water heating is 1.0, and gas <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> is 0.88 including <a href="/wiki/Pilot_light" title="Pilot light">pilot light</a> loss.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
