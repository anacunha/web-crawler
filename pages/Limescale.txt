   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Limescale</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div>

     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Kesselstein_k.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Kesselstein_k.jpg/250px-Kesselstein_k.jpg" width="250" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Kesselstein_k.jpg/375px-Kesselstein_k.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Kesselstein_k.jpg/500px-Kesselstein_k.jpg 2x" data-file-width="571" data-file-height="735"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Kesselstein_k.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Limescale (
        <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscopy" title="Scanning electron microscopy" class="mw-redirect">scanning electron microscopy</a> micrograph, field of view 64 x 90 µm)
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><b>Limescale</b> is the hard, off-white, chalky deposit found in kettles, hot-water boilers and the inside of inadequately maintained hot-water central heating systems.</p> 
     <p>It is also often found as a similar deposit on the inner surface of old pipes and other surfaces where "<a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a>" has evaporated. In addition to being unsightly and hard to clean, limescale seriously impairs the operation or damages various components.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The type found deposited on the heating elements of water heaters has a main component of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>. Hard water contains <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> (and often <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>) <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> or similar ions. Calcium salts, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">calcium bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> are both more soluble in hot water than cold water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Limescale#Dubious" title="Talk:Limescale">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> Thus, heating water does not cause calcium carbonate to precipitate per se. However, there is an equilibrium between dissolved calcium bicarbonate and dissolved calcium carbonate:</p> 
     <p>Ca<sup>2+</sup> + 2HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> ⇋ Ca<sup>2+</sup> + CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</p> 
     <p>where the equilibrium is driven by the carbonate/bicarbonate, not the calcium. Note that the CO<sub>2</sub> is dissolved in the water.</p> 
     <p>There is also an equilibrium of carbon dioxide between dissolved in water (dis) and the gaseous state (g): CO<sub>2(dis)</sub> ⇋ CO<sub>2(g)</sub></p> 
     <p>The equilibrium of CO<sub>2</sub> also moves to the right towards gaseous CO<sub>2</sub> when the water temperature rises. When water that contains dissolved calcium carbonate is warmed, CO<sub>2</sub> is removed from the water as gas causing the equilibrium of bicarbonate and carbonate to shift to the right, increasing the concentration of dissolved carbonate. As the concentration of carbonate increases, calcium carbonate precipitates as the salt: Ca<sup>2+</sup> + CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> ⇋ CaCO<sub>3</sub>.</p> 
     <p>As new cold water with dissolved calcium carbonate/bicarbonate is added and heated, CO<sub>2</sub> gas is removed, carbonate concentration increases, and more calcium carbonate precipitates.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Descaling_agent" title="Descaling agent">Descaling agents</a> are used to remove scale. Prevention of scale build-up relies on the technologies of <a href="/wiki/Water_softening" title="Water softening">water softening</a>.</p>

         <a href="/wiki/File:Limescale-in-pipe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Limescale buildup inside a pipe reduces both liquid flow through the pipe and thermal conduction from the liquid to the outer pipe shell. Both effects will reduce the pipe's overall thermal efficiency when used as a 
        <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a>.
       </div>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_materials">Related materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limescale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Related materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soap_scum">Soap scum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limescale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soap scum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Calcium <a href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation" class="mw-redirect">cations</a> from hard water can also combine with <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, which would dissolve in soft water. This combination often forms <a href="/wiki/Soap_scum" title="Soap scum">soap scum</a> which precipitates out in a thin film on the interior surfaces of baths, sinks, and drainage pipes.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_components_of_scale">Iron components of scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limescale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iron components of scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Scale is often coloured because of the presence of iron-containing compounds. The three main iron compounds are <a href="/wiki/Wustite" title="Wustite" class="mw-redirect">wustite</a> (FeO), <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>).</p>
