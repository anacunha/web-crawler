   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ton</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
      Jump to: 
     <a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 
     <a href="#p-search">search</a> 
    </div> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Not to be confused with 
      <a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">Tonnage</a>&nbsp;or 
      <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">Tonne</a>.
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      For other uses, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Ton_(disambiguation)" title="Ton (disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig">Ton (disambiguation)</a>.
     </div> 
     <p>The <b>ton</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit of measure</a>. It has a long history and has acquired a number of meanings and uses over the years. It is used principally as a unit of mass. Its original use as a measurement of volume has continued in the capacity of cargo ships and in terms such as the freight ton. It can also be used as a measure of energy, for truck classification, or as a colloquial term.</p> 
     <p>It is derived from the <i><a href="/wiki/Tun_(volume)" title="Tun (volume)" class="mw-redirect">tun</a></i>, the term applied to a barrel of the largest size. This could contain a volume between 175 and 213 <a href="/wiki/Imperial_gallon" title="Imperial gallon" class="mw-redirect">imperial gallons</a> (210 and 256&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/US_gallon" title="US gallon" class="mw-redirect">US&nbsp;gal</a>), which could weigh around 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pounds</a> (910&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>) and occupy some 60 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic feet</a> (1.7&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">m<sup>3</sup></a>) of space.<sup id="cite_ref-BTS.GOV_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTS.GOV-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the United Kingdom the ton is defined as 2,240 pounds (1,016&nbsp;kg) (<a href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" title="Avoirdupois">avoirdupois</a> <a href="/wiki/Avoirdupois_pound" title="Avoirdupois pound" class="mw-redirect">pounds</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> From 1965 the UK embarked upon a programme of <a href="/wiki/Metrication_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Metrication in the United Kingdom">metrication</a> and gradually introduced metric units, including the <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonne</a> (metric ton), defined as 1000&nbsp;kg (2,204.6 lbs). The UK <a href="/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Act_1985" title="Weights and Measures Act 1985" class="mw-redirect">Weights and Measures Act 1985</a> explicitly excluded from use for trade many units and terms, including the ton and the term "metric ton" for "tonne".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the United States and formerly Canada<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> a ton is defined to be 2,000 pounds (907&nbsp;kg).</p> 
     <p>Where confusion is possible, the 2240&nbsp;lb ton is called "<a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long ton</a>" and the 2000&nbsp;lb ton "<a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short ton</a>"; the tonne is distinguished by its spelling, but usually pronounced the same as ton, hence the US term "metric ton". In the UK the final "e" of "tonne" can also be pronounced (/ˈtʌnɪ/),<sup id="cite_ref-oedtonne_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedtonne-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> or "metric ton" when it is necessary to make the distinction.</p> 
     <p>Where accuracy is required the correct term must be used, but for many purposes this is not necessary: the metric and long tons differ by only 1.6%, and the short ton is within 11% of both. The ton is the heaviest unit of weight referred to in colloquial speech.</p> 
     <p>The term "ton" is also used to refer to a number of units of <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>, ranging from 35 to 100 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic feet</a> (0.99 to 2.83&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">m<sup>3</sup></a>) in capacity.</p> 
     <p>It can also be used as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, expressed as an equivalent of coal burnt or <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> detonated.</p> 
     <p>In refrigeration, a ton is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a>, sometimes called a <i><a href="/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration" title="Ton of refrigeration">ton of refrigeration</a></i>. It is the power required to melt or freeze one short ton of ice per day. The refrigeration ton·hour is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, the energy required to melt or freeze <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>24</sub></span> short ton of ice.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <ul> 
      <li>The long ton is used for petroleum products such as <a href="/wiki/Aviation_fuel" title="Aviation fuel">aviation fuel</a>.</li> 
      <li><b>Deadweight ton</b> (abbreviation 'DWT' or 'dwt') is a measure of a ship's carrying capacity, including bunker oil, fresh water, ballast water, crew and provisions. It is expressed in tonnes (1000&nbsp;kg) or long tons (2240 pounds, about 1016&nbsp;kg). This measurement is also used in the U.S. tonnage of naval ships.</li> 
      <li>Increasingly, <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> are being used rather than long tons in measuring the displacement of ships. <i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">tonnage</a>.</li> 
      <li><b>Harbour ton</b> used in South Africa in the 20th century, 2000 pounds or one short ton.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Both the long ton and the short ton are 20 <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">hundredweight</a>, being 112 and 100 pounds respectively. Before the twentieth century there were several definitions. Prior to the 15th century in England, the ton was 20&nbsp;hundredweight, each of 108&nbsp;lb, giving a ton of 2,160 pounds (980&nbsp;kg).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the nineteenth century in different parts of Britain, definitions of 2240, 2352, and 2400&nbsp;lb were used, with 2000&nbsp;lb for explosives; the legal ton was usually [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] 2240&nbsp;lb.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><span id="Assay_ton"></span> <b>Assay ton</b> (abbreviation 'AT') is not a unit of measurement, but a standard quantity used in <a href="/wiki/Assay" title="Assay">assaying</a> ores of precious metals; it is <span class="frac nowrap">29<span class="visualhide">&nbsp;</span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>6</sub></span> grams (short assay ton) or <span class="frac nowrap">32<span class="visualhide">&nbsp;</span><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> grams (long assay ton), the amount which bears the same ratio to a milligram as a short or long ton bears to a <a href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">troy ounce</a>. In other words, the number of milligrams of a particular metal found in a sample of this size gives the number of troy ounces contained in a short or long ton of ore.</p> 
     <p>In documents that predate 1960 the word <i>ton</i> is sometimes spelled <i>tonne</i>, but in more recent documents <i>tonne</i> refers exclusively to the <a href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton" class="mw-redirect">metric ton</a>.</p> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a> <b>tHM</b> and <b>MTHM</b> mean tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals" class="mw-redirect">heavy metals</a>, and <b>MTU</b> means tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> industry, the abbreviation <b>THM</b> means 'tons/tonnes hot metal', which refers to the amount of liquid iron or steel that is produced, particularly in the context of <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> production or specific consumption.</p> 
     <p>A <b>dry ton</b> or <b>dry tonne</b> has the same mass value, but the material (<a href="/wiki/Sludge" title="Sludge">sludge</a>, slurries, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a>, and similar mixtures in which solid material is soaked with or suspended in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>) has been dried to a relatively low, consistent <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> level (<a href="/wiki/Dry_weight" title="Dry weight">dry weight</a>). If the material is in its natural, wet state, it is called a <b>wet ton</b> or <b>wet tonne</b>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_of_volume">Units of volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Units of volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">Tonnage</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(ship)" title="Displacement (ship)">displacement</a>, essentially the weight, of a ship is traditionally expressed in <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>. To simplify measurement it is determined by measuring the <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>, rather than weight, of water displaced, and calculating the weight from the volume and density.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> For practical purposes the <b>displacement ton</b> (DT) is a unit of volume, 35 cubic feet (0.9911&nbsp;m<sup>3</sup>), the approximate volume occupied by one ton of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> (the actual volume varies with salinity and temperature).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> It is slightly less than the 224 imperial gallons (1.018&nbsp;m<sup>3</sup>) of the <b>water ton</b> (based on <a href="/wiki/Distilled_water" title="Distilled water">distilled water</a>).</p> 
     <p>One <b>measurement ton</b> or <b>freight ton</b> is equal to 40 cubic feet (1.133&nbsp;m<sup>3</sup>), but historically it has had several different definitions. It is sometimes abbreviated as "MTON".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The freight ton represents the volume of a truck, train or other freight carrier. In the past it has been used for a cargo ship but the register ton is now preferred. It is correctly abbreviated as "FT"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but some users are now using freight ton to represent a weight of 1 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonne</a> (1,000&nbsp;kg; 2,205&nbsp;lb), thus the more common abbreviations are now <b>M/T</b>, <b>MT</b>, or <b>MTON</b> (for measurement ton), which still cause it to be confused with the tonne, or even the <a href="/wiki/Megatonne" title="Megatonne" class="mw-redirect">megatonne</a>.</p> 
     <p>The <b>register ton</b> is a unit of volume used for the cargo capacity of a ship, defined as 100 cubic feet (2.832&nbsp;m<sup>3</sup>). It is often abbreviated <b>RT</b> or <b>GRT</b> for <b>gross registered ton</b> (The former providing confusion with the refrigeration ton). It is known as a <i>tonneau de mer</i> in Belgium, but, in France, a <i>tonneau de mer</i> is 1.44 cubic metres (50.85&nbsp;cu&nbsp;ft).</p> 
     <p>The <b>Panama Canal/Universal Measurement System (PC/UMS)</b> is based on <a href="/wiki/Net_tonnage" title="Net tonnage">net tonnage</a>, modified for Panama Canal billing purposes. PC/UMS is based on a mathematical formula to calculate a vessel's total volume; a <b>PC/UMS net ton</b> is equivalent to 100 cubic feet of capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <b>water ton</b> is used chiefly in Great Britain, in statistics dealing with petroleum products, and is defined as 224 imperial gallons (35.96&nbsp;cu&nbsp;ft; 1.018&nbsp;m<sup>3</sup>),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> the volume occupied by 1 long ton (2,240&nbsp;lb; 1,016&nbsp;kg) of water under the conditions that define the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial</a> <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_of_energy_and_power">Units of energy and power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Units of energy and power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ton_of_TNT">Ton of TNT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ton of TNT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">TNT equivalent</a>
     </div> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A <b>ton of TNT</b> or <i>tonne of TNT</i> is a unit of energy equal to 10<sup>9</sup> (thermochemical) <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a>, also known as a gigacalorie (Gcal), equal to 4.184 <a href="/wiki/Gigajoule" title="Gigajoule" class="mw-redirect">gigajoules</a> (GJ).</li> 
      <li>A <b>kiloton of TNT</b> or <i>kilotonne of TNT</i> is a unit of energy equal to 10<sup>12</sup> calories, also known as a teracalorie (Tcal), equal to 4.184 terajoules (TJ).</li> 
      <li>A <b>megaton of TNT</b> (1,000,000 metric tonnes) or <i>megatonne of TNT</i> is a unit of energy equal to 10<sup>15</sup> calories, also known (infrequently) as a petacalorie (Pcal), equal to 4.184 petajoules (PJ).</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Note that these are small calories (cal). The large or dietary calorie (Cal) is equal to one kilocalorie (kcal), and is gradually being replaced by the latter correct term.</p> 
     <p>Early values for the explosive energy released by <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">trinitrotoluene</a> (TNT) ranged from 900 to 1100 calories per gram. In order to standardise the use of the term <i>TNT</i> as a unit of energy, an arbitrary value was assigned based on 1000 calories (1&nbsp;kcal or 4.184&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Kilojoule" title="Kilojoule" class="mw-redirect">kJ</a>) per gram. Thus there is no longer a direct connection to the chemical TNT itself. It is now merely a unit of energy that happens to be expressed using words normally associated with mass (e.g., kilogram, tonne, pound).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The definition applies for both spellings: <i>ton of TNT</i> and <i>tonne of TNT</i>.</p> 
     <p>Measurements in tons of TNT have been used primarily to express <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_yield" title="Nuclear weapon yield">nuclear weapon yields</a>, though they have also been used since in <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a> as well.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonne_of_oil_equivalent"><span id="Ton.23Ton_of_oil_equivalent"></span> Tonne of oil equivalent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tonne of oil equivalent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration">Refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration" title="Ton of refrigeration">Ton of refrigeration</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The unit <i>ton</i> is used in <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration_ton" title="Refrigeration ton" class="mw-redirect">air conditioning</a> to measure the rate of heat absorption. Prior to the introduction of mechanical refrigeration, cooling was accomplished by delivering ice. Installing one <a href="/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration" title="Ton of refrigeration">ton of mechanical refrigeration</a> capacity replaced the daily delivery of one ton of ice.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>In North America, a <b>standard ton of refrigeration</b> is 12,000&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTU</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">h</a> (3,517&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a>). "The <a href="/wiki/Heat_absorption" title="Heat absorption" class="mw-redirect">heat absorption</a> per day is approximately the <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_fusion" title="Heat of fusion" class="mw-redirect">heat of fusion</a> of 1&nbsp;<i>ton</i> of ice at 32&nbsp;°F (0&nbsp;°C)."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> This is approximately the power required to melt one short ton (2,000&nbsp;lb or 907&nbsp;kg) of ice at 0&nbsp;°C (32&nbsp;°F) in 24&nbsp;hours, thus representing the delivery of 1 short ton (0.893 long tons; 0.907&nbsp;t) of ice per day.</li> 
      <li>A less common usage is the power required to cool 1 long ton (2,240&nbsp;lb or 1,016&nbsp;kg = 1 long ton or 1.120 short tons or 1.016&nbsp;t) of water by 1&nbsp;°F (0.56&nbsp;°C) every 10 minutes = 13,440&nbsp;BTU/h (3,939&nbsp;W).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>A refrigeration ton should be regarded as power produced by a chiller when operating in standard <a href="/wiki/Air_Conditioning,_Heating_and_Refrigeration_Institute" title="Air Conditioning, Heating and Refrigeration Institute">AHRI</a> conditions, which are typically 44&nbsp;°F (7&nbsp;°C) for chilled water unit, and 95&nbsp;°F (35&nbsp;°C) air entering the condenser. This is commonly referred to as "true ton". Manufacturers can also provide tables for chillers operating at other chilled water temperature conditions (as 65&nbsp;°F or 18.3&nbsp;°C) which can show more favorable data, which are not valid when making performance comparisons among units unless conversion rates are applied.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The refrigeration ton is commonly abbreviated as <b>RT</b>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Informal_tons">Informal tons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Informal tons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Ton</b> is also used informally, often as <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a>, to mean a large amount of something, material or not. For example, "I have a ton of homework to do this weekend."</li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, a ton is colloquially used to refer to 100 of a given unit. Ton can thus refer to a speed of 100 miles per hour, and is prefixed by an indefinite article, e.g. "Lee was doing a ton down the motorway"; to money e.g. "How much did you pay for that?" "A ton" (£100); to 100 points in a game e.g. "Eric just threw a ton in our darts game" (in some games, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>, more commonly called a century); or to a hundred of any other countable figure.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, when talking about money a ton is used to indicate <a href="/wiki/100000_(number)" title="100000 (number)">100,000</a>. For example a house costing 2 ton would cost 200,000 euros.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> This convention has been in use since at least the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <i>tonni</i> is often used as a synonym for one thousand (<a href="/wiki/1000_(number)" title="1000 (number)">1000</a>), especially when referring to money. For example, "tonnin seteli" was a 1000 <a href="/wiki/Finnish_mark" title="Finnish mark" class="mw-redirect">mark</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknote</a> and a popular TV show was called "Kymppitonni" ("ten tons" = 10,000 marks).</li> 
     </ul>
