   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Three-phase electric power</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p><b>Three-phase electric power</b> is a common method of <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating-current</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_generation" title="Electric power generation" class="mw-redirect">generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">distribution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is a type of <a href="/wiki/Polyphase_system" title="Polyphase system">polyphase system</a> and is the most common method used by <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grids</a> worldwide to transfer power. It is also used to power large <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">motors</a> and other heavy loads. A <a href="/wiki/Three-phase" title="Three-phase">three-phase</a> system is usually more economical than an equivalent <a href="/wiki/Single-phase_electric_power" title="Single-phase electric power">single-phase</a> at the same line to ground <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> because it uses less conductor material to transmit electrical power.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The three-phase system was independently invented by <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Ferraris" title="Galileo Ferraris">Galileo Ferraris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Dolivo-Dobrovolsky" title="Mikhail Dolivo-Dobrovolsky">Mikhail Dolivo-Dobrovolsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Wenstr%C3%B6m" title="Jonas Wenström">Jonas Wenström</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">Nikola Tesla</a> in the late 1880s.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle">Principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>In a symmetric three-phase power supply system, three conductors each carry an <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> of the same frequency and voltage amplitude relative to a common reference but with a phase difference of one third the period. The common reference is usually connected to ground and often to a current-carrying conductor called the neutral. Due to the phase difference, the voltage on any conductor reaches its peak at one third of a cycle after one of the other conductors and one third of a cycle before the remaining conductor. This phase delay gives constant power transfer to a balanced linear load. It also makes it possible to produce a rotating magnetic field in an <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> and generate other phase arrangements using transformers (For instance, a two phase system using a <a href="/wiki/Scott-T_transformer" title="Scott-T transformer">Scott-T transformer</a>).</p> 
     <p>The symmetric three‐phase systems described here are simply referred to as <i>three‐phase systems</i> because, although it is possible to design and implement asymmetric three‐phase power systems (i.e., with unequal voltages or phase shifts), they are not used in practice because they lack the most important advantages of symmetric systems.</p> 
     <p>In a three‐phase system feeding a balanced and linear load, the sum of the instantaneous currents of the three conductors is zero. In other words, the current in each conductor is equal in magnitude to, but with the opposite sign of, the sum of the currents in the other two. The return path for the current in any phase conductor is the other two phase conductors.</p> 
     <p>Compared to a single-phase AC power supply that uses two conductors (phase and <a href="/wiki/Ground_and_neutral" title="Ground and neutral">neutral</a>), a three-phase supply with no neutral, the same phase-to-ground voltage and current capacity per phase can transmit three times as much power using just 1.5 times as many wires (i.e., three instead of two). Thus, the ratio of capacity to conductor material is doubled. The same (but not the other properties of three-phase power) can also be attained with a center-grounded single-phase system.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Three-phase systems may also utilize a fourth wire, particularly in low-voltage distribution. This is the <a href="/wiki/Ground_and_neutral" title="Ground and neutral">neutral</a> wire. The neutral allows three separate single-phase supplies to be provided at a constant voltage and is commonly used for supplying groups of domestic properties which are each <a href="/wiki/Single-phase_electric_power" title="Single-phase electric power">single-phase</a> loads. The connections are arranged so that, as far as possible in each group, equal power is drawn from each phase. Further up the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">distribution system</a>, the currents are usually well balanced. Transformers may be wired in a way that they have a four‐wire secondary but a three‐wire primary while allowing unbalanced loads and the associated secondary‐side neutral currents.</p> 
     <p>Three-phase supplies have properties that make them very desirable in electric power distribution systems:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>The phase currents tend to cancel out one another, summing to zero in the case of a linear balanced load. This makes it possible to reduce the size of the neutral conductor because it carries little or no current. With a balanced load, all the phase conductors carry the same current and so can be the same size.</li> 
      <li>Power transfer into a linear balanced load is constant, which helps to reduce generator and motor vibrations.</li> 
      <li>Three-phase systems can produce a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_magnetic_field" title="Rotating magnetic field">rotating magnetic field</a> with a specified direction and constant magnitude, which simplifies the design of electric motors.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Most household loads are single-phase. In North American residences, three-phase power might feed a multiple-unit apartment block, but the household loads are connected only as single phase. In lower-density areas, only a single phase might be used for distribution. Some large European appliances may be powered by three-phase power, such as electric stoves and clothes dryers.</p> 
     <p>Wiring for the three phases is typically identified by color codes which vary by country. Connection of the phases in the right order is required to ensure the intended direction of rotation of three-phase motors. For example, pumps and fans may not work in reverse. Maintaining the identity of phases is required if there is any possibility two sources can be connected at the same time; a direct interconnection between two different phases is a short-circuit.</p>

     <p>At the <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator" class="mw-redirect">electrical generator</a> converts mechanical power into a set of three <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric currents</a>, one from each coil (or winding) of the generator. The windings are arranged such that the currents vary <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoidally</a> at the same frequency but with the peaks and troughs of their <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a> forms offset to provide three complementary currents with a phase separation of one-third cycle (<a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">120° or <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2π</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> radians</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Utility_frequency" title="Utility frequency">generator frequency</a> is typically 50 or 60 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a>, varying by country.</p> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Mains_power_systems" title="Mains power systems" class="mw-redirect">Mains power systems</a>
     </div> 
     <p>At the power station, <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a> change the voltage from generators to a level suitable for <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a> minimizing losses.</p> 
     <p>After further voltage conversions in the transmission network, the voltage is finally transformed to the standard utilization before power is supplied to customers.</p> 
     <p>Most automotive alternators generate three phase AC and rectify it to DC with a <a href="/wiki/Diode_bridge" title="Diode bridge">diode bridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transformer_connections">Transformer connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transformer connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A "delta" connected transformer winding is connected between phases of a three-phase system. A "wye" ("star") transformer connects each winding from a phase wire to a common neutral point.</p> 
     <p>In an "open delta" or "V" system, only two transformers are used. A closed delta system can operate as an open delta if one of the transformers has failed or needs to be removed.<sup id="cite_ref-Fowler_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-7">[7]</a></sup> In open delta, each transformer must carry current for its respective phases as well as current for the third phase, therefore capacity is reduced to 87%. With one of three transformers missing and the remaining two at 87% efficiency, the capacity is 58% (<span class="texhtml">(2/3) × 87%</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-power_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-power-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Where a delta-fed system must be grounded for detection of stray current to ground or protection from surge voltages, a grounding transformer (usually a <a href="/wiki/Zigzag_transformer" title="Zigzag transformer">zigzag transformer</a>) may be connected to allow ground fault currents to return from any phase to ground. Another variation is a "corner grounded" delta system, which is a closed delta that is grounded at one of the junctions of transformers.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-wire_and_four-wire_circuits">Three-wire and four-wire circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Three-wire and four-wire circuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg/220px-The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg.png" width="220" height="406" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg/330px-The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg/440px-The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="324" data-file-height="598"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:The_basic_3-phase_configurations.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Wye (Y) and Delta (Δ) circuits
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>There are two basic three-phase configurations: delta and wye (star). As shown in the diagram, a delta configuration requires only 3 wires for transmission but a wye (star) configuration may utilise a fourth wire. The fourth wire, if present, is provided as a Neutral and is normally Grounded. The "3-wire" and "4-wire" designations do not count the <a href="/wiki/Ground_wire" title="Ground wire" class="mw-redirect">ground wire</a> used above many transmission lines, which is solely for fault protection and does not carry current under non-fault conditions.</p> 
     <p>A four-wire system with symmetrical voltages between phase and neutral is obtained when the neutral is connected to the "common star point" of all supply windings. In such a system, all three phases will have the same magnitude of voltage relative to the Neutral. Other non-symmetrical systems have been used.</p> 
     <p>The four-wire wye system is used when ground referenced voltages or the flexibility of more voltage selections are required. Faults on one phase to ground will cause a protection event (fuse or breaker open) locally and not involve other phases or other connected equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An example of application is local distribution in Europe (and elsewhere), where each customer may be only fed from one phase and the neutral (which is common to the three phases). When a group of customers sharing the neutral draw unequal phase currents, the common neutral wire carries the currents resulting from these imbalances. Electrical engineers try to design the system so the loads are balanced as much as possible within premises where 3-phase power is utilized.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These same principles apply to the wide scale distribution of power to individual premises. Hence, every effort is made by supply authorities to distribute all three phases over a large number of premises so that, on average, as nearly as possible a balanced load is seen at the point of supply. For domestic use, some countries such as the UK may supply one phase and neutral at a high current (up to 100A) to one property, while others such as Germany may supply 3 phases and neutral to each customer, but at a lower fuse rating, typically 32 A per phase, and "shuffled" to avoid the effect that more load tends to be put on the first phase.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:High_leg_delta_transformer.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/High_leg_delta_transformer.svg/220px-High_leg_delta_transformer.svg.png" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/High_leg_delta_transformer.svg/330px-High_leg_delta_transformer.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/High_leg_delta_transformer.svg/440px-High_leg_delta_transformer.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="230" data-file-height="270"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:High_leg_delta_transformer.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A transformer for a "high-leg delta" system; (Assuming a 200 V, 3-phase supply) 200 V 3-phase motors would be connected to L1, L2 and L3. 200 V single-phase load would be connected between L1 and L2. Single-phase 100 V supplies (180 degrees "out of phase") would be obtained between either L1 or L2 and the neutral (N). L3 (wild or high leg) will be 173.2 V with respect to the neutral.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In North America, a <a href="/wiki/High-leg_delta" title="High-leg delta">high-leg delta</a> supply is sometimes used, where one winding of a delta connected transformer feeding the load is center-tapped and that center tap is grounded and connected as a Neutral, as shown in the second diagram. This setup produces three different voltages. If the voltage between the center tap (neutral) and each of the two adjacent phases is 120 V (100%), the voltage across any two phases is 240 V (200%), and the neutral to "high leg" voltage is ≈ 208 V (173%).<sup id="cite_ref-Fowler_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The reason for providing the delta connected supply is usually to power large motors requiring a rotating field. However, the premises concerned will also require the "normal" North American 120 V supplies, two of which are derived (180 degrees "out of phase") between the "Neutral" and either of the center tapped phase points.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-phase_loads">Single-phase loads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Single-phase loads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Single-phase loads may be connected across any two phases, or a load can be connected from phase to neutral.<sup id="cite_ref-IAEI_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAEI-13">[13]</a></sup> Distributing single-phase loads among the phases of a three-phase system balances the load and makes most economical use of conductors and transformers.</p> 
     <p>In a symmetrical three-phase four-wire, wye system, the three phase conductors have the same voltage to the system neutral. The voltage between line conductors is <a href="/wiki/Square_root_of_3" title="Square root of 3"><span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">3</span></span></a> times the phase conductor to neutral voltage:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_\text{LL} = \sqrt{3} V_\text{LN}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/b/4/8b4da069646b07ae30555a456d858678.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The currents returning from the customers' premises to the supply transformer all share the neutral wire. If the loads are evenly distributed on all three phases, the sum of the returning currents in the neutral wire is approximately zero. Any unbalanced phase loading on the secondary side of the transformer will use the transformer capacity inefficiently.</p> 
     <p>If the supply neutral is broken, phase-to-neutral voltage is no longer maintained. Phases with higher relative loading will experience reduced voltage, and phases with lower relative loading will experience elevated voltage, up to the phase-to-phase voltage.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/High-leg_delta" title="High-leg delta">high-leg delta</a> provides phase-to-neutral relationship of <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>LL</sub> = 2 <i>V</i><sub>LN</sub> </span>, however, LN load is imposed on one phase.<sup id="cite_ref-Fowler_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-7">[7]</a></sup> A transformer manufacturer's page suggests that LN loading not exceed 5% of transformer capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">3</span></span> ≈ 1.73, defining <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>LN</sub></span> as 100% gives <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>LL</sub></span> <span class="texhtml">≈ 100% × 1.73 = 173%</span>. If <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>LL</sub></span> was set as 100%, then <span class="texhtml"><i>V</i><sub>LN</sub> ≈ 57.7%</span>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unbalanced_loads">Unbalanced loads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Unbalanced loads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>When the currents on the three live wires of a three-phase system are not equal or are not at an exact 120° phase angle, the power loss is greater than for a perfectly balanced system. The method of <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_components" title="Symmetrical components">symmetrical components</a> is used to analyze unbalanced systems.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-linear_loads">Non-linear loads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non-linear loads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>With linear loads, the neutral only carries the current due to imbalance between the phases. Devices that utilize rectifier-capacitor front-end such as <a href="/wiki/Switch-mode_power_supplies" title="Switch-mode power supplies" class="mw-redirect">switch-mode power supplies</a>, computers, office equipment and such produce <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-order_harmonic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Third-order harmonic (page does not exist)">third-order harmonics</a> that are in-phase on all the supply phases. Consequently, such harmonic currents add in the neutral, which can cause the neutral current to exceed the phase current.<sup id="cite_ref-IAEI_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAEI-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-phase_loads">Three-phase loads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Three-phase loads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>An important class of three-phase load is the <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a>. A three-phase induction motor has a simple design, inherently high starting torque and high efficiency. Such motors are applied in industry for many applications. A three-phase motor is more compact and less costly than a single-phase motor of the same voltage class and rating and single-phase AC motors above 10 HP (7.5&nbsp;kW) are uncommon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Three-phase motors also vibrate less and hence last longer than single-phase motors of the same power used under the same conditions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Line frequency flicker in light can be reduced by evenly spreading three phases across line frequency operated light sources so that illuminated area is provided light from all three phases. The effect of line frequency flicker is detrimental to super slow motion cameras used in sports event broadcasting. Three phase lighting has been applied successfully at the 2008 Beijing Olympics to provide consistent light level for each frame for SSM cameras.<sup id="cite_ref-3phlight_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3phlight-17">[17]</a></sup> Resistance heating loads such as electric <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a> or space heating may be connected to three-phase systems. Electric lighting may also be similarly connected.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">Rectifiers</a> may use a three-phase source to produce a six-pulse DC output.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The output of such rectifiers is much smoother than rectified single phase and, unlike single-phase, does not drop to zero between pulses. Such rectifiers may be used for battery charging, <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> processes such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_production" title="Aluminium production" class="mw-redirect">aluminium production</a> or for operation of DC motors. "Zig-zag" transformers may make the equivalent of six-phase full-wave rectification, twelve pulses per cycle, and this method is occasionally employed to reduce the cost of the filtering components, while improving the quality of the resulting DC.</p> 
     <p>One example of a three-phase load is the <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steelmaking</a> and in refining of ores.</p> 
     <p>In many European countries electric stoves are usually designed for a three-phase feed. However, the individual heating units are often connected between phase and neutral to allow for connection to a single-phase circuit e.g. if within an older domestic property a three-phase feed is not yet available.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Other usual three-phase loads in the domestic field are <a href="/wiki/Tankless_water_heating" title="Tankless water heating">tankless water heating systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Storage_heater" title="Storage heater">storage heaters</a>. However, since those references appeared homes in Europe and the UK have standardised on a single-phase supply with a nominal 230 V (in practice 240 V in the UK), which is used for all purposes. Most groups of houses are fed from a three-phase supply so that individual premises with above-average demand can be fed with a second or third phase connection, although domestic appliances are invariably designed for a single-phase supply.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_converters">Phase converters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Phase converters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Phase converters are used when three-phase equipment needs to be operated on a single-phase power source. They are used when three-phase power is not available or cost is not justifiable. Such converters may also allow the frequency to be varied (resynthesis) allowing speed control. Some railway locomotives use a single-phase source to drive three-phase motors fed through an electronic drive.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical">Mechanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mechanical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>One method to generate three-phase power from a single-phase source is the <a href="/wiki/Rotary_phase_converter" title="Rotary phase converter">rotary phase converter</a>, essentially a three-phase motor with special starting arrangements and <a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">power factor</a> correction that produces balanced three-phase voltages. When properly designed, these rotary converters can allow satisfactory operation of a three-phase motor on a single-phase source. In such a device, the energy storage is performed by the <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> (flywheel effect) of the rotating components. An external flywheel is sometimes found on one or both ends of the shaft.</p> 
     <p>A three-phase generator can be driven by a single-phase motor. This motor-generator combination can provide a frequency changer function as well as phase conversion, but requires two machines with all their expense and losses. The motor-generator method can also form an <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptable_power_supply" title="Uninterruptable power supply" class="mw-redirect">uninterruptable power supply</a> when used in conjunction with a large flywheel and a battery-powered DC motor for really constant power, a standby generator set gives more frequency drop until standby generator kicks in.</p>

     <p>A second method that was popular in the 1940s and 1950s was the <i>transformer method</i>. At that time, capacitors were more expensive than transformers, so an <a href="/wiki/Autotransformer" title="Autotransformer">autotransformer</a> was used to apply more power through fewer capacitors. Separated it from another common method, the static converter, as both methods have no moving parts, which separates them from the rotary converters.</p> 
     <p>Another method often attempted is with a device referred to as a static phase converter. This method of running three-phase equipment is commonly attempted with motor loads though it only supplies 2/3 power and can cause the motor loads to run hot and in some cases overheat. This method does not work when sensitive circuitry is involved such as <a href="/wiki/Computer_numerical_control" title="Computer numerical control" class="mw-redirect">CNC</a> devices or in induction and rectifier-type loads.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Variable-frequency_drive" title="Variable-frequency drive">Variable-frequency drives</a> (also known as solid-state <a href="/wiki/Inverter_(electrical)" title="Inverter (electrical)" class="mw-redirect">inverters</a> and adjustable speed drives) are used to provide precise speed and torque control of three-phase motors. Some models can be powered by a single-phase supply. VFDs work by converting the supply voltage to DC and then converting the DC to a suitable three-phase source for the motor.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Digital_phase_converter" title="Digital phase converter" class="mw-redirect">Digital phase converters</a> are designed for fixed-frequency operation from a single-phase source. Similar to a variable-frequency drive, they use a microprocessor to control solid-state power switching components to maintain balanced three-phase voltages.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_three-phase">Alternatives to three-phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-phase_electric_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Alternatives to three-phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Split-phase_electric_power" title="Split-phase electric power">Split-phase electric power</a> is used when three-phase power is not available and allows double the normal utilization voltage to be supplied for high-power loads.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Two-phase_electric_power" title="Two-phase electric power">Two-phase electric power</a>, like three-phase, gives constant power transfer to a linear load. For loads that connect each phase to neutral, assuming the load is the same power draw, the two-wire system has a neutral current that is greater than neutral current in a three-phase system. Also motors are not entirely linear, which means that despite the theory, motors running on three-phase tend to run smoother than those on two-phase. The generators in the <a href="/wiki/Adams_Power_Plant" title="Adams Power Plant" class="mw-redirect">Adams Power Plant</a> at <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a> that were installed in 1895 were the largest generators in the world at the time and were two-phase machines. True two-phase power distribution is obsolete for "new work" applications, but still exists for "old work" applications, perhaps most particularly in Buffalo and Niagara Falls, NY, Toronto and Niagara Falls, Ontario, Philadelphia and Reading, PA, and Camden, NJ. "New work" three-phase installations may be supplied by old two-phase feeders, and "old work" two-phase installations may be supplied by new three-phase feeders using a <a href="/wiki/Scott-T_transformer" title="Scott-T transformer">Scott-T transformer</a>, invented by <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Scott_(engineer)" title="Charles F. Scott (engineer)">Charles F. Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brittain_.282007.29_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brittain_.282007.29-21">[21]</a></sup> Special-purpose systems may use a two-phase system for frequency control.</li> 
      <li><i>Monocyclic power</i> was a name for an asymmetrical modified two-phase power system used by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> around 1897, championed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Proteus_Steinmetz" title="Charles Proteus Steinmetz">Charles Proteus Steinmetz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elihu_Thomson" title="Elihu Thomson">Elihu Thomson</a>. This system was devised to avoid patent infringement. In this system, a generator was wound with a full-voltage single-phase winding intended for lighting loads and with a small fraction (usually 1/4 of the line voltage) winding that produced a voltage in quadrature with the main windings. The intention was to use this "power wire" additional winding to provide starting torque for induction motors, with the main winding providing power for lighting loads. After the expiration of the Westinghouse patents on symmetrical two-phase and three-phase power distribution systems, the monocyclic system fell out of use; it was difficult to analyze and did not last long enough for satisfactory energy metering to be developed.</li> 
      <li>High-phase-order systems for power transmission have been built and tested. Such transmission lines typically would use six phases or twelve phases. High-phase-order transmission lines allow transfer of slightly less than proportionately higher power through a given volume without the expense of a <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">high-voltage direct current</a> (HVDC) converter at each end of the line. However, they require correspondingly more pieces of equipment.</li> 
     </ul>
