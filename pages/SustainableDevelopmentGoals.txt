     <p>The <b>Sustainable Development Goals</b> (<b>SDGs</b>), officially known as Transforming our world: the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, are an intergovernmental set of aspiration Goals with 169 targets. The Goals are contained in paragraph 54 United Nations Resolution A/RES/70/1 of 25 September 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The Resolution is a broader intergovernmental agreement that, while acting as the Post 2015 Development Agenda (successor to the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>), builds on the Principles agreed upon under Resolution A/RES/66/288, popularly known as The Future We Want.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>On 19 July 2014, the <a href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly" title="UN General Assembly" class="mw-redirect">UN General Assembly</a>'s Open Working Group on Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) forwarded a proposal for the SDGs to the Assembly. The proposal contained 17 goals with 169 targets covering a broad range of sustainable development issues. These included ending <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a>, improving <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, making cities more sustainable, combating <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and protecting oceans and forests.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> On 5 December 2014, the UN General Assembly accepted the Secretary-General's Synthesis Report which stated that the agenda for the post-2015 SDG process would be based on the OWG proposals.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Intergovernmental Negotiations on the Post 2015 Development Agenda (IGN) began in January 2015 and ended in August 2015. Following the negotiations, a final document was adopted at the UN Sustainable Development Summit September 25–27, 2015 in New York, USA.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The title of the agenda is <i>Transforming our world: the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development</i><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The history of the SDGs can be traced to 1972 when governments met under the auspices of the United Nations Human and Environment Conference, to consider the rights of the human family to a healthy and productive environment.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It was not until 1983 that the United Nations agreed to create the World Commission on Environment and Development as an independent body of the UN. In 1992 the first UN conference on Environment and Development was held in Rio. It was here that the first agenda for Environment and Development was developed and adopted, also known as Agenda 21. Twenty years later, a resolution, known as The Future We Want<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> was reached by member states. Among the key themes agreed on were on poverty eradication, energy, water and sanitation, health, and human settlement. Paragraph 246 of the Future We Want outcome document forms the link between The Rio +20 agreement and the Millennium Development Goals: "We recognize that the development of goals could also be useful for pursuing focused and coherent action on sustainable development." The goals should address and incorporate in a balanced way all three dimensions of sustainable development (environment, economics, and society) and their interlinkages. The development of these goals should not divert focus or effort from the achievement of the Millennium Development Goals" Paragraph 249 states that, "the process needs to be coordinated and coherent with the processes to consider the post-2015 development agenda." Taken together, these two paragraphs paved the way to bring together the development agenda centered on the <i>Millennium Development Goals</i> (MDGs),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> which were officially established following the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Summit" title="Millennium Summit">Millennium Summit</a> of the United Nations in 2000, and the agreement under the Future We Want outcome document. The Rio+20 summit also agreed that the process of designing sustainable development goals, should be “action-oriented, concise and easy to communicate, limited in number, aspirational, global in nature and universally applicable to all countries while taking into account different national realities, capacities and levels of development and respecting national policies and priorities”.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The MDGs were supposed to be achieved by 2015. A further process was needed to agree and develop development goals from 2015-2030. Discussion on the post-2015 framework for international development began well in advance, with the United Nations System Task Team on Post 2015 Development Agenda<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> releasing the first report known as Realizing The Future We Want<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The Report was the first attempt to achieve the requirements under paragraph 246 and 249 of the Future We Want Outcome Document. It identified four dimensions as part of a global vision for sustainable development:Inclusive Social Development, Environmental Sustainability, Inclusive Economic Development, and Peace and Security. Other processes included the UN Secretary General's High Level Panel on Post 2015 Development Agenda,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> whose report<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> was submitted to the Secretary General in 2013.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_development_goals">Sustainable development goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sustainable development goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Post-2015_Development_Agenda" title="Post-2015 Development Agenda">Post-2015 Development Agenda</a>
     </div> 
     <p>On 25 September 2015, the 193 countries of the UN General Assembly adopted the 2030 Development Agenda titled <i>Transforming our world: the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development</i>. Following the adoption, UN agencies, under the umbrella of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Group" title="United Nations Development Group">United Nations Development Group</a>, decided to support a campaign by several independent entities, among them corporate institutions and International Organizations. The Campaign, known as Project Everyone,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> introduced the term <i>Global Goals</i> and is intended to help communicate the agreed Sustainable Development Goals to a wider constituency. However the decision to support what is an independent campaign, without the approval of the member states, has met resistance<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> from several sections of civil society and governments, who accuse<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> the UNDG of ignoring the most important communication aspect of the agreement: Sustainability. There are also concerns that Global Goals is a term used to refer to several other processes that are not related to the United Nations.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_agenda">Description and agenda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description and agenda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Official Agenda for Sustainable Development adopted on 25 September 2015 has 92 paragraphs, with the main paragraph (51) outlining the 17 Sustainable Development Goals and its associated 169 targets.This included the following goals:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ol> 
      <li><b>Poverty</b> - <a href="/wiki/End_poverty_in_all_its_forms_everywhere" title="End poverty in all its forms everywhere" class="mw-redirect">End poverty in all its forms everywhere</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Food</b> - <a href="/wiki/Hunger#Global_initiatives_to_end_hunger" title="Hunger">End hunger</a>, achieve <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> and improved nutrition and promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Health</b> - Ensure healthy lives and promote <a href="/wiki/Well-being" title="Well-being">well-being</a> for all at all ages<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Education</b> - Ensure <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_(education)" title="Inclusion (education)">inclusive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Educational_equity" title="Educational equity">equitable</a> quality education and promote <a href="/wiki/Lifelong_learning" title="Lifelong learning">lifelong learning</a> opportunities for all<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Women</b> - Achieve <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Empowerment" title="Empowerment">empower</a> all women and girls<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Water</b> - Ensure <a href="/wiki/Water_availability" title="Water availability" class="mw-disambig">availability</a> and sustainable management of water and <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> for all<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Energy</b> - Ensure access to affordable, reliable, sustainable and <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy" class="mw-redirect">clean energy</a> for all<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Economy</b> - Promote sustained, <a href="/wiki/Inclusion" title="Inclusion" class="mw-disambig">inclusive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable economic growth</a>, full and productive employment and <a href="/wiki/Decent_work" title="Decent work">decent work</a> for all<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Infrastructure</b> - Build <a href="/wiki/Resilience" title="Resilience" class="mw-disambig">resilient</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, promote inclusive and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_industries" title="Sustainable industries">sustainable industrialization</a> and foster <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Inequality</b> - Reduce <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">inequality</a> within and among countries<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Habitation</b> - Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Consumption</b> - Ensure sustainable consumption and production patterns<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Climate</b> - Take urgent action to combat <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and its impacts<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Marine-ecosystems</b> - <a href="/wiki/Ocean_conservation" title="Ocean conservation" class="mw-redirect">Conserve</a> and sustainably use the oceans, seas and <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine resources</a> for sustainable development<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Ecosystems</b> - Protect, restore and promote sustainable use of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, sustainably manage forests, combat <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, and halt and reverse <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a> and halt <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> loss<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Institutions</b> - Promote peaceful and inclusive societies for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, provide <a href="/wiki/Right_to_fair_trial" title="Right to fair trial" class="mw-redirect">access to justice</a> for all and build effective, accountable and inclusive institutions at all levels<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Sustainability</b> - Strengthen the means of implementation and revitalize the global partnership for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-2015_development_agenda_process">Post-2015 development agenda process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-2015 development agenda process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Since Rio+20 did not elaborate specific goals, a 30-member Open Working Group (OWG) was established on 22 January 2013 by the decision of the UN General Assembly. The OWG was tasked with preparing a proposal on the SDGs for consideration during the 68th session of the General Assembly, September 2013 – September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The OWG used a constituency-based system of representation, which meant that most of the seats in the working group are shared by several countries. After 13 sessions, the OWG submitted their proposal of 17 SDGs and 169 targets to the 68th session of the UN General Assembly in September, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Rio+20 outcome document stated that, “at the outset, the OWG will decide on its methods of work, including developing modalities to ensure the full involvement of relevant stakeholders and expertise from civil society, the scientific community and the United Nations system in its work, in order to provide a diversity of perspectives and experience”.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Future_We_Want_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Future_We_Want-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critique">Critique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A report by the International <a href="/wiki/Food_Policy_Research_Institute" title="Food Policy Research Institute" class="mw-redirect">Food Policy Research Institute</a> (IFPRI) of 2013 criticized the efforts of the SDGs as not ambitious enough. Instead of aiming for an end to poverty by 2030, the report "An Ambitious Development Goal: Ending Hunger and Undernutrition by 2025" by <a href="/wiki/Shenggen_Fan" title="Shenggen Fan">Shenggen Fan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Polman" title="Paul Polman">Paul Polman</a> calls for a greater emphasis on eliminating hunger and undernutrition and achieving that in 5 years less, by <a href="/wiki/2025" title="2025">2025</a>. It bases its claims on an analysis of the experiences from China, Vietnam, Brazil and Thailand and identifies 3 pathways to achieving this goal: agriculture-led, social protection– and nutrition intervention–led, or a combination of both of these approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-IFPRI2013_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFPRI2013-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Jason Hickel of the London School of Economics has criticised the SDGs for being contradictory, arguing that in seeking high levels of global <a href="/wiki/GDP_growth" title="GDP growth" class="mw-redirect">GDP growth</a>, they will undermine their own <a href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological" class="mw-redirect">ecological</a> objectives. He also notes, in relation to the headline goal of eliminating extreme poverty, that "a growing number of scholars are pointing out that $1.25 is actually not adequate for human <a href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence" class="mw-redirect">subsistence</a>," and the poverty line should be revised to as high as $5.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A commentary in The Economist argued that the 169 targets for the SDGs are too many, calling them "sprawling," "misconceived," and "a mess" compared to the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>. It also criticised the goals for ignoring local context and promoting "cookie-cutter development policies."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Achieving the SDGs requires economic growth that will provide the resources for achieving the range of goals that are considered. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hanushek" title="Eric Hanushek">Eric Hanushek</a> and Ludger Woessmann argue that the most important determinant of <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> is the “knowledge capital” of nations, which they define as the aggregate skills of the country’s population and which they measure by achievement scores on international mathematics and science examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> This argument indicates that the importance of the education goal should be elevated, because achieving it would provide the resources to reduce poverty, to improve health, and to provide for inclusive growth that lessens inequality within and between countries. In related analysis, they provide a measurable definition of quality: basic skills, or achieving Level 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Programme_for_International_Student_Assessment" title="Programme for International Student Assessment">Programme for International Student Assessment</a> (PISA).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> With this definition, they show the economic gains for each of the 76 countries with test data that can be achieved by reaching universal secondary schooling along with all students having basic skills. While access to school is important (consistent with the prior Millennium Development Goals), improving the quality of schools provides a much larger economic impact for both developing and developed countries.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water.2C_sanitation.2C_and_hygiene">Water, sanitation, and hygiene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Water, sanitation, and hygiene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a> experts have stated that without progress on Goal 6, the other goals and targets will not be able to be achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-batty_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-batty-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris_climate_deal">Paris climate deal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_Development_Goals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Paris climate deal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Nations and other parties negotiating at the UN have highlighted the links between the post-2015 SDG process, the Funding for Development process to be concluded in Addis Ababa in July and the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">COP 21</a> Climate Change conference in Paris in December.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In May 2015, a report concluded that only a very ambitious climate deal in Paris in 2015 will enable countries to reach the sustainable development goals and targets. <sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The report also states that tackling climate change will only be possible if the SDGs are met; and that development and climate are inextricably linked, particularly around poverty, gender equality, and energy.</p>
