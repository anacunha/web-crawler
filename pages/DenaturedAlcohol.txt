   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Denatured alcohol</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
      Jump to: 
     <a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 
     <a href="#p-search">search</a> 
    </div> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b>Denatured alcohol</b>, also called <b>methylated spirits</b> or <b>denatured rectified spirit</b>, is <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> that has additives to make it <a href="/wiki/Poisonous" title="Poisonous" class="mw-redirect">poisonous</a>, somewhat bad tasting, foul smelling or <a href="/wiki/Emetic" title="Emetic" class="mw-redirect">nauseating</a>, to discourage recreational consumption. In some cases it is also dyed.</p> 
     <p>Denatured alcohol is used as a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> and as fuel for alcohol burners and camping stoves. Because of the diversity of industrial uses for denatured alcohol, hundreds of additives and denaturing methods have been used. The main additive has traditionally been 10% <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, giving rise to the term "methylated spirits". Other typical additives include <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">isopropyl alcohol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_ethyl_ketone" title="Methyl ethyl ketone" class="mw-redirect">methyl ethyl ketone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isobutyl_ketone" title="Methyl isobutyl ketone">methyl isobutyl ketone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denatonium" title="Denatonium">denatonium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-denaturants_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-denaturants-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(food)" title="Denaturation (food)">Denaturing</a> alcohol does not <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">chemically</a> alter the ethanol molecule. Rather, the ethanol is <a href="/wiki/Adulterant" title="Adulterant">mixed with other chemicals</a> to form an undrinkable solution. For many of these solutions, there is no practical way to separate the components. Labels such as "cannot be made nonpoisonous" communicate to users that they should not trust any alcohol claiming to be rendered safe by distilling, filtering, or additives.</p> 
     <p></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denatured_alcohol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_and_Tobacco_Tax_and_Trade_Bureau" title="Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau">Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau</a> (TTB), formerly known as the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms</a> (ATF), regulates sales of alcoholic beverages. Alcoholic beverages (liquor, wine, beer, etc.) are heavily taxed. In order to avoid paying beverage taxes on alcohol that is not meant to be consumed, the alcohol must be denatured per specific formulations given by the TTB. There are approximately 50 formulations provided by the TTB, most of which are indeed toxic to humans, but not all.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Denaturat.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Denaturat.jpg/220px-Denaturat.jpg" width="220" height="512" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Denaturat.jpg/330px-Denaturat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Denaturat.jpg/440px-Denaturat.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1061" data-file-height="2470"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Denaturat.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Polish denatured alcohol
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Denatured alcohol is not, in itself, a preferred product — that is, it is not something that would be normally demanded if given the alternative of normal ethanol. Denatured alcohol and its manufacture are a public policy compromise. The supply and demand for denatured alcohol arises from the fact that normal alcohol (which in everyday language refers specifically to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, suitable for human ingestion as a recreational drink or extractive medium for <a href="/wiki/Tincture" title="Tincture">medicinal tinctures</a>) is usually very expensive in comparison with similar chemicals, being highly taxed for revenue and public health policy purposes (see <a href="/wiki/Pigovian_tax" title="Pigovian tax">Pigovian tax</a>). If pure ethanol were made cheaply available for fuel, solvents, or medicinal purposes, some people might ingest it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Denatured alcohol provides a solution to permit industrial use and manufacture of ethanol, whereby cheap ethanol can be made available for non-consumption use without the risk of its being converted for consumption. The process creates an ethanol-containing solution that is not suitable for drinking, but is otherwise similar to ethanol for most purposes. As a result, there is no <a href="/wiki/Duty_(economics)" title="Duty (economics)">duty</a> on denatured alcohol in most countries, making it considerably cheaper than pure <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. As a consequence, its composition is tightly defined by government regulations that vary between countries.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formulations">Formulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denatured_alcohol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Different additives are used to make it difficult to use <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> or other simple processes to reverse the denaturation. Methanol is commonly used both because its boiling point is close to that of ethanol and because it is toxic. Another typical denaturant is <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>. Often the denatured alcohol is dyed with <a href="/wiki/Methyl_violet" title="Methyl violet">methyl violet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are several grades of denatured alcohol, but in general the denaturants used are similar. As an example, the formulation for <i>completely denatured alcohol</i>, according to 2005 British regulations was as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>Completely denatured alcohol must be made in accordance with the following formulation: with every 90 parts by volume of alcohol mix 9.5 parts by volume of <a href="/wiki/Wood_naphtha" title="Wood naphtha" class="mw-redirect">wood naphtha</a> or a substitute and 0.5 parts by volume of crude <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>, and to the resulting mixture add mineral naphtha (<a href="/wiki/Petroleum_oil" title="Petroleum oil" class="mw-redirect">petroleum oil</a>) in the proportion of 3.75 litres to every 1000 litres of the mixture and synthetic organic dyestuff (<a href="/wiki/Methyl_violet" title="Methyl violet">methyl violet</a>) in the proportion of 1.5 grams to every 1000 litres of the mixture.</p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> agreed in February 2013 to the mutual procedures for the complete denaturing of alcohol:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       Per hectolitre (100 L) of absolute ethanol: 3 litres of 
       <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">isopropyl alcohol</a>, 3 litres of 
       <a href="/wiki/Butanone" title="Butanone">methyl ethyl ketone</a> and 1 gram 
       <a href="/wiki/Denatonium" title="Denatonium">denatonium benzoate</a>.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denatured_alcohol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Ethanol#Uses" title="Ethanol">Ethanol § Uses</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Denatured alcohol is used identically to ethanol itself except for applications that involve ingestion. Un-denatured ethanol is required for food and beverage applications and certain chemical reactions where the denaturant would interfere.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_and_toxicity">Consumption and toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denatured_alcohol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consumption and toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Harm_reduction#Alcohol" title="Harm reduction">Harm reduction § Alcohol</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Despite its poisonous content, denatured alcohol is sometimes consumed as a <a href="/wiki/Surrogate_alcohol" title="Surrogate alcohol">surrogate alcohol</a>, which can result in blindness or death if (as was typical) it contains <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. For instance, during <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">American prohibition</a>, Federal law required methanol in domestically manufactured industrial alcohols. On Christmas Day 1926 and the two following days, roughly at the midpoint of the "Great Experiment" of prohibition, 31 people in New York City died of methanol poisoning.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> To help prevent this, <a href="/wiki/Denatonium" title="Denatonium">denatonium</a> is often added to give the substance an extremely bitter flavor. Substances such as <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a> help to give the mixture an <a href="/wiki/Unpleasant_odor" title="Unpleasant odor" class="mw-redirect">unpleasant odor</a>, and emetic (vomiting) agents such as <a href="/wiki/Syrup_of_ipecac" title="Syrup of ipecac">syrup of ipecac</a> may also be included.</p> 
     <p>New Zealand has removed methanol from its government-approved "methylated spirits" formulation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Consuming methylated spirits in South Africa is referred to as 'riding the blue train' as the product has a blue color.</p>
