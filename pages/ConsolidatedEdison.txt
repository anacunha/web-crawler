     <p><b>Consolidated Edison, Inc.</b>, commonly known as <b>Con Edison</b> or <b>Con Ed</b>, is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Investor-owned_utility" title="Investor-owned utility">investor-owned</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_company" title="Energy company" class="mw-redirect">energy companies</a> in the United States, with approximately $13 billion in annual revenues as of 2010, and over $36 billion in assets. The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through its subsidiaries:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc.</b>, <b>(CECONY)</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Public_utilities_commission" title="Public utilities commission">regulated utility</a> providing electric, gas, and steam service in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westchester_County,_New_York" title="Westchester County, New York">Westchester County, New York</a>;</li> 
      <li><b>Orange and Rockland Utilities, Inc.</b>, a regulated utility serving customers in a 1,350-square-mile (3,500&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) area in southeastern <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a> and adjacent sections of northern <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>;</li> 
      <li><b>Con Edison Solutions</b>, a retail energy supply and services company;</li> 
      <li><b>Con Edison Energy</b>, a wholesale energy supply company; and</li> 
      <li><b>Con Edison Development</b>, a company that owns and operates generating plants and participates in other infrastructure projects.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In 2014, electric revenues accounted for 70.54% of consolidated sales (70.84 in 2013); gas revenues 14.96% (14.74%); steam revenues 4.86% (5.52%); and non-utility revenues 9.62% (8.85%).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Though the company provides an indispensable service to New York residents, a number of major incidents and service problems have negatively impacted its reputation with the public.</p>

     <p>In 1823, Con Edison’s earliest corporate predecessor, the New York Gas Light Company, was founded by a consortium of New York City investors. A year later, it was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>. In 1884, six gas companies combined into the Consolidated Gas Company.</p> 
     <p>The New York Steam Company began providing service in lower <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> in 1882. Today, Con Edison operates the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">largest commercial steam system</a> in the world, providing steam service to nearly 1,600 commercial and residential establishments in Manhattan from <a href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> to 96th Street.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Con Edison’s electric business also dates back to 1882, when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Edison_Illuminating_Company" title="Edison Illuminating Company">Edison Illuminating Company</a> of New York began supplying electricity to 59 customers in a square-mile area in lower Manhattan. After the “<a href="/wiki/War_of_Currents" title="War of Currents">War of Currents</a>”, there were more than 30 companies generating and distributing electricity in New York City and Westchester County. But by 1920 there were far fewer, and the New York Edison Company (then part of Consolidated Gas) was clearly the leader.</p> 
     <p>In 1936, with electric sales far outstripping gas sales, the company incorporated and the name was changed to Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. The years that followed brought further amalgamations as Consolidated Edison acquired or merged with more than a dozen companies between 1936 and 1960. Con Edison today is the result of <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">acquisitions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(law)" title="Dissolution (law)">dissolutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merger" title="Merger" class="mw-redirect">mergers</a> of more than 170 individual <a href="/wiki/Electric" title="Electric" class="mw-redirect">electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> companies.</p> 
     <p>On January 1, 1998, following the <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> of the utility industry in New York state, a holding company, Consolidated Edison, Inc., was formed. It is one of the nation’s largest investor-owned energy companies, with approximately $14 billion in annual revenues and $33 billion in assets. The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through two regulated utility subsidiaries and three competitive energy businesses. Under a number of corporate names, the company has been traded on the NYSE without interruption since 1824—longer than any other NYSE stock. Its largest subsidiary, Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. provides electric, gas and steam service to more than 3 million customers in New York City and Westchester County, New York, an area of 660 square miles (1,700&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>) with a population of nearly 9 million.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical">Electrical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electrical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Con Edison electrical transmission system utilizes voltages of 138 kilovolts (kV), 345 kV, and 500 kV. The company has two 345 kV interconnections with upstate New York that enable it to import power from <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">Hydro-Québec</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and one 345 kV interconnection each with <a href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" title="Public Service Electric and Gas" class="mw-redirect">Public Service Electric and Gas</a> in New Jersey and <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Power_Authority" title="Long Island Power Authority">LIPA</a> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>. Con Edison is also interconnected with <a href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" title="Public Service Electric and Gas" class="mw-redirect">Public Service Electric and Gas</a> via the Branchburg-Ramapo 500 kV line. Con Ed's distribution voltages are 33 kV, 27 kV, 13 kV, and 4 kV.</p> 
     <p>The 93,000 miles (150,000&nbsp;km) of underground cable in the Con Edison system could wrap around the Earth 3.6 times. Nearly 36,000 miles (58,000&nbsp;km) of overhead electric wires complement the underground system—enough cable to stretch between New York and Los Angeles 13 times.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas">Gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Con Edison gas system has nearly 7,200 miles (11,600&nbsp;km) of pipes—if laid end to end, long enough to reach Paris and back to New York City, and serves Westchester County, the Bronx, Manhattan and parts of Queens. Gas service in the other boroughs is provided by <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_USA" title="National Grid USA" class="mw-redirect">National Grid USA</a>. The average volume of gas that travels through Con Edison’s gas system annually could fill the Empire State Building nearly 6,100 times.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam">Steam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Steam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">New York City steam system</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Con Edison produces 30 billion pounds of steam each year through its seven power plants which boil water to 1,000&nbsp;°F (538&nbsp;°C) before pumping it to hundreds of buildings in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">New York City steam system</a>, which is the biggest <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district steam system</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Steam traveling through the system is used to heat and cool some of New York’s most famous addresses, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Building" title="United Nations Building" class="mw-redirect">United Nations complex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

     <p>The <b>Consolidated Edison Company Building</b>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_J._Hardenbergh" title="Henry J. Hardenbergh" class="mw-redirect">Henry J. Hardenbergh</a>, was built between 1910 and 1914. The building, at 4 <a href="/wiki/Irving_Place" title="Irving Place" class="mw-redirect">Irving Place</a>, takes up the entire block between East <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th</a> and <a href="/wiki/15th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="15th Street (Manhattan)" class="mw-redirect">15th</a> Streets and Irving Place and <a href="/wiki/Third_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Third Avenue (Manhattan)" class="mw-redirect">Third Avenue</a>, and was originally built for the Consolidated Gas Company, although its predecessor companies, such as the Manhattan Gas Light Company, were located at the same address as early as 1854. The new building's location had been the site of the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Music_(New_York_City)" title="Academy of Music (New York City)">Academy of Music</a>, New York's third <a href="/wiki/Opera_house" title="Opera house">opera house</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-tourflat_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tourflat-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as well as the original <a href="/wiki/Tammany_Hall" title="Tammany Hall">Tammany Hall</a> building.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Warren_%26_Wetmore" title="Warren &amp; Wetmore" class="mw-redirect">Warren &amp; Wetmore</a>'s 26-story tower—topped by a "Tower of Light" designed to look like a miniature temple and capped by a bronze lantern which lights up at night<sup id="cite_ref-tourflat_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tourflat-7">[7]</a></sup>—was added between 1926 and 1929.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_accidents_and_incidents">Major accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Major accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>1989:</b> A steam pipe explosion in <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park</a> killed three, injured 24, and required the evacuation of a damaged apartment building due to high levels of asbestos in the air. Workers had failed to drain water from the pipe before turning the steam on. The utility also eventually pleaded guilty to lying about the absence of asbestos contamination, and paid a $2 million fine.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1989-08-24_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1989-08-24-11">[11]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2004:</b> In Manhattan, <a href="/wiki/Stray_voltage" title="Stray voltage">stray voltage</a> killed a woman walking her dog in the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> when she stepped on an electrified metal plate.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_2006-03-04_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_2006-03-04-12">[12]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2006:</b> After the <a href="/wiki/2006_Queens_blackout" title="2006 Queens blackout">blackout in Queens</a>, the company was criticized by public officials for a poor record in the restoration of service to its customers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2007:</b> On July 18, <a href="/wiki/2007_New_York_City_steam_explosion" title="2007 New York City steam explosion">an explosion occurred in midtown Manhattan</a> near <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a> when an 83-year-old Con Edison steam pipe failed, resulting in one death, over 40 injuries, as well as subway and surface disruptions.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2007:</b> The day before Thanksgiving, an explosion critically burned Queens resident Kunta Oza when an 80-year-old cast iron gas main ruptured. Oza died on Thanksgiving Day, and her family later settled with Con Edison for $3.75 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2009:</b> Another <a href="/wiki/Gas_explosion" title="Gas explosion">gas explosion</a> claimed a life in Queens while Con Edison personnel were on the scene. There was a leak in a manhole and a fault in an electrical feeder at the same time. The fault in the feeder caused the explosion due to the sparks being generated. When the mechanic opened the manhole more oxygen entered and the explosion took place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Due to that event Con Edison has changed its procedure on outside gas leak calls.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>2012:</b> 
       <ul> 
        <li>On October 29, flooding from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> caused a transformer explosion at a Con-Ed plant on New York City's East Side.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CBSLocal_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBSLocal-18">[18]</a></sup></li> 
        <li>During the storm, Con Edison used social media to get outage and restoration information out to customers. The company’s Twitter account gained an extra 16,000 followers during the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li> 
        <li>Con Edison's subsidiary, Orange &amp; Rockland Utilities, was criticized for its response to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>. Some customers experienced a loss of electrical power for 11 days.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li> 
       </ul> </li> 
      <li><b>2014:</b> On March 12, <a href="/wiki/2014_East_Harlem_gas_explosion" title="2014 East Harlem gas explosion">two apartment buildings exploded</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> after a reported Con Edison gas leak. Eight people were killed in the massive explosion that reduced the conjoining buildings to rubble.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_criticism">Honors and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Honors and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In March 2002, <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine named the company as one of "America's Most Admired Companies" in the publication's newest corporate ranking survey. In 2003, Con Edison ranked second on the top ten list for electric and gas utilities.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In December 2011, the non-partisan organization <a href="/wiki/Public_Campaign#Reports" title="Public Campaign">Public Campaign</a> released a report criticizing ConEd for spending $1.8 million on <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">not paying any taxes</a> during 2008–2010, instead getting $127 million in tax rebates, despite making a profit of $4.2 billion, and increasing executive pay by 82% to $17.4 million in 2010 for its top five executives.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2014, Con Edison was named one of the 50 best companies for Latinas by <i>Latina Style Magazine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In its "Best of the Best" issue in 2015, <i>Hispanic Network Magazine</i> named the company a top employer among energy, gas, and oil companies.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Con Edison was also selected as a top regional utility by <i>DiversityInc</i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
