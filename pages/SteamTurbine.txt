   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Steam turbine</h1>

     <p>A <b>steam turbine</b> is a device that extracts <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> from pressurized <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> and uses it to do <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">mechanical work</a> on a rotating output shaft. Its modern manifestation was invented by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Algernon_Parsons" title="Charles Algernon Parsons">Sir Charles Parsons</a> in 1884.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> generates <a href="/wiki/Rotational_motion" title="Rotational motion" class="mw-redirect">rotary motion</a>, it is particularly suited to be used to drive an <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electrical generator</a>&nbsp;– about 90% of all electricity generation in the United States (1996) is by use of steam turbines.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiser_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiser-3">[3]</a></sup> The steam turbine is a form of <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> that derives much of its improvement in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_efficiency" title="Thermodynamic efficiency" class="mw-redirect">thermodynamic efficiency</a> from the use of multiple stages in the expansion of the steam, which results in a closer approach to the ideal reversible expansion process.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg/220px-TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg" width="220" height="79" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg/330px-TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg/440px-TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6048" data-file-height="2168"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:TMW_773_-_Steam_turbine_generator_set.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A 250&nbsp;kW industrial steam turbine from 1910 (right) directly linked to a 
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">generator</a> (left).
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The first device that may be classified as a reaction steam turbine was little more than a toy, the classic <a href="/wiki/Aeolipile" title="Aeolipile">Aeolipile</a>, described in the 1st century by <a href="/wiki/Greek_mathematics" title="Greek mathematics">Greek</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt" class="mw-redirect">Roman Egypt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_look_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_look-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-O.27Connor.2C_Heron_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connor.2C_Heron-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1551, <a href="/wiki/Taqi_al-Din_Muhammad_ibn_Ma%27ruf" title="Taqi al-Din Muhammad ibn Ma'ruf" class="mw-redirect">Taqi al-Din</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Egypt" title="Ottoman Egypt">Ottoman Egypt</a> described a steam turbine with the practical application of rotating a <a href="/wiki/Spit_(cooking_aide)" title="Spit (cooking aide)" class="mw-redirect">spit</a>. Steam turbines were also described by the Italian <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Branca" title="Giovanni Branca">Giovanni Branca</a> (1629)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkins" title="John Wilkins">John Wilkins</a> in England (1648).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The devices described by Taqi al-Din and Wilkins are today known as <a href="/wiki/Steam_jack" title="Steam jack" class="mw-redirect">steam jacks</a>. In 1672 an impulse steam turbine driven car was designed by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Verbiest" title="Ferdinand Verbiest">Ferdinand Verbiest</a>. A more modern version of this car was produced some time in the late 18th century by an unknown German mechanic.</p> 
     <p>The modern steam turbine was invented in 1884 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Algernon_Parsons" title="Charles Algernon Parsons">Sir Charles Parsons</a>, whose first model was connected to a <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> that generated 7.5&nbsp;kW (10&nbsp;hp) of electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-birrcastle.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birrcastle.com-9">[9]</a></sup> The invention of Parsons' steam turbine made cheap and plentiful electricity possible and revolutionized marine transport and naval warfare.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Parsons' design was a <a href="#Reaction_turbines">reaction</a> type. His patent was licensed and the turbine scaled-up shortly after by an American, <a href="/wiki/George_Westinghouse" title="George Westinghouse">George Westinghouse</a>. The Parsons turbine also turned out to be easy to scale up. Parsons had the satisfaction of seeing his invention adopted for all major world power stations, and the size of generators had increased from his first 7.5&nbsp;kW set up to units of 50,000&nbsp;kW capacity. Within Parson's lifetime, the generating capacity of a unit was scaled up by about 10,000 times,<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons.2C_Steam_Turbine_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons.2C_Steam_Turbine-11">[11]</a></sup> and the total output from turbo-generators constructed by his firm <a href="/wiki/C._A._Parsons_and_Company" title="C. A. Parsons and Company">C. A. Parsons and Company</a> and by their licensees, for land purposes alone, had exceeded thirty million horse-power.<sup id="cite_ref-birrcastle.com_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birrcastle.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A number of other variations of turbines have been developed that work effectively with steam. The <i>de Laval turbine</i> (invented by <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_de_Laval" title="Gustaf de Laval">Gustaf de Laval</a>) accelerated the steam to full speed before running it against a turbine blade. De Laval's <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine#Impulse_turbines" title="Steam turbine">impulse turbine</a> is simpler, less expensive and does not need to be pressure-proof. It can operate with any pressure of steam, but is considerably less efficient.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Rateau" class="extiw" title="fr:Auguste Rateau">fr:Auguste Rateau</a> developed a pressure compounded impulse turbine using the de Laval principle as early as 1896,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> obtained a US patent in 1903, and applied the turbine to a French torpedo boat in 1904. He taught at the École des mines de Saint-Étienne for a decade until 1897, and later founded a successful company that was incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a> firm after his death. One of the founders of the modern theory of steam and gas turbines was <a href="/wiki/Aurel_Stodola" title="Aurel Stodola">Aurel Stodola</a>, a Slovak physicist and engineer and professor at the Swiss Polytechnical Institute (now <a href="/wiki/ETH" title="ETH" class="mw-redirect">ETH</a>) in Zurich. His work <i>Die Dampfturbinen und ihre Aussichten als Wärmekraftmaschinen</i> (English: The Steam Turbine and its prospective use as a Mechanical Engine) was published in Berlin in 1903. A further book <i>Dampf und Gas-Turbinen</i> (English: Steam and Gas Turbines) was published in 1922.</p> 
     <p>The <i>Brown-Curtis turbine</i>, an <a href="#Impulse_turbines">impulse</a> type, which had been originally developed and patented by the U.S. company International Curtis Marine Turbine Company, was developed in the 1900s in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_%26_Company" title="John Brown &amp; Company">John Brown &amp; Company</a>. It was used in John Brown-engined merchant ships and warships, including liners and Royal Navy warships.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The manufacturing industry for steam turbines is dominated by Chinese power equipment makers. <a href="/wiki/Harbin_Electric" title="Harbin Electric">Harbin Electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Electric" title="Shanghai Electric">Shanghai Electric</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dongfang_Electric" title="Dongfang Electric">Dongfang Electric</a>, the top three power equipment makers in China collectively hold a majority stake in the worldwide market share for steam turbines in 2009-10 according to <a href="/wiki/Platts" title="Platts">Platts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-socgen_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socgen-13">[13]</a></sup> Other manufacturers with minor market share include <a href="/wiki/Bharat_Heavy_Electricals_Limited" title="Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited" class="mw-redirect">Bhel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>, <a href="/wiki/GE" title="GE" class="mw-redirect">GE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-socgen_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socgen-13">[13]</a></sup> The consulting firm Frost &amp; Sullivan projects that manufacturing of steam turbines will become more consolidated by 2020 as Chinese power manufacturers win increasing business outside of China.<sup id="cite_ref-pei_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pei-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Steam turbines are made in a variety of sizes ranging from small &lt;0.75&nbsp;kW (&lt;1&nbsp;hp) units (rare) used as mechanical drives for pumps, compressors and other shaft driven equipment, to 1&nbsp;500&nbsp;000&nbsp;kW (1.5&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt" class="mw-redirect">GW</a>; 2&nbsp;000&nbsp;000&nbsp;hp) turbines used to generate electricity. There are several classifications for modern steam turbines.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blade_and_stage_design">Blade and stage design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blade and stage design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg/250px-Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg.png" width="250" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg/375px-Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg/500px-Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="216"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Turbines_impulse_v_reaction.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Schematic diagram outlining the difference between an impulse and a 50% reaction turbine
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Turbine blades are of two basic types, blades and <a href="/wiki/Nozzles" title="Nozzles" class="mw-redirect">nozzles</a>. Blades move entirely due to the impact of steam on them and their profiles do not converge. This results in a steam velocity drop and essentially no pressure drop as steam moves through the blades. A turbine composed of blades alternating with fixed nozzles is called an <a href="#Impulse_turbines">impulse turbine</a>, <span id="Curtis_turbine">Curtis turbine</span>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure_compounding_in_turbines" title="Pressure compounding in turbines">Rateau turbine</a>, or <a href="#History">Brown-Curtis turbine</a>. Nozzles appear similar to blades, but their profiles converge near the exit. This results in a steam pressure drop and velocity increase as steam moves through the nozzles. Nozzles move due to both the impact of steam on them and the reaction due to the high-velocity steam at the exit. A turbine composed of moving nozzles alternating with fixed nozzles is called a <a href="#Reaction_turbines">reaction turbine</a> or <a href="#History">Parsons turbine</a>.</p> 
     <p>Except for low-power applications, turbine blades are arranged in multiple stages in series, called <a href="/wiki/Compounding_of_steam_turbines" title="Compounding of steam turbines">compounding</a>, which greatly improves <a href="#Turbine_efficiency">efficiency</a> at low speeds.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A reaction stage is a row of fixed nozzles followed by a row of moving nozzles. Multiple reaction stages divide the pressure drop between the steam inlet and exhaust into numerous small drops, resulting in a <b>pressure-compounded</b> turbine. Impulse stages may be either pressure-compounded, velocity-compounded, or pressure-velocity compounded. A pressure-compounded impulse stage is a row of fixed nozzles followed by a row of moving blades, with multiple stages for compounding. This is also known as a Rateau turbine, after its inventor. A <b>velocity-compounded</b> impulse stage (invented by Curtis and also called a "Curtis wheel") is a row of fixed nozzles followed by two or more rows of moving blades alternating with rows of fixed blades. This divides the velocity drop across the stage into several smaller drops.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A series of velocity-compounded impulse stages is called a <b>pressure-velocity compounded</b> turbine.</p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>By 1905, when steam turbines were coming into use on fast ships (such as <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dreadnought_(1906)" title="HMS Dreadnought (1906)">HMS&nbsp;<i>Dreadnought</i></a>) and in land-based power applications, it had been determined that it was desirable to use one or more Curtis wheels at the beginning of a multi-stage turbine (where the steam pressure is highest), followed by reaction stages. This was more efficient with high-pressure steam due to reduced leakage between the turbine rotor and the casing.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This is illustrated in the drawing of the German 1905 <a href="/wiki/AEG" title="AEG">AEG</a> marine steam turbine. The steam from the <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a> enters from the right at high pressure through a <a href="/wiki/Throttle" title="Throttle">throttle</a>, controlled manually by an operator (in this case a <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">sailor</a> known as the throttleman). It passes through five Curtis wheels and numerous reaction stages (the small blades at the edges of the two large rotors in the middle) before exiting at low pressure, almost certainly to a <a href="/wiki/Surface_condenser" title="Surface condenser">condenser</a>. The condenser provides a vacuum that maximizes the energy extracted from the steam, and condenses the steam into <a href="/wiki/Boiler_feedwater" title="Boiler feedwater">feedwater</a> to be returned to the boilers. On the left are several additional reaction stages (on two large rotors) that rotate the turbine in reverse for astern operation, with steam admitted by a separate throttle. Since ships are rarely operated in reverse, efficiency is not a priority in astern turbines, so only a few stages are used to save cost.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blade_Design_Challenges">Blade Design Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Blade Design Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A major challenge facing turbine design is reducing the <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a> experienced by the blades. Because of the high temperatures and high stresses of operation, steam turbine materials become damaged through these mechanisms. As temperatures are increased in an effort to improve turbine efficiency, creep becomes more significant. To limit creep, thermal coatings and <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> with solid-solution strengthening and <a href="/wiki/Grain_boundary_strengthening" title="Grain boundary strengthening">grain boundary strengthening</a> are used in blade designs.</p> 
     <p>Protective coatings are used to reduce the thermal damage and to limit <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a>. These coatings are often stabilized <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_dioxide" title="Zirconium dioxide">zirconium dioxide</a>-based ceramics. Using a thermal protective coating limits the temperature exposure of the nickel superalloy. This reduces the creep mechanisms experienced in the blade. Oxidation coatings limit efficiency losses caused by a buildup on the outside of the blades, which is especially important in the high-temperature environment.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The nickel-based blades are alloyed with aluminum and titanium to improve strength and creep resistance. The <a href="/wiki/Microstructure" title="Microstructure">microstructure</a> of these alloys is composed of different regions of composition. A uniform dispersion of the gamma-prime phase – a combination of nickel, aluminum, and titanium – promotes the strength and creep resistance of the blade due to the microstructure.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">Refractory</a> elements such as <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> can be added to the alloy to improve creep strength. The addition of these elements reduces the diffusion of the gamma prime phase, thus preserving the <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> resistance, strength, and creep resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_supply_and_exhaust_conditions">Steam supply and exhaust conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Steam supply and exhaust conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:BalNPP_m_st2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/BalNPP_m_st2.jpg/220px-BalNPP_m_st2.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/BalNPP_m_st2.jpg/330px-BalNPP_m_st2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/BalNPP_m_st2.jpg/440px-BalNPP_m_st2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3543" data-file-height="2480"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:BalNPP_m_st2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A low-pressure steam turbine in a nuclear power plant. These turbines exhaust steam at a pressure below atmospheric.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>These types include condensing, non-condensing, reheat, extraction and induction.</p> 
     <p>Condensing turbines are most commonly found in electrical power plants. These turbines receive steam from a <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> and exhaust it to a <a href="/wiki/Surface_condenser" title="Surface condenser">condenser</a>. The exhausted steam is at a pressure well below atmospheric, and is in a partially condensed state, typically of a <a href="/wiki/Steam_quality" title="Steam quality" class="mw-redirect">quality</a> near 90%.</p> 
     <p>Non-condensing or back pressure turbines are most widely used for process steam applications. The exhaust pressure is controlled by a regulating valve to suit the needs of the process steam pressure. These are commonly found at refineries, district heating units, pulp and paper plants, and <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalination</a> facilities where large amounts of low pressure process steam are needed.</p> 
     <p>Reheat turbines are also used almost exclusively in electrical power plants. In a reheat turbine, steam flow exits from a high pressure section of the turbine and is returned to the boiler where additional superheat is added. The steam then goes back into an intermediate pressure section of the turbine and continues its expansion. Using reheat in a cycle increases the work output from the turbine and also the expansion reaches conclusion before the steam condenses, thereby minimizing the erosion of the blades in last rows. In most of the cases, maximum number of reheats employed in a cycle is 2 as the cost of super-heating the steam negates the increase in the work output from turbine.</p> 
     <p>Extracting type turbines are common in all applications. In an extracting type turbine, steam is released from various stages of the turbine, and used for industrial process needs or sent to boiler <a href="/wiki/Feedwater_heater" title="Feedwater heater">feedwater heaters</a> to improve overall cycle efficiency. Extraction flows may be controlled with a valve, or left uncontrolled.</p> 
     <p>Induction turbines introduce low pressure steam at an intermediate stage to produce additional power.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casing_or_shaft_arrangements">Casing or shaft arrangements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Casing or shaft arrangements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>These arrangements include single casing, tandem compound and cross compound turbines. Single casing units are the most basic style where a single casing and shaft are coupled to a generator. Tandem compound are used where two or more casings are directly coupled together to drive a single generator. A cross compound turbine arrangement features two or more shafts not in line driving two or more generators that often operate at different speeds. A cross compound turbine is typically used for many large applications. A typical 1930s-1960s naval installation is illustrated below; this shows high- and low-pressure turbines driving a common reduction gear, with a geared cruising turbine on one high-pressure turbine.</p>

     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg/220px-Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg/330px-Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg/440px-Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Turbine_Philippsburg-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

     <p>The moving steam imparts both a tangential and axial thrust on the turbine shaft, but the axial thrust in a simple turbine is unopposed. To maintain the correct rotor position and balancing, this force must be counteracted by an opposing force. <a href="/wiki/Thrust_bearings" title="Thrust bearings" class="mw-redirect">Thrust bearings</a> can be used for the shaft bearings, the rotor can use dummy pistons, it can be <b>double flow</b>- the steam enters in the middle of the shaft and exits at both ends, or a combination of any of these. In a <b>double flow</b> rotor, the blades in each half face opposite ways, so that the axial forces negate each other but the tangential forces act together. This design of rotor is also called <b>two-flow</b>, <b>double-axial-flow</b>, or <b>double-exhaust</b>. This arrangement is common in low-pressure casings of a compound turbine.<sup id="cite_ref-phdengineer_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phdengineer-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle_of_operation_and_design">Principle of operation and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Principle of operation and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Turbine1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Steam_Turbine1.jpg/220px-Steam_Turbine1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Steam_Turbine1.jpg/330px-Steam_Turbine1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Steam_Turbine1.jpg/440px-Steam_Turbine1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Turbine1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A simple turbine schematic of the Parsons type: rotating and fixed stators alternate and steam pressure drops by a fraction of the total across each pair. The stators grow larger as pressure drops.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>An ideal steam turbine is considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropic process</a>, or constant entropy process, in which the entropy of the steam entering the turbine is equal to the entropy of the steam leaving the turbine. No steam turbine is truly isentropic, however, with typical isentropic efficiencies ranging from 20–90% based on the application of the turbine. The interior of a turbine comprises several sets of blades or <i>buckets</i>. One set of stationary blades is connected to the casing and one set of rotating blades is connected to the shaft. The sets intermesh with certain minimum clearances, with the size and configuration of sets varying to efficiently exploit the expansion of steam at each stage.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turbine_efficiency">Turbine efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Turbine efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>To maximize turbine efficiency the steam is expanded, doing work, in a number of stages. These stages are characterized by how the energy is extracted from them and are known as either impulse or reaction turbines. Most steam turbines use a mixture of the reaction and impulse designs: each stage behaves as either one or the other, but the overall turbine uses both. Typically, higher pressure sections are reaction type and lower pressure stages are impulse type.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impulse_turbines">Impulse turbines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impulse turbines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:TurbineBlades.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/TurbineBlades.jpg/220px-TurbineBlades.jpg" width="220" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/TurbineBlades.jpg/330px-TurbineBlades.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/TurbineBlades.jpg/440px-TurbineBlades.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="271"></a>

     <p>An impulse turbine has fixed nozzles that orient the steam flow into high speed jets. These jets contain significant kinetic energy, which is converted into shaft rotation by the bucket-like shaped rotor blades, as the steam jet changes direction. A pressure drop occurs across only the stationary blades, with a net increase in steam velocity across the stage. As the steam flows through the nozzle its pressure falls from inlet pressure to the exit pressure (atmospheric pressure, or more usually, the condenser vacuum). Due to this high ratio of expansion of steam, the steam leaves the nozzle with a very high velocity. The steam leaving the moving blades has a large portion of the maximum velocity of the steam when leaving the nozzle. The loss of energy due to this higher exit velocity is commonly called the carry over velocity or leaving loss.</p> 
     <p>The law of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">moment of momentum</a> states that the sum of the moments of external forces acting on a fluid which is temporarily occupying the <a href="/wiki/Control_volume" title="Control volume">control volume</a> is equal to the net time change of angular momentum flux through the control volume.</p>

       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/9/3/d935739c38e96e79b11289689e716747.png"> are the velocities of the blade at the inlet and outlet respectively.
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"> is the guide vane angle and 
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\beta " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/7/1/071997f13634882f823041b057f90923.png"> is the blade angle.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Then by the law of moment of momentum, the torque on the fluid is given by:</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="
T = \dot{m} ( r_2 V_{w2} - r_1 V_{w1} )
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/2/e/b2e59099ffb75885a4e49441e4d46f13.png"></p> 
     <p>For an impulse steam turbine: <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" r_2 = r_1 = r" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/3/5433da5a916e481e7f966083c2b66814.png">. Therefore, the tangential force on the blades is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" F_u = \dot{m}(V_{w1}-V_{w2}) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/5/70517d47281233a4e7c6766535b159a8.png">. The work done per unit time or power developed: <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{W} = {T*\omega}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/2/702b7efc7f4b087e26e5fb82b7fbb5fb.png">.</p> 
     <p>When ω is the angular velocity of the turbine, then the blade speed is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{U} = {\omega*r}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/1/f/c1f3182c3384e1cc4ac84cc57b671cbd.png">. The power developed is then <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W = \dot{m}U({\Delta }V_w)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/a/8/1a8e909659ed37ea08628b2f7587fee0.png">.</p> 
     <p><b>Blade efficiency</b></p> 
     <p>Blade efficiency (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_b}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/5/885193920ec407fd001c9fe5e2bdc508.png">) can be defined as the ratio of the work done on the blades to kinetic energy supplied to the fluid, and is given by</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_b} = \frac{Work~Done}{Kinetic~Energy~Supplied} = \frac{2UV_w}{V_1^2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/0/5/e0599223cfad24a1036a496bde11edd7.png"></p>

     <p>A stage of an impulse turbine consists of a nozzle set and a moving wheel. The stage efficiency defines a relationship between enthalpy drop in the nozzle and work done in the stage.</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_{stage}} = \frac{Work~done~on~blade}{Energy~supplied~per~stage} = \frac{U\Delta V_w}{\Delta h}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/d/e/2dea2c68509eb61901274ed402ac30d0.png"></p> 
     <p>Where <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h} = h_2-h_1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/f/3/9f3f45636e8c41249b89e89bf3ab919d.png"> is the specific enthalpy drop of steam in the nozzle.</p> 
     <p>By the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a>: <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{h_1} + \frac{V_1^2}{2} = {h_2} + \frac{V_2^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/0/2/c024bfdf1ad3daf4c630b10fa4c9a433.png"></p> 
     <p>Assuming that <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/5/d/15df402d99d5ae494c0eec6b149b3dd9.png"> is appreciably less than <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/9/2/4925ee5356b4ace0e28f0324cf3a81de.png">, we get <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/9/4b9c04e1c7021f8b1e3e8940f83b139f.png"> ≈ <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{V_2^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/a/b/dab35fd813bc182c704012a93f0b802d.png"> Furthermore, stage efficiency is the <a href="/wiki/Product_(mathematics)" title="Product (mathematics)">product</a> of blade efficiency and nozzle efficiency, or <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_{stage}} = {\eta_b}*{\eta_N}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/3/f/a3f50d5a6a612f6945ad9da6e3ab8b7b.png"></p> 
     <p>Nozzle efficiency is given by <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_N}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/6/7/b67f6f7378a866529d597994abfd872c.png"> = <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{V_2^2}{2(h_1-h_2)}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/a/beac70102a4e80905f2eee8aff9cf4a3.png">, where the enthalpy (in J/Kg) of steam at the entrance of the nozzle is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" h_1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/2/f/e2f66b6f97eb982536588a1bfa05ffbf.png"> and the enthalpy of steam at the exit of the nozzle is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" h_2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/d/3/bd3e5f3199896dfe9791ade3db413154.png">. <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta V_w} = V_{w1}-(-V_{w2})" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/9/d/c9d698b06d4f3db46ab0a227888c22ce.png"> <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta V_w} = V_{w1}+V_{w2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/e/e/9eeb5f0b6d323dc07c7a74466f1dd41e.png"> <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta V_w} = {V_{r1}\cos \beta_1+V_{r2}\cos \beta_2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/5/235101f4a78840ffcbaadc07ce628eb5.png"> <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta V_w} = {V_{r1}\cos \beta_1}(1+\frac{V_{r2}\cos \beta_2}{V_{r1}\cos \beta_1})" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/c/6/ac6021512aa57fb1160a155e76c80711.png"></p> 
     <p>The ratio of the cosines of the blade angles at the outlet and inlet can be taken and denoted <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{c} = \frac{\cos \beta_2}{\cos \beta_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/c/1/1c1c9789d9a16c6502f68194336353a7.png">. The ratio of steam velocities relative to the rotor speed at the outlet to the inlet of the blade is defined by the friction coefficient <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{k} = \frac{V_{r2}}{V_{r1}}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/e/a/9eaf028c7ff1666c36d21ef4c0b80b7d.png">.</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="k < 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/b/6/db63bac74bb8f32316c68c6aae23eaf7.png"> and depicts the loss in the relative velocity due to friction as the steam flows around the blades (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="k = 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/e/e/ceef78b61bf01306cc7e80344c92c19d.png"> for smooth blades).</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_b} = \frac{2 U \Delta V_w}{V_1^2} = \frac{2 U(\cos \alpha_1-U/V_1)(1+kc)}{V_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/e/0/5e0cc580d22641dba6177d961ddcc7b2.png"></p> 
     <p>The ratio of the blade speed to the absolute steam velocity at the inlet is termed the blade speed ratio <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\rho}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/2/4129f3b5eb57a6b9e106e82ff2c5d398.png"> = <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{U}{V_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/1/e/d1e3810c82028bce802765a9218bcc46.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_b}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/5/885193920ec407fd001c9fe5e2bdc508.png"> is maximum when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{d\eta_b\over d\rho} = 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/2/e/52e7c07b131e57887caee6f03f3f2a0b.png"> or, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{d}{d\rho}(2{\cos \alpha_1-\rho^2 }(1+kc)) = 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/8/b/c8b93310009f1a907a22899ea3845c76.png">. That implies <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\rho} = \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/b/eebe4245812d06a3a5fcd53a340a5c72.png"> and therefore <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/0/370aacb8005eaa674d957fe4eb8dad74.png">. Now <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\rho_{opt}} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/3/4/134fbb13a925571f9614f8bcd142c8cb.png"> (for a single stage impulse turbine)</p> 
     <p>Therefore, the maximum value of stage efficiency is obtained by putting the value of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/0/370aacb8005eaa674d957fe4eb8dad74.png"> in the expression of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\eta_b}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/5/885193920ec407fd001c9fe5e2bdc508.png">/</p> 
     <p>We get: <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{(\eta_b)_{max}} = 2(\rho\cos\alpha_1-\rho^2)(1+kc) = \frac{\cos^2\alpha_1 (1+kc)}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/f/b/dfb2d364b05be448f68b19d1d2b5c7fd.png">.</p> 
     <p>For equiangular blades, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\beta_1  = \beta_2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/2/7029b887c8dca2fb3283d77ad440ce72.png">, therefore <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="c = 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/0/27071423b9a403c9ffa1895ef3fefb6e.png">, and we get <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{(\eta_b)_{max}} = \frac{cos^2\alpha_1(1+k)}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/f/20ff0900cc328ea8267eee54177cf4fa.png">. If the friction due to the blade surface is neglected then <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{(\eta_b)_{max}} = {\cos^2\alpha_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/0/1/00106989fcc6981fdae5c9c92c5c9044.png">.</p> 
     <p><b>Conclusions on maximum efficiency</b></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{(\eta_b)_{max}} = {\cos^2\alpha_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/0/1/00106989fcc6981fdae5c9c92c5c9044.png"></p> 
     <p>1. For a given steam velocity work done per kg of steam would be maximum when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\cos^2\alpha_1} = 1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/a/d/6ad224ff09347a32b296d0a3c19b53e9.png"> or <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha_1 = 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/1/e/01ea9cd752278d6011ee9b2b7fe75e94.png">.</p> 
     <p>2. As <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha_1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/3/f/b3f5f33c109a4724e67a30dccb803ba9.png"> increases, the work done on the blades reduces, but at the same time surface area of the blade reduces, therefore there are less frictional losses.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_turbines">Reaction turbines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reaction turbines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In the <i>reaction turbine</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Rotor_(turbine)" title="Rotor (turbine)" class="mw-redirect">rotor</a> blades themselves are arranged to form convergent <a href="/wiki/Nozzle" title="Nozzle">nozzles</a>. This type of turbine makes use of the reaction force produced as the steam accelerates through the nozzles formed by the rotor. Steam is directed onto the rotor by the fixed vanes of the <a href="/wiki/Stator" title="Stator">stator</a>. It leaves the stator as a jet that fills the entire circumference of the rotor. The steam then changes direction and increases its speed relative to the speed of the blades. A pressure drop occurs across both the stator and the rotor, with steam accelerating through the stator and decelerating through the rotor, with no net change in steam velocity across the stage but with a decrease in both pressure and temperature, reflecting the work performed in the driving of the rotor.</p> 
     <p><b>Blade efficiency</b></p> 
     <p>Energy input to the blades in a stage:</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E = {\Delta h}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/9/b/c9b427b4189b1478abc36791dc514929.png"> is equal to the kinetic energy supplied to the fixed blades (f) + the kinetic energy supplied to the moving blades (m).</p> 
     <p>Or, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{E}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/c/5/dc59d8ec8a43738af266cc48b60a4194.png"> = enthalpy drop over the fixed blades, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h_f}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/b/0bb4621945518973d0bb6767a91bd64c.png"> + enthalpy drop over the moving blades, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h_m}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/5/9/a59eee328b198218bd375ec8b2de3d40.png">.</p> 
     <p>The effect of expansion of steam over the moving blades is to increase the relative velocity at the exit. Therefore, the relative velocity at the exit <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_{r2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/5/2/a52f4b91345dabfe4d1ad652a05fade0.png"> is always greater than the relative velocity at the inlet <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_{r1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/9/6/996eb40a9e335b9a18abf55fb8e0f3df.png">.</p> 
     <p>In terms of velocities, the enthalpy drop over the moving blades is given by:</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h_m} = \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/7/f/47ffd3668892127e5df2680664586224.png"> (it contributes to a change in static pressure)</p> 
     <p>The enthalpy drop in the fixed blades, with the assumption that the velocity of steam entering the fixed blades is equal to the velocity of steam leaving the previously moving blades is given by:</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Edited_blade_design.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Edited_blade_design.png/450px-Edited_blade_design.png" width="450" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Edited_blade_design.png/675px-Edited_blade_design.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Edited_blade_design.png 2x" data-file-width="721" data-file-height="469"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Edited_blade_design.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Velocity diagram
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h_f}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/b/0bb4621945518973d0bb6767a91bd64c.png"> = <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{V_1^2 - V_0^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/9/539ed88b67c1233502081479c570232c.png"> where V<sub>0</sub> is the inlet velocity of steam in the nozzle</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_{0}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/4/d/84d9a0ea0b223d004a89e01131c209aa.png"> is very small and hence can be neglected</p> 
     <p>Therefore, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{\Delta h_f}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/b/0bb4621945518973d0bb6767a91bd64c.png"> = <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{V_1^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/1/0/310122d3f91485ba83923cec96dad6de.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E = {\Delta h_f+\Delta h_m}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/d/7ada7d4e65b6e441233ad00c0d5986ad.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E = \frac{V_1^2}{2} + \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/c/2/2c25f1e567c1e4cd507f6d8fce913b90.png"></p> 
     <p>A very widely used design has half <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_reaction" title="Degree of reaction">degree of reaction</a> or 50% reaction and this is known as <b>Parson’s turbine</b>. This consists of symmetrical rotor and stator blades. For this turbine the velocity triangle is similar and we have:</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\alpha_1  = \beta_2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/7/b/a7bffdd1c453dad7e8a5794d9d41a90c.png">, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\beta_1  = \alpha_2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/4/3/a43ca47db72a895d0f0c90c4b6b239a5.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_1 = V_{r2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/b/7abbb86b7c48dc11ef7bf57204a6e9ab.png">, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="V_{r1} = V_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/f/2/1f294a08efc852fbb86072bf4a880d14.png"></p> 
     <p>Assuming <i>Parson’s turbine</i> and obtaining all the expressions we get</p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{E} = {V_1^2}-\frac{V_{r1}^2}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/d/8/3d8f009074b13e6bedcc27b3b4f1a59f.png"></p> 
     <p>From the inlet velocity triangle we have <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{V_{r1}^2} = {V_1^2+U^2-2UV_1\cos\alpha_1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/8/1/58145be9e24b5c4e88689e804401d3a3.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{E} = {V_1^2-\frac{V_1^2}{2}-\frac{U^2}{2}+\frac{2UV_1\cos\alpha_1}{2}}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/f/8df3e049b62b1277cced61aaadbd7f1c.png"></p> 
     <p><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{E} = \frac{V_1^2-U^2+2UV_1\cos\alpha_1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/d/2/7d2f1c5f4055df23c974fa76210a8fa0.png"></p> 
     <p>Work done (for unit mass flow per second): <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="{W} = {U * \Delta V_w} = {U*(2*V_1\cos\alpha_1-U)}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/3/f037af56767b3c4c4831e8c57f988103.png"></p> 
     <p>Therefore, the <b>blade efficiency</b> is given by</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_and_maintenance">Operation and maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Operation and maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg/220px-Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg/330px-Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg/440px-Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2700" data-file-height="1800"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Modern_Steam_Turbine_Generator.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A modern steam turbine generator installation
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Because of the high pressures used in the steam circuits and the materials used, steam turbines and their casings have high <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_heat_capacity#Thermal_inertia" title="Volumetric heat capacity">thermal inertia</a>. When warming up a steam turbine for use, the main steam stop valves (after the boiler) have a bypass line to allow superheated steam to slowly bypass the valve and proceed to heat up the lines in the system along with the steam turbine. Also, a <a href="/wiki/Turning_gear" title="Turning gear" class="mw-redirect">turning gear</a> is engaged when there is no steam to slowly rotate the turbine to ensure even heating to prevent <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">uneven expansion</a>. After first rotating the turbine by the turning gear, allowing time for the rotor to assume a straight plane (no bowing), then the turning gear is disengaged and steam is admitted to the turbine, first to the astern blades then to the ahead blades slowly rotating the turbine at 10–15&nbsp;RPM (0.17–0.25&nbsp;Hz) to slowly warm the turbine. The warm up procedure for large steam turbines may exceed ten hours.<sup id="cite_ref-EEA_EPA_report_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEA_EPA_report-22">[22]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>During normal operation, rotor imbalance can lead to vibration, which, because of the high rotation velocities, could lead to a blade breaking away from the rotor and through the casing. To reduce this risk, considerable efforts are spent to balance the turbine. Also, turbines are run with high quality steam: either <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated (dry) steam</a>, or saturated steam with a high dryness fraction. This prevents the rapid impingement and erosion of the blades which occurs when condensed water is blasted onto the blades (moisture carry over). Also, liquid water entering the blades may damage the thrust bearings for the turbine shaft. To prevent this, along with controls and baffles in the boilers to ensure high quality steam, condensate drains are installed in the steam piping leading to the turbine.</p> 
     <p>Maintenance requirements of modern steam turbines are simple and incur low costs (typically around $0.005 per kWh);<sup id="cite_ref-EEA_EPA_report_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEA_EPA_report-22">[22]</a></sup> their operational life often exceeds 50 years.<sup id="cite_ref-EEA_EPA_report_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEA_EPA_report-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

        <div class="magnify">

        </div> Diagram of a steam turbine generator system

     <p>The control of a turbine with a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine_governing" title="Steam turbine governing">governor</a> is essential, as turbines need to be run up slowly to prevent damage and some applications (such as the generation of alternating current electricity) require precise speed control.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Uncontrolled acceleration of the turbine rotor can lead to an overspeed trip, which causes the nozzle valves that control the flow of steam to the turbine to close. If this fails then the turbine may continue accelerating until it breaks apart, often catastrophically. Turbines are expensive to make, requiring precision manufacture and special quality materials.</p> 
     <p>During normal operation in synchronization with the electricity network, power plants are governed with a five percent <a href="/wiki/Droop_speed_control" title="Droop speed control">droop speed control</a>. This means the full load speed is 100% and the no-load speed is 105%. This is required for the stable operation of the network without hunting and drop-outs of power plants. Normally the changes in speed are minor. Adjustments in power output are made by slowly raising the droop curve by increasing the spring pressure on a <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_governor" title="Centrifugal governor">centrifugal governor</a>. Generally this is a basic system requirement for all power plants because the older and newer plants have to be compatible in response to the instantaneous changes in frequency without depending on outside communication.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

     <p>The steam turbine operates on basic principles of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> using the part 3-4 of the <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> shown in the adjoining diagram. <a href="/wiki/Superheated" title="Superheated" class="mw-redirect">Superheated</a> steam (or dry saturated steam, depending on application) leaves the boiler at high temperature and high pressure. At entry to the turbine, the steam gains kinetic energy by passing through a nozzle (a fixed nozzle in an impulse type turbine or the fixed blades in a reaction type turbine). When the steam leaves the nozzle it is moving at high velocity towards the blades of the turbine rotor. A force is created on the blades due to the pressure of the vapor on the blades causing them to move. A generator or other such device can be placed on the shaft, and the energy that was in the steam can now be stored and used. The steam leaves the turbine as a <a href="/wiki/Saturated_vapor" title="Saturated vapor" class="mw-redirect">saturated vapor</a> (or liquid-vapor mix depending on application) at a lower temperature and pressure than it entered with and is sent to the condenser to be cooled.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The first law enables us to find an formula for the rate at which work is developed per unit mass. Assuming there is no heat transfer to the surrounding environment and that the changes in kinetic and potential energy are negligible compared to the change in specific <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> we arrive at the following equation</p>

      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Ẇ</b> is the rate at which work is developed per unit time</li> 
      <li><b>ṁ</b> is the rate of mass flow through the turbine</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Isentropic_efficiency">Isentropic efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Isentropic efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>To measure how well a turbine is performing we can look at its <a href="/wiki/Isentropic" title="Isentropic" class="mw-redirect">isentropic</a> efficiency. This compares the actual performance of the turbine with the performance that would be achieved by an ideal, isentropic, turbine.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup> When calculating this efficiency, heat lost to the surroundings is assumed to be zero. The starting pressure and temperature is the same for both the actual and the ideal turbines, but at turbine exit the energy content ('specific enthalpy') for the actual turbine is greater than that for the ideal turbine because of irreversibility in the actual turbine. The specific enthalpy is evaluated at the same pressure for the actual and ideal turbines in order to give a good comparison between the two.</p> 
     <p>The isentropic efficiency is found by dividing the actual work by the ideal work.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><i><b>h<sub>3</sub></b></i> is the specific enthalpy at state three</li> 
      <li><i><b>h<sub>4</sub></b></i> is the specific enthalpy at state 4 for the actual turbine</li>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">Electrical power stations</a> use large steam turbines driving <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generators</a> to produce most (about 80%) of the world's electricity. The advent of large steam turbines made central-station electricity generation practical, since reciprocating steam engines of large rating became very bulky, and operated at slow speeds. Most central stations are <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant" class="mw-redirect">fossil fuel power plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a>; some installations use <a href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal" class="mw-redirect">geothermal</a> steam, or use <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> (CSP) to create the steam. Steam turbines can also be used directly to drive large <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_pump" title="Centrifugal pump">centrifugal pumps</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Feedwater_pump" title="Feedwater pump" class="mw-redirect">feedwater pumps</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_plant" title="Thermal power plant" class="mw-redirect">thermal power plant</a>.</p> 
     <p>The turbines used for electric power generation are most often directly coupled to their generators. As the generators must rotate at constant synchronous speeds according to the frequency of the electric power system, the most common speeds are 3,000&nbsp;RPM for 50&nbsp;Hz systems, and 3,600&nbsp;RPM for 60&nbsp;Hz systems. Since nuclear reactors have lower temperature limits than fossil-fired plants, with lower steam <a href="/wiki/Vapor_quality" title="Vapor quality">quality</a>, the turbine generator sets may be arranged to operate at half these speeds, but with four-pole generators, to reduce erosion of turbine blades.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamships</a>, advantages of steam turbines over reciprocating engines are smaller size, lower maintenance, lighter weight, and lower vibration. A steam turbine is only efficient when operating in the thousands of RPM, while the most effective propeller designs are for speeds less than 300 RPM; consequently, precise (thus expensive) reduction gears are usually required, although numerous early ships through <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Turbinia" title="Turbinia">Turbinia</a></i>, had direct drive from the steam turbines to the propeller shafts. Another alternative is <a href="/wiki/Turbo-electric_transmission" title="Turbo-electric transmission">turbo-electric transmission</a>, in which an electrical generator run by the high-speed turbine is used to run one or more slow-speed electric motors connected to the propeller shafts; precision gear cutting may be a production bottleneck during wartime. Turbo-electric drive was most used in large US warships designed during World War I and in some fast liners, and was used in some troop transports and mass-production <a href="/wiki/Buckley-class_destroyer_escort" title="Buckley-class destroyer escort">destroyer escorts</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p> 
     <p>The higher cost of turbines and the associated gears or generator/motor sets is offset by lower maintenance requirements and the smaller size of a turbine when compared to a reciprocating engine having an equivalent power, although the fuel costs are higher than a diesel engine because steam turbines have lower thermal efficiency. To reduce fuel costs the thermal efficiency of both types of engine have been improved over the years. Today, propulsion steam turbine cycle efficiencies have yet to break 50%, yet diesel engines routinely exceed 50%, especially in marine applications.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Diesel power plants also have lower operating costs since fewer operators are required. Thus, conventional steam power is used in very few new ships. An exception is <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a> which often find it more economical to use <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier#Reliquefaction_and_boil-off" title="LNG carrier">boil-off gas</a> with a steam turbine than to re-liquify it.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_marine_propulsion" title="Nuclear marine propulsion">Nuclear-powered ships and submarines</a> use a nuclear reactor to create steam for turbines. Nuclear power is often chosen where diesel power would be impractical (as in <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> applications) or the logistics of refuelling pose significant problems (for example, <a href="/wiki/Icebreaker" title="Icebreaker">icebreakers</a>). It has been estimated that the reactor fuel for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Vanguard-class_submarine" title="Vanguard-class submarine"><i>Vanguard</i>-class&nbsp;submarines</a> is sufficient to last 40 circumnavigations of the globe – potentially sufficient for the vessel's entire service life. Nuclear propulsion has only been applied to a very few commercial vessels due to the expense of maintenance and the regulatory controls required on nuclear systems and fuel cycles.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The development of steam turbine marine propulsion from 1894-1935 was dominated by the need to reconcile the high efficient speed of the turbine with the low efficient speed (less than 300 rpm) of the ship's propeller at an overall cost competitive with <a href="/wiki/Marine_steam_engine" title="Marine steam engine">reciprocating engines</a>. In 1894, efficient reduction <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">gears</a> were not available for the high powers required by ships, so <a href="/wiki/Direct_drive" title="Direct drive" class="mw-redirect">direct drive</a> was necessary. In <i>Turbinia</i>, which has direct drive to each propeller shaft, the efficient speed of the turbine was reduced after initial trials by directing the steam flow through all three direct drive turbines (one on each shaft) in series, probably totaling around 200 turbine stages operating in series. Also, there were three propellers on each shaft for operation at high speeds.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The high shaft speeds of the era are represented by one of the first US turbine-powered <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Smith_(DD-17)" title="USS Smith (DD-17)">USS&nbsp;<i>Smith</i></a>, launched in 1909, which had direct drive turbines and whose three shafts turned at 724 rpm at 28.35 knots.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The use of turbines in several casings exhausting steam to each other in series became standard in most subsequent marine propulsion applications, and is a form of <a href="#Types">cross-compounding</a>. The first turbine was called the high pressure (HP) turbine, the last turbine was the low pressure (LP) turbine, and any turbine in between was an intermediate pressure (IP) turbine. A much later arrangement than <i>Turbinia</i> can be seen on <a href="/wiki/RMS_Queen_Mary" title="RMS Queen Mary">RMS&nbsp;<i>Queen Mary</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach, California</a>, launched in 1934, in which each shaft is powered by four turbines in series connected to the ends of the two input shafts of a single-reduction gearbox. They are the HP, 1st IP, 2nd IP, and LP turbines.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cruising_machinery_and_gearing">Cruising machinery and gearing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cruising machinery and gearing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The quest for economy was even more important when cruising speeds were considered. Cruising speed is roughly 50% of a warship's maximum speed and 20-25% of its maximum power level. This would be a speed used on long voyages when fuel economy is desired. Although this brought the propeller speeds down to an efficient range, turbine efficiency was greatly reduced, and early turbine ships had poor cruising ranges. A solution that proved useful through most of the steam turbine propulsion era was the cruising turbine. This was an extra turbine to add even more stages, at first attached directly to one or more shafts, exhausting to a stage partway along the HP turbine, and not used at high speeds. As reduction gears became available around 1911, some ships, notably the <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Nevada_(BB-36)" title="USS Nevada (BB-36)">USS&nbsp;<i>Nevada</i></a>, had them on cruising turbines while retaining direct drive main turbines. Reduction gears allowed turbines to operate in their efficient range at a much higher speed than the shaft, but were expensive to manufacture.</p> 
     <p>Cruising turbines competed at first with reciprocating engines for fuel economy. An example of the retention of reciprocating engines on fast ships was the famous <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS&nbsp;<i>Titanic</i></a> of 1911, which along with her sisters <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic">RMS&nbsp;<i>Olympic</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMHS_Britannic" title="HMHS Britannic">HMHS&nbsp;<i>Britannic</i></a> had triple-expansion engines on the two outboard shafts, both exhausting to an LP turbine on the center shaft. After adopting turbines with the <a href="/wiki/Delaware-class_battleship" title="Delaware-class battleship"><i>Delaware</i>-class&nbsp;battleships</a> launched in 1909, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> reverted to reciprocating machinery on the <a href="/wiki/New_York-class_battleship" title="New York-class battleship"><i>New York</i>-class&nbsp;battleships</a> of 1912, then went back to turbines on <i>Nevada</i> in 1914. The lingering fondness for reciprocating machinery was because the US Navy had no plans for capital ships exceeding 21 knots until after World War I, so top speed was less important than economical cruising. The United States had acquired the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> as territories in 1898, and lacked the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s worldwide network of <a href="/wiki/Coaling_station" title="Coaling station" class="mw-redirect">coaling stations</a>. Thus, the US Navy in 1900-1940 had the greatest need of any nation for fuel economy, especially as the prospect of war with <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> arose following World War I. This need was compounded by the US not launching any cruisers 1908-1920, so destroyers were required to perform long-range missions usually assigned to cruisers. So, various cruising solutions were fitted on US destroyers launched 1908-1916. These included small reciprocating engines and geared or ungeared cruising turbines on one or two shafts. However, once fully geared turbines proved economical in initial cost and fuel they were rapidly adopted, with cruising turbines also included on most ships. Beginning in 1915 all new Royal Navy destroyers had fully geared turbines, and the United States followed in 1917.</p> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, speed was a priority until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jutland" title="Battle of Jutland">Battle of Jutland</a> in mid-1916 showed that in the <a href="/wiki/Battlecruiser" title="Battlecruiser">battlecruisers</a> too much armour had been sacrificed in its pursuit. The British used exclusively turbine-powered warships from 1906. Because they recognized that a significant cruising range would be desirable given their world-wide empire, some warships, notably the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth-class_battleship" title="Queen Elizabeth-class battleship"><i>Queen Elizabeth</i>-class&nbsp;battleships</a>, were fitted with cruising turbines from 1912 onwards following earlier experimental installations.</p> 
     <p>In the US Navy, the <a href="/wiki/Mahan-class_destroyer" title="Mahan-class destroyer"><i>Mahan</i>-class&nbsp;destroyers</a>, launched 1935-36, introduced double-reduction gearing. This further increased the turbine speed above the shaft speed, allowing smaller turbines than single-reduction gearing. Steam pressures and temperatures were also increasing progressively, from 300 psi/425 F (2.07 MPa/218 C)(<a href="/wiki/Saturation_temperature" title="Saturation temperature" class="mw-redirect">saturation temperature</a>) on the World War I-era <a href="/wiki/Wickes-class_destroyer" title="Wickes-class destroyer"><i>Wickes</i>&nbsp;class</a> to 615 psi/850 F (4.25 MPa/454 C) <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated steam</a> on some World War II <a href="/wiki/Fletcher-class_destroyer" title="Fletcher-class destroyer"><i>Fletcher</i>-class&nbsp;destroyers</a> and later ships.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A standard configuration emerged of an axial-flow high pressure turbine (sometimes with a cruising turbine attached) and a double-axial-flow low pressure turbine connected to a double-reduction gearbox. This arrangement continued throughout the steam era in the US Navy and was also used in some Royal Navy designs.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Machinery of this configuration can be seen on many preserved World War II-era warships in several countries.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> When US Navy warship construction resumed in the early 1950s, most surface combatants and aircraft carriers used 1,200 psi/950 F (8.28 MPa/510 C) steam.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This continued until the end of the US Navy steam-powered warship era with the <a href="/wiki/Knox-class_frigate" title="Knox-class frigate"><i>Knox</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> of the early 1970s. Amphibious and auxiliary ships continued to use 600 psi (4.14 MPa) steam post-World War II, with <a href="/wiki/USS_Iwo_Jima_(LHD-7)" title="USS Iwo Jima (LHD-7)">USS&nbsp;<i>Iwo Jima</i></a>, launched in 2001, possibly the last non-nuclear steam-powered ship built for the US Navy.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turbo-electric_drive">Turbo-electric drive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Turbo-electric drive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

       </div>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Turbo-electric_transmission" title="Turbo-electric transmission">Turbo-electric drive</a> was introduced on the battleship <a href="/wiki/USS_New_Mexico_(BB-40)" title="USS New Mexico (BB-40)">USS&nbsp;<i>New Mexico</i></a>, launched in 1917. Over the next eight years the US Navy launched five additional turbo-electric-powered battleships and two aircraft carriers (initially ordered as <a href="/wiki/Lexington-class_battlecruiser" title="Lexington-class battlecruiser"><i>Lexington</i>-class&nbsp;battlecruisers</a>). Ten more turbo-electric capital ships were planned, but cancelled due to the limits imposed by the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Treaty" title="Washington Naval Treaty">Washington Naval Treaty</a>. Although <i>New Mexico</i> was refitted with geared turbines in a 1931-33 refit, the remaining turbo-electric ships retained the system throughout their careers. This system used two large steam turbine generators to drive an electric motor on each of four shafts. The system was less costly initially than reduction gears and made the ships more maneuverable in port, with the shafts able to reverse rapidly and deliver more reverse power than with most geared systems. Some ocean liners were also built with turbo-electric drive, as were some troop transports and mass-production <a href="/wiki/Buckley-class_destroyer_escort" title="Buckley-class destroyer escort">destroyer escorts</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. However, when the US designed the "treaty cruisers", beginning with <a href="/wiki/USS_Pensacola_(CA-24)" title="USS Pensacola (CA-24)">USS&nbsp;<i>Pensacola</i></a> launched in 1927, geared turbines were used for all fast steam-powered ships thereafter.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_usage">Current usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Current usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Since the 1980s, steam turbines have been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a> on fast ships and by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a> on other ships; exceptions are <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_marine_propulsion" title="Nuclear marine propulsion">nuclear-powered ships and submarines</a> and <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_ship" title="Auxiliary ship">auxiliary ships</a> continue to use steam propulsion. In the U.S. Navy, the conventionally powered steam turbine is still in use on all but one of the <a href="/wiki/Wasp-class_amphibious_assault_ship" title="Wasp-class amphibious assault ship">Wasp-class</a> amphibious assault ships. The U.S. Navy also operates steam turbines on their nuclear powered <a href="/wiki/Nimitz-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Nimitz-class aircraft carrier">Nimitz-class</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Ford-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carrier">Ford-class</a> aircraft carriers along with all of their nuclear submarines (<a href="/wiki/Ohio-class_submarine" title="Ohio-class submarine">Ohio-</a>,<a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles-class_submarine" title="Los Angeles-class submarine">Los Angeles-</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seawolf-class_submarine" title="Seawolf-class submarine">Seawolf-</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia-class_submarine" title="Virginia-class submarine">Virginia-classes</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> decommissioned its last conventional steam-powered <a href="/wiki/Leander-class_frigate" title="Leander-class frigate"><i>Leander</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a> in 1993, also converting its sole <a href="/wiki/Type_82_destroyer" title="Type 82 destroyer">Type 82 destroyer</a>, HMS <i>Bristol</i>, into a training ship that same year. In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> ended its steam era with the decommissioning of its last <a href="/wiki/Tourville-class_frigate" title="Tourville-class frigate"><i>Tourville</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a>. Amongst the other <a href="/wiki/Blue-water_navies" title="Blue-water navies" class="mw-redirect">blue-water navies</a>, the Russian Navy currently operates steam-powered <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Kuznetsov-class aircraft carrier"><i>Kuznetsov</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sovremenny-class_destroyer" title="Sovremenny-class destroyer" class="mw-redirect"><i>Sovremenny</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a> currently operates two conventional steam-powered carriers, INS <i>Viraat</i>, a former British <a href="/wiki/Centaur-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Centaur-class aircraft carrier"><i>Centaur</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> (to be decommissioned in 2016), and INS <i>Vikramaditya</i>, a modified <a href="/wiki/Kiev-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Kiev-class aircraft carrier"><i>Kiev</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a>; it also operates three <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra-class_frigate" title="Brahmaputra-class frigate"><i>Brahmaputra</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> commissioned in the early 2000s and two <a href="/wiki/Godavari-class_frigate" title="Godavari-class frigate"><i>Godavari</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> currently in the process of being decommissioned.</p> 
     <p>Most other naval forces either retired or re-engined their steam-powered warships by 2010. The Chinese Navy currently operates steam-powered Russian <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Kuznetsov-class aircraft carrier"><i>Kuznetsov</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sovremenny-class_destroyer" title="Sovremenny-class destroyer" class="mw-redirect"><i>Sovremenny</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>; it also operates steam-powered <a href="/wiki/Luda-class_destroyer" title="Luda-class destroyer" class="mw-redirect"><i>Luda</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a>. The JS <i>Kurama</i>, the last steam-powered <a href="/wiki/JMSDF" title="JMSDF" class="mw-redirect">JMSDF</a> <a href="/wiki/Shirane-class_destroyer" title="Shirane-class destroyer"><i>Shirane</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a>, will be decommissioned and replaced in 2017. as of 2016, the Brazilian Navy operates <i>São Paulo</i>, a former French <a href="/wiki/Clemenceau-class_aircraft_carrier" title="Clemenceau-class aircraft carrier"><i>Clemenceau</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Navy" title="Mexican Navy">Mexican Navy</a> currently operates four former U.S. <a href="/wiki/Knox-class_frigate" title="Knox-class frigate"><i>Knox</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> and two former U.S. <a href="/wiki/Bronstein-class_frigate" title="Bronstein-class frigate"><i>Bronstein</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_Navy" title="Royal Thai Navy">Royal Thai Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Navy" title="Egyptian Navy">Egyptian Navy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_Navy" title="Republic of China Navy">Republic of China Navy</a> respectively operate one, two and six former U.S. <a href="/wiki/Knox-class_frigate" title="Knox-class frigate"><i>Knox</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Navy" title="Peruvian Navy">Peruvian Navy</a> currently operates the former Dutch <a href="/wiki/De_Zeven_Provinci%C3%ABn-class_cruiser" title="De Zeven Provinciën-class cruiser"><i>De Zeven Provinciën</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">cruiser</a> <i><a href="/wiki/BAP_Almirante_Grau_(CLM-81)" title="BAP Almirante Grau (CLM-81)">BAP Almirante Grau</a></i>; the <a href="/wiki/Ecuadorian_Navy" title="Ecuadorian Navy">Ecuadorian Navy</a> currently operates two <a href="/wiki/Condell-class_frigate" title="Condell-class frigate"><i>Condell</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> (modified <a href="/wiki/Leander-class_frigate" title="Leander-class frigate"><i>Leander</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotives">Locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine_locomotive" title="Steam turbine locomotive">Steam turbine locomotive</a>
     </div> 
     <p>A steam turbine locomotive engine is a <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> driven by a steam turbine.</p> 
     <p>The main advantages of a steam turbine locomotive are better rotational balance and reduced <a href="/wiki/Hammer_blow" title="Hammer blow">hammer blow</a> on the track. However, a disadvantage is less flexible output power so that turbine locomotives were best suited for long-haul operations at a constant output power.<sup id="cite_ref-Streeter_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Streeter-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The first steam turbine rail locomotive was built in 1908 for the Officine Meccaniche Miani Silvestri Grodona Comi, Milan, Italy. In 1924 <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a> built the steam turbine locomotive T18 001, operational in 1929, for <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Reichsbahn" title="Deutsche Reichsbahn">Deutsche Reichsbahn</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>British, German, other national and international test codes are used to standardize the procedures and definitions used to test steam turbines. Selection of the test code to be used is an agreement between the purchaser and the manufacturer, and has some significance to the design of the turbine and associated systems. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/ASME" title="ASME">ASME</a> has produced several performance test codes on steam turbines. These include ASME PTC 6-2004, Steam Turbines, ASME PTC 6.2-2011, Steam Turbines in <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">Combined Cycles</a>, PTC 6S-1988, Procedures for Routine Performance Test of Steam Turbines. These ASME performance test codes have gained international recognition and acceptance for testing steam turbines. The single most important and differentiating characteristic of ASME performance test codes, including PTC 6, is that the test uncertainty of the measurement indicates the quality of the test and is not to be used as a commercial tolerance.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
