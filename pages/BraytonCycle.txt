     <p>The <b>Brayton cycle</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycle</a> that describes the workings of a constant pressure heat engine. The original Brayton engines used a piston compressor / expander <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">Gas turbine</a> engines and <a href="/wiki/Airbreathing_jet_engine" title="Airbreathing jet engine">airbreathing jet engines</a> use the Brayton cycle. Although the Brayton cycle is usually run as an <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open system</a> (and indeed <i>must</i> be run as such if internal combustion is used), it is conventionally assumed for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> analysis that the exhaust gases are reused in the intake, enabling analysis as a closed system.</p> 
     <p>The engine cycle is named after <a href="/wiki/George_Brayton" title="George Brayton">George Brayton</a> (1830–1892), the American <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> who developed it originally for use in piston engines , although it was originally proposed and patented by Englishman <a href="/wiki/John_Barber_(engineer)" title="John Barber (engineer)">John Barber</a> in 1791.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also sometimes known as the <b><a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">Joule</a> cycle</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Ericsson_cycle" title="Ericsson cycle">Ericsson cycle</a> is similar to the Brayton cycle but uses external heat and incorporates the use of a regenerator. There are two types of Brayton cycles, open to the atmosphere and using internal <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a> or closed and using a heat exchanger.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>In 1872, <a href="/wiki/George_Brayton" title="George Brayton">George Brayton</a> applied for a patent for his "Ready Motor," a reciprocating constant pressure engine. The engine used a separate piston compressor and expander, with compressed air heated by internal fire as it entered the expander cylinder. The first versions of the Brayton engine mixed vaporized fuel with air as it entered the compressor by means of a <a href="/wiki/Heated-surface_carburetor" title="Heated-surface carburetor" class="mw-redirect">heated-surface carburetor</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The fuel / air was contained in a reservoir / tank and then it was admitted to the expansion cylinder and burned. As the fuel / air mixture entered the expansion cylinder it was ignited by a pilot flame. A screen was used to prevent the fire from entering / returning to the reservoir. In early versions of the engine, this screen sometimes failed and an explosion would occur, but in 1874 Brayton solved the explosion problem by adding the fuel just prior to the expander cylinder. The engine now used heavier fuels such as kerosine and fuel oil. Ignition remained pilot flame.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Brayton produced and sold "Ready Motors" to perform a variety of tasks like water pumping, mill operation, even marine propulsion. Critics of the day claimed the engines ran smoothly and had an efficiency of about 17%.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Brayton cycle engines were some of the first internal combustion engines used for motive power. In 1881, John Holland used a Brayton engine to power the world's first successful self-propelled submarine, the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_Ram" title="Fenian Ram">Fenian Ram</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Holland" title="John Philip Holland">John Philip Holland</a>'s submarine is preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Paterson_Museum" title="Paterson Museum">Paterson Museum</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Great_Falls_Historic_District" title="Old Great Falls Historic District">Old Great Falls Historic District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paterson,_New_Jersey" title="Paterson, New Jersey">Paterson, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg/220px-George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg" width="220" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg/330px-George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg/440px-George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg 2x" data-file-width="606" data-file-height="480"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:George_B_Selden_driving_automobile_in_1905.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> George B Selden driving a Brayton powered automobile in 1905
       </div>

     <p>In 1878, <a href="/wiki/George_B._Selden" title="George B. Selden">George B. Selden</a> produced the first internal combustion automobile. Inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> invented by <a href="/wiki/George_Brayton" title="George Brayton">George Brayton</a> displayed at the <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Exposition" title="Centennial Exposition">Centennial Exposition</a> in Philadelphia in 1876, Selden began working on a smaller lighter version, succeeding by 1878, some eight years before the public introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Benz_Patent_Motorwagen" title="Benz Patent Motorwagen" class="mw-redirect">Benz Patent Motorwagen</a> in Europe. The Selden auto was powered by a 3-cylinder, 400-pound version of the Brayton Cycle engine which featured an enclosed <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshaft</a>. Selden designed and constructed the engine with the help of Rochester machinist Frank H. Clement and his assistant William Gomm. He filed for a patent on May 8, 1879 (in a historical cross of people, the witness Selden chose was a local bank-teller, George Eastman, later to become famous for the <a href="/wiki/Kodak" title="Kodak">Kodak</a> camera<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup>). His application included not only the engine but its use in a 4 wheeled car. He then filed a series of amendments to his application which stretched out the legal process resulting in a delay of 16 years before the patent<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> was granted on November 5, 1895. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> fought the Selden patent. Ford argued his cars used the four-stroke <a href="/wiki/Otto_cycle" title="Otto cycle">Otto cycle</a> and not the Brayton engine shown used in the Selden auto. Ford won the appeal of the original case.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A Brayton-type <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> consists of three components:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>a <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a></li> 
      <li>a mixing chamber</li> 
      <li>an <a href="/wiki/Turboexpander" title="Turboexpander">expander</a></li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>In the original 19th-century Brayton engine, ambient air is drawn into a piston compressor, where it is <a href="/wiki/Gas_compression" title="Gas compression" class="mw-redirect">compressed</a>; ideally an <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropic process</a>. The compressed air then runs through a mixing chamber where fuel is added, an <a href="/wiki/Isobaric_process" title="Isobaric process">isobaric process</a>. The pressurized air and fuel mixture is then ignited in an expansion cylinder and energy is released, causing the heated air and combustion products to expand through a piston/cylinder; another ideally isentropic process. Some of the work extracted by the piston/cylinder is used to drive the compressor through a crankshaft arrangement.</p> 
     <p>The term Brayton cycle has more recently been given to the <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> engine. This also has three components:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>a gas compressor</li> 
      <li>a burner (or <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> chamber)</li> 
      <li>an <a href="/wiki/Expansion_turbine" title="Expansion turbine" class="mw-redirect">expansion turbine</a></li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>Ideal Brayton cycle:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropic process</a> – ambient air is drawn into the compressor, where it is pressurized.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Isobaric_process" title="Isobaric process">isobaric process</a> – the compressed air then runs through a combustion chamber, where fuel is burned, heating that air—a constant-pressure process, since the chamber is open to flow in and out.</li> 
      <li>isentropic process – the heated, pressurized air then gives up its energy, expanding through a turbine (or series of turbines). Some of the work extracted by the turbine is used to drive the compressor.</li> 
      <li>isobaric process – heat rejection (in the atmosphere).</li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>Actual Brayton cycle:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a> – compression</li> 
      <li>isobaric process – heat addition</li> 
      <li>adiabatic process – expansion</li> 
      <li>isobaric process – heat rejection</li> 
     </ol>

     <p>Since neither the compression nor the expansion can be truly isentropic, losses through the compressor and the expander represent sources of inescapable working <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_efficiency" title="Thermodynamic efficiency" class="mw-redirect">inefficiencies</a>. In general, increasing the <a href="/wiki/Compression_ratio" title="Compression ratio">compression ratio</a> is the most direct way to increase the overall <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> output of a Brayton system.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The efficiency of the ideal Brayton cycle is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" \eta = 1 - \frac {T_1}{T_2} = 1 - \left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/b/4/2b44e299d47435f6e11a7722696c2302.png">, where <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\gamma" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/3/4/334de1ea38b615839e4ee6b65ee1b103.png"> is the <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity_ratio" title="Heat capacity ratio">heat capacity ratio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Figure 1 indicates how the cycle efficiency changes with an increase in pressure ratio. Figure 2 indicates how the specific power output changes with an increase in the gas turbine inlet temperature for two different pressure ratio values.</p>

     <p>The highest temperature in the cycle occurs at the end of the combustion process, and it is limited by the maximum temperature that the turbine blades can withstand. This also limits the pressure ratios that can be used in the cycle. For a fixed turbine inlet temperature, the net work output per cycle increases with the pressure ratio (thus the thermal efficiency) and the net work output. With less work output per cycle, a larger mass flow rate (thus a larger system) is needed to maintain the same power output, which may not be economical. In most common designs, the pressure ratio of a gas turbine ranges from about 11 to 16.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_to_increase_power">Methods to increase power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methods to increase power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The power output of a Brayton engine can be improved in the following manners:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><i>Reheat</i>, wherein the <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a>—in most cases air—expands through a series of turbines, then is passed through a second combustion chamber before expanding to ambient pressure through a final set of turbines. This has the advantage of increasing the power output possible for a given compression ratio without exceeding any <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgical</a> constraints (typically about 1000&nbsp;°C). The use of an <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a> for jet aircraft engines can also be referred to as "reheat"; it is a different process in that the reheated air is expanded through a thrust nozzle rather than a turbine. The metallurgical constraints are somewhat alleviated, enabling much higher reheat temperatures (about 2000&nbsp;°C). Reheat is most often used to improve the specific power (per throughput of air), and is usually associated with a drop in efficiency, this effect is especially pronounced in afterburners due to the extreme amounts of extra fuel used.</li> 
      <li><i>Overspray</i>, wherein, after a first compressor stage, water is injected into the compressor, thus increasing the mass-flow inside the compressor, increasing the turbine output power significantly and reducing compressor outlet temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In a second compressor stage the water is completely converted to a gas form, offering some intercooling via its latent heat of vaporization.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_to_improve_efficiency">Methods to improve efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methods to improve efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The efficiency of a Brayton engine can be improved in the following manners:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><i>Increasing pressure ratio</i> – As Figure 1 above shows, increasing the pressure ratio increases the efficiency of the Brayton cycle. This is analogous to the increase of efficiency seen in the <a href="/wiki/Otto_cycle" title="Otto cycle">Otto cycle</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Compression_ratio" title="Compression ratio">compression ratio</a> is increased. However, there are practical limits when it comes to increasing the pressure ratio. First of all, increasing the pressure ratio increases the compressor discharge temperature. This can cause the temperature of the gasses leaving the combustor to exceed the metallurgical limits of the turbine. Also, the diameter of the compressor blades becomes progressively smaller in higher pressure stages of the compressor. Because the gap between the blades and the engine casing increases in size as a percentage of the compressor blade height as the blades get smaller in diameter, a greater percentage of the compressed air can leak back past the blades in higher pressure stages. This causes a drop in compressor efficiency, and is most likely to occur in smaller gas turbines (since blades are inherently smaller to begin with). Finally, as can be seen in Figure 1, the efficiency levels off as pressure ratio increases. Hence, there is little to gain by increasing the pressure ratio further if it is already at a high level.</li> 
      <li><i>Recuperator</i><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> – If the Brayton cycle is run at a low pressure ratio and a high temperature increase in the combustion chamber, the exhaust gas (after the last turbine stage) might still be hotter than the compressed inlet gas (after the last compression stage but before the combustor). In that case, a heat exchanger can be used to transfer thermal energy from the exhaust to the already compressed gas, before it enters the combustion chamber. The thermal energy transferred is effectively re-used, thus increasing efficiency. However, this form of heat recycling is only possible, if the engine is run in a low efficiency mode with low pressure ratio in the first place.<br> Note, that transferring heat from the outlet (after the last turbine) to the inlet (before the first compressor stage) would reduce efficiency, as hotter inlet air means more volume and thus more work for the compressor. For engines with liquid cryogenic fuels, namely <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a>, it might be feasible, though, to use the fuel to cool the inlet air before compression to increase efficiency. This concept is extensively studied for the <a href="/wiki/SABRE_(rocket_engine)" title="SABRE (rocket engine)">SABRE</a> engine.</li> 
      <li>A Brayton engine also forms half of the '<a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a>' system, which combines with a <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine engine</a> to further increase overall efficiency. However, although this increases overall efficiency, it does not actually increase the efficiency of the Brayton cycle itself.</li> 
      <li><i><a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Cogeneration</a></i> systems make use of the waste heat from Brayton engines, typically for hot water production or space heating.</li> 
     </ul>

     <p>A closed Brayton cycle recirculates the <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a>, the air expelled from the turbine is reintroduced into the compressor, this cycle uses a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> to heat the working fluid instead of an internal combustion chamber. The closed Brayton cycle is used for example in <a href="/wiki/Closed-cycle_gas_turbine" title="Closed-cycle gas turbine">closed-cycle gas turbine</a> and space power generation.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_Brayton_cycle">Solar Brayton cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solar Brayton cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In 2002, a hybrid open solar Brayton cycle was operated for the first time consistently and effectively with relevant papers published, in the frame of the EU SOLGATE program.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The air was heated from 570&nbsp;K to over 1000&nbsp;K into the combustor chamber. Further hybridization was achieved during the EU Solhyco project running a hybridized Brayton cycle with solar energy and Biodiesel only.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> This technology was scaled up to 4.6&nbsp;MW within the project Solugas located near Seville where it is currently demonstrated at pre-commercial scale.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_Brayton_cycle">Reverse Brayton cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brayton_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reverse Brayton cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A Brayton cycle that is driven in reverse, via net work input, and when air is the working fluid, is the <b>air <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> cycle</b> or <b>Bell Coleman cycle</b>. Its purpose is to move heat, rather than produce work. This air cooling technique is used widely in jet aircraft for air conditioning systems utilizing air tapped from the engine compressors.</p>
