   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hard water</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p><b>Hard water</b> is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> that has high <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> content (in contrast with "<a href="/wiki/Soft_water" title="Soft water">soft water</a>"). Hard water is formed when water <a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">percolates</a> through deposits of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> which are largely made up of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> carbonates.</p> 
     <p>Hard <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> may have moderate health benefits, but can pose serious problems in industrial settings, where water hardness is monitored to avoid costly breakdowns in <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling towers</a>, and other equipment that handles water. In domestic settings, hard water is often indicated by a lack of <a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">suds</a> formation when <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a> is agitated in water, and by the formation of <a href="/wiki/Limescale" title="Limescale">limescale</a> in kettles and water heaters.<sup id="cite_ref-who_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Wherever water hardness is a concern, <a href="/wiki/Water_softening" title="Water softening">water softening</a> is commonly used to reduce hard water's adverse effects.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_hardness">Sources of hardness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources of hardness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Water's hardness is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Multivalent" title="Multivalent" class="mw-redirect">multivalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Cations" title="Cations" class="mw-redirect">cations</a> in the water. Multivalent cations are positively charged <a href="/wiki/Metal_complex" title="Metal complex" class="mw-redirect">metal complexes</a> with a charge greater than 1+. Usually, the cations have the charge of 2+. Common cations found in hard water include Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup>. These ions enter a water supply by leaching from minerals within an <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>. Common <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>-containing minerals are <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>. A common <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> mineral is <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> (which also contains calcium). <a href="/wiki/Rainwater" title="Rainwater" class="mw-redirect">Rainwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a> water are <a href="/wiki/Soft_water" title="Soft water">soft</a>, because they contain few <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The following <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_reaction" title="Equilibrium reaction" class="mw-redirect">equilibrium reaction</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolving</a> and formation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>&nbsp;:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       CaCO
       <sub>3</sub> (s) + CO
       <sub>2</sub> (aq) + H
       <sub>2</sub>O (l) ⇋ Ca
       <sup>2+</sup> (aq) + 2HCO
       <sub>3</sub>
       <sup>−</sup> (aq)
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The reaction can go in either direction. Rain containing dissolved carbon dioxide can react with calcium carbonate and carry calcium ions away with it. The calcium carbonate may be re-deposited as calcite as the carbon dioxide is lost to atmosphere, sometimes forming <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalactites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a>.</p> 
     <p>Calcium and magnesium ions can sometimes be removed by water softeners.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_hardness">Temporary hardness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Temporary hardness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_hardness" title="Carbonate hardness">Carbonate hardness</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Temporary hardness is a type of water hardness caused by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">calcium bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_bicarbonate" title="Magnesium bicarbonate">magnesium bicarbonate</a>). When dissolved, these minerals yield calcium and magnesium <a href="/wiki/Cations" title="Cations" class="mw-redirect">cations</a> (Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>) and carbonate and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> <a href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion" class="mw-redirect">anions</a> (CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>, HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>). The presence of the metal cations makes the water hard. However, unlike the <a href="#Permanent_hardness">permanent hardness</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Sulphate" title="Sulphate" class="mw-redirect">sulphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> <a href="/wiki/Compound_(chemistry)" title="Compound (chemistry)" class="mw-redirect">compounds</a>, this "temporary" hardness can be reduced either by boiling the water, or by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)" class="mw-redirect">lime</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>) through the softening process of <a href="/wiki/Lime_softening" title="Lime softening">lime softening</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Boiling promotes the formation of carbonate from the bicarbonate and precipitates calcium carbonate out of solution, leaving water that is softer upon cooling.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_hardness">Permanent hardness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Permanent hardness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Permanent hardness is hardness (mineral content) that cannot be removed by <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boiling</a>. When this is the case, it is usually caused by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulphate" title="Calcium sulphate" class="mw-redirect">calcium sulphate</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_sulphate" title="Magnesium sulphate" class="mw-redirect">magnesium sulphates</a> in the water, which do not precipitate out as the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> increases. Ions causing permanent hardness of water can be removed using a water softener, or <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange column</a>.</p> 
     <p>Total Permanent Hardness = Calcium Hardness + Magnesium Hardness</p> 
     <p>The calcium and magnesium hardness is the concentration of calcium and magnesium ions expressed as equivalent of calcium carbonate.</p> 
     <p>Total permanent water hardness expressed as equivalent of CaCO<sub>3</sub> can be calculated with the following formula: Total Permanent Hardness (CaCO<sub>3</sub>) = 2.5(Ca<sup>2+</sup>) + 4.1(Mg<sup>2+</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_hard_water">Effects of hard water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects of hard water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>With hard water, soap solutions form a white precipitate (<a href="/wiki/Soap_scum" title="Soap scum">soap scum</a>) instead of producing <a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">lather</a>, because the 2+ ions destroy the <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactant</a> properties of the soap by forming a solid precipitate (the soap scum). A major component of such scum is <a href="/wiki/Calcium_stearate" title="Calcium stearate">calcium stearate</a>, which arises from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_stearate" title="Sodium stearate">sodium stearate</a>, the main component of <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       2 C
       <sub>17</sub>H
       <sub>35</sub>COO
       <sup>−</sup> (aq) + Ca
       <sup>2+</sup> (aq) → (C
       <sub>17</sub>H
       <sub>35</sub>COO)
       <sub>2</sub>Ca (s)
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Hardness can thus be defined as the soap-consuming capacity of a water sample, or the capacity of precipitation of soap as a characteristic property of water that prevents the lathering of soap. Synthetic <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a> do not form such scums.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg/210px-Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg" width="210" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg/315px-Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg/420px-Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1227" data-file-height="1194"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Eifelwasserleitung05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A portion of the ancient Roman 
        <a href="/wiki/Eifel_aqueduct" title="Eifel aqueduct" class="mw-redirect">Eifel aqueduct</a> in Germany. In service for about 180 years, the aqueduct had deposits of scale up to 20&nbsp;cm thick along the walls.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Hard water also forms deposits that clog plumbing. These deposits, called "<a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">scale</a>", are composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (CaCO<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_hydroxide" title="Magnesium hydroxide">magnesium hydroxide</a> (Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate</a> (CaSO<sub>4</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Calcium and magnesium carbonates tend to be deposited as off-white solids on the inside surfaces of pipes and <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>. This precipitation (formation of an insoluble solid) is principally caused by thermal decomposition of bicarbonate ions but also happens in cases where the carbonate ion is at saturation concentration.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The resulting build-up of scale restricts the flow of water in pipes. In boilers, the deposits impair the flow of heat into water, reducing the heating efficiency and allowing the metal boiler components to overheat. In a pressurized system, this overheating can lead to failure of the boiler.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The damage caused by calcium carbonate deposits varies on the crystalline form, for example, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The presence of <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>, in this case, hard water, can also lead to <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">galvanic corrosion</a>, in which one metal will preferentially <a href="/wiki/Corrode" title="Corrode" class="mw-redirect">corrode</a> when in contact with another type of metal, when both are in contact with an electrolyte. The softening of hard water by ion exchange does not increase its <a href="/wiki/Corrosivity" title="Corrosivity" class="mw-redirect">corrosivity</a> <i>per se</i>. Similarly, where lead plumbing is in use, softened water does not substantially increase <a href="/wiki/Lead_(element)" title="Lead (element)" class="mw-redirect">plumbo</a>-solvency.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In swimming pools, hard water is manifested by a <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbid</a>, or cloudy (milky), appearance to the water. Calcium and magnesium hydroxides are both soluble in water. The solubility of the hydroxides of the alkaline-earth metals to which calcium and magnesium belong (<a href="/wiki/Periodic_table#Groups" title="Periodic table">group 2 of the periodic table</a>) increases moving down the column. Aqueous solutions of these metal hydroxides absorb carbon dioxide from the air, forming the insoluble carbonates, giving rise to the turbidity. This often results from the pH being excessively high (pH &gt; 7.6). Hence, a common solution to the problem is, while maintaining the chlorine concentration at the proper level, to lower the pH by the addition of hydrochloric acid, the optimum value being in the range of 7.2 to 7.6.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Softening">Softening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Softening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Water_softening" title="Water softening">Water softening</a>
     </div> 
     <p>It is often desirable to soften hard water. Most detergents contain ingredients that counteract the effects of hard water on the surfactants. For this reason, water softening is often unnecessary. Where softening is practised, it is often recommended to soften only the water sent to domestic hot water systems so as to prevent or delay inefficiencies and damage due to scale formation in water heaters. A common method for water softening involves the use of <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange_resin" title="Ion exchange resin" class="mw-redirect">ion exchange resins</a>, which replace ions like Ca<sup>2+</sup> by twice the number of monocations such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> ions.</p> 
     <p>Washing soda (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> - Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) is easily obtained and has long been used as a water softener for domestic laundry, in conjunction with the usual soap or detergent.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_considerations">Health considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> says that "there does not appear to be any convincing evidence that water hardness causes adverse health effects in humans".<sup id="cite_ref-who_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In fact, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council" class="mw-redirect">United States National Research Council</a> has found that hard water can actually serve as a dietary supplement for calcium and magnesium.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some studies have shown a weak <a href="/wiki/Inverse_relationship" title="Inverse relationship" class="mw-redirect">inverse relationship</a> between water hardness and <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a> in men, up to a level of 170&nbsp;mg calcium carbonate per litre of water. The World Health Organization has reviewed the evidence and concluded the data was inadequate to allow for a recommendation for a level of hardness.<sup id="cite_ref-who_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Recommendations have been made for the maximum and minimum levels of calcium (40–80 <a href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million" class="mw-redirect">ppm</a>) and magnesium (20–30 ppm) in drinking water, and a total hardness expressed as the sum of the calcium and magnesium concentrations of 2–4&nbsp;mmol/L.<sup id="cite_ref-Koz_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koz-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Other studies have shown weak correlations between cardiovascular health and water hardness.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some studies correlate domestic hard water usage with increased <a href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema" class="mw-redirect">eczema</a> in <a href="/wiki/Children" title="Children" class="mw-redirect">children</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Softened-Water_Eczema_Trial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Softened-Water Eczema Trial (page does not exist)">Softened-Water Eczema Trial</a> (SWET), a multicenter randomized controlled trial of ion-exchange softeners for treating childhood <a href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema" class="mw-redirect">eczema</a>, was undertaken in 2008. However, no meaningful difference in symptom relief was found between children with access to a home water softener and those without.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Hardness can be quantified by <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_chemistry" title="Instrumental chemistry">instrumental analysis</a>. The total water hardness is the sum of the <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar concentrations</a> of Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup>, in mol/L or mmol/L units. Although water hardness usually measures only the total concentrations of calcium and magnesium (the two most prevalent <a href="/wiki/Divalent" title="Divalent">divalent</a> metal ions), <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> can also be present at elevated levels in some locations. The presence of iron characteristically confers a brownish (<a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>-like) colour to the calcification, instead of white (the color of most of the other compounds).</p> 
     <p>Water hardness is often not expressed as a molar concentration, but rather in various units, such as degrees of general hardness (<a href="/wiki/DGH" title="DGH">dGH</a>), German degrees (°dH), parts per million (ppm, mg/L, or American degrees), grains per gallon (gpg), English degrees (°e, e, or <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clark_(chemist)" title="Thomas Clark (chemist)">°Clark</a>), or French degrees (°fH, °f or °F; lowercase <i>f</i> is used to prevent confusion with degrees <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>).       <li>A <i>French degree (°fH or °f)</i> is defined as 10&nbsp;mg/L CaCO<sub>3</sub>, equivalent to 10 ppm.</li> 
     </ul>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indices">Indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Several indices are used to describe the behaviour of calcium carbonate in water, oil, or gas mixtures.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Langelier_saturation_index_.28LSI.29">Langelier saturation index (LSI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Langelier saturation index (LSI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

     <p>The Langelier saturation index (sometimes Langelier stability index) is a calculated number used to predict the calcium carbonate stability of water. It indicates whether the water will precipitate, dissolve, or be in equilibrium with calcium carbonate. In 1936, Wilfred Langelier developed a method for predicting the pH at which water is saturated in calcium carbonate (called pHs). The LSI is expressed as the difference between the actual system pH and the saturation pH:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       LSI = pH (measured) − pHs
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <ul> 
      <li>For LSI &gt; 0, water is super saturated and tends to precipitate a scale layer of CaCO<sub>3</sub>.</li> 
      <li>For LSI = 0, water is saturated (in equilibrium) with CaCO<sub>3</sub>. A scale layer of CaCO<sub>3</sub> is neither precipitated nor dissolved.</li> 
      <li>For LSI &lt; 0, water is under saturated and tends to dissolve solid CaCO<sub>3</sub>.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>If the actual pH of the water is below the calculated saturation pH, the LSI is negative and the water has a very limited scaling potential. If the actual pH exceeds pHs, the LSI is positive, and being supersaturated with CaCO<sub>3</sub>, the water has a tendency to form scale. At increasing positive index values, the scaling potential increases.</p> 
     <p>In practice, water with an LSI between -0.5 and +0.5 will not display enhanced mineral dissolving or scale forming properties. Water with an LSI below -0.5 tends to exhibit noticeably increased dissolving abilities while water with an LSI above +0.5 tends to exhibit noticeably increased scale forming properties.</p> 
     <p>The LSI is temperature sensitive. The LSI becomes more positive as the water temperature increases. This has particular implications in situations where well water is used. The temperature of the water when it first exits the well is often significantly lower than the temperature inside the building served by the well or at the laboratory where the LSI measurement is made. This increase in temperature can cause scaling, especially in cases such as hot water heaters. Conversely, systems that reduce water temperature will have less scaling.</p>

     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ryznar_Stability_Index_.28RSI.29">Ryznar Stability Index (RSI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ryznar Stability Index (RSI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The Ryznar stability index (RSI) uses a database of scale thickness measurements in municipal water systems to predict the effect of water chemistry.</p> 
     <p>Ryznar saturation index (RSI) was developed from empirical observations of corrosion rates and film formation in steel mains. It is defined as:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       RSI = 2 pHs – pH (measured)
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <ul> 
      <li>For 6,5 &lt; RSI &lt; 7 water is considered to be approximately at saturation equilibrium with calcium carbonate</li> 
      <li>For RSI &gt; 8 water is under saturated and, therefore, would tend to dissolve any existing solid CaCO3</li> 
      <li>For RSI &lt; 6,5 water tends to be scale forming</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Puckorius_Scaling_Index_.28PSI.29">Puckorius Scaling Index (PSI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Puckorius Scaling Index (PSI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The Puckorius Scaling Index (PSI) uses slightly different parameters to quantify the relationship between the saturation state of the water and the amount of limescale deposited.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_indices">Other indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Other indices include the Larson-Skold Index,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> the Stiff-Davis Index,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and the Oddo-Tomson Index.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_information">Regional information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Regional information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The hardness of local water supplies depends on the source of water. Water in streams flowing over volcanic (igneous) rocks will be soft, while water from boreholes drilled into porous rock is normally very hard.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_water_in_Australia">Hard water in Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hard water in Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Analysis of water hardness in major Australian cities by the Australian Water Association shows a range from very soft (Melbourne) to very hard (Adelaide). Total Hardness levels of calcium carbonate in ppm are: <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>: 40;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>: 10–26;<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>: 39.4–60.1;<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>: 29–226;<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>: 100;<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>: 134–148;<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hobart" title="Hobart">Hobart</a>: 5.8–34.4;<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>: 31.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_water_in_Canada">Hard water in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hard water in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">Prairie</a> provinces (mainly <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>) contain high quantities of calcium and magnesium, often as <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>, which are readily soluble in the groundwater that contains high concentrations of trapped <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the last <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation" class="mw-redirect">glaciation</a>. In these parts of Canada, the total hardness in ppm of calcium carbonate equivalent frequently exceed 200 ppm, if groundwater is the only source of potable water. The west coast, by contrast, has unusually soft water, derived mainly from mountain lakes fed by glaciers and snowmelt.</p> 
     <p>Some typical values are: <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> 116 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a> 165 ppm, <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina</a> 496 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a> 160-180 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a> 77 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> 121 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> &lt; 3 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charlottetown" title="Charlottetown">Charlottetown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">PEI</a> 140–150 ppm,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Regional_Municipality_of_Waterloo" title="Regional Municipality of Waterloo">Waterloo Region</a> 400 ppm, <a href="/wiki/Guelph" title="Guelph">Guelph</a> 460 ppm.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Information from the British Drinking Water Inspectorate<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> shows that drinking water in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> is generally considered to be 'very hard', with most areas of England, particularly east of a line between the Severn and Tees estuaries, exhibiting above 200 ppm for the calcium carbonate equivalent. Water in London, for example, is mostly obtained from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Lea" title="River Lea">River Lea</a> both of which derive significant proportion of their dry weather flow from springs in limestone and chalk aquifers. <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North-West England</a> are softer water areas, and range from 0 to 200 ppm.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a> industry in England and Wales, water is often deliberately hardened with <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> in the process of <a href="/wiki/Burtonisation" title="Burtonisation" class="mw-redirect">Burtonisation</a>.</p> 
     <p>Generally water is mostly hard in urban areas of England where soft water sources are unavailable. A number of cities built water supply sources in the 18th century as the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution" class="mw-redirect">industrial revolution</a> and urban population burgeoned. <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> was a notable such city in North West England and its wealthy corporation built a number of reservoirs at <a href="/wiki/Thirlmere" title="Thirlmere">Thirlmere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haweswater" title="Haweswater" class="mw-redirect">Haweswater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> to the north. There is no exposure to <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Headwaters" title="Headwaters" class="mw-redirect">headwaters</a> and consequently the water quality in Manchester is rated as 'very soft'.<sup id="cite_ref-manchester_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manchester-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Similarly, tap water in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> is also soft as it is sourced from the <a href="/wiki/Elan_Valley_Reservoirs" title="Elan Valley Reservoirs">Elan Valley Reservoirs</a> in Wales.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_water_in_Ireland">Hard water in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hard water in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The EPA has published a standards handbook for the interpretation of water quality in Ireland in which definitions of water hardness are given.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In this section, reference to original EU documentation is given, which sets out no limit for hardness. In turn, the handbook also gives no "Recommended or Mandatory Limit Values" for Hardness. The handbooks does indicate that above the midpoint of the ranges defined as "Moderately Hard", effects are seen increasingly: "The chief disadvantages of hard waters are that they neutralise the lathering power of soap.... and, more important, that they can cause blockage of pipes and severely reduced boiler efficiency because of scale formation. These effects will increase as the hardness rises to and beyond 200 mg/l CaCO3."</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_water_in_the_United_States">Hard water in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hard water in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A collection of data from the United States found that about half the water stations tested had hardness over 120&nbsp;mg per litre of calcium carbonate equivalent, placing them in the categories "hard" or "very hard".<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The other half were classified as soft or moderately hard. More than 85% of American homes have hard water.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The softest waters occur in parts of the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, South Atlantic-Gulf, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> regions. Moderately hard waters are common in many of the rivers of the Tennessee, <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, and Alaska regions. Hard and very hard waters are found in some of the streams in most of the regions throughout the country. The hardest waters (greater than 1,000 ppm) are in streams in Texas, New Mexico, Kansas, Arizona, and southern California.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
