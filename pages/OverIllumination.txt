   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Over-illumination</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">

     <p><b>Over-illumination</b> is the presence of <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">lighting intensity</a> higher than that which is appropriate for a specific activity. Over-illumination was commonly ignored between 1950 and 1995, especially in office and <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retail</a> environments.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Since then, however, the <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">interior design</a> community has begun to reconsider this practice. Over-illumination encompasses two separate concerns:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Unnecessary electric lighting is expensive and energy-intensive. Lighting accounts for approximately 9% of residential electricity use as of 2001<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOE_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and about 40% of commercial electricity use.<sup id="cite_ref-EIA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Over-illumination can be reduced by installing <a href="/wiki/Occupancy_sensor" title="Occupancy sensor">occupancy sensors</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">natural sunlight</a> whenever possible, turning off lights when leaving a room, or changing the type of lightbulb. Over-illumination does not refer to <a href="/wiki/Photokeratitis" title="Photokeratitis">snowblindness</a>, where high exposure to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light" class="mw-redirect">ultraviolet light</a> causes physical damage to the eye. Too little light, the opposite of over-illumination, is associated with <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_affective_disorder" title="Seasonal affective disorder">seasonal affective disorder</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Bigboxrazorback.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bigboxrazorback.jpg/200px-Bigboxrazorback.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bigboxrazorback.jpg/300px-Bigboxrazorback.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bigboxrazorback.jpg/400px-Bigboxrazorback.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112"></a>

     <p>Over-illumination can be caused by several factors:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Illuminating an unoccupied area</li> 
      <li>Using electrical lights instead of natural light</li> 
      <li>Providing lighting for an occupied area, but with too much intensity</li> 
      <li>Installing too few electrical controls. This results in an area that must either be over-illuminated or not illuminated at all</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In addition, there are ancillary reasons why over-illumination might be decided to be necessary. For example, retail stores with large windows will sometimes stay illuminated overnight as a method of crime prevention.</p> 
     <p>While some aspects of illumination are readily controllable, such as turning off lights when exiting a room, others are determined by the architecture and construction of the building. For example, <a href="/wiki/Skylight_(window)" title="Skylight (window)" class="mw-redirect">skylights</a> decrease the amount of artificial lighting that is required during the daytime, but most buildings do not have them installed. In addition, too few light switches can also cause issues. If an office building with large windows only has one switch per floor, then electric lights will illuminate the perimeter areas (with abundant natural light) to same level as the interior zones (which receive less sunlight).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:FluorescentT8razorback.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/FluorescentT8razorback.jpg/210px-FluorescentT8razorback.jpg" width="210" height="90" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/FluorescentT8razorback.jpg/315px-FluorescentT8razorback.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/FluorescentT8razorback.jpg/420px-FluorescentT8razorback.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2352" data-file-height="1008"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:FluorescentT8razorback.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Common T8 
        <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting" class="mw-redirect">fluorescent lighting</a> fixture frequently used in office environments
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Over-illumination has been linked to various negative health effects. While some effects might happen because the <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp#Comparison_with_incandescent_lamps" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">color spectrum of fluorescent lighting</a> is significantly different from <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature#Categorizing_different_lighting" title="Color temperature">sunlight</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lumina_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lumina-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boyce_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boyce-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> other symptoms might be caused by light that is simply too intense. In particular, over-illumination has been linked to <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">fatigue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)" class="mw-redirect">medically defined stress</a>, anxiety, and decreases in <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexual</a> function.<sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burks_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burks-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pijnenburg_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pijnenburg-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Knez_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knez-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some studies attribute migraine headaches to overly intense light,<sup id="cite_ref-Burks_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burks-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> while others linked it with certain spectral distributions.<sup id="cite_ref-Boyce_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boyce-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In one survey bright light was the number two trigger (affecting 47% of respondents) for causing a migraine episode.<sup id="cite_ref-Burks_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burks-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Fatigue is another common complaint from individuals exposed to over-illumination, especially with fluorescent media.<sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Likewise, over-illumination can also cause stress and anxiety. In fact, natural light was preferred over purely artificial light by office workers from both eastern and western cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-Nagy_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nagy-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, over-illumination can cause <a href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)" class="mw-redirect">medical stress</a><sup id="cite_ref-Pijnenburg_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pijnenburg-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Basso_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basso-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and even aggravate other psychological disorders like <a href="/wiki/Agoraphobia" title="Agoraphobia">agoraphobia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The replacement of natural light with artificial light also decreases task performance under certain conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Knez_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knez-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulatory_and_circadian_rhythm_effects">Circulatory and circadian rhythm effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Circulatory and circadian rhythm effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">Hypertension</a> effects of over-illumination can lead to aggravation of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erectile_dysfunction" title="Erectile dysfunction">erectile dysfunction</a>, which impacts are outcomes of long term cumulative exposure and associated systematic increases in blood pressure. The mechanism of this effect seems to be stress by related <a href="/wiki/Upregulation" title="Upregulation" class="mw-redirect">upregulation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">adrenaline</a> production akin to the <a href="/wiki/Fight-or-flight_response" title="Fight-or-flight response">fight-or-flight response</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">Circadian rhythm</a> disruption is primarily caused by the wrong timing of light in reference to the circadian phase. It can also be affected by too much light, too little light, or incorrect spectral composition of light. This effect is driven by stimulus (or lack of stimulus) to photosensitive ganglion cells in the retina. The "time of day", the circadian phase, is signalled to the <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a>, the body’s <a href="/wiki/Photometer" title="Photometer">photometer</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Suprachiasmatic_nucleus" title="Suprachiasmatic nucleus">suprachiasmatic nucleus</a>. Bright light in the evening or in the early morning shifts the phase of the production of <a href="/wiki/Melatonin" title="Melatonin">melatonin</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Phase_response_curve" title="Phase response curve">phase response curve</a>). An out-of-sync melatonin rhythm can worsen <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrhythmia" title="Cardiac arrhythmia">cardiac arrhythmias</a> and increase <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidized</a> <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. Melatonin also reduces <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> production and myeloperoxide (an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> in neutrophils which produces <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">hypochlorous acid</a>) during ischemia-reperfusion.<sup id="cite_ref-Boyce_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boyce-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reiter_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reiter-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_and_economic_considerations">Energy and economic considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy and economic considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Using too much lighting will lead to higher electricity consumption, and therefore higher electricity costs, for the owner of the building. The solution to this issue is multi-faceted because there are several causes of too much light.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-intensity_light">High-intensity light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: High-intensity light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>First, over-illumination happens when the intensity of lighting is too high for a given activity. For example, an office building might have many sets of fluorescent lights to keep the area illuminated after sundown. During the daytime, however, large windows might allow for abundant amounts of natural sunlight to enter the office. Therefore, keeping all of the fluorescent lights illuminated during the daytime might cause unnecessary costs and energy consumption.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaving_lights_turned_on">Leaving lights turned on</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leaving lights turned on">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Likewise, not switching off the lights when exiting a room will also cause higher energy consumption. Some people avoid frequently turning off CFL bulbs because they think that doing so will cause them to burn out prematurely. While this is true to a certain extent,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> the US Department of Energy recommends that 15 minutes is an appropriate time frame. If someone plans to leave the room for less than 15 minutes, then the light should be left on. If the room will be unoccupied for more than 15 minutes, then the light should be switched off.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.gov_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.gov-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Another concern is that turning on a fluorescent bulb consumes large amounts of energy. While fluorescent bulbs do need more energy turn on, the amount of electricity consumed is equal to only a few seconds of normal operation.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.gov_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.gov-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Sometimes people do not turn off lights for other reasons, such as an office worker whose company actually pays for the electricity. In these cases <a href="/wiki/Building_automation" title="Building automation">building automation</a> provides more control. These solutions provide centralized control of all lighting within a home or commercial building, allowing easy implementation of scheduling, <a href="/wiki/Occupancy_sensor" title="Occupancy sensor">occupancy control</a>, daylight harvesting and more. Many systems also support <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">demand response</a> and will automatically dim or turn off lights to take advantage of DR incentives and cost savings. Many newer control systems are using wireless mesh open standards (such as <a href="/wiki/ZigBee" title="ZigBee">ZigBee</a>), which provides benefits including easier installation (no need to run control wires) and interoperability with other standards-based building control systems (e.g., security).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_design_and_the_type_of_light_bulbs">Architectural design and the type of light bulbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Architectural design and the type of light bulbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Architectural design can also provide ways of reducing energy usage. There are technological aspects of <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">window</a> design where window angles can be calculated to minimize interior glare and reduce interior over-illumination, while at the same time reducing solar heat loading and subsequent demand for air conditioning as energy conservation techniques. For the <a href="/wiki/Dakin_Building" title="Dakin Building">Dakin Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brisbane,_California" title="Brisbane, California">Brisbane, California</a> the angled window projections effectively provide permanent sunscreens, obviating interior blinds or shades.</p> 
     <p>Ideally, the design of a building would create multiple switches for the overhead lighting. Adjusting these settings would allow the optimal light intensity to be delivered,<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> the most common version of this control being the "three-level switch", also called A/B switching. Much of the benefit of the excess illumination reduction comes from a better ratio of natural light to fluorescent light that can result from any of the above changes. Research has been conducted showing worker productivity gains in settings where each worker selects his or her own lighting level.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Lastly, the type of light bulbs that are installed has a significant effect on energy consumption. The efficiency of light sources vary greatly. Fluorescent lights produce several times as much light, for given power input, as incandescent lights do, and LEDs are continuing to improve beyond that. Shades vary in their absorption. Light colored ceilings, walls and other surfaces increase ambient light by reflecting.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_phenomena">Related phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over-illumination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Related phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Over-illumination can also contribute to <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> if <a href="/wiki/Stray_light" title="Stray light">stray light</a> can be seen a neighboring property or building. Over-illumination is a topic normally addressed in the process of building design, whereas light pollution is normally addressed by zoning regulations.</p>
