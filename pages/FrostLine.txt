   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Frost line</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      For other uses, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(disambiguation)" title="Frost line (disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig">Frost line (disambiguation)</a>.
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Not to be confused with the geographical feature 
      <a href="/wiki/Snow_line" title="Snow line">Snow line</a>.
     </div> 
     <p>The <b>frost line</b>—also known as <b>frost depth</b> or <b>freezing depth</b>—is most commonly the depth to which the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> is expected to freeze. The frost depth depends on the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic conditions</a> of an area, the <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a> properties of the soil and adjacent materials, and on nearby heat sources. For example, <a href="/wiki/Snow_cover" title="Snow cover" class="mw-redirect">snow cover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulate</a> the ground and homes can heat the ground (see also <i><a href="/wiki/Heat_island" title="Heat island" class="mw-redirect">heat island</a></i>). The line varies by latitude, it is deeper closer to the poles. It ranges in the United States from about zero to six feet. Below that depth, the temperature varies, but is always above 0&nbsp;°C (32&nbsp;°F).</p> 
     <p>Alternatively, in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> locations the freezing depth is so deep that it becomes year-round <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, and the term "<a href="/wiki/Thaw_depth" title="Thaw depth">thaw depth</a>" is used instead. Finally, in tropical regions, frost line may refer to the vertical geographic <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> below which <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a> does not occur.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><i>Frost front</i> refers to the varying position of the frost line during seasonal periods of freezing and thawing.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_codes">Building codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frost_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Building codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">Building codes</a> sometimes take frost depth into account because of <a href="/wiki/Frost_heaving" title="Frost heaving">frost heaving</a> which can damage buildings by moving their foundations. Foundations are normally built below the frost depth for this reason. Water pipes are normally buried below the frost line, or insulated to prevent them from freezing.</p> 
     <p>There are many ways to predict frost depth including factors which relate air temperature to soil temperature.</p>
