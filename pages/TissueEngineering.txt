   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tissue engineering</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p><b>Tissue engineering</b> is the use of a combination of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials</a> methods, and suitable <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> and physicochemical factors to improve or replace <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> functions. Tissue engineering involves the use of a scaffold for the formation of new viable tissue for a medical purpose. While it was once categorized as a sub-field of <a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biomaterials</a>, having grown in scope and importance it can be considered as a field in its own right.</p> 
     <p>While most definitions of tissue engineering cover a broad range of applications, in practice the term is closely associated with applications that repair or replace portions of or whole tissues (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autologous_chondrocyte_implantation" title="Autologous chondrocyte implantation">cartilage</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels" class="mw-redirect">blood vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urinary_bladder" title="Urinary bladder">bladder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> etc.). Often, the tissues involved require certain mechanical and structural properties for proper functioning. The term has also been applied to efforts to perform specific biochemical functions using <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> within an artificially-created support system (e.g. an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_pancreas" title="Artificial pancreas">artificial pancreas</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Bioartificial_liver_device" title="Bioartificial liver device">bio artificial liver</a>). The term <i><a href="/wiki/Stem_cell_treatments" title="Stem cell treatments" class="mw-redirect">regenerative medicine</a></i> is often used synonymously with tissue engineering, although those involved in <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_medicine" title="Regenerative medicine">regenerative medicine</a> place more emphasis on the use of <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Progenitor_cells" title="Progenitor cells" class="mw-redirect">progenitor cells</a> to produce tissues.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>A commonly applied definition of tissue engineering, as stated by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langer" title="Robert Langer" class="mw-redirect">Langer</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Vacanti,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is "an <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect">interdisciplinary</a> field that applies the principles of engineering and life sciences toward the development of biological substitutes that restore, maintain, or improve [Biological tissue] function or a whole organ".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Tissue engineering has also been defined as "understanding the principles of tissue growth, and applying this to produce functional replacement tissue for clinical use."<sup id="cite_ref-definition_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition-5">[5]</a></sup> A further description goes on to say that an "underlying supposition of tissue engineering is that the employment of natural biology of the system will allow for greater success in developing therapeutic strategies aimed at the replacement, repair, maintenance, and/or enhancement of tissue function."<sup id="cite_ref-definition_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Powerful developments in the multidisciplinary field of tissue engineering have yielded a novel set of tissue replacement parts and implementation strategies. Scientific advances in <a href="/wiki/Biomaterials" title="Biomaterials" class="mw-redirect">biomaterials</a>, stem cells, <a href="/wiki/Growth_factor" title="Growth factor">growth</a> and differentiation factors, and <a href="/wiki/Biomimetic" title="Biomimetic" class="mw-redirect">biomimetic</a> environments have created unique opportunities to fabricate tissues in the laboratory from combinations of engineered extracellular matrices ("scaffolds"), cells, and biologically active molecules. Among the major challenges now facing tissue engineering is the need for more complex functionality, as well as both functional and biomechanical stability in laboratory-grown tissues destined for transplantation. The continued success of tissue engineering, and the eventual development of true human replacement parts, will grow from the convergence of engineering and basic research advances in tissue, matrix, growth factor, stem cell, and developmental biology, as well as materials science and bio informatics.</p> 
     <p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">NSF</a> published a report entitled "The Emergence of Tissue Engineering as a Research Field", which gives a thorough description of the history of this field.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cells_as_building_blocks">Cells as building blocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cells as building blocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Epithelial-cells.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/Epithelial-cells.jpg" width="202" height="202" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="202" data-file-height="202"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Epithelial-cells.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Stained cells in culture
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Tissue engineering utilizes living cells as engineering materials. Examples include using living <a href="/wiki/Fibroblast" title="Fibroblast">fibroblasts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> replacement or repair, <a href="/wiki/Autologous_chondrocyte_implantation" title="Autologous chondrocyte implantation">cartilage</a> repaired with living <a href="/wiki/Chondrocyte" title="Chondrocyte">chondrocytes</a>, or other types of cells used in other ways.</p> 
     <p>Cells became available as engineering materials when scientists at Geron Corp. discovered how to extend <a href="/wiki/Telomere" title="Telomere">telomeres</a> in 1998, producing <a href="/wiki/Biological_immortality#Cell_lines" title="Biological immortality">immortalized cell lines</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Before this, laboratory cultures of healthy, noncancerous mammalian cells would only divide a fixed number of times, up to the <a href="/wiki/Hayflick_limit" title="Hayflick limit">Hayflick limit</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction">Extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>From fluid tissues such as <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>, cells are extracted by bulk methods, usually <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apheresis" title="Apheresis">apheresis</a>. From solid tissues, extraction is more difficult. Usually the tissue is minced, and then digested with the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Collagenase" title="Collagenase">collagenase</a> to remove the <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> (ECM) that holds the cells. After that, the cells are free floating, and extracted using centrifugation or apheresis.<br> Digestion with trypsin is very dependent on temperature. Higher temperatures digest the matrix faster, but create more damage. Collagenase is less temperature dependent, and damages fewer cells, but takes longer and is a more expensive reagent.</p>

         <a href="/wiki/File:Mouse_embryonic_stem_cells.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Mus_musculus" title="Mus musculus" class="mw-redirect">Mouse</a> 
        <a href="/wiki/Mammalian_embryogenesis" title="Mammalian embryogenesis" class="mw-redirect">embryonic</a> stem cells
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Cells are often categorized by their source:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><i><a href="/wiki/Autologous" title="Autologous" class="mw-redirect">Autologous</a></i> cells are obtained from the same individual to which they will be reimplanted. Autologous cells have the fewest problems with rejection and pathogen transmission, however in some cases might not be available. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorders" title="Genetic disorders" class="mw-redirect">genetic disease</a> suitable autologous cells are not available. Also very ill or elderly persons, as well as patients suffering from severe burns, may not have sufficient quantities of autologous cells to establish useful cell lines. Moreover, since this category of cells needs to be harvested from the patient, there are also some concerns related to the necessity of performing such surgical operations that might lead to donor site infection or chronic pain. Autologous cells also must be cultured from samples before they can be used: this takes time, so autologous solutions may not be very quick. Recently there has been a trend towards the use of <a href="/wiki/Mesenchymal_stem_cell" title="Mesenchymal stem cell">mesenchymal stem cells</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>. These cells can differentiate into a variety of tissue types, including <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerve</a>. A large number of cells can be easily and quickly isolated from fat, thus opening the potential for large numbers of cells to be quickly and easily obtained.</li> 
      <li><i>Allogeneic</i> cells come from the body of a donor of the same species. While there are some ethical constraints to the use of human cells for <i>in vitro</i> studies, the employment of dermal <a href="/wiki/Fibroblast" title="Fibroblast">fibroblasts</a> from human foreskin has been demonstrated to be immunologically safe and thus a viable choice for tissue engineering of skin.</li> 
      <li><i>Xenogenic</i> cells are these isolated from individuals of another species. In particular animal cells have been used quite extensively in experiments aimed at the construction of cardiovascular implants.</li> 
      <li><i>Syngenic</i> or <i>isogenic</i> cells are isolated from genetically identical organisms, such as twins, clones, or highly inbred research animal models.</li> 
      <li><i>Primary</i> cells are from an organism.</li> 
      <li><i>Secondary</i> cells are from a cell bank.</li> 
      <li><i><a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">Stem cells</a></i> are undifferentiated cells with the ability to divide in culture and give rise to different forms of specialized cells. According to their source stem cells are divided into "adult" and "embryonic" stem cells, the first class being <a href="/wiki/Multipotency" title="Multipotency" class="mw-redirect">multipotent</a> and the latter mostly <a href="/wiki/Pluripotency" title="Pluripotency" class="mw-redirect">pluripotent</a>; some cells are <a href="/wiki/Totipotency" title="Totipotency" class="mw-redirect">totipotent</a>, in the earliest stages of the embryo. While there is still a large ethical debate related with the use of embryonic stem cells, it is thought that another alternative source - <a href="/wiki/Induced_stem_cells" title="Induced stem cells">induced stem cells</a> may be useful for the repair of diseased or damaged tissues, or may be used to grow new organs.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scaffolds">Scaffolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scaffolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Scaffolds are materials that have been engineered to cause desirable cellular interactions to contribute to the formation of new functional tissues for medical purposes. Cells are often 'seeded' into these structures capable of supporting <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">three-dimensional</a> tissue formation. Scaffolds mimic the native extracellular matrix of the native tissue, recapitulating the <i>in vivo</i> milieu and allowing cells to influence their own microenvironments. They usually serve for at least one of the following purposes:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Allow cell attachment and migration</li> 
      <li>Deliver and retain cells and biochemical factors</li> 
      <li>Enable diffusion of vital cell nutrients and expressed products</li> 
      <li>Exert certain mechanical and biological influences to modify the behaviour of the cell phase</li> 
     </ul> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Kohlenstoffnanoroehre_Animation.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Kohlenstoffnanoroehre_Animation.gif" width="240" height="240" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="240"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Kohlenstoffnanoroehre_Animation.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> This animation of a rotating 
        <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotube</a> shows its 3D structure. Carbon nanotubes are among the numerous candidates for tissue engineering scaffolds since they are 
        <a href="/wiki/Biocompatible" title="Biocompatible" class="mw-redirect">biocompatible</a>, resistant to 
        <a href="/wiki/Biodegrade" title="Biodegrade" class="mw-redirect">biodegradation</a> and can be functionalized with 
        <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a>. However, the possibility of toxicity with non-biodegradable nano-materials is not fully understood.
        <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>To achieve the goal of tissue reconstruction, scaffolds must meet some specific requirements. A high porosity and an adequate pore size are necessary to facilitate cell seeding and diffusion throughout the whole structure of both cells and nutrients. <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">Biodegradability</a> is often an essential factor since scaffolds should preferably be absorbed by the surrounding tissues without the necessity of a surgical removal. The rate at which degradation occurs has to coincide as much as possible with the rate of tissue formation: this means that while cells are fabricating their own natural matrix structure around themselves, the scaffold is able to provide structural integrity within the body and eventually it will break down leaving the neotissue, newly formed tissue which will take over the mechanical load. Injectability is also important for clinical uses. Recent research on organ printing is showing how crucial a good control of the 3D environment is to ensure reproducibility of experiments and offer better results.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Many different materials (natural and synthetic, biodegradable and permanent) have been investigated. Most of these materials have been known in the medical field before the advent of tissue engineering as a research topic, being already employed as bioresorbable <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">sutures</a>. Examples of these materials are <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyesters</a>.</p> 
     <p>New biomaterials have been engineered to have ideal properties and functional customization: injectability, synthetic manufacture, <a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">biocompatibility</a>, non-immunogenicity, transparency, nano-scale fibers, low concentration, resorption rates, etc. PuraMatrix, originating from the MIT labs of Zhang, Rich, Grodzinsky and Langer is one of these new biomimetic scaffold families which has now been commercialized and is impacting clinical tissue engineering.</p> 
     <p>A commonly used synthetic material is <a href="/wiki/Polylactide" title="Polylactide" class="mw-redirect">PLA</a> - polylactic acid. This is a polyester which degrades within the human body to form <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, a naturally occurring chemical which is easily removed from the body. Similar materials are <a href="/wiki/Polyglycolide" title="Polyglycolide">polyglycolic acid</a> (PGA) and <a href="/wiki/Polycaprolactone" title="Polycaprolactone">polycaprolactone</a> (PCL): their degradation mechanism is similar to that of PLA, but they exhibit respectively a faster and a slower rate of degradation compared to PLA.</p> 
     <p>Scaffolds may also be constructed from natural materials: in particular different derivatives of the <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> have been studied to evaluate their ability to support cell growth. Proteic materials, such as collagen or <a href="/wiki/Fibrin" title="Fibrin">fibrin</a>, and polysaccharidic materials, like <a href="/wiki/Chitosan" title="Chitosan">chitosan</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Glycosaminoglycan" title="Glycosaminoglycan">glycosaminoglycans</a> (GAGs), have all proved suitable in terms of cell compatibility, but some issues with potential immunogenicity still remains. Among GAGs <a href="/wiki/Hyaluronan" title="Hyaluronan" class="mw-redirect">hyaluronic acid</a>, possibly in combination with cross linking agents (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Glutaraldehyde" title="Glutaraldehyde">glutaraldehyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl(dimethylaminopropyl)_carbodiimide" title="Ethyl(dimethylaminopropyl) carbodiimide" class="mw-redirect">water-soluble carbodiimide</a>, etc.), is one of the possible choices as scaffold material. Functionalized groups of scaffolds may be useful in the delivery of small molecules (drugs) to specific tissues. Another form of scaffold under investigation is decellularised tissue extracts whereby the remaining cellular remnants/extracellular matrices act as the scaffold. Recently a range of nanocomposites <a href="/wiki/Biomaterials" title="Biomaterials" class="mw-redirect">biomaterials</a> are fabricated by incorporating <a href="/wiki/Nanomaterials" title="Nanomaterials">nanomaterials</a> within polymeric matrix to engineer bioactive scaffolds.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A 2009 study by Ratmir et al. aimed to improve in vivo-like conditions for 3D tissue via "stacking and de-stacking layers of paper impregnated with suspensions of cells in <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogel" title="Hydrogel" class="mw-redirect">hydrogel</a>, making it possible to control oxygen and nutrient gradients in 3D, and to analyze molecular and genetic responses".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is possible to manipulate <a href="/wiki/Gradients" title="Gradients" class="mw-redirect">gradients</a> of <a href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble" class="mw-redirect">soluble</a> molecules, and to characterize cells in these complex gradients more effectively than conventional 3D cultures based on hydrogels, cell spheroids, or 3D perfusion reactors.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139-21">[21]</a></sup> Different thicknesses of paper and types of medium can support a variety of experimental environments. Upon deconstruction, these sheets can be useful in cell-based <a href="/wiki/High-throughput_screening" title="High-throughput screening">high-throughput screening</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">drug discovery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>A number of different methods have been described in literature for preparing porous structures to be employed as tissue engineering scaffolds. Each of these techniques presents its own advantages, but none are free of drawbacks.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dt>
       Nanofiber self-assembly
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       Molecular self-assembly is one of the few methods for creating biomaterials with properties similar in scale and chemistry to that of the natural 
       <i>in vivo</i> extracellular matrix (ECM), a crucial step toward tissue engineering of complex tissues.
       <sup id="cite_ref-cassidy_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cassidy-22">[22]</a></sup> Moreover, these hydrogel scaffolds have shown superiority in in vivo toxicology and biocompatibility compared to traditional macroscaffolds and animal-derived materials.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       Textile technologies
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       These techniques include all the approaches that have been successfully employed for the preparation of 
       <a href="/wiki/Non-woven_textiles" title="Non-woven textiles" class="mw-redirect">non-woven meshes</a> of different 
       <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>. In particular, non-woven 
       <a href="/wiki/Polyglycolide" title="Polyglycolide">polyglycolide</a> structures have been tested for tissue engineering applications: such fibrous structures have been found useful to grow different types of cells. The principal drawbacks are related to the difficulties in obtaining high 
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> and regular pore size.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Solvent_casting_and_particulate_leaching" title="Solvent casting and particulate leaching">Solvent casting and particulate leaching</a> (SCPL)
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       This approach allows for the preparation of structures with regular porosity, but with limited thickness. First, the polymer is dissolved into a suitable organic solvent (e.g. 
       <a href="/wiki/Polylactic_acid" title="Polylactic acid">polylactic acid</a> could be dissolved into 
       <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>), then the solution is cast into a mold filled with porogen particles. Such porogen can be an inorganic salt like 
       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, crystals of 
       <a href="/wiki/Saccharose" title="Saccharose" class="mw-redirect">saccharose</a>, 
       <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a> spheres or 
       <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin</a> spheres. The size of the porogen particles will affect the size of the scaffold pores, while the polymer to porogen ratio is directly correlated to the amount of porosity of the final structure. After the polymer solution has been cast the solvent is allowed to fully evaporate, then the composite structure in the mold is immersed in a bath of a liquid suitable for dissolving the porogen: water in the case of sodium chloride, saccharose and gelatin or an 
       <a href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic" class="mw-redirect">aliphatic</a> solvent like 
       <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> for use with paraffin. Once the porogen has been fully dissolved, a porous structure is obtained. Other than the small thickness range that can be obtained, another drawback of SCPL lies in its use of organic solvents which must be fully removed to avoid any possible damage to the cells seeded on the scaffold.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       Gas foaming
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       To overcome the need to use organic solvents and solid porogens, a technique using gas as a porogen has been developed. First, disc-shaped structures made of the desired polymer are prepared by means of compression molding using a heated mold. The discs are then placed in a chamber where they are exposed to high pressure 
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> for several days. The pressure inside the chamber is gradually restored to atmospheric levels. During this procedure the pores are formed by the carbon dioxide molecules that abandon the polymer, resulting in a sponge-like structure. The main problems resulting from such a technique are caused by the excessive heat used during compression molding (which prohibits the incorporation of any temperature labile material into the polymer matrix) and by the fact that the pores do not form an interconnected structure.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       Emulsification/Freeze-drying
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       This technique does not require the use of a solid porogen like SCPL. First, a synthetic polymer is dissolved into a suitable solvent (e.g. polylactic acid in dichloromethane) then water is added to the polymeric solution and the two liquids are mixed in order to obtain an 
       <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a>. Before the two phases can separate, the emulsion is cast into a mold and quickly frozen by means of immersion into 
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a>. The frozen emulsion is subsequently 
       <a href="/wiki/Freeze_drying" title="Freeze drying" class="mw-redirect">freeze-dried</a> to remove the dispersed water and the solvent, thus leaving a solidified, porous polymeric structure. While emulsification and freeze-drying allow for a faster preparation when compared to SCPL (since it does not require a time consuming leaching step), it still requires the use of solvents. Moreover, pore size is relatively small and porosity is often irregular. Freeze-drying by itself is also a commonly employed technique for the fabrication of scaffolds. In particular, it is used to prepare collagen sponges: collagen is dissolved into acidic solutions of 
       <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> or 
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> that are cast into a mold, frozen with liquid nitrogen and then lyophilized.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       Thermally induced phase separation (TIPS)
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       Similar to the previous technique, this phase separation procedure requires the use of a solvent with a low melting point that is easy to sublime. For example, 
       <a href="/wiki/Dioxane" title="Dioxane" class="mw-redirect">dioxane</a> could be used to dissolve polylactic acid, then phase separation is induced through the addition of a small quantity of water: a polymer-rich and a polymer-poor phase are formed. Following cooling below the solvent melting point and some days of vacuum-drying to sublime the solvent, a porous scaffold is obtained. Liquid-liquid phase separation presents the same drawbacks of emulsification/freeze-drying.
       <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Electrospinning" title="Electrospinning">Electrospinning</a>
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       A highly versatile technique that can be used to produce continuous fibers from submicrometer to nanometer diameters. In a typical electrospinning set-up, a solution is fed through a spinneret and a high voltage is applied to the tip. The buildup of electrostatic repulsion within the charged solution, causes it to eject a thin fibrous stream. A mounted collector plate or rod with an opposite or grounded charge draws in the continuous fibers, which arrive to form a highly porous network. The primary advantages of this technique are its simplicity and ease of variation. At a laboratory level, a typical electrospinning set-up only requires a high voltage power supply (up to 30 kV), a syringe, a flat tip needle and a conducting collector. For these reasons, electrospinning has become a common method of scaffold manufacture in many labs. By modifying variables such as the distance to collector, magnitude of applied voltage, or solution flow rateâ€”researchers can dramatically change the overall scaffold architecture.
      </dd> 
      <dt>
       CAD/CAM technologies
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       Because most of the above techniques are limited when it comes to the control of porosity and pore size, 
       <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer assisted design</a> and 
       <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">manufacturing</a> techniques have been introduced to tissue engineering. First, a three-dimensional structure is designed using CAD software. The porosity can be tailored using algorithms within the software.
       <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The scaffold is then realized by using ink-jet printing of polymer powders or through 
       <a href="/wiki/Fused_Deposition_Modeling" title="Fused Deposition Modeling" class="mw-redirect">Fused Deposition Modeling</a> of a polymer melt.
       <sup id="cite_ref-Elisseeff05_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elisseeff05-25">[25]</a></sup>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>A 2011 study by El-Ayoubi et al. investigated "3D-plotting technique to produce (<a href="/wiki/Biocompatible" title="Biocompatible" class="mw-redirect">biocompatible</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable" title="Biodegradable" class="mw-redirect">biodegradable</a>) poly-L-Lactide macroporous scaffolds with two different pore sizes" via solid free-form fabrication (SSF) with computer-aided-design (CAD), to explore therapeutic <a href="/wiki/Articular_cartilage" title="Articular cartilage" class="mw-redirect">articular cartilage</a> replacement as an "alternative to conventional tissue repair".<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup> The study found the smaller the pore size paired with mechanical stress in a bioreactor (to induce in vivo-like conditions), the higher the cell <a href="/w/index.php?title=Viable_cells&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Viable cells (page does not exist)">viability</a> in potential therapeutic functionality via decreasing recovery time and increasing transplant effectiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dt>
       Laser-assisted BioPrinting (LaBP)
      </dt> 
      <dd>
       In a 2012 study,
       <sup id="cite_ref-Lai.2C_Y_2011_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lai.2C_Y_2011-27">[27]</a></sup> Koch et al. focused on whether Laser-assisted BioPrinting (LaBP) can be used to build multicellular 3D patterns in natural matrix, and whether the generated constructs are functioning and forming tissue. LaBP arranges small volumes of living cell suspensions in set high-resolution patterns.
       <sup id="cite_ref-Lai.2C_Y_2011_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lai.2C_Y_2011-27">[27]</a></sup> The investigation was successful, the researchers foresee that "generated tissue constructs might be used for 
       <a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a> testing by implanting them into 
       <a href="/wiki/Animal_models" title="Animal models" class="mw-redirect">animal models</a>" (14). As of this study, only human skin tissue has been synthesized, though researchers project that "by integrating further cell types (e.g. 
       <a href="/wiki/Melanocytes" title="Melanocytes" class="mw-redirect">melanocytes</a>, 
       <a href="/wiki/Schwann_cells" title="Schwann cells" class="mw-redirect">Schwann cells</a>, hair follicle cells) into the printed cell construct, the behavior of these cells in a 3D in vitro 
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Microenvironment_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Microenvironment (biology) (page does not exist)">microenvironment</a> similar to their natural one can be analyzed", useful for drug discovery and 
       <a href="/wiki/Toxicology" title="Toxicology">toxicology</a> studies.
       <sup id="cite_ref-Lai.2C_Y_2011_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lai.2C_Y_2011-27">[27]</a></sup>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assembly_methods">Assembly methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Assembly methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>One of the continuing, persistent problems with tissue engineering is mass transport limitations. Engineered tissues generally lack an initial blood supply, thus making it difficult for any implanted cells to obtain sufficient oxygen and nutrients to survive, and/or function properly.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-assembly">Self-assembly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Self-assembly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Self-assembly may play an important role here, both from the perspective of encapsulating cells and proteins, as well as creating scaffolds on the right physical scale for engineered tissue constructs and cellular ingrowth. The <a href="/wiki/Micromasonry" title="Micromasonry">micromasonry</a> is a prime technology to get cells grown in a lab to assemble into three-dimensional shapes. To break down tissue into single-cell building blocks, researchers have to dissolve the extracellular mortar that normally binds them together. But once that glue is removed, it's quite difficult to get cells to reassemble into the complex structures that make up our natural tissues. While cells aren't easily stackable, building blocks are. So the <a href="/wiki/Micromasonry" title="Micromasonry">micromasonry</a> starts with the encapsulation of living cells in polymer cubes. From there, the blocks self-assemble in any shape using templates.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid-based_template_assembly">Liquid-based template assembly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Liquid-based template assembly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The air-liquid surface established by <a href="/wiki/Faraday_waves" title="Faraday waves" class="mw-redirect">Faraday waves</a> is explored as a template to assemble biological entities for bottom-up tissue engineering. This liquid-based template can be dynamically reconfigured in a few seconds, and the assembly on the template can be achieved in a scalable and parallel manner. Assembly of microscale hydrogels, cells, neuron-seeded micro-carrier beads, cell spheroids into various symmetrical and periodic structures was demonstrated with good cell viability. Formation of 3D neural network was achieved after 14-day tissue culture.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additive_manufacturing">Additive manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Additive manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Organ_printing" title="Organ printing">Organ printing</a>
     </div> 
     <p>It might be possible to print organs, or possibly entire organisms using <a href="/wiki/Additive_manufacturing" title="Additive manufacturing" class="mw-redirect">additive manufacturing</a> techniques. A recent innovative method of construction uses an ink-jet mechanism to print precise layers of cells in a matrix of thermoreversible gel. Endothelial cells, the cells that line blood vessels, have been printed in a set of stacked rings. When incubated, these fused into a tube.<sup id="cite_ref-Elisseeff05_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elisseeff05-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The field of three-dimensional and highly accurate models of biological systems is pioneered by multiple projects and technologies including a rapid method for creating tissues and even whole organs involves a 3D printer that can print the scaffolding and cells layer by layer into a working tissue sample or organ. The device is presented in a <a href="/wiki/TED_talk" title="TED talk" class="mw-redirect">TED talk</a> by Dr. Anthony Atala, M.D. the Director of the <a href="/wiki/Wake_Forest_University" title="Wake Forest University">Wake Forest</a> Institute for <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_Medicine" title="Regenerative Medicine" class="mw-redirect">Regenerative Medicine</a>, and the W.H. Boyce Professor and Chair of the Department of <a href="/wiki/Urology" title="Urology">Urology</a> at Wake Forest University, in which a kidney is printed on stage during the seminar and then presented to the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It is anticipated that this technology will enable the production of livers in the future for transplantation and theoretically for <a href="/wiki/Toxicology" title="Toxicology">toxicology</a> and other biological studies as well.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scaffolding">Scaffolding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scaffolding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In 2013, using a 3-d scaffolding of <a href="/wiki/Matrigel" title="Matrigel">Matrigel</a> in various configurations, substantial pancreatic <a href="/wiki/Organoid" title="Organoid">organoids</a> was produced in vitro. Clusters of small numbers of cells proliferated into 40,000 cells within one week. The clusters transform into cells that make either digestive <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a> like <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>, self-organizing into branched pancreatic organoids that resemble the pancreas.<sup id="cite_ref-kurz1013_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kurz1013-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The cells are sensitive to the environment, such as gel stiffness and contact with other cells. Individual cells do not thrive; a minimum of four proximate cells was required for subsequent organoid development. Modifications to the medium composition produced either hollow spheres mainly composed of pancreatic progenitors, or complex organoids that spontaneously undergo pancreatic morphogenesis and differentiation. Maintenance and expansion of pancreatic progenitors require active <a href="/wiki/Notch_signaling" title="Notch signaling" class="mw-redirect">Notch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fibroblast_growth_factor" title="Fibroblast growth factor">FGF</a> signaling, recapitulating in vivo niche signaling interactions.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The organoids were seen as potentially offering mini-organs for drug testing and for spare insulin-producing cells.<sup id="cite_ref-kurz1013_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kurz1013-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tissue_culture">Tissue culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tissue culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In many cases, creation of functional tissues and biological structures <i>in vitro</i> requires extensive <a href="/wiki/Cell_culture" title="Cell culture">culturing</a> to promote survival, growth and inducement of functionality. In general, the basic requirements of cells must be maintained in culture, which include <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_pressure" title="Osmotic pressure">osmotic pressure</a> maintenance.</p> 
     <p>Tissue engineered cultures also present additional problems in maintaining culture conditions. In standard cell culture, <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> is often the sole means of nutrient and metabolite transport. However, as a culture becomes larger and more complex, such as the case with engineered organs and whole tissues, other mechanisms must be employed to maintain the culture, such as the creation of capillary networks within the tissue.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Bioreaktor.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bioreaktor.JPG/150px-Bioreaktor.JPG" width="150" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bioreaktor.JPG/225px-Bioreaktor.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bioreaktor.JPG/300px-Bioreaktor.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2736" data-file-height="3648"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Bioreaktor.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Bioreactor for cultivation of vascular grafts
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Another issue with tissue culture is introducing the proper factors or stimuli required to induce functionality. In many cases, simple maintenance culture is not sufficient. <a href="/wiki/Growth_factor" title="Growth factor">Growth factors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, specific metabolites or nutrients, chemical and physical stimuli are sometimes required. For example, certain cells respond to changes in oxygen tension as part of their normal development, such as <a href="/wiki/Chondrocyte" title="Chondrocyte">chondrocytes</a>, which must adapt to low oxygen conditions or <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a> during skeletal development. Others, such as endothelial cells, respond to <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear stress</a> from fluid flow, which is encountered in <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels" class="mw-redirect">blood vessels</a>. Mechanical stimuli, such as pressure pulses seem to be beneficial to all kind of cardiovascular tissue such as heart valves, blood vessels or pericardium.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioreactors">Bioreactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bioreactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Bioreactor" title="Bioreactor">Bioreactor</a>
     </div> 
     <p>A bioreactor in tissue engineering, as opposed to industrial bioreactors, is a device that attempts to simulate a physiological environment in order to promote cell or tissue growth in vivo. A physiological environment can consist of many different parameters such as temperature and oxygen or carbon dioxide concentration, but can extend to all kinds of biological, chemical or mechanical stimuli. Therefore, there are systems that may include the application of forces or stresses to the tissue or even of electric current in two- or three-dimensional setups.</p> 
     <p>In academic and industry research facilities, it is typical for bioreactors to be developed to replicate the specific physiological environment of the tissue being grown (e.g., flex and fluid shearing for heart tissue growth).<sup id="cite_ref-CM_and_PNIPAAm_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CM_and_PNIPAAm-34">[34]</a></sup> Several general-use and application-specific bioreactors are also commercially available, and may provide static chemical stimulation or combination of chemical and mechanical stimulation.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Bioreactors" title="Bioreactors" class="mw-redirect">Bioreactors</a> used for 3D cell cultures are small plastic cylindrical chambers with regulated internal humidity and moisture specifically engineered for the purpose of growing cells in three dimensions.<sup id="cite_ref-mc2biotek.com_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc2biotek.com-35">[35]</a></sup> The bioreactor uses <a href="/wiki/Bioactive" title="Bioactive" class="mw-redirect">bioactive</a> synthetic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> membranes to surround the spheroid cells in an environment that maintains high levels of nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich.2C_GD_2008_pp._139-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> They are easy to open and close, so that cell spheroids can be removed for testing, yet the chamber is able to maintain 100% humidity throughout.<sup id="cite_ref-Marx.2C_Vivien_2013_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marx.2C_Vivien_2013-37">[37]</a></sup> This humidity is important to achieve maximum cell growth and function. The bioreactor chamber is part of a larger device that rotates to ensure equal cell growth in each direction across three dimensions.<sup id="cite_ref-Marx.2C_Vivien_2013_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marx.2C_Vivien_2013-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/MC2_Biotek" title="MC2 Biotek">MC2 Biotek</a> has developed a bioreactor known as ProtoTissue<sup id="cite_ref-mc2biotek.com_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc2biotek.com-35">[35]</a></sup> that uses <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">gas exchange</a> to maintain high oxygen levels within the cell chamber; improving upon previous bioreactors, because the higher oxygen levels help the cell grow and undergo normal <a href="/wiki/Cell_respiration" title="Cell respiration" class="mw-redirect">cell respiration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_fiber_generation">Long fiber generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tissue_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Long fiber generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In 2013, a group from the University of Tokyo developed cell laden fibers up to a meter in length and on the order of 100&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Îœm" class="mw-redirect">Âµm</a> in size.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> These fibers were created using a <a href="/wiki/Microfluidic_device" title="Microfluidic device" class="mw-redirect">microfluidic device</a> that forms a double coaxial laminar flow. Each 'layer' of the microfluidic device (cells seeded in <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">ECM</a>, a hydrogel sheath, and finally a calcium chloride solution). The seeded cells culture within the hydrogel sheath for several days, and then the sheath is removed with viable cell fibers. Various cell types were inserted into the ECM core, including <a href="/wiki/Myocytes" title="Myocytes" class="mw-redirect">myocytes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endothelial_cells" title="Endothelial cells" class="mw-redirect">endothelial cells</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nerve_cell_fibers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nerve cell fibers (page does not exist)">nerve cell fibers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Epithelial_cell" title="Epithelial cell" class="mw-redirect">epithelial cell</a> fibers. This group then showed that these fibers can be woven together to fabricate tissues or organs in a mechanism similar to textile <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a>. Fibrous morphologies are advantageous in that they provide an alternative to traditional scaffold design, and many organs (such as muscle) are composed of fibrous cells.</p>
