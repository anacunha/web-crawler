     <p><b>Slinky</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Toy" title="Toy">toy</a>; a precompressed <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">helical</a> <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">spring</a> invented by <a href="/wiki/Richard_T._James" title="Richard T. James">Richard James</a> in the early 1940s. It can perform a number of tricks, including travelling down a flight of steps end-over-end as it stretches and re-forms itself with the aid of gravity and its own momentum, or appear to levitate for a period of time after it has been dropped. These interesting characteristics have contributed to its success as a toy in its home country of <a href="/wiki/USA" title="USA" class="mw-redirect">USA</a>, resulting in many popular toys with slinky components in a wide range of countries.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The toy was invented and developed by naval engineer <a href="/wiki/Richard_T._James" title="Richard T. James">Richard T. James</a> in 1943 and demonstrated at <a href="/wiki/Gimbels" title="Gimbels">Gimbels</a> department store in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> in November 1945. The toy was a hit, selling its entire inventory of 400 units in ninety minutes. James and his wife <a href="/wiki/Betty_James" title="Betty James">Betty</a> formed James Industries in <a href="/wiki/Clifton_Heights,_Pennsylvania" title="Clifton Heights, Pennsylvania">Clifton Heights, Pennsylvania</a> to manufacture Slinky and several related toys such as the Slinky Dog and Suzie, the Slinky Worm. In 1960, James' wife Betty became president of James Industries, and, in 1964, moved the operation to <a href="/wiki/Hollidaysburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Hollidaysburg, Pennsylvania">Hollidaysburg, Pennsylvania</a>. In 1998, Betty James sold the company to Poof Products, Inc..</p> 
     <p>Slinky was originally priced at $1, but many paid much more due to price increases of spring steel throughout the state of Pennsylvania, and has remained modestly priced throughout its history as a result of Betty James' concern about the toy's affordability for financially disadvantaged customers. Slinky has seen uses other than as a toy in the playroom: it has appeared in the classroom as a teaching tool, in wartime as a radio antenna, and in physics experiments with NASA. Slinky was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/National_Toy_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Toy Hall of Fame">National Toy Hall of Fame</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_Strong" title="The Strong">The Strong</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, in 2000. In 2002, Slinky became Pennsylvania's official state toy, and, in 2003, was named to the <a href="/wiki/Toy_Industry_Association" title="Toy Industry Association">Toy Industry Association</a>'s "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://web.archive.org/web/20030401214230/http://www.toy-tia.org/industry/news/press/tf03_centuryoftoys.html">Century of Toys List</a>." In its first 60 years Slinky has sold 300 million units.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Slinky_ad_1946.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2b/Slinky_ad_1946.jpg/220px-Slinky_ad_1946.jpg" width="220" height="318" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2b/Slinky_ad_1946.jpg/330px-Slinky_ad_1946.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2b/Slinky_ad_1946.jpg/440px-Slinky_ad_1946.jpg 2x" data-file-width="519" data-file-height="750"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Slinky_ad_1946.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Slinky (1946)
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In 1943, <a href="/wiki/Richard_T._James" title="Richard T. James">Richard James</a>, a naval mechanical engineer stationed at the <a href="/wiki/William_Cramp_and_Sons" title="William Cramp and Sons" class="mw-redirect">William Cramp and Sons</a> shipyards in Philadelphia, was developing springs that could support and stabilize sensitive instruments aboard ships in rough seas.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> James accidentally knocked one of the springs from a shelf, and watched as the spring "stepped" in a series of arcs to a stack of books, to a tabletop, and to the floor, where it re-coiled itself and stood upright.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> James' wife Betty later recalled, "He came home and said, 'I think if I got the right property of steel and the right tension; I could make it walk.'"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> James experimented with different types of steel wire over the next year, and finally found a spring that would walk. Betty was dubious at first, but changed her mind after the toy was fine-tuned and neighborhood children expressed an excited interest in it.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> She dubbed the toy Slinky (meaning "sleek and graceful"), after finding the word in a dictionary,<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> and deciding that the word aptly described the sound of a metal spring expanding and collapsing.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes3_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes3-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>With a <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>500 loan, the couple formed James Industries (originally James Spring &amp; Wire Company), had 400 Slinky units made by a local machine shop, hand-wrapped each in yellow paper, and priced them at $1 a piece.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> Each was 2<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>" tall, and included 98 coils of high-grade blue-black Swedish steel.<sup id="cite_ref-Rich_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-7">[7]</a></sup> The Jameses had difficulty selling Slinky to toy stores but, in November 1945, they were granted permission to set up an <a href="/wiki/Inclined_plane" title="Inclined plane">inclined plane</a> in the toy section of <a href="/wiki/Gimbels" title="Gimbels">Gimbels</a> department store in Philadelphia to demonstrate the toy. Slinky was a hit, and the first 400 units were sold within ninety minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rich_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1946, Slinky was introduced at the <a href="/wiki/American_International_Toy_Fair" title="American International Toy Fair">American Toy Fair</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_developments">Subsequent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Richard James opened shop in Albany, New York after developing a machine that could produce a Slinky within seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rich_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-7">[7]</a></sup> The toy was packaged in a black-lettered box, and advertising saturated America. James often appeared on television shows to promote Slinky. In 1952, the Slinky Dog debuted. Other Slinky toys introduced in the 1950s included the Slinky train Loco, the Slinky worm Suzie, and the Slinky Crazy Eyes, a pair of glasses that uses Slinkys over the eyeholes attached to plastic eyeballs. James Industries licensed the patent to several other manufacturers including Wilkening Mfg. Co. of Philadelphia and Toronto which produced spring-centered toys such as Mr. Wiggle's Leap Frog and Mr. Wiggle's Cowboy.<sup id="cite_ref-Rich_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-7">[7]</a></sup> In its first 2 years, James Industries sold 100 million Slinkys (At $1 apiece, that would be the equivalent to $1 Billion, adjusted for inflation, in gross revenue over those 2 years).<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1960, Richard James left the company after his wife filed for divorce and he became an evangelical missionary in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> with <a href="/wiki/Wycliffe_Bible_Translators" title="Wycliffe Bible Translators" class="mw-redirect">Wycliffe Bible Translators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Betty James managed the company, juggled creditors, and in 1964 moved the company to <a href="/wiki/Hollidaysburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Hollidaysburg, Pennsylvania">Hollidaysburg, Pennsylvania</a>. Richard James died in 1974. The company and its product line expanded under Betty James' leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1995, she explained the toy's success to the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> by saying, "It's the simplicity of it."<sup id="cite_ref-Betty_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Betty-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Betty James insisted upon keeping the original Slinky affordable. In 1996, when the price ranged from $1.89 to $2.69, she told <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>: “So many children can't have expensive toys, and I feel a real obligation to them. I'm appalled when I go Christmas shopping and $60 to $80 for a toy is nothing." In 2008, Slinkys cost $4 to $5, and Slinky Dogs about $20.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1998, James Industries was sold to Poof Products, Inc. of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Michigan" title="Plymouth, Michigan">Plymouth, Michigan</a>, a manufacturer of foam sports balls.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-10">[10]</a></sup> Slinky continued production in Hollidaysburg.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003, James Industries merged with Poof Products, Inc. to create Poof-Slinky, Inc.</p> 
     <p>Betty James died of <a href="/wiki/Congestive_heart_failure" title="Congestive heart failure" class="mw-redirect">congestive heart failure</a> in November 2008, aged 90, after having served as president of James Industries from 1960 to 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-10">[10]</a></sup> Over 300 million Slinkys have been sold between 1945 and 2005, and the original Slinky is still a bestseller.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rich_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The rules that govern the mechanics of a slinky are due to <a href="/wiki/Hooke%27s_law" title="Hooke's law">Hooke's law</a> and the effects of <a href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation" class="mw-redirect">gravitation</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Period_of_oscillation">Period of oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Period of oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Due to <a href="/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion" title="Simple harmonic motion">simple harmonic motion</a> the period of oscillation of a dangling slinky is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/e/e/fee6809cefc85d3f7924bf6a7d0a8d94.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Where T is the time of the period of oscillation, m is the mass of the slinky and k is the <a href="/wiki/Spring_constant" title="Spring constant" class="mw-redirect">spring constant</a> of the slinky.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equilibrium">Equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In the state of equilibrium of a slinky, all net force is cancelled throughout the entire slinky. This results in a stationary slinky with zero velocity. As the positions of each part of the slinky is governed by the slinky's mass, the force of gravity and the spring constant, various other properties of the slinky may be induced. The length of a perfect slinky with zero length when extended is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="L = \frac{W}{2k}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/e/f/1ef5ba940bbf4c4b90ae7d23d855320e.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Where L is the length of the slinky, W is the weight of the slinky, and k is the spring constant of the slinky.</p> 
     <p>Due to the forces of gravity, the slinky appears bunched up towards the bottom end, as the function of the position of a slinky is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="p(n) = L(n-1)^2." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/e/c6e296908897812d40d6474929c91fe0.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Where n is the dimensionless variable ranging from 0 to 1, 0 being the top and 1 being the bottom of the slinky, and each number between is the percentage of the slinky's mass above point n, and p(n) gives the position that n is above the bottom of the slinky.</p> 
     <p>This quadratic equation means that rather than the center of mass being at the middle of the slinky, it lies one quarter of the length above the bottom end,</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="p(0.5) = L(0.5-1)^2=0.25*L." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/e/4/fe41292b0b713ffed589fe0a7c8b9291.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomenon">Phenomenon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phenomenon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_of_stairs">Flight of stairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flight of stairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>When set in motion on a stepped platform such as a stairway, the slinky transfers energy along its length in a longitudinal wave. The whole spring descends end over end in a periodical motion, as if it were somersaulting down one step at a time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_strength">Changing strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Changing strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>When any slinky is shortened, by means of cutting the slinky (or simply by holding a large portion of the top), the result is a stronger spring strength. Through observation of the length and position equations this becomes apparent because by removing three quarters the length, only half the mass is removed. The result is a spring constant twice as high as the original strength, and the effects can be easily observed through the effects on the period of oscillation.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Levitation">Levitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Levitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>When the top end of the Slinky is dropped, the information of the tension change must propagate to the bottom end before both sides begin to fall; the top of an extended Slinky will drop while the bottom initially remains in its original position, compressing the spring.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This creates a suspension time of ~0.3 s for an original Slinky,<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv.free_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv.free-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Journal.abstract_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Journal.abstract-14">[14]</a></sup> but has potential to create a much larger suspension time.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_history">Commercial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commercial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jingle">Jingle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jingle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The famous jingle for the SLINKY television commercial was created in Columbia, South Carolina in 1962 with Johnny McCullough and Homer Fesperman writing the music and Charles Weagly penning the lyrics. It became the longest-running jingle in advertising history.<sup id="cite_ref-Walsh_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The jingle has itself been parodied and referenced in popular culture. It is seen in the "Log" commercial on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ren_%26_Stimpy_Show" title="The Ren &amp; Stimpy Show">The Ren &amp; Stimpy Show</a></i> and sung by actor <a href="/wiki/Jim_Carrey" title="Jim Carrey">Jim Carrey</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Ace_Ventura:_When_Nature_Calls" title="Ace Ventura: When Nature Calls">Ace Ventura: When Nature Calls</a></i>. The song is also referenced in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Dogtown" title="Lords of Dogtown">Lords of Dogtown</a></i>, where it is sung in full by <a href="/wiki/Emile_Hirsch" title="Emile Hirsch">Emile Hirsch</a>, and is sung by <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy" title="Eddie Murphy">Eddie Murphy</a> as part of the final routine in the stand-up comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy_Raw" title="Eddie Murphy Raw">Eddie Murphy Raw</a></i>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slinky_Dog">Slinky Dog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Slinky Dog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Early in the history of James Industries, Helen (Herrick) Malsed of the state of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)" class="mw-redirect">Washington</a> sent the company a letter and drawings for developing Slinky pull-toys. The company liked her ideas, and Slinky Dog and Slinky Train were added to the company's product line. Slinky Dog, a small plastic dog whose front and rear ends were joined by a metal Slinky, debuted in 1952. Malsed received royalties of $60,000 to $70,000 annually for 17 years on her patent for the Slinky pull-toy idea, but never visited the plant.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes2-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1995, the Slinky Dog was redesigned for all of <a href="/wiki/Pixar" title="Pixar">Pixar</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_(franchise)" title="Toy Story (franchise)">Toy Story</a></i>. James Industries had discontinued their Slinky Dog a few years previously. Betty James approved of the new Slinky Dog, telling the press, "[The earlier Slinky Dog] wasn't nearly as cute as this one." The molds used in manufacturing the new toy created problems for James Industries so the plastic front and rear ends were manufactured in China with James Industries doing the assembly and packaging. The entire run of 825,000 redesigned Slinky Dogs sold out well before Christmas 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes1-16">[16]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_Slinky">Plastic Slinky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plastic Slinky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Slinky_rainbow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Slinky_rainbow.jpg/220px-Slinky_rainbow.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Slinky_rainbow.jpg/330px-Slinky_rainbow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Slinky_rainbow.jpg/440px-Slinky_rainbow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067"></a>

        </div> Rainbow colored plastic Slinky toy
       </div>

     <p>Plastic Slinkys are also available. They can be made in different colors. Many of them are made with the colors of the rainbow in rainbow order. They were marketed in the 1970s as a safer alternative to metal slinkys as they did not present a hazard when inserted into electrical sockets. The plastic spring toy, known as the Plastic Slinky was invented by Donald James Reum, Sr. of Master Mark Plastics in Albany, Minnesota. Mr. Reum came up with the idea as he was playing with different techniques to produce a spiral hose for watering plants. However, as it came off the assembly line, according to his children, it looked more like a "Slinky". He worked at it until it came out perfectly and then went to Betty James with his prototype. Mr. Reum manufactured the Plastic Slinky for Betty James for several years. Eventually Betty James decided to manufacture the product exclusively through James manufacturing effectively ending the production of the toy by the small Minnesota company. Reum's patent number, 4120929 was filed on Dec 28, 1976 and issued by the US Patent Office on Oct 17, 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In 1999, the United States Postal Service issued a Slinky postage stamp.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Slinky was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Toy_Hall_of_Fame" title="Toy Hall of Fame" class="mw-redirect">Toy Hall of Fame</a> in 2000 in their <a href="/wiki/Celebrate_the_Century" title="Celebrate the Century">Celebrate the Century</a> stamp series. A bill to nominate the slinky as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._state_toys" title="List of U.S. state toys" class="mw-redirect">state toy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Geist" title="Richard Geist">Richard Geist</a> in 2001 but not enacted.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The same year, Betty James was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Toy_Industry_Association" title="Toy Industry Association">Toy Industry Association</a>'s Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2003, Slinky was named to the Toy Industry Association's "Century of Toys List", a roll call of the 100 most memorable and most creative toys of the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slinky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>High school teachers and college professors have used Slinkys to simulate the properties of <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> United States troops in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> used them as mobile radio antennas<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (June 2013)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>(as have amateur radio operators<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>), and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> has used them in zero-gravity physics experiments in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Slinkys and similar springs can be used to create a 'laser gun' like sound effect.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This is done by holding up a slinky in the air and striking one end, resulting in a metallic tone which sharply lowers in pitch. This is due to the properties of the metal; higher frequencies travel faster than the lower ones, so as to the listener the high-pitched sound is heard first, then gets progressively lower.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The effect can be amplified by attaching a plastic cup to one end of the Slinky.</p> 
     <p>In 1959, <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a> composed an avant garde work called <i>Sounds of Venice</i> scored for (among other things) a piano, a slab of marble and Venetian broom, a birdcage of canaries, and an amplified Slinky.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1985 in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center" class="mw-redirect">Johnson Space Center</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Museum_of_Natural_Science" title="Houston Museum of Natural Science">Houston Museum of Natural Science</a>, Discovery astronauts created a video demonstrating how familiar toys behave in space. "It won't slink at all," Dr. M. Rhea Seddon said of Slinky, "It sort of droops." The video was prepared to stimulate interest in school children about the basic principles of physics and the phenomenon of weightlessness.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes4_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes4-27">[27]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Museum" title="Bishop Museum">Bishop Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu,_Hawaii" title="Honolulu, Hawaii" class="mw-redirect">Honolulu, Hawaii</a>, hosted an interactive traveling exhibit developed by the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, called "What Makes Music?" Among other things, visitors could examine what makes musical sound by creating waves on an eight-foot-long version of a Slinky toy.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes5_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes5-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
