   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Annual fuel utilization efficiency</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
      Jump to: 
     <a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 
     <a href="#p-search">search</a> 
    </div> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p>The <b>annual fuel utilization efficiency</b> (<b>AFUE</b>; pronounced 'A'-'Few' or 'A'-'F'-'U'-'E') is a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> measure of combustion equipment like <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_heater" title="Water heater" class="mw-redirect">water heaters</a>. The AFUE differs from the true 'thermal efficiency' in that it is not a steady-state, peak measure of conversion efficiency, but instead attempts to represent the actual, season-long, average efficiency of that piece of equipment, including the operating transients.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is a dimensionless ratio of useful energy output to energy input, expressed as a percentage. For example, a 90% AFUE for a gas furnace means it outputs 90 BTUs of useful heating for every 100 BTUs of natural gas input (where the rest may be wasted heat in the exhaust). A higher AFUE means higher efficiency.</p> 
     <p>The method for determining the AFUE for residential furnaces is the subject of <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">ASHRAE</a> Standard 103. A furnace with a thermal efficiency (η<sub>th</sub>) of 78% may yield an AFUE of only 64% or so, for example, under the standard's test conditions. When estimating annual or seasonal energy used by combustion devices, the AFUE is the better efficiency measure to use in the calculations.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> But for an instantaneous fuel consumption rate, the thermal efficiency may be better.</p> 
     <p>Note that the theoretical limit for a conventional furnace's instantaneous efficiency is 100%, whereas a heat pump used for building heating may exceed 100%. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">COP</a> of 1.5 is equivalent to 150%. Heat pumps are readily available for electric and gas sources.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> So from a theoretical perspective, in some use cases the name "efficiency" may be misleading.</p>
