     <p><b>Blood pressure</b> (<b>BP</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> exerted by circulating <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> upon the walls of <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>. When used without further specification, "blood pressure" usually refers to the <a href="/wiki/Arterial" title="Arterial" class="mw-redirect">arterial</a> pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation" class="mw-redirect">systemic circulation</a>. It is usually measured at a person's upper arm. Blood pressure is usually expressed in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Systole" title="Systole">systolic</a> (maximum) pressure over <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" title="Diastolic" class="mw-redirect">diastolic</a> (minimum) pressure and is measured in millimeters of mercury (<a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mm Hg</a>). It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Vital_signs" title="Vital signs">vital signs</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_rate" title="Respiratory rate">respiratory rate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate" title="Heart rate">heart rate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation" title="Oxygen saturation">oxygen saturation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Body_temperature" title="Body temperature" class="mw-redirect">body temperature</a>. Normal resting blood pressure in an adult is approximately 120/80&nbsp;mm Hg.</p> 
     <p>Blood pressure varies depending on situation, activity, and disease states. It is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine</a> systems. Blood pressure that is low <a href="/wiki/Pathologically" title="Pathologically" class="mw-redirect">due to a disease state</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>, and pressure that is consistently high is <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>. Both have many causes which can range from mild to severe. Both may be of sudden onset or of long duration. Long term hypertension is a risk factor for many diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure" class="mw-redirect">kidney failure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease" class="mw-redirect">heart disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>. Long term hypertension is more common than long term hypotension in Western countries. Long term hypertension often goes undetected because of infrequent monitoring and the absence of symptoms.</p>

     <p>The table presented here shows the classification of blood pressure adopted by the American Heart Association for adults who are 18 years and older.<sup id="cite_ref-Heartorg_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heartorg-1">[1]</a></sup> It assumes the values are a result of averaging resting blood pressure readings measured at two or more visits to the doctor.<sup id="cite_ref-Chobanian2003_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chobanian2003-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHLBI2008_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLBI2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/UK" title="UK" class="mw-redirect">UK</a>, clinic blood pressures are usually categorised into three groups; low (90/60 or lower), normal (between 90/60 and 139/89), and high (140/90 or higher).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Blood pressure fluctuates from minute to minute and normally shows a circadian rhythm over a 24-hour period, with highest readings in the early morning and evenings and lowest readings at night.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Loss of the normal fall in blood pressure at night is associated with a greater future risk of cardiovascular disease and there is evidence that night-time blood pressure is a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than day-time blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-HansenLi2010_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HansenLi2010-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Various factors, such as age and sex, influence a person's blood pressure and variations in it. In children, the normal ranges are lower than for adults and depend on height.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Reference blood pressure values have been developed for children in different countries, based on the distribution of blood pressure in children of these countries.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As adults age, systolic pressure tends to rise and diastolic tends to fall.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p145a_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p145a-12">[12]</a></sup> In the elderly, systolic blood pressure tends to be above the normal adult range,<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p144_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p144-13">[13]</a></sup> thought to be largely because of reduced flexibility of the arteries. Also, an individual's blood pressure varies with exercise, emotional reactions, sleep, digestion and time of day (<a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>).</p> 
     <p>Differences between left and right arm blood pressure measurements tend to be random and average to nearly zero if enough measurements are taken. However, in a small percentage of cases there is a consistent difference greater than 10&nbsp;mm Hg which may need further investigation, e.g. for <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">obstructive arterial disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The risk of cardiovascular disease increases progressively above 115/75&nbsp;mm Hg.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the past, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> was only diagnosed if secondary signs of high arterial pressure were present, along with a prolonged high systolic pressure reading over several visits. Regarding hypotension, in practice blood pressure is considered too low only if noticeable <a href="/wiki/Hypotension#Signs_and_symptoms" title="Hypotension">symptoms</a> are present.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayo2009causes_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayo2009causes-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Clinical trials demonstrate that people who maintain arterial pressures at the low end of these pressure ranges have much better long term cardiovascular health. The principal medical debate concerns the aggressiveness and relative value of methods used to lower pressures into this range for those who do not maintain such pressure on their own. Elevations, more commonly seen in older people, though often considered normal, are associated with increased <a href="/wiki/Morbidity" title="Morbidity" class="mw-redirect">morbidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortality</a>.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulse_pressure">Pulse pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pulse pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg/220px-Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg.png" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg/330px-Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg/440px-Arterial-blood-pressure-curve.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="524" data-file-height="372"></a>

     <p>The up and down fluctuation of the <a href="/wiki/Arterial" title="Arterial" class="mw-redirect">arterial</a> pressure results from the pulsatile nature of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a>, i.e. the heartbeat. Pulse pressure is determined by the interaction of the <a href="/wiki/Stroke_volume" title="Stroke volume">stroke volume</a> of the heart, the compliance (ability to expand) of the arterial system—largely attributable to the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> and large elastic arteries—and the <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">resistance</a> to flow in the <a href="/wiki/Arterial_tree" title="Arterial tree">arterial tree</a>. By expanding under pressure, the aorta absorbs some of the force of the blood surge from the heart during a heartbeat. In this way, the pulse pressure is reduced from what it would be if the aorta were not compliant.<sup id="cite_ref-KlabundePulse2007_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KlabundePulse2007-19">[19]</a></sup> The loss of arterial compliance that occurs with aging explains the elevated pulse pressures found in elderly patients.</p>

     <p>Blood pressure generally refers to the arterial pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation" class="mw-redirect">systemic circulation</a>. However, measurement of pressures in the venous system and the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary vessels</a> plays an important role in <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_medicine" title="Intensive care medicine">intensive care medicine</a> but requires invasive measurement of pressure using a <a href="/wiki/Catheter" title="Catheter">catheter</a>.</p> 
     <p>Venous pressure is the vascular pressure in a <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">vein</a> or in the <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(anatomy)" title="Atrium (anatomy)" class="mw-redirect">atria of the heart</a>. It is much less than arterial pressure, with common values of 5&nbsp;mm Hg in the <a href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium" class="mw-redirect">right atrium</a> and 8&nbsp;mm Hg in the left atrium.</p> 
     <p>Variants of venous pressure include:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Central_venous_pressure" title="Central venous pressure">Central venous pressure</a>, which is a good approximation of right atrial pressure,<sup id="cite_ref-urlCentral_Venous_Catheter_Physiology_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlCentral_Venous_Catheter_Physiology-21">[21]</a></sup> which is a major determinant of right ventricular end diastolic volume. (However, there can be exceptions in some cases.)<sup id="cite_ref-pmid12533747_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12533747-22">[22]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>The <a href="/wiki/Jugular_venous_pressure" title="Jugular venous pressure">jugular venous pressure</a> (JVP) is the indirectly observed pressure over the venous system. It can be useful in the differentiation of different forms of <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease" class="mw-redirect">heart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lung_disease" title="Lung disease" class="mw-redirect">lung disease</a>.</li> 
      <li>The <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_pressure" title="Portal venous pressure">portal venous pressure</a> is the blood pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein" class="mw-redirect">portal vein</a>. It is normally 5–10&nbsp;mm Hg<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulmonary_pressure">Pulmonary pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pulmonary pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery_pressure" title="Pulmonary artery pressure" class="mw-redirect">Pulmonary artery pressure</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Normally, the pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a> is about 15&nbsp;mm Hg at rest.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Increased blood pressure in the capillaries of the lung cause <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_hypertension" title="Pulmonary hypertension">pulmonary hypertension</a>, leading to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstitial_edema&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Interstitial edema (page does not exist)">interstitial edema</a> if the pressure increases to above 20&nbsp;mm Hg, and to <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a> at pressures above 25&nbsp;mm Hg.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disorders">Disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Disorders of blood pressure control include: <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">high blood pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a>, and blood pressure that shows excessive or maladaptive fluctuation.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High">High</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">Hypertension</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/300px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png" width="300" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/450px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/600px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1164" data-file-height="1076"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Overview of main complications of persistent high blood pressure.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Arterial_hypertension" title="Arterial hypertension" class="mw-redirect">Arterial hypertension</a> can be an indicator of other problems and may have long-term adverse effects. Sometimes it can be an acute problem, for example <a href="/wiki/Hypertensive_emergency" title="Hypertensive emergency">hypertensive emergency</a>.</p> 
     <p>Levels of arterial pressure put mechanical stress on the arterial walls. Higher pressures increase heart workload and progression of unhealthy tissue growth (<a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheroma</a>) that develops within the walls of arteries. The higher the pressure, the more stress that is present and the more <a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheroma</a> tend to progress and the <a href="/wiki/Myocardium" title="Myocardium" class="mw-redirect">heart muscle</a> tends to thicken, enlarge and become weaker over time.</p> 
     <p>Persistent <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> is one of the risk factors for <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">strokes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> and arterial aneurysms, and is the leading cause of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_failure" title="Chronic kidney failure" class="mw-redirect">chronic kidney failure</a>. Even moderate elevation of arterial pressure leads to shortened <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>. At severely high pressures, mean arterial pressures 50% or more above average, a person can expect to live no more than a few years unless appropriately treated.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the past, most attention was paid to <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" title="Diastolic" class="mw-redirect">diastolic</a> pressure; but nowadays it is recognised that both high <a href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" title="Systole (medicine)" class="mw-redirect">systolic</a> pressure and high <a href="/wiki/Pulse_pressure" title="Pulse pressure">pulse pressure</a> (the numerical difference between systolic and diastolic pressures) are also risk factors. In some cases, it appears that a decrease in excessive diastolic pressure can actually increase risk, due probably to the increased difference between systolic and diastolic pressures (see the article on <a href="/wiki/Pulse_pressure" title="Pulse pressure">pulse pressure</a>). If systolic blood pressure is elevated (&gt;140) with a normal diastolic blood pressure (&lt;90), it is called "isolated systolic hypertension" and may present a health concern.<sup id="cite_ref-urlIsolated_systolic_hypertension:_A_health_concern.3F_.E2.80.93_MayoClinic.com_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlIsolated_systolic_hypertension:_A_health_concern.3F_.E2.80.93_MayoClinic.com-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urlThe_Cleveland_Clinic_Center_for_Continuing_Education_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlThe_Cleveland_Clinic_Center_for_Continuing_Education-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>For those with <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">heart valve</a> regurgitation, a change in its severity may be associated with a change in diastolic pressure. In a study of people with heart valve regurgitation that compared measurements 2 weeks apart for each person, there was an increased severity of <a href="/wiki/Aortic_insufficiency" title="Aortic insufficiency">aortic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitral_regurgitation" title="Mitral regurgitation" class="mw-redirect">mitral regurgitation</a> when diastolic blood pressure increased, whereas when diastolic blood pressure decreased, there was a decreased severity.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottdiener2002_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottdiener2002-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low">Low</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Low">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">Hypotension</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Blood pressure that is too low is known as <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>. Hypotension is a medical concern if it causes signs or symptoms, such as dizziness, fainting, or in extreme cases, <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NHLBI2008_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLBI2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>When arterial pressure and blood <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_fluid_flow" title="Rate of fluid flow" class="mw-redirect">flow</a> decrease beyond a certain point, the <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a> of the brain becomes critically decreased (i.e., the blood supply is not sufficient), causing lightheadedness, dizziness, weakness or fainting.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Sometimes the arterial pressure drops significantly when a patient stands up from sitting. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">orthostatic hypotension</a> (postural hypotension); gravity reduces the rate of blood return from the body veins below the heart back to the heart, thus reducing stroke volume and cardiac output.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>When people are healthy, the veins below their heart quickly constrict and the heart rate increases to minimize and compensate for the gravity effect. This is carried out involuntarily by the autonomic nervous system. The system usually requires a few seconds to fully adjust and if the compensations are too slow or inadequate, the individual will suffer reduced blood flow to the brain, dizziness and potential blackout. Increases in G-loading, such as routinely experienced by aerobatic or combat pilots '<a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">pulling Gs</a>', greatly increases this effect. Repositioning the body perpendicular to gravity largely eliminates the problem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Other causes of low arterial pressure include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">Sepsis</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect">Hemorrhage</a> – blood loss</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Toxins" title="Toxins" class="mw-redirect">Toxins</a> including toxic doses of blood pressure medicine</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">Hormonal</a> abnormalities, such as <a href="/wiki/Addison%27s_disease" title="Addison's disease">Addison's disease</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">Eating disorders</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulimia" title="Bulimia" class="mw-redirect">bulimia</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">Shock</a> is a complex condition which leads to critically decreased <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a>. The usual mechanisms are loss of blood volume, pooling of blood within the veins reducing adequate return to the heart and/or low effective heart pumping. Low arterial pressure, especially low pulse pressure, is a sign of shock and contributes to and reflects decreased perfusion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>If there is a significant difference in the pressure from one arm to the other, that may indicate a narrowing (for example, due to <a href="/wiki/Aortic_coarctation" title="Aortic coarctation" class="mw-redirect">aortic coarctation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aortic_dissection" title="Aortic dissection">aortic dissection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thrombosis" title="Thrombosis">thrombosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Embolism" title="Embolism">embolism</a>) of an <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">artery</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluctuating_blood_pressure">Fluctuating blood pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fluctuating blood pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Normal fluctuation in blood pressure is adaptive and necessary. Fluctuations in pressure that are significantly greater than the norm are associated with greater <a href="/wiki/White_matter_hyperintensity" title="White matter hyperintensity" class="mw-redirect">white matter hyperintensity</a>, a finding consistent with reduced local cerebral blood flow<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and a heightened risk of <a href="/wiki/Cerebrovascular" title="Cerebrovascular" class="mw-redirect">cerebrovascular</a> disease.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-32">[32]</a></sup> Within both high and low blood pressure groups, a greater degree of fluctuation was found to correlate with an increase in cerebrovascular disease compared to those with less variability, suggesting the consideration of the clinical management of blood pressure fluctuations, even among <a href="/wiki/Normotensive" title="Normotensive" class="mw-redirect">normotensive</a> older adults.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-32">[32]</a></sup> Older individuals and those who had received blood pressure medications were more likely to exhibit larger fluctuations in pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Systolevs_Diastole.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/220px-Systolevs_Diastole.png" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/330px-Systolevs_Diastole.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/440px-Systolevs_Diastole.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="889"></a>

     <p>During each heartbeat, blood pressure varies between a maximum (systolic) and a minimum (diastolic) pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The blood pressure in the circulation is principally due to the pumping action of the heart.<sup id="cite_ref-Caro_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-34">[34]</a></sup> Differences in mean blood pressure are responsible for blood flow from one location to another in the circulation. The rate of mean blood flow depends on both blood pressure and the resistance to flow presented by the blood vessels. Mean blood pressure decreases as the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulating blood</a> moves away from the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> through arteries and <a href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries" class="mw-redirect">capillaries</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> losses of energy. Mean blood pressure drops over the whole circulation, although most of the fall occurs along the small arteries and <a href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles" class="mw-redirect">arterioles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Klabunde2005p93-4_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klabunde2005p93-4-35">[35]</a></sup> Gravity affects blood pressure via <a href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" title="Fluid statics" class="mw-redirect">hydrostatic</a> forces (e.g., during standing), and valves in veins, <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>, and pumping from contraction of skeletal muscles also influence blood pressure in veins.<sup id="cite_ref-Caro_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-34">[34]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hemodynamics">Hemodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hemodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Hemodynamics" title="Hemodynamics">Hemodynamics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>There are many physical factors that influence arterial pressure. Each of these may in turn be influenced by physiological factors, such as: diet, exercise, disease, drugs or alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Stress_(biology)" title="Stress (biology)">stress</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some physical factors are:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Volume of fluid or <a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">blood volume</a>, the amount of blood that is present in the body. The more blood present in the body, the higher the rate of blood return to the heart and the resulting cardiac output. There is some relationship between dietary salt intake and increased blood volume, potentially resulting in higher arterial pressure, though this varies with the individual and is highly dependent on autonomic nervous system response and the <a href="/wiki/Renin%E2%80%93angiotensin_system" title="Renin–angiotensin system">renin–angiotensin system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Resistance. In the circulatory system, this is the resistance of the blood vessels. The higher the resistance, the higher the arterial pressure upstream from the resistance to blood flow. Resistance is related to vessel radius (the larger the radius, the lower the resistance), vessel length (the longer the vessel, the higher the resistance), blood viscosity, as well as the smoothness of the blood vessel walls. Smoothness is reduced by the buildup of fatty deposits on the arterial walls. Substances called <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstrictor" title="Vasoconstrictor" class="mw-redirect">vasoconstrictors</a> can reduce the size of blood vessels, thereby increasing blood pressure. <a href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator" class="mw-redirect">Vasodilators</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>) increase the size of blood vessels, thereby decreasing arterial pressure. Resistance, and its relation to volumetric flow rate (Q) and pressure difference between the two ends of a vessel are described by <a href="/wiki/Poiseuille%27s_Law" title="Poiseuille's Law" class="mw-redirect">Poiseuille's Law</a>.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">Viscosity</a>, or thickness of the fluid. If the blood gets thicker, the result is an increase in arterial pressure. Certain <a href="/wiki/Medical_conditions" title="Medical conditions" class="mw-redirect">medical conditions</a> can change the viscosity of the blood. For instance, anemia (low <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cell</a> concentration), reduces viscosity, whereas increased red blood cell concentration increases viscosity. It had been thought that <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a> and related "<a href="/wiki/Blood_thinner" title="Blood thinner" class="mw-redirect">blood thinner</a>" drugs decreased the viscosity of blood, but instead studies found<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> that they act by reducing the tendency of the blood to clot.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In practice, each individual's autonomic nervous system responds to and regulates all these interacting factors so that, although the above issues are important, the actual arterial pressure response of a given individual varies widely because of both split-second and slow-moving responses of the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> and end organs. These responses are very effective in changing the variables and resulting blood pressure from moment to moment.</p> 
     <p>Moreover, blood pressure is the result of cardiac output increased by peripheral resistance: <i>blood pressure = <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a> X <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_resistance" title="Peripheral resistance" class="mw-redirect">peripheral resistance</a></i>. As a result, an abnormal change in blood pressure is often an indication of a problem affecting the heart's output, the blood vessels' resistance, or both. Thus, knowing the patient's blood pressure is critical to assess any pathology related to output and resistance.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Endogenous" title="Endogenous" class="mw-redirect">endogenous</a> regulation of arterial pressure is not completely understood, but the following mechanisms of regulating arterial pressure have been well-characterized:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor_reflex" title="Baroreceptor reflex" class="mw-redirect">Baroreceptor reflex</a>: <a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor" title="Baroreceptor">Baroreceptors</a> in the <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_receptor_zones" title="High pressure receptor zones">high pressure receptor zones</a> detect changes in arterial pressure. These baroreceptors send signals ultimately to the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla of the brain stem</a>, specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Rostral_ventrolateral_medulla" title="Rostral ventrolateral medulla">rostral ventrolateral medulla</a> (RVLM). The medulla, by way of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic nervous system</a>, adjusts the mean arterial pressure by altering both the force and speed of the heart's contractions, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Total_peripheral_resistance" title="Total peripheral resistance" class="mw-redirect">total peripheral resistance</a>. The most important arterial baroreceptors are located in the left and right <a href="/wiki/Carotid_sinus" title="Carotid sinus">carotid sinuses</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arch" title="Aortic arch">aortic arch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-KlabundeArtBar2007_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KlabundeArtBar2007-41">[41]</a></sup></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Renin-angiotensin_system" title="Renin-angiotensin system" class="mw-redirect">Renin-angiotensin system</a> (RAS): This system is generally known for its long-term adjustment of arterial pressure. This system allows the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> to compensate for loss in <a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">blood volume</a> or drops in arterial pressure by activating an endogenous <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstrictor" title="Vasoconstrictor" class="mw-redirect">vasoconstrictor</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II" title="Angiotensin II" class="mw-redirect">angiotensin II</a>.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">Aldosterone</a> release: This <a href="/wiki/Steroid_hormone" title="Steroid hormone">steroid hormone</a> is released from the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_cortex" title="Adrenal cortex">adrenal cortex</a> in response to angiotensin II or high serum <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> levels. Aldosterone stimulates <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> retention and potassium excretion by the kidneys. Since sodium is the main ion that determines the amount of fluid in the blood vessels by <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>, aldosterone will increase fluid retention, and indirectly, arterial pressure.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor" title="Baroreceptor">Baroreceptors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_receptor_zones" title="Low pressure receptor zones">low pressure receptor zones</a> (mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_veins" title="Pulmonary veins" class="mw-redirect">pulmonary veins</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(heart)" title="Atrium (heart)">atria</a>) result in feedback by regulating the secretion of <a href="/wiki/Antidiuretic_hormone" title="Antidiuretic hormone" class="mw-redirect">antidiuretic hormone</a> (ADH/Vasopressin), <a href="/wiki/Renin" title="Renin">renin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">aldosterone</a>. The resultant increase in <a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">blood volume</a> results in an increased <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Starling_law_of_the_heart" title="Frank–Starling law of the heart" class="mw-redirect">Frank–Starling law of the heart</a>, in turn increasing arterial blood pressure.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>These different mechanisms are not necessarily independent of each other, as indicated by the link between the RAS and aldosterone release. When blood pressure falls many physiological cascades commence in order to return the blood pressure to a more appropriate level.</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>The blood pressure fall is detected by a decrease in blood flow and thus a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Glomerular_filtration_rate" title="Glomerular filtration rate" class="mw-redirect">Glomerular filtration rate</a> (GFR).</li> 
      <li>Decrease in GFR is sensed as a decrease in Na<sup>+</sup> levels by the <a href="/wiki/Macula_densa" title="Macula densa">macula densa</a>.</li> 
      <li>The macula densa cause an increase in Na<sup>+</sup> reabsorption, which causes water to follow in via <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a> and leads to an ultimate increase in <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a> volume. Further, the macula densa releases adenosine which causes constriction of the afferent arterioles.</li> 
      <li>At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Juxtaglomerular_cells" title="Juxtaglomerular cells" class="mw-redirect">juxtaglomerular cells</a> sense the decrease in blood pressure and release <a href="/wiki/Renin" title="Renin">renin</a>.</li> 
      <li>Renin converts <a href="/wiki/Angiotensinogen" title="Angiotensinogen" class="mw-redirect">angiotensinogen</a> (inactive form) to <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_I" title="Angiotensin I" class="mw-redirect">angiotensin I</a> (active form).</li> 
      <li>Angiotensin I flows in the bloodstream until it reaches the capillaries of the lungs where <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_converting_enzyme" title="Angiotensin converting enzyme" class="mw-redirect">angiotensin converting enzyme</a> (ACE) acts on it to convert it into <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II" title="Angiotensin II" class="mw-redirect">angiotensin II</a>.</li> 
      <li>Angiotensin II is a vasoconstrictor which will increase bloodflow to the heart and subsequently the preload, ultimately increasing the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a>.</li> 
      <li>Angiotensin II also causes an increase in the release of <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">aldosterone</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_gland" title="Adrenal gland">adrenal glands</a>.</li> 
      <li>Aldosterone further increases the Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O reabsorption in the <a href="/wiki/Distal_convoluted_tubule" title="Distal convoluted tubule">distal convoluted tubule</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nephron" title="Nephron">nephron</a>.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>Currently, the RAS is targeted pharmacologically by <a href="/wiki/ACE_inhibitor" title="ACE inhibitor">ACE inhibitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II_receptor_antagonist" title="Angiotensin II receptor antagonist">angiotensin II receptor antagonists</a>. The aldosterone system is directly targeted by <a href="/wiki/Spironolactone" title="Spironolactone">spironolactone</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone_antagonist" title="Aldosterone antagonist" class="mw-redirect">aldosterone antagonist</a>. The fluid retention may be targeted by <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretics</a>; the antihypertensive effect of diuretics is due to its effect on blood volume. Generally, the baroreceptor reflex is not targeted in <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> because if blocked, individuals may suffer from <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">orthostatic hypotension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fainting" title="Fainting" class="mw-redirect">fainting</a>.</p>

     <p>Arterial pressure is most commonly measured via a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a>, which historically used the height of a column of mercury to reflect the circulating pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Booth1977_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Booth1977-42">[42]</a></sup> Blood pressure values are generally reported in <a href="/wiki/Millimetre_of_mercury" title="Millimetre of mercury" class="mw-redirect">millimetres of mercury</a> (mm Hg), though aneroid and electronic devices do not contain <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>.</p> 
     <p>For each heartbeat, blood pressure varies between systolic and diastolic pressures. Systolic pressure is peak pressure in the arteries, which occurs near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_cycle" title="Cardiac cycle">cardiac cycle</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricles</a> are contracting. Diastolic pressure is minimum pressure in the arteries, which occurs near the beginning of the cardiac cycle when the ventricles are filled with blood. An example of normal measured values for a resting, healthy adult human is 120&nbsp;mm Hg <a href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" title="Systole (medicine)" class="mw-redirect">systolic</a> and 80&nbsp;mm Hg <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" title="Diastolic" class="mw-redirect">diastolic</a> (written as 120/80&nbsp;mm Hg, and spoken as "one-twenty over eighty").</p> 
     <p>Systolic and diastolic arterial blood pressures are not static but undergo natural variations from one heartbeat to another and throughout the day (in a <a href="/wiki/Circadian" title="Circadian" class="mw-redirect">circadian</a> rhythm). They also change in response to <a href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)" class="mw-redirect">stress</a>, nutritional factors, <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication" class="mw-redirect">drugs</a>, disease, exercise, and <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">momentarily from standing up</a>. Sometimes the variations are large. <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">Hypertension</a> refers to arterial pressure being abnormally high, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>, when it is abnormally low. Along with <a href="/wiki/Body_temperature" title="Body temperature" class="mw-redirect">body temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_rate" title="Respiratory rate">respiratory rate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulse_rate" title="Pulse rate" class="mw-redirect">pulse rate</a>, blood pressure is one of the four main vital signs routinely monitored by medical professionals and healthcare providers.<sup id="cite_ref-OHSU_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHSU-43">[43]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Measuring pressure <a href="/wiki/Invasive_blood_pressure" title="Invasive blood pressure">invasively</a>, by penetrating the arterial wall to take the measurement, is much less common and usually restricted to a hospital setting.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noninvasive">Noninvasive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Noninvasive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The noninvasive <a href="/wiki/Auscultation" title="Auscultation">auscultatory</a> and oscillometric measurements are simpler and quicker than invasive measurements, require less expertise, have virtually no complications, are less unpleasant and less painful for the patient. However, noninvasive methods may yield somewhat lower accuracy and small systematic differences in numerical results. Noninvasive measurement methods are more commonly used for routine examinations and monitoring.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palpation">Palpation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Palpation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>A minimum systolic value can be roughly estimated by <a href="/wiki/Palpation" title="Palpation">palpation</a>, most often used in <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_service" title="Emergency medical service" class="mw-redirect">emergency situations</a>, but should be used with caution.<sup id="cite_ref-Deakin2000_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deakin2000-44">[44]</a></sup> It has been estimated that, using 50% <a href="/wiki/Percentile" title="Percentile">percentiles</a>, carotid, femoral and radial pulses are present in patients with a systolic blood pressure &gt; 70&nbsp;mm Hg, carotid and femoral pulses alone in patients with systolic blood pressure of &gt; 50&nbsp;mm Hg, and only a carotid pulse in patients with a systolic blood pressure of &gt; 40&nbsp;mm Hg.<sup id="cite_ref-Deakin2000_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deakin2000-44">[44]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A more accurate value of systolic blood pressure can be obtained with a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a> and palpating the radial pulse.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The diastolic blood pressure cannot be estimated by this method. The American Heart Association recommends that palpation be used to get an estimate before using the auscultatory method.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Auscultatory">Auscultatory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Auscultatory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>The auscultatory method (from the Latin word for "listening") uses a <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a>. This comprises an inflatable (<i><a href="/wiki/Scipione_Riva-Rocci" title="Scipione Riva-Rocci">Riva-Rocci</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Cuff" title="Cuff">cuff</a> placed around the upper <a href="/wiki/Arm" title="Arm">arm</a> at roughly the same vertical height as the heart, attached to a mercury or <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Aneroid" title="Pressure measurement">aneroid</a> <a href="/wiki/Manometer" title="Manometer" class="mw-redirect">manometer</a>. The mercury manometer, considered the gold standard, measures the height of a column of mercury, giving an absolute result without need for calibration and, consequently, not subject to the errors and drift of calibration which affect other methods. The use of mercury manometers is often required in <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a> and for the clinical measurement of <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> in high-risk patients, such as <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnant women</a>.</p> 
     <p>A cuff of appropriate size<sup id="cite_ref-cuff-size_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuff-size-46">[46]</a></sup> is fitted smoothly and also snugly, then inflated manually by repeatedly squeezing a rubber bulb until the artery is completely occluded. It is important that the cuff size is correct: undersized cuffs record too high a pressure; oversized cuffs may yield too low a pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-OBrien2003_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien2003-47">[47]</a></sup> Usually three or four cuff sizes should be available to allow measurements in arms of different size.<sup id="cite_ref-OBrien2003_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien2003-47">[47]</a></sup> Listening with the stethoscope to the <a href="/wiki/Brachial_artery" title="Brachial artery">brachial artery</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cubital_fossa" title="Cubital fossa">antecubital area</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Elbow" title="Elbow">elbow</a>, the examiner slowly releases the pressure in the cuff. When blood just starts to flow in the artery, the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent flow</a> creates a "whooshing" or pounding (first <a href="/wiki/Korotkoff_sounds" title="Korotkoff sounds">Korotkoff sound</a>). The pressure at which this sound is first heard is the systolic blood pressure. The cuff pressure is further released until no sound can be heard (fifth Korotkoff sound), at the diastolic arterial pressure.</p> 
     <p>The auscultatory method is the predominant method of clinical measurement.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p146_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p146-48">[48]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oscillometric">Oscillometric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Oscillometric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The oscillometric method was first demonstrated in 1876 and involves the observation of oscillations in the sphygmomanometer cuff pressure<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p147_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p147-49">[49]</a></sup> which are caused by the oscillations of <a href="/wiki/Blood_flow" title="Blood flow">blood flow</a>, i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Laurent2003_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laurent2003-50">[50]</a></sup> The electronic version of this method is sometimes used in long-term measurements and general practice. It uses a sphygmomanometer cuff, like the auscultatory method, but with an electronic <a href="/wiki/Pressure_sensor" title="Pressure sensor">pressure sensor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">transducer</a>) to observe cuff pressure oscillations, electronics to automatically interpret them, and automatic inflation and deflation of the cuff. The pressure sensor should be calibrated periodically to maintain accuracy.</p> 
     <p>Oscillometric measurement requires less skill than the auscultatory technique and may be suitable for use by untrained staff and for automated patient home monitoring. As for the auscultatory technique it is important that the cuff size is appropriate for the arm. There are some single cuff devices that may be used for arms of differing sizes, although experience with these is limited.<sup id="cite_ref-OBrien2003_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien2003-47">[47]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The cuff is inflated to a pressure initially in excess of the systolic arterial pressure and then reduced to below diastolic pressure over a period of about 30&nbsp;seconds. When blood flow is nil (cuff pressure exceeding systolic pressure) or unimpeded (cuff pressure below diastolic pressure), cuff pressure will be essentially constant. When blood flow is present, but restricted, the cuff pressure, which is monitored by the pressure sensor, will vary periodically in synchrony with the cyclic expansion and contraction of the brachial artery, i.e., it will <a href="/wiki/Oscillate" title="Oscillate" class="mw-redirect">oscillate</a>.</p> 
     <p>Over the deflation period, the recorded pressure waveform forms a signal known as the cuff deflation curve. A bandpass filter is utilized to extract the oscillometric pulses from the cuff deflation curve. Over the deflation period, the extracted oscillometric pulses form a signal known as the oscillometric waveform (OMW). The amplitude of the oscillometric pulses increases to a maximum and then decreases with further deflation. A variety of analysis algorithms can be employed in order to estimate the systolic, diastolic, and mean arterial pressure.</p> 
     <p>Oscillometric monitors may produce inaccurate readings in patients with heart and circulation problems, which include <a href="/wiki/Arteriosclerosis" title="Arteriosclerosis">arteriosclerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arrhythmia" title="Arrhythmia" class="mw-redirect">arrhythmia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preeclampsia" title="Preeclampsia" class="mw-redirect">preeclampsia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulsus_alternans" title="Pulsus alternans">pulsus alternans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulsus_paradoxus" title="Pulsus paradoxus">pulsus paradoxus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OBrien2003_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien2003-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In practice the different methods do not give identical results; an algorithm and experimentally obtained coefficients are used to adjust the oscillometric results to give readings which match the auscultatory results as well as possible. Some equipment uses computer-aided analysis of the instantaneous arterial pressure <a href="/wiki/Waveform" title="Waveform">waveform</a> to determine the systolic, mean, and diastolic points. Since many oscillometric devices have not been validated, caution must be given as most are not suitable in clinical and acute care settings.</p> 
     <p>Recently, several coefficient-free oscillometric algorithms have developed for estimation of blood pressure. These algorithms do not rely on experimentally obtained coefficients and have been shown to provide more accurate and robust estimation of blood pressure. These algorithms are based on finding the fundamental relationship between the oscillometric waveform and the BP using modeling <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and learning <sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> approaches.</p> 
     <p>The term NIBP, for non-invasive blood pressure, is often used to describe oscillometric monitoring equipment.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous_noninvasive_techniques_.28CNAP.29">Continuous noninvasive techniques (CNAP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Continuous noninvasive techniques (CNAP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Continuous_Noninvasive_Arterial_Pressure" title="Continuous Noninvasive Arterial Pressure" class="mw-redirect">Continuous Noninvasive Arterial Pressure</a> (CNAP) is the method of measuring arterial blood pressure in real-time without any interruptions and without cannulating the human body. CNAP combines the advantages of the following two clinical “gold standards”: it measures blood pressure continuously in real-time like the invasive <a href="/wiki/Arterial_catheter" title="Arterial catheter" class="mw-redirect">arterial catheter</a> system and it is noninvasive like the standard upper arm <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a>. Latest developments in this field show promising results in terms of accuracy, ease of use and clinical acceptance.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-occlusive_techniques:_The_Pulse_Wave_Velocity_.28PWV.29_principle">Non-occlusive techniques: The Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV) principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Non-occlusive techniques: The Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV) principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Since the 1990s a novel family of techniques based on the so-called <a href="/wiki/Pulse_wave_velocity" title="Pulse wave velocity">pulse wave velocity</a> (PWV) principle have been developed. These techniques rely on the fact that the velocity at which an arterial pressure pulse travels along the arterial tree depends, among others, on the underlying blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Accordingly, after a calibration maneuver, these techniques provide indirect estimates of blood pressure by translating PWV values into blood pressure values.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The main advantage of these techniques is that it is possible to measure PWV values of a subject continuously (beat-by-beat), without medical supervision, and without the need of inflating brachial cuffs. PWV-based techniques are still in the research domain and are not adapted to clinical settings.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambulatory_and_home_monitoring">Ambulatory and home monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ambulatory and home monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Ambulatory_blood_pressure" title="Ambulatory blood pressure">Ambulatory blood pressure</a> devices take readings regularly (e.g. every half hour throughout the day and night). They have been used to exclude measurement problems like <a href="/wiki/White-coat_hypertension" title="White-coat hypertension" class="mw-redirect">white-coat hypertension</a> and provide more reliable estimates of usual blood pressure and cardiovascular risk. Blood pressure readings outside of a clinical setting are usually slightly lower in the majority of people; however studies that quantified the risks from <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> and the benefits of lowering blood pressure have mostly been based on readings in a clinical environment. Use of ambulatory measurements is not widespread but guidelines developed by the UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence and the British Hypertension Society recommended that 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring should be used for diagnosis of hypertension.<sup id="cite_ref-NICEBHS2011_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NICEBHS2011-57">[57]</a></sup> Health economic analysis suggested that this approach would be cost effective compared with repeated clinic measurements.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Home monitoring is a cheap and simple alternative to ambulatory blood pressure monitoring, although it does not usually allow assessment of blood pressure during sleep which may be a disadvantage.<sup id="cite_ref-Mancia2007_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mancia2007-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2008_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2008-60">[60]</a></sup> Automatic self-contained blood pressure monitors are available at reasonable prices, however measurements may not be accurate in patients with atrial fibrillation or other arrhythmias such as frequent ectopic beats.<sup id="cite_ref-Mancia2007_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mancia2007-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2008_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2008-60">[60]</a></sup> Home monitoring may be used to improve hypertension management and to monitor the effects of lifestyle changes and medication related to blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Chobanian2003_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chobanian2003-3">[3]</a></sup> Compared to ambulatory blood pressure measurements, home monitoring has been found to be an effective and lower cost alternative,<sup id="cite_ref-Mancia2007_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mancia2007-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Niiranen2006_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Niiranen2006-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shimbo2007_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shimbo2007-62">[62]</a></sup> but ambulatory monitoring is more accurate than both clinic and home monitoring in diagnosing hypertension.</p> 
     <p>When measuring blood pressure in the home, an accurate reading requires that one not drink coffee, smoke cigarettes, or engage in strenuous exercise for 30&nbsp;minutes before taking the reading. A full bladder may have a small effect on blood pressure readings; if the urge to urinate arises, one should do so before the reading. For 5&nbsp;minutes before the reading, one should sit upright in a chair with one's feet flat on the floor and with limbs uncrossed. The blood pressure cuff should always be against bare skin, as readings taken over a shirt sleeve are less accurate. The same arm should be used for all measurements. During the reading, the arm that is used should be relaxed and kept at heart level, for example by resting it on a table.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since blood pressure varies throughout the day, home measurements should be taken at the same time of day. A Joint Scientific Statement From the American Heart Association, American Society of Hypertension, and Preventive Cardiovascular Nurses Association on home monitoring in 2008<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2008_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2008-60">[60]</a></sup> recommended that 2 to 3 readings should be taken in the morning (after awakening, before washing/dressing, taking breakfast/drink or taking medication) and another 2 to 3 readings at night, each day over a period of 1 week. It was also recommended that the readings from the first day should be discarded and that a total of ≥12 readings (i.e. at least two readings per day for the remaining 6 days of the week) should be used for making clinical decisions.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="White-coat_hypertension">White-coat hypertension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: White-coat hypertension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>For some patients, blood pressure measurements taken in a doctor's office may not correctly characterize their typical blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot2007_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot2007-64">[64]</a></sup> In up to 25% of patients, the office measurement is higher than their typical blood pressure. This type of error is called <a href="/wiki/White-coat_hypertension" title="White-coat hypertension" class="mw-redirect">white-coat hypertension</a> (WCH) and can result from anxiety related to an examination by a health care professional.<sup id="cite_ref-Jhalani2005_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jhalani2005-65">[65]</a></sup> White coat hypertension can also occur because, in a clinical setting, patients are seldom given the opportunity to rest for five minutes before blood pressure readings are taken. The misdiagnosis of hypertension for these patients can result in needless and possibly harmful medication. WCH can be reduced (but not eliminated) with automated blood pressure measurements over 15 to 20 minutes in a quiet part of the office or clinic.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p145_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p145-66">[66]</a></sup> In some cases a lower blood pressure reading occurs at the doctor's - this has been termed 'masked hypertension'.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p146a_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p146a-67">[67]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive">Invasive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Invasive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Arterial blood pressure (BP) is most accurately measured invasively through an <a href="/wiki/Arterial_line" title="Arterial line">arterial line</a>. Invasive arterial pressure measurement with intravascular <a href="/wiki/Cannulae" title="Cannulae" class="mw-redirect">cannulae</a> involves direct measurement of arterial pressure by placing a cannula needle in an artery (usually <a href="/wiki/Radial_artery" title="Radial artery">radial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Femoral_artery" title="Femoral artery">femoral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorsalis_pedis_artery" title="Dorsalis pedis artery">dorsalis pedis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brachial_artery" title="Brachial artery">brachial</a>).</p> 
     <p>The cannula must be connected to a sterile, fluid-filled system, which is connected to an electronic pressure transducer. The advantage of this system is that pressure is constantly monitored beat-by-beat, and a waveform (a graph of pressure against time) can be displayed. This invasive technique is regularly employed in human and veterinary <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_medicine" title="Intensive care medicine">intensive care medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anesthesiology" title="Anesthesiology">anesthesiology</a>, and for research purposes.</p> 
     <p>Cannulation for invasive vascular pressure monitoring is infrequently associated with complications such as <a href="/wiki/Thrombosis" title="Thrombosis">thrombosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect">bleeding</a>. Patients with invasive arterial monitoring require very close supervision, as there is a danger of severe bleeding if the line becomes disconnected. It is generally reserved for patients where rapid variations in arterial pressure are anticipated.</p> 
     <p>Invasive vascular pressure monitors are pressure monitoring systems designed to acquire pressure information for display and processing. There are a variety of invasive vascular pressure monitors for trauma, critical care, and <a href="/wiki/Operating_theatre" title="Operating theatre" class="mw-redirect">operating room</a> applications. These include single pressure, dual pressure, and multi-parameter (i.e. pressure / temperature). The monitors can be used for measurement and follow-up of arterial, central venous, pulmonary arterial, left atrial, right atrial, femoral arterial, umbilical venous, umbilical arterial, and intracranial pressures.</p>

     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Fetal_circulation#Blood_pressure" title="Fetal circulation">Fetal circulation § Blood pressure</a>
     </div> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, it is the fetal heart and not the mother's heart that builds up the fetal blood pressure to drive its blood through the fetal circulation.</p>
