   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Deconstruction (building)</h1>

     <p>In the context of physical <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, <b>deconstruction</b> is the selective dismantlement of building components, specifically for <a href="/wiki/Re-use" title="Re-use" class="mw-redirect">re-use</a>, <a href="/wiki/Repurposed_building_stone" title="Repurposed building stone" class="mw-redirect">repurposing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a>. It differs from <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition</a> where a site is cleared of its building by the most expedient means. Deconstruction has also been defined as “construction in reverse”. The process of dismantling structures is an ancient activity that has been revived by the growing field of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable" class="mw-redirect">sustainable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green method of building</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Buildings, like everything, have a life-cycle. Deconstruction focuses on giving the materials within a building a new life once the building as a whole can no longer continue.</p> 
     <p>When buildings reach the end of their useful life, they are typically demolished and hauled to <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>. <a href="/wiki/Building_implosion" title="Building implosion">Building implosions</a> or ‘wrecking-ball’ style <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolitions</a> are relatively inexpensive and offer a quick method of clearing sites for new structures. On the other hand, these methods create substantial amounts of waste. Components within old buildings may still be valuable, sometimes more valuable than at the time the building was constructed. Deconstruction is a method of harvesting what is commonly considered “<a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>” and reclaiming it into useful building material.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contribution_to_sustainability">Contribution to sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deconstruction_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contribution to sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Deconstruction has strong ties to environmental <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. In addition to giving materials a new life cycle, deconstructing buildings helps to lower the need for virgin resources. This in turn leads to <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">emissions</a> reductions from the <a href="/wiki/Refining" title="Refining">refining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacture" title="Manufacture" class="mw-redirect">manufacture</a> of new materials. As deconstruction is often done on a local level, many times on-site, energy and emissions are also saved in the <a href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation" class="mw-redirect">transportation</a> of materials. Deconstruction can potentially support communities by providing local jobs and renovated structures. Deconstruction work typically employs 3-6 workers for every one employed in a comparable demolition job.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Solid_waste" title="Solid waste" class="mw-redirect">solid waste</a> from conventional demolition is diverted from landfills. This is a major benefit because construction and demolition (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Construction_and_demolition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Construction and demolition (page does not exist)">C&amp;D</a>) waste accounts for approximately 20% of the solid waste stream.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_avoiding_wood_waste">Benefits of avoiding wood waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deconstruction_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits of avoiding wood waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In Canada, the CO2 Neutral Alliance has created a website<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> with resources for regulators and municipalities, developers and contractors, business owners and operators, and individuals and households. Benefits for municipalities include:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Reducing disposal costs where waste collection, hauling or disposal is supported by the tax base</li> 
      <li>Establishing additional revenue streams</li> 
      <li>Making existing landfills last longer</li> 
      <li>Reducing greenhouse gas emissions caused by the decomposition of wood waste into methane from landfills</li> 
      <li>Stimulating local economies with new industries and employment</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Improving the local environment and overall sustainability of your community For every three square feet of deconstruction, enough lumber can be salvaged to build one square foot of new construction. At this rate, if deconstruction replaced residential demolition, the United States could generate enough recovered wood to construct 120,000 new affordable homes each year. The deconstruction of a typical 2,000-square-foot (190&nbsp;m<sup>2</sup>) wood frame home can yield 6,000 board feet of reusable lumber.<sup id="cite_ref-dontwastewood_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dontwastewood-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Every year the United States buries about 33 million tons of wood-related construction and demolition debris in landfills. As anaerobic microorganisms decompose this wood, it will release about five million tons of carbon equivalent in the form of methane gas.<sup id="cite_ref-dontwastewood_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dontwastewood-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_methods_of_deconstruction">Typical methods of deconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deconstruction_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typical methods of deconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Deconstruction is commonly separated into two categories; <a href="/wiki/Structural" title="Structural" class="mw-redirect">structural</a> and non-structural. Non-structural deconstruction, also known as “soft-stripping”, consists of reclaiming non-structural components, <a href="/wiki/Home_appliances" title="Home appliances" class="mw-redirect">appliances</a>, <a href="/wiki/Door" title="Door">doors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">windows</a>, and finish materials. The reuse of these types of materials is commonplace and considered to be a mature market in many locales.</p> 
     <p>Structural deconstruction involves dismantling the structural components of a building. Traditionally this had only been performed to reclaim expensive or rare materials such as used <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimension_stone" title="Dimension stone">dimension stone</a>, and extinct <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>. In antiquity, it was common to raze stone buildings and reuse the stone; it was also common to steal stones from a building that was not being totally demolished: this is the literal meaning of the word <i><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dilapidated" class="extiw" title="wikt:dilapidated">dilapidated</a>.</i> Used brick and dimension limestone in particular have a long tradition of reuse due to their durability and color changes over time. Recently, the rise of environmental awareness and sustainable building has made a much wider range of materials worthy of structural deconstruction. Low-end, commonplace materials such as dimensional <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">lumber</a> have become part of this newly emerging market.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_military" title="United States military" class="mw-redirect">United States military</a> has utilized structural deconstruction in many of its bases. The construction methods of <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a>, among other base structures, are usually relatively simple. They typically contained large amounts of lumber and used minimal <a href="/wiki/Adhesives" title="Adhesives" class="mw-redirect">adhesives</a> and finish-work. In addition, the buildings are often identical, making the process of deconstructing multiple buildings much easier. Many barracks were built during the era prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WWII</a>, and have aged to the point where they now need to be torn down. Deconstruction was deemed very practical due to the abundance of labor the military has access to and the value of the materials themselves.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Natural_disasters" title="Natural disasters" class="mw-redirect">Natural disasters</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes" class="mw-redirect">hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods" class="mw-redirect">floods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsunamis" title="Tsunamis" class="mw-redirect">tsunamis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes" class="mw-redirect">earthquakes</a> often leave a vast amount of usable building materials in their wake. Structures that remain standing are often deconstructed to provide materials for rebuilding the <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_potential">Economic potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deconstruction_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Deconstruction’s economic viability varies from project to project. The amount of time and cost of labor are the main drawbacks. Harvesting materials from a structure can take weeks, whereas demolition may be completed in roughly a day. However, some of the costs, if not all, can be recovered. Reusing the materials in a new on-site structure, selling reclaimed materials, donating materials for <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a> write-offs, and avoiding landfill “tipping fees” are all ways in which the cost of deconstruction can be made comparable to demolition.</p> 
     <p>Reclaiming the materials for a new on-site structure is the most economically and environmentally efficient option. Tipping fees and the costs of new materials are avoided; in addition, the transportation of the materials is non-existent. Selling the used materials or donating them to <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization" class="mw-redirect">non-profit organizations</a> are another effective way of gaining capital. Donations to NPO’s such as <a href="/wiki/Habitat_for_Humanity" title="Habitat for Humanity">Habitat for Humanity</a>’s ReStore are tax deductible. Many times it is possible to claim the value to be half of what that particular material would cost new. When donating rare or <a href="/wiki/Antique" title="Antique">antique</a> components it is sometimes possible to claim a higher value than a comparable, brand-new material.</p> 
     <p>Value can also be added to new structures that are built by implementing reused materials. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Green_Building_Council" title="United States Green Building Council" class="mw-redirect">United States Green Building Council</a>'s program entitled <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (<b>LEED</b>) offers seven credits relating to reusing materials. (This accounts for seven out of a maximum sixty-nine credits) These include credits for building-shell reuse, material reuse, and diverting waste from landfills. Building shell-reuse is particularly appropriate for shells made of <a href="/wiki/Dimension_stone" title="Dimension stone">dimension stone</a>.</p> 
     <p>Deconstruction is well suited to job training for the construction trades. Taking down a building is an excellent way for a worker to learn how to put a building up. This is vital for the economic recovery of inner-city communities. Unskilled and low-skilled workers can receive on-the-job training in use of basic carpentry tools and techniques, as well as learning teamwork, problem-solving, critical thinking and good work habits.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deconstruction_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>When choosing to deconstruct a building there are some important aspects that need to be taken into consideration. Developing a list of local contacts that are able to take used materials is an essential first step. These might include commercial architectural salvage businesses, reclamation yards, not-for-profit and social enterprise salvage warehouses, and dismantling contractors. Materials that cannot be salvaged may be recycled on-site or off-site, or taken to landfills. The next step involves identifying which, if any, are <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_material" title="Hazardous material" class="mw-redirect">hazardous materials</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lead_paint" title="Lead paint">Lead paint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> are two substances in particular that need to be handled extremely cautiously and disposed of properly.</p> 
     <p>It is common practice, and common sense, to “soft-strip” the structure first; remove all appliances, windows, doors, and other finishing materials. These will account for a large percentage of the marketable components. After the non-structural deconstruction, structural is the next step. It is best to start at the <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roof</a> and work down to the <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(architecture)" title="Foundation (architecture)" class="mw-redirect">foundation</a>.</p> 
     <p>Building components that are dismantled will need to be stored in a secure, dry location. This will protect them from <a href="/wiki/Water_damage" title="Water damage">water damage</a> and theft. Once separated from the structure, materials can also be cleaned and/or refinished to increase value. Building an inventory list of the materials at hand will help determine where each item will be sent.</p>

     <p>"The end of the building’s useful life generates a stream of used materials that can be reprocessed for new construction. The selection of materials for reuse or recycling should not start at the end of the building’s life cycle, it should start at the design stage. Architects and engineers should keep the whole life cycle of the building in mind and select construction materials based on their capacity to be reused or recycled after the building has served its purpose."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>An upstream approach to deconstruction can be implemented into buildings during their design process. This is a current trend in sustainable <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>. Often, simple construction methods combined with high-grade, durable materials work best for DfD structures. Separating layers of a building’s infrastructure and making them visible can significantly simplify its deconstruction. Making components within systems separable also assists in being able to dismantle materials quickly and efficiently. This can be achieved by using mechanical fasteners such as bolts to connect parts. Allowing physical access to the fasteners is another needed aspect of this design. Also, it is important to use standardized materials and assemble them in a consistent manner throughout the project.</p> 
     <p>Some conventional construction methods and materials are difficult or impossible to deconstruct and should be avoided when designing for deconstruction. The use of nails and adhesives significantly slows down the deconstruction process and has a tendency to ruin otherwise reusable materials. Avoid hazardous materials altogether as they are detrimental to the natural environment and are non-reusable. Using mixed material grades makes the process of identifying pieces for resale difficult.</p> 
     <p>Deconstruction is important for more than just the end of a building’s life-cycle. Buildings that have been designed with deconstruction in mind are often easier to maintain and adapt to new uses. Saving the shell of a building or adapting interior spaces to meet new needs ensures that new structures have a small environmental impact.</p> 
     <p>An alternative worth considering is <a href="/wiki/Modular_building" title="Modular building">modular building</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Habitat_67" title="Habitat 67">Habitat 67</a> project in <a href="/wiki/Montreal,_Canada" title="Montreal, Canada" class="mw-redirect">Montreal, Canada</a>. This was a residential structure consisting of separate, functional apartments that could be put together in a variety of ways. As people moved in or out, the units could be reconfigured as desired.</p>
