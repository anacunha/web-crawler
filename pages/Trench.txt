   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Trench</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p>A <b>trench</b> is a type of excavation or depression in the ground that is generally deeper than it is wide (as opposed to a wider <a href="/wiki/Gully" title="Gully">gully</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ditch" title="Ditch">ditch</a>), and narrow compared to its length (as opposed to a simple hole).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, trenches are created as a result of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> by rivers or by geological movement of <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_plates" title="Tectonic plates" class="mw-redirect">tectonic plates</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> field, trenches are often created to install underground <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Utilities" title="Utilities" class="mw-redirect">utilities</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_main" title="Gas main" class="mw-redirect">gas mains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_main" title="Water main" class="mw-redirect">water mains</a> or <a href="/wiki/Telephone_line" title="Telephone line">telephone lines</a>), or later to access these installations. Trenches have also often been dug for military defensive purposes. In archaeology, the "trench method" is used for searching and <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">excavating</a> ancient <a href="/wiki/Ruin" title="Ruin" class="mw-redirect">ruins</a> or to dig into <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> of sedimented material.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_uses">Types and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Some trenches are created as a result of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> by running water or by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> (which may have long since disappeared). Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valleys</a> or more commonly <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trenches</a>, are created by geological movement of <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_plates" title="Tectonic plates" class="mw-redirect">tectonic plates</a>. Some oceanic trenches include the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Trench" title="Aleutian Trench">Aleutian Trench</a>. The former geoform is relatively deep (approximately 10 kilometres (6.2&nbsp;mi)), linear and narrow, and is formed by <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">plate subduction</a> when plates converge.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_engineering">Civil engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Civil engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Utility_tunnel" title="Utility tunnel">Utility tunnel</a>
     </div> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> field of construction or maintenance of infrastructure, trenches play a major role. They are used to place underground easily damaged and obstructive infrastructure or <a href="/wiki/Utilities" title="Utilities" class="mw-redirect">utilities</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_main" title="Gas main" class="mw-redirect">gas mains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_main" title="Water main" class="mw-redirect">water mains</a> or <a href="/wiki/Telephone_line" title="Telephone line">telephone lines</a>). A similar use for higher bulk would be in <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline transport</a>. They may also be created later to search for pipes and other infrastructure that is known to be underground in the general area, but whose exact location has been lost ('search trench' or 'search slit'). Finally, trenches may be created as the first step of creating a <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(architecture)" title="Foundation (architecture)" class="mw-redirect">foundation wall</a>. <a href="/wiki/Trench_shoring" title="Trench shoring">Trench shoring</a> is often used in trenchworks to protect workers and stabilise embankments.</p> 
     <p>An alternative to digging trenches is to create a <a href="/wiki/Utility_tunnel" title="Utility tunnel">utility tunnel</a>. The advantages of utility tunnels are the reduction of maintenance manholes, one-time relocation, and less excavation and repair, compared to separate cable ducts for each service. When they are well mapped, they also allow rapid access to all utilities without having to dig access trenches or resort to confused and often inaccurate utility maps. One of the greatest advantages is public safety. Underground power lines, whether in common or separate channels, prevent downed utility cables from blocking roads, thus speeding emergency access after natural disasters such as earthquakes, hurricanes, and tsunamis. For a comparison of utility tunnels vs. direct burial, see the article referred to above.</p> 
     <p>In some cases, a large trench is dug and deliberately preserved (not filled in), often for transport purposes. This is typically done to install depressed <a href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway" class="mw-redirect">motorways</a>, open <a href="/wiki/Railway_cutting" title="Railway cutting" class="mw-redirect">railway cuttings</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>.</p>

     <p>Trenches have often been dug for defensive purposes. In the pre-<a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearm</a> eras, they were mainly a type of hindrance to an attacker of a fortified location, such as the <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> around a <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> (this is technically called a <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(fortification)" title="Ditch (fortification)">ditch</a>). An early example of this can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Trench" title="Battle of the Trench">Battle of the Trench</a>, one of the early <a href="/wiki/List_of_expeditions_of_Muhammad" title="List of expeditions of Muhammad">Battles of the Islamic Prophet Muhammad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sa.27d_1967_82.E2.80.9384_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sa.27d_1967_82.E2.80.9384-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>With the advent of accurate firearms, trenches were used to shelter troops. Trench warfare and tactics evolved further in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, until systems of extensive main trenches, backup trenches (in case the first lines were overrun) and communication trenches often stretched dozens of kilometres along a front without interruption, and some kilometres further back from the front line.</p>

     <p>This is used for searching and <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">excavating</a> ancient <a href="/wiki/Ruin" title="Ruin" class="mw-redirect">ruins</a> or to dig into <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> of sedimented material to get a sideways (layered) view of the deposits – with a hope of being able to place found objects or materials in a chronological order. The advantage of this method is that it destroys only a small part of the site (those areas where the trenches, often arranged in a grid pattern, are located). However, this method also has the disadvantage of only revealing small slices of the whole volume, and modern archeological digs usually employ combination methods.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
