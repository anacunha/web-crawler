     <p>The <b>dew point</b> (<b>dew point temperature</b> or <b>dewpoint</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> at which <a href="/wiki/Dew" title="Dew">dew</a> forms and is a measure of atmospheric moisture. It is the temperature to which air must be cooled at constant pressure and water content to reach saturation.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WallaceHobbs2006_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WallaceHobbs2006-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> A higher dew point indicates more moisture in the air; a dew point greater than 20&nbsp;°C (68&nbsp;°F) is considered uncomfortable and greater than 22&nbsp;°C (72&nbsp;°F) is considered to be extremely humid.<sup id="cite_ref-WallaceHobbs2006_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WallaceHobbs2006-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <b>Frost point</b> is the dew point when temperatures are below freezing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity">Humidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Humidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Other things being equal, as the temperature falls, the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> rises, reaching 100% at the dew point, at least at ground level. Dew point temperature is never greater than the air temperature, since the relative humidity cannot exceed 100%.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A technical definition follows: The dew point is the temperature at which the <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in a sample of <a href="/wiki/Air" title="Air" class="mw-redirect">air</a> at constant <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure" class="mw-redirect">barometric pressure</a> <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condenses</a> into liquid water at the same rate at which it evaporates.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> At temperatures below the dew point, the rate of condensation will be greater than that of evaporation, forming more liquid water. The condensed water is called dew when it forms on a solid surface. The condensed water is called either <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a>, depending on its altitude, when it forms in the air.</p> 
     <p>The dew point is the saturation temperature for water in air. The dew point is associated with relative <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. A high <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> implies that the dew point is closer to the current air temperature. Relative humidity of 100% indicates the dew point is equal to the current temperature and that the air is maximally saturated with water. When the moisture content remains constant and temperature increases, relative humidity decreases.<sup id="cite_ref-Horstmeyer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horstmeyer-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">General aviation</a> pilots use dew point data to calculate the likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Carburetor_icing" title="Carburetor icing">carburetor icing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, and to estimate the height of a <a href="/wiki/Cumuliform" title="Cumuliform" class="mw-redirect">cumuliform</a> <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a>.</p>

     <p>At a given temperature but <i>independent</i> of <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure" class="mw-redirect">barometric pressure</a>, the dew point is a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Humidity#Absolute_humidity" title="Humidity">absolute humidity</a>, the mass of water per unit volume of air. If both the temperature and pressure rise, however, the dew point will increase and the relative humidity will decrease accordingly. Reducing the absolute humidity without changing other variables will bring the dew point back down to its initial value. In the same way, increasing the absolute humidity after a temperature drop brings the dew point back down to its initial level. If the temperature rises in conditions of constant pressure, then the dew point will remain constant but the relative humidity will drop. For this reason, a constant relative humidity (%) with different temperatures implies that when it's hotter, a higher fraction of the air is water vapor than when it's cooler.</p> 
     <p>At a given barometric pressure but <i>independent</i> of temperature, the dew point indicates the <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> of water vapor in the air, or, put differently, determines the <a href="/wiki/Specific_humidity" title="Specific humidity" class="mw-redirect">specific humidity</a> of the air. If the pressure rises without changing this mole fraction, the dew point will rise accordingly; Reducing the mole fraction, i.e., making the air less humid, would bring the dew point back down to its initial value. In the same way, increasing the mole fraction after a pressure drop brings the relative humidity back up to its initial level. Considering New York (33&nbsp;ft elevation) and Denver (5,280&nbsp;ft elevation),<sup id="cite_ref-denfacts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-denfacts-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> for example, this means that if the dew point and temperature in both cities are the same, then the mass of water vapor per cubic meter of air will be the same, but the mole fraction of water vapor in the air will be greater in Denver.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_human_comfort">Relationship to human comfort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to human comfort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>When the air temperature is high, the body's <i><a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a></i> uses evaporation of <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">perspiration</a> to cool down, with the cooling effect directly related to how fast the perspiration evaporates. The rate at which perspiration can evaporate depends on how much <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">moisture</a> is in the air and how much moisture the air can hold. If the air is already saturated with moisture, perspiration will not evaporate. The body's cooling system will produce perspiration in an effort to keep the body at its normal temperature even when the rate it is producing sweat exceeds the evaporation rate. So even without generating additional body heat by exercising, one can become coated with sweat on humid days.</p> 
     <p>As the air surrounding one's body is warmed by body heat, it will rise and be replaced with other air. If air is moved away from one's body with a natural breeze or a fan, sweat will evaporate faster, making perspiration more effective at cooling the body. The more unevaporated perspiration, the greater the discomfort.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Wet_bulb_thermometer" title="Wet bulb thermometer" class="mw-redirect">wet bulb thermometer</a> also uses <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling" class="mw-redirect">evaporative cooling</a>, so it provides a good measure for use in evaluating comfort level.</p> 
     <p>Discomfort also exists when the dew point is low (below around −30&nbsp;°C (−22&nbsp;°F)). The drier air can cause skin to crack and become irritated more easily. It will also dry out the respiratory paths. <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> recommends indoor air be maintained at 20 to 24.5&nbsp;°C (68.0 to 76.1&nbsp;°F) with a 20-60% relative humidity (a dew point of −4.5 to 15.5&nbsp;°C (23.9 to 59.9&nbsp;°F)).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Lower dew points, less than 10&nbsp;°C (50&nbsp;°F), correlate with lower ambient temperatures and the body requires less cooling. A lower dew point can go along with a high temperature only at extremely low relative humidity (see graph below), allowing for relatively effective cooling.</p> 
     <p>Those accustomed to <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate" class="mw-redirect">continental climates</a> often begin to feel uncomfortable when the dew point reaches between 15 and 20&nbsp;°C (59 and 68&nbsp;°F). Most inhabitants of these areas will consider dew points above 21&nbsp;°C (70&nbsp;°F) oppressive.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Devices called dew point meters are used to measure dew point over a wide range of temperatures. These devices consist of a polished metal mirror which is cooled as air is passed over it. The temperature at which dew forms is, by definition, the dew point. Manual devices of this sort can be used to calibrate other types of humidity sensors, and automatic sensors may be used in a control loop with a humidifier or dehumidifier to control the dew point of the air in a building or in a smaller space for a manufacturing process.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_values">Extreme values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extreme values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A dew point of 33&nbsp;°C (91&nbsp;°F) was observed at 2:00&nbsp;p.m. on July 12, 1987, in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Florida" title="Melbourne, Florida">Melbourne, Florida</a>. A dew point of 32&nbsp;°C (90&nbsp;°F) has been observed in the United States on at least two occasions: <a href="/wiki/Appleton,_Wisconsin" title="Appleton, Wisconsin">Appleton, Wisconsin</a>, at 5:00&nbsp;p.m. on July 13, 1995, and New Orleans Naval Air Station at 5:00&nbsp;p.m. on July 30, 1987. A dew point of 35&nbsp;°C (95&nbsp;°F) was observed at <a href="/wiki/Dhahran" title="Dhahran">Dhahran</a>, Saudi Arabia, at 3:00&nbsp;p.m. on July 8, 2003. Dew points this high are extremely rare occurrences.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_dew_point">Calculating the dew point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calculating the dew point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">

     <p>A well-known approximation used to calculate the dew point, <i>T<sub>dp</sub></i>, given just the actual ("dry bulb") air temperature, <i>T</i> (in degrees Celsius) and <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> (in percent), <i>RH</i>, is the <i>Magnus formula</i>:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd> 
       <dl> 
        <dd> 
         <dl> 
          <dd>
           <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\begin{align}
\gamma(T,R\!H)&amp;=\ln\left(\frac{R\!H}{100}\right)+\frac{bT}{c+T};\\
T_{dp}&amp;= \frac{c\gamma(T,R\!H)}{b-\gamma(T,R\!H)};\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/b/b/6bbbe9e6c5d1cff794adaeb4c4a9b14e.png">

      <dd>

P_\text{a}(T) &amp; = \frac{R\!H}{100}P_s(T)=a\exp(\gamma(T,R\!H)),\\

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_approximation">Simple approximation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Simple approximation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>There is also a very simple approximation that allows conversion between the dew point, temperature and relative humidity. This approach is accurate to within about ±1&nbsp;°C as long as the relative humidity is above 50%:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_{dp}\approx T-\frac{100-R\!H}{5};" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/9/e/f9e0f3379bfcea746d7b107df293527c.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>and</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="R\!H\approx 100-5(T-T_{dp});\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/0/b/b0b5c85c691202b0dbdb570e09927e70.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>This can be expressed as a simple rule of thumb:</p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p><i>For every 1°C difference in the dew point and dry bulb temperatures, the relative humidity decreases by 5%, starting with RH&nbsp;=&nbsp;100% when the dew point equals the dry bulb temperature.</i></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The derivation of this approach, a discussion of its accuracy, comparisons to other approximations, and more information on the history and applications of the dew point are given in the Bulletin of the American Meteorological Society.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For temperatures in degrees Fahrenheit, these approximations work out to</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" T_{dp:f}\approx T_{f}-\frac{9}{25}(100-R\!H);" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/b/f/fbfa42ffd7316d2b55255b50acecc112.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>and</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" R\!H\approx 100-\frac{25}{9}(T_{f}-T_{dp:f});" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/5/8/a5841ed797eada6163ea41c9fb13bc2b.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>For example, a relative humidity of 100% means dew point is the same as air temp. For 90% RH, dew point is 3 degrees Fahrenheit lower than air temp. For every 10 percent lower, dew point drops 3&nbsp;°F.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frost_point">Frost point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dew_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Frost point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The frost point is similar to the dew point, in that it is the temperature to which a given parcel of humid air must be cooled, at constant barometric pressure, for water vapor to be <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(phase_transition)" title="Deposition (phase transition)">deposited</a> on a surface as ice without going through the liquid phase. (Compare with <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a>.) The frost point for a given parcel of air is always higher than the dew point, as the stronger bonding between water molecules on the surface of ice requires higher temperature to break.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
