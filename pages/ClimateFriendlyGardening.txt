    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b>Climate-friendly gardening</b> is <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a> in ways which reduce emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases" class="mw-redirect">greenhouse gases</a> from <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> and encourage the absorption of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> by <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> in order to aid the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> To be a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">climate-friendly</a> <a href="/wiki/Gardener" title="Gardener">gardener</a> means considering both what happens in a garden and the materials brought into it and the impact they have on <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardening_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardening-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It can also include garden features or activities in the garden that help to reduce greenhouse gas emissions elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use_and_greenhouse_gases">Land use and greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Land use and greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Most of the excess <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> causing <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> have come from burning <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>. But a special report from the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) estimated that in the last 150 years fossil fuels and <a href="/wiki/Cement#CO2_emissions" title="Cement">cement production</a> were responsible for only about two-thirds of climate change: the other third has been caused by human <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The three main greenhouse gases produced by <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">unsustainable</a> land use are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mitigating_Climate_Change_through_Food_and_Land_Use_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitigating_Climate_Change_through_Food_and_Land_Use-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">Black carbon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> can also be caused by unsustainable land use, and, although not a gas, can behave like greenhouse gases and contribute to climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullstein_editor_2011_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullstein_editor_2011-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bond_2013_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bond_2013-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide">Carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a>, CO<sub>2</sub>, is a natural part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, but human <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land uses</a> often add more, especially from <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">cultivation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, and other natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> are turned into <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable fields</a>, buildings and roads, the <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">carbon held in the soil</a> and vegetation becomes extra carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> to trap more heat in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Gardener" title="Gardener">Gardeners</a> may cause extra carbon dioxide to be added to the atmosphere in several ways:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Using <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potting_compost" title="Potting compost" class="mw-redirect">potting compost</a> containing peat;<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Buying <a href="/wiki/Garden_furniture" title="Garden furniture">garden furniture</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wooden</a> products made from woodland which has been destroyed rather than taken as a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable crop</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_land_management" title="Sustainable land management">sustainably managed</a> woodland;<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Digging soil and leaving it bare so that the carbon in soil organic matter is <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidised</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Using <a href="/wiki/Power_tool" title="Power tool">power tools</a> which burn <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> generated by burning fossil fuel;<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ecological_Gardening_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecological_Gardening-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Using <a href="/wiki/Patio_heater" title="Patio heater">patio heaters</a>;</li> 
      <li>Heating <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> by burning fossil fuel or electricity generated by burning fossil fuel;<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Burning <a href="/wiki/Pruning" title="Pruning">garden prunings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Bonfire" title="Bonfire">bonfire</a>;</li> 
      <li>Buying <a href="/wiki/Tools" title="Tools" class="mw-redirect">tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">synthetic nitrogen fertilizers</a> (over 2 kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent is produced in the manufacture of each kilogram of ammonium nitrate<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup>), and other materials which have been manufactured using fossil fuel;<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Julian_Allwood_and_Jonathan_Cullen_.27Sustainable_Materials_-_with_both_eyes_open.27_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Julian_Allwood_and_Jonathan_Cullen_.27Sustainable_Materials_-_with_both_eyes_open.27-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Heating and treating <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pools</a> by burning fossil fuel or electricity generated by burning fossil fuel;<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">Watering</a> their gardens with <a href="/wiki/Tapwater" title="Tapwater" class="mw-redirect">tapwater</a>, which has been treated and pumped by burning fossil fuel, with a greenhouse gas impact of about 1&nbsp;kg CO<sub>2</sub>e/m<sup>3</sup> water.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Livesley_2010_273.E2.80.93293_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livesley_2010_273.E2.80.93293-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Gardeners will also be responsible for extra carbon dioxide when they buy garden products which have been transported by vehicles powered by fossil fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane">Methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>, CH<sub>4</sub>, is a natural part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, but human <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land uses</a> often add more, especially from <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaerobic" class="extiw" title="wikt:anaerobic">anaerobic</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, artificial <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Rice#Environmental_impacts" title="Rice">rice fields</a>, and from the <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">guts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">farm animals</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dave_Reay_2010_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dave_Reay_2010-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Gardener" title="Gardener">Gardeners</a> may cause extra methane to be added to the atmosphere in several ways:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Soil_compaction" title="Soil compaction">Compacting</a> soil so that it becomes anaerobic, for example by treading on soil when it is wet;</li> 
      <li>Allowing <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> heaps to become compacted and anaerobic;<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Creating homemade liquid feed by putting the leaves of plants such as <a href="/wiki/Comfrey#Fertilizer_uses" title="Comfrey">comfrey under water</a>, with the unintended consequence that the plants may release methane as they <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decay</a>;</li> 
      <li>Killing pernicious <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> by covering them with water, with the unintended consequence that the plants may release methane as they decay;</li> 
      <li>Allowing <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a> to become anaerobic, for example by adding unsuitable <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> species which stir up sediment that then blocks light from and kills <a href="/wiki/Water_aeration#Natural_aeration" title="Water aeration">submerged oxygenating plants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Garden_Pond_for_Wildlife_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Garden_Pond_for_Wildlife-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrous_oxide">Nitrous oxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nitrous oxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a>, N<sub>2</sub>O, is a natural part of the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a>, but human <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land uses</a> often add more.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Gardeners may cause extra nitrous oxide to be added to the atmosphere by:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Using synthetic nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>, for example "weed and feed" on <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawns</a>, especially if it is applied when plants are not actively growing, the soil is compacted, or when other factors are limiting so that the plants cannot make use of the nitrogen;<sup id="cite_ref-Livesley_2010_273.E2.80.93293_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livesley_2010_273.E2.80.93293-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_21_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_21-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Compacting the soil (for example by working in the garden when the soil is wet) which will increase the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Nitrate#Uses" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a> to nitrous oxide by soil bacteria;<sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Burning garden waste on <a href="/wiki/Bonfire" title="Bonfire">bonfires</a>.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_carbon">Black carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Black carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Black carbon is not a gas, but it acts like a greenhouse gas because it can be <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspended</a> in the atmosphere and absorb <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">heat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullstein_editor_2011_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullstein_editor_2011-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bond_2013_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bond_2013-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Gardeners may cause extra black carbon to be added to the atmosphere by burning garden <a href="/wiki/Pruning" title="Pruning">prunings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> on bonfires, especially if the waste is wet and becomes black carbon in the form of soot.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Gardeners will also be responsible for extra black carbon produced when they buy garden products which have been transported by vehicles powered by <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> especially the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">diesel</a> used in most lorries.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gardening_to_reduce_greenhouse_gas_emissions_and_absorb_carbon_dioxide">Gardening to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and absorb carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gardening to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and absorb carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>There are many ways in which climate-friendly gardeners may reduce their contribution to climate change and help their gardens absorb <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_20-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners can find good ideas in many other <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a> approaches:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">Agroforestry</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">Forest gardening</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">Orchards</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Organic_gardening" title="Organic gardening" class="mw-redirect">Organic gardening</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">Permaculture</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Rain_garden" title="Rain garden">Rain garden</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Vegan_organic_gardening" title="Vegan organic gardening">Vegan organic gardening</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Water-wise_gardening" title="Water-wise gardening" class="mw-redirect">Water-wise gardening</a>;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Wildlife_garden" title="Wildlife garden">Wildlife garden</a>.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_and_enhancing_carbon_stores">Protecting and enhancing carbon stores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protecting and enhancing carbon stores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>Climate-friendly gardening includes actions which protect <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Main_components" title="Carbon cycle">carbon stores</a> beyond gardens. The biggest carbon stores in land are in soil; the two habitat types with the biggest carbon stores per hectare are <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>; and woods absorb more carbon dioxide per hectare per year than most other habitats. Climate-friendly gardeners therefore aim to ensure that nothing they do will harm these habitats.</p> 
     <p>According to Morison and Morecroft&nbsp;(eds.)'s <i>Plant Growth and Climate Change</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Plant_Growth_and_Climate_Change_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plant_Growth_and_Climate_Change-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Net_primary_productivity" title="Net primary productivity" class="mw-redirect">net primary productivity</a> (the net amount of carbon absorbed each year) of various habitats is:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest" class="mw-redirect">Tropical forests</a>: 12.5 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Temperate_forest" title="Temperate forest">Temperate forests</a>: 7.7 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">Temperate grasslands</a>: 3.7 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year;</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Cropland" title="Cropland" class="mw-redirect">Croplands</a>: 3.1 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>'s Special Report <i>Land Use, Land-Use Change and Forestry</i>&nbsp;<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> lists the carbon contained in different global habitats as:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Wetlands: 643 tonnes carbon per hectare in soil&nbsp;+ 43 tonnes carbon per hectare in vegetation&nbsp;= total 686 tonnes carbon per hectare;</li> 
      <li>Tropical forests: 123 tonnes carbon per hectare in soil&nbsp;+ 120 tonnes carbon per hectare in vegetation&nbsp;= total 243 tonnes carbon per hectare;</li> 
      <li>Temperate forests: 96 tonnes carbon per hectare in soil&nbsp;+ 57 tonnes carbon per hectare in vegetation&nbsp;= total 153 tonnes carbon per hectare;</li> 
      <li>Temperate grasslands: 164 tonnes carbon per hectare in soil&nbsp;+ 7 tonnes carbon per hectare in vegetation&nbsp;= total 171 tonnes carbon per hectare;</li> 
      <li>Croplands: 80 tonnes carbon per hectare in soil&nbsp;+ 2 tonnes carbon per hectare in vegetation&nbsp;= total 82 tonnes carbon per hectare.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The figures quoted above are global averages. More recent research in 2009 has found that the habitat with the world's highest known total carbon density&nbsp;- 1,867 tonnes of carbon per hectare&nbsp;- is temperate moist forest of <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">Eucalyptus regnans</a> in the Central Highlands of south-east <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>; and, in general, that temperate forests contain more carbon than either <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest" class="mw-redirect">boreal forests</a> or tropical forests.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_stores_in_Britain">Carbon stores in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Carbon stores in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>According to Milne and Brown's 1997 paper "Carbon in the vegetation and soils of Great Britain",<sup id="cite_ref-Carbon_in_the_vegetation_and_soils_of_Great_Britain_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carbon_in_the_vegetation_and_soils_of_Great_Britain-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> and soil are estimated to contain 9952 million tonnes of carbon, of which almost all is in the soil, and most in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">peatland</a> soil:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Soils in Scotland: 6948 million tonnes carbon;</li> 
      <li>Soils in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>: 2890 million tonnes carbon;</li> 
      <li>Vegetation in British <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> (which cover only 11% of Britain's land area): 91 million tonnes carbon;</li> 
      <li>Other vegetation: 23 million tonnes carbon.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>A 2005 report<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> suggested that British woodland soil may contain as much as 250 tonnes of carbon per hectare.</p> 
     <p>Many studies of <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a> only study the carbon in the top 30 centimetres, but soil is often much deeper than that, especially below woodland. One 2009 study of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Main_components" title="Carbon cycle">carbon stores</a> by Keith Dyson and others gives figures for soil carbon down to 100&nbsp;cm below the <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>, including "Forestland", "Cropland" and "Grassland", covered by the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> reporting requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Forestland soils: average figures in tonnes carbon per hectare are 160 (England), 428 (Scotland), 203 (Wales), and 366 (<a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>).</li> 
      <li>Grassland soils: average figures in tonnes carbon per hectare are 148 (England), 386 (Scotland), 171 (Wales), and 304 (Northern Ireland).</li> 
      <li>Cropland soils: average figures in tonnes carbon per hectare are 110 (England), 159 (Scotland), 108 (Wales), and 222 (Northern Ireland).</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_carbon_stores_in_wetland">Protecting carbon stores in wetland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Protecting carbon stores in wetland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners choose <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>-free <a href="/wiki/Potting_compost" title="Potting compost" class="mw-redirect">composts</a><sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> because some of the planet's biggest <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Main_components" title="Carbon cycle">carbon stores</a> are in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, and especially in the <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">peatland</a> soil of <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>'s Special Report <i>Land Use, Land-Use Change and Forestry</i> gives a figure of 2011 gigatonnes of carbon for global carbon stocks in the top 1 metre of soils, much more than the carbon stores in the vegetation or the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners also avoid using <a href="/wiki/Tapwater" title="Tapwater" class="mw-redirect">tapwater</a> not only because of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> emitted when fossil fuels are burnt to treat and pump water,<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> but because if water is taken from wetlands then carbon stores are more likely to be oxidised to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A climate-friendly garden therefore does not contain large <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigated</a> <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawns</a>, but instead includes <a href="/wiki/Water-butt" title="Water-butt" class="mw-redirect">water-butts</a> to collect <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainwater</a>; water-thrifty plants which survive on rainwater and do not need watering after they are established; <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedges</a> to <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">shelter</a> gardens from the drying effects of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Groundcover" title="Groundcover">groundcover</a> plants and <a href="/wiki/Mulch#Organic_mulches" title="Mulch">organic mulch</a> to protect the soil and keep it moist.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup>p.&nbsp;242</sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup>p.&nbsp;80–82</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gardening_Without_Water_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardening_Without_Water-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners will ensure that any paved surfaces in their gardens (which are kept to a minimum to increase carbon stores) are <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">permeable</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and may also make <a href="/wiki/Rain_garden" title="Rain garden">rain gardens</a>, sunken areas into which rainwater from buildings and paving is directed, so that the rain can then be fed back into <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> rather than going into <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a>. The plants in rain gardens must be able to grow in both dry and wet soils.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_carbon_stores_in_woodland">Protecting carbon stores in woodland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protecting carbon stores in woodland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Wetlands may store the most carbon in their soils, but woods store more carbon in their living <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> than any other type of vegetation, and their soils store the most carbon after wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Climate-friendly gardeners therefore ensure that any wooden products they buy, such as <a href="/wiki/Garden_furniture" title="Garden furniture">garden furniture</a>, have been made of wood from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_forest_management" title="Sustainable forest management">sustainably managed</a> woodland.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_and_increasing_carbon_stores_in_gardens">Protecting and increasing carbon stores in gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Protecting and increasing carbon stores in gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>After <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rocks" title="Carbonate rocks" class="mw-redirect">rocks containing carbonate compounds</a>, soil is the biggest <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Main_components" title="Carbon cycle">store of carbon</a> on land.<sup id="cite_ref-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Use.2C_Land-Use_Change_and_Forestry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Carbon is found in <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a>, including living organisms (<a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">plant roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi" class="mw-redirect">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>), dead organisms, and <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> One study of the environmental benefits of gardens estimates that 86% of carbon stores in gardens is in the soil.<sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The first priorities for climate-friendly gardeners are, therefore, to:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Protect the soil's existing carbon stores;</li> 
      <li>Increase the soil's carbon stores.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>To protect the soil, climate-friendly gardens:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Are based on <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> rather than buildings and paving;<sup id="cite_ref-Ecological_Gardening_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecological_Gardening-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Have soil that is kept at a relatively stable temperature by <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">shelter</a> from trees, shrubs and/or hedges;<sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Have soil that is always kept covered and therefore moist and at a relatively stable temperature by <a href="/wiki/Groundcover" title="Groundcover">groundcover</a> plants, fast-growing <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manures</a> (which can be used as an <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercrop</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_garden" title="Kitchen garden">kitchen gardens</a> of <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual</a> vegetables) and/or <a href="/wiki/Mulch#Organic_mulches" title="Mulch">organic mulches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners avoid things which may harm soil. They do not tread on the soil when it is wet, because it is then most vulnerable to compaction. They <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">dig</a> as little is possible, and only when the soil is moist rather than wet, because cultivation increases the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a> of soil organic matter and produces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup>p.&nbsp;54–55</sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Department_of_the_Environment.2C_Food_and_Rural_Affairs_2013_12_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Department_of_the_Environment.2C_Food_and_Rural_Affairs_2013_12-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>To increase soil carbon stores, climate-friendly gardeners ensure that their gardens create optimal conditions for vigorous healthy growth of plants, and other garden organisms above and below ground, and reduce the impact of any <a href="/wiki/Limiting_factor" title="Limiting factor">limiting factors</a>.</p> 
     <p>In general, the more <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> that the plants can create each year, the more carbon will be added to the soil.<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup>p.&nbsp;54–55</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> However only some biomass each year becomes long-term <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a> or humus. In <i>Soil Carbon and Organic Farming</i>, a 2009 report for the <a href="/wiki/Soil_Association" title="Soil Association">Soil Association</a>, Gundula Azeez discusses several <a href="/wiki/Environmental_factor" title="Environmental factor">factors</a> which increase how much biomass is turned into humus. These include good <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">soil structure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_biology" title="Soil biology">soil organisms</a> such as fine <a href="/wiki/Root_hair" title="Root hair">root hairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mycorrhiza" title="Mycorrhiza">mycorrhizas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> which increase <a href="/wiki/Soil#Structure" title="Soil">soil aggregation</a>, residues from plants (such as trees and shrubs) which have a high content of resistant chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, and plant residues with a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_to_nitrogen_ratio" title="Carbon to nitrogen ratio" class="mw-redirect">carbon to nitrogen ratio</a> lower than about 32:1.<sup id="cite_ref-Soil_Carbon_and_Organic_Farming_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soil_Carbon_and_Organic_Farming-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardens therefore include:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Hedges for shelter from wind;<sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>A light canopy of late-leafing <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> trees to let in enough <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> for growth but not so much that the garden becomes too hot and dry<sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> (this is one of the principles behind many <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a> systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Paulownia" title="Paulownia">Paulownia</a>'s use in China partly because it is late-leafing and its <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopy</a> is sparse so that <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> below it get shelter but also enough light<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup>);</li> 
      <li>Groundcover plants and organic mulches (such as <a href="/wiki/Woodchips" title="Woodchips">woodchips</a> over <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> made from kitchen and garden "waste") to keep soil moist and at relatively stable temperatures;<sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation#Root_nodule_symbioses" title="Nitrogen fixation">Nitrogen-fixing plants</a>, because soil nitrogen may be a limiting factor (but climate-friendly gardeners avoid <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer#Inorganic_commercial_fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">synthetic nitrogen fertilizers</a>, because these may cause mycorrhizal associations to break down);<sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Many layers<sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> of plants, including <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">woody</a> plants such as trees<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and shrubs, other <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennials</a>, groundcover plants, deep-rooted plants, all chosen according to 'right plant, right place',<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> so that they are suited to their growing conditions and will grow well;</li> 
      <li>A wide <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">diversity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plant_disease_resistance" title="Plant disease resistance">disease-resistant</a>, vigorous plants for <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" title="Resilience (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">resilience</a> and to make the most of all available <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niches</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Plants to <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_garden" title="Wildlife garden">feed and shelter</a> wildlife, to increase total biomass, and to ensure <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological control</a> of pests and diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ecological_Gardening_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecological_Gardening-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Make_a_Wildlife_Garden_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Make_a_Wildlife_Garden-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Compost made from garden and kitchen "waste".<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Lawns, like other grasslands, can build up good levels of <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Soil_Carbon_and_Organic_Farming_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soil_Carbon_and_Organic_Farming-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> but they will grow more vigorously and store more carbon if besides <a href="/wiki/Lawn#Grasses" title="Lawn">grasses</a> they also contain nitrogen-fixing plants such as <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and if they are cut using a <a href="/wiki/Lawn_mower#Other_notable_types" title="Lawn mower">mulching mower</a> which returns finely-chopped mowings to the lawn. More carbon, however, may be stored by other perennial plants such as trees<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and shrubs. They also do not need to be maintained using <a href="/wiki/Power_tool" title="Power tool">power tools</a>.</p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners will also aim to increase biodiversity not only for the sake of the wildlife itself, but so that the garden ecosystem is resilient and more likely to store as much carbon as possible as long as possible. They will therefore avoid pesticides,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and increase the diversity of the habitats within their gardens.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_greenhouse_gas_emissions">Reducing greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reducing greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners can directly reduce the greenhouse gas emissions from their own gardens, but can also use their gardens to indirectly reduce greenhouse gas emissions elsewhere.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_gardens_to_reduce_greenhouse_gas_emissions">Using gardens to reduce greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Using gardens to reduce greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners can use their gardens in ways which reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> elsewhere, for example by using the <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> to dry <a href="/wiki/Laundry" title="Laundry">washing</a> on <a href="/wiki/Clothes_line" title="Clothes line">washing lines</a> in the garden instead of using <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">generated</a> by <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> to dry washing in <a href="/wiki/Clothes_dryer" title="Clothes dryer">tumble dryers</a>.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_farmland">From farmland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: From farmland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Juglans_regia,_walnut,_with_ripening_nuts.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG/220px-Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG/330px-Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG/440px-Juglans_regia%2C_walnut%2C_with_ripening_nuts.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Juglans_regia,_walnut,_with_ripening_nuts.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Food is a major contributor to climate change. In the United Kingdom, according to Tara Garnett of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fcrn.org.uk/">Food Food Climate Research Network</a>, food contributes 19% of the country's greenhouse gas emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooking_up_a_Storm_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooking_up_a_Storm-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Soil is the biggest <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Main_components" title="Carbon cycle">store of carbon</a> on <a href="/wiki/Land" title="Land">land</a>. It is therefore important to protect the <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">farmland</a>. Farm animals, however, especially free-range pigs, may cause erosion, and cultivation of the soil increases the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a> of soil organic matter into <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Department_of_the_Environment.2C_Food_and_Rural_Affairs_2013_12_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Department_of_the_Environment.2C_Food_and_Rural_Affairs_2013_12-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Other sources of greenhouse gases from farmland include: compaction caused by farm machinery or overgrazing by <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">farm animals</a> can make soil <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaerobic" class="extiw" title="wikt:anaerobic">anaerobic</a> and produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>; farm animals produce methane; and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer#Inorganic_commercial_fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">nitrogen fertilizers</a> can be converted to <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>.</p> 
     <p>Most farmland consists of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">fields growing annual arable crops</a> which are eaten directly by people or fed to farm animals, and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> used as <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hay" title="Hay">hay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a> to feed farm animals. Some <a href="/wiki/Perennial_crop" title="Perennial crop">perennial food plants</a> are also grown, such as <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Watercress" title="Watercress">watercress</a> grown in water.</p> 
     <p>Although all cultivation of the soil in arable fields produces carbon dioxide, some arable crops cause more damage to soil than others. <a href="/wiki/List_of_root_vegetables" title="List of root vegetables">Root crops</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar-beet" title="Sugar-beet" class="mw-redirect">sugar-beet</a>, and crops which are harvested not just once a year but over a long period such as <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">green vegetables and salads</a>, are considered "high risk" in <a href="/wiki/Catchment-sensitive_farming" title="Catchment-sensitive farming">catchment-sensitive farming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_10_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_10-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners therefore grow at least some of their food,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and may choose <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">food crops</a> which therefore help to keep carbon in farmland <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> if they grow such high-risk crops in small <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_garden" title="Kitchen garden">vegetable plots</a> in their gardens, where it is easier to protect the soil than in large fields under <a href="/wiki/Commercial_agriculture" title="Commercial agriculture" class="mw-redirect">commercial</a> pressures. Climate-friendly gardeners may grow and eat plants such as sweet <a href="/wiki/Cicely" title="Cicely">cicely</a> which sweeten food, and so reduce the land area needed for sugar-beet.<sup id="cite_ref-Plants_for_a_Future_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plants_for_a_Future-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> They may also choose to grow <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> food plants to not only reduce their indirect greenhouse gas emissions from farmland, but also to increase carbon stores in their own gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-Creating_a_Forest_Garden_37-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Creating_a_Forest_Garden-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Plants_for_a_Future_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plants_for_a_Future-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Grassland contains more carbon per hectare than arable fields, but farm animals, especially <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, produce large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, directly and from manure heaps and slurry.<sup id="cite_ref-Dave_Reay_2010_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dave_Reay_2010-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Slurry and manure may also produce nitrous oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_21_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_21-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_4_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_4-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Gardeners who want to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions can help themselves to eat less <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dairy_produce" title="Dairy produce" class="mw-redirect">dairy produce</a> by growing <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a> trees which are a good source of <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">tasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>-rich <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnuts</a> which are an excellent source of the <a href="/wiki/Omega-3" title="Omega-3" class="mw-redirect">omega-3 fatty acid</a> <a href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid" class="mw-redirect">alpha-linolenic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Researchers and farmers are investigating and improving ways of farming which are more sustainable, such as <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest_farming" title="Forest farming">forest farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_agriculture" title="Conservation agriculture">wildlife-friendly farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_management" title="Soil management">soil management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catchment-sensitive_farming" title="Catchment-sensitive farming">catchment-sensitive farming</a> (or water-friendly farming<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup>). For example, the organisation <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.farmingfutures.org.uk">Farming Futures</a> assists farmers in the United Kingdom to reduce their farms' greenhouse gas emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_23_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farming_Futures_Fact_Sheet_23-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Farmers are aware that consumers are increasingly asking for "green credentials". Gardeners who understand climate-friendly practices can advocate their use by farmers.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_industry">From industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: From industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners aim to reduce their consumption in general.<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In particular, they try to avoid or reduce their consumption of tapwater because of the greenhouse gases emitted when fossil fuels are burnt to supply the energy needed to treat and pump it to them.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Instead, gardeners can garden using only rainwater.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gardening_Without_Water_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardening_Without_Water-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Greenhouse gases are produced in the <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacture</a> of many materials and products used by <a href="/wiki/Gardener" title="Gardener">gardeners</a>. For example, it takes a lot of <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">energy</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer#Inorganic_commercial_fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">synthetic fertilizers</a>, especially nitrogen fertilizers. <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">Ammonium nitrate</a>, for example, has an <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> of 67000 kilojoules/kilogramme,<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Gardens_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Gardens-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> so <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">climate-friendly</a> gardeners will choose alternative ways of ensuring the soil in their gardens has optimal levels of nitrogen by alternative means such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation#Root_nodule_symbioses" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing plants</a>.</p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners will also aim to follow "<a href="/wiki/Cradle-to-cradle_design" title="Cradle-to-cradle design">cradle-to-cradle design</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Circular_economy" title="Circular economy">circular economy</a>" principles: when they choose to buy or make something, it should be possible to take it apart again and <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> every part, so that there is no waste, only <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">raw materials</a> to be made into something else.<sup id="cite_ref-Cradle_to_Cradle:_Re-making_the_Way_We_Make_Things_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle_to_Cradle:_Re-making_the_Way_We_Make_Things-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> This will reduce the greenhouse gases otherwise produced when <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource#Extraction" title="Natural resource">extracting</a> raw materials.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_transport">From transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: From transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Gardeners can reduce not only their <a href="/wiki/Food_miles" title="Food miles">food miles</a> by growing some of their own food, but also their "gardening miles" by reducing the amount of plants and other materials they import, obtaining them as locally as possible and with as little packaging as possible. This might include ordering plants by <a href="/wiki/Mail_order" title="Mail order">mail order</a> from a specialist nursery if the plants are sent out <a href="/wiki/Bare_root" title="Bare root">bare-root</a>, reducing transport demand and the use of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>-based composts; or growing plants from <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>, which will also increase <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> and therefore <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" title="Resilience (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">resilience</a>; or growing plants <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetatively</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(plant)" title="Cutting (plant)">cuttings</a> or <a href="/wiki/Offset_(botany)" title="Offset (botany)">offsets</a> from other local gardeners; or buying reclaimed materials from salvage firms.<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_houses">From houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: From houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners can use their gardens in ways which reduce greenhouse gas emissions from homes by:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Using sunlight and wind to dry washing on washing lines instead of fossil fuel-generated electricity to run <a href="/wiki/Clothes_dryer" title="Clothes dryer">tumble dryers</a>;</li> 
      <li>Planting <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">climbers</a> on houses and planting deciduous trees at suitable distances from the house to provide shade during the summer, reducing the consumption of electricity for <a href="/wiki/HVAC#Air_conditioning" title="HVAC">air conditioning</a>, but also such that at cooler times of year, sunlight can reach and warm a house, reducing <a href="/wiki/HVAC#Heating" title="HVAC">heating</a> costs and consumption;<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Planting hedges, trees, shrubs and climbers to shelter houses from wind, reducing heating costs and consumption during the winter (as long as any planting does not create a <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind-tunnel</a> effect).<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup>p.&nbsp;243</sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure_35-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_domestic_garden_.E2.80.93_its_contribution_to_urban_green_infrastructure-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners may also choose to reduce their own personal greenhouse gas emissions by growing and eating <a href="/wiki/Carminative" title="Carminative">carminative</a> plants such as <a href="/wiki/Fennel" title="Fennel">fennel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a> which reduce intestinal gases such as methane.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_greenhouse_gas_emissions_from_gardens">Reducing greenhouse gas emissions from gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate-friendly_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Reducing greenhouse gas emissions from gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>There are some patent sources of greenhouse gas emissions in gardens and some more latent.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Power_tool" title="Power tool">Power tools</a> which are powered by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol" class="mw-redirect">petrol</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">generated</a> by burning other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, emit <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Climate-friendly gardeners may therefore choose to use <a href="/wiki/Garden_tool" title="Garden tool">hand tools</a> rather than power tools, or power tools powered by renewable electricity,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Garden_design" title="Garden design">design their gardens</a> to reduce or remove a need to use power tools. For example, they may choose dense, slow-growing species for <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedges</a> so that the hedges only need to be cut once a year.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecological_Gardening_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecological_Gardening-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Lawns need[?] to be cut by lawn mowers and, in drier parts of the world, are often irrigated by tapwater. Climate-friendly gardeners will therefore do what they can to reduce this consumption by:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Replacing part of or all lawns with other perennial planting such as trees and shrubs with less ecologically demanding maintenance requirements;</li> 
      <li>Cut some or all lawns only once or twice a year, i.e. convert them into <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>;</li> 
      <li>Make lawn shapes simple so that they may be cut quickly;</li> 
      <li>Increase the cutting height of mower blades;</li> 
      <li>Use a mulching mower to return organic matter to the soil;</li> 
      <li>Sow <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a> to increase vigour (without the need for synthetic fertilisers) and resilience in dry periods;</li> 
      <li>Cut lawns with <a href="/wiki/Lawn_mower#Electricity" title="Lawn mower">electric mowers</a> using electricity from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>;</li> 
      <li>Cut lawns with hand tools such as <a href="/wiki/Lawn_mower#By_hand" title="Lawn mower">push mowers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scythe" title="Scythe">scythes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Climate-Friendly_Gardener-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_the_Garden_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_the_Garden-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_Gardening_36-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Gardening-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Greenhouses can be used to grow crops which might otherwise be imported from warmer climates, but if they are heated by fossil fuel then they may cause more greenhouse gas emissions than they save. Climate-friendly gardeners will therefore use their greenhouses carefully by:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Choosing only annual plants which will only be in the greenhouse during warmer months, or perennial plants which do not need any extra heat during winter;</li> 
      <li>Using water tanks as heat stores and compost heaps as heat sources inside greenhouses so that they stay frost-free in winter.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners will not put woody prunings on bonfires, which will emit carbon dioxide and black carbon,<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_for_Gardeners_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology_for_Gardeners-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> but instead burn them indoors in a <a href="/wiki/Wood-burning_stove" title="Wood-burning stove">wood-burning stove</a> and therefore cut emissions from fossil fuel, or cut them up to use as <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a> and increase soil carbon stores,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> or add the smaller prunings to compost heaps to keep them <a href="/wiki/Aeration" title="Aeration">aerated</a>, reducing methane emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> To reduce the risk of fire, they will also choose fire-resistant plants from habitats which are not prone to <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a> and which do not catch fire easily, rather than fire-adapted <a href="/wiki/Wildfire#Plant_adaptation" title="Wildfire">plants from fire-prone habitats</a> which are flammable and adapted to encourage fires and then gain a competitive advantage over less resistant species.</p> 
     <p>Climate-friendly gardeners may use deep-rooted plants such as <a href="/wiki/Comfrey" title="Comfrey">comfrey</a> to bring nutrients closer to the surface topsoil, but will do so without making the leaves into a liquid feed, because the rotting leaves in the <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)#Aquatic_hypoxia" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> conditions under water may emit methane.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Fertilizer#Inorganic_commercial_fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">Nitrogen fertilizers</a> may be <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide#Soil" title="Nitrous oxide">oxidised</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden_12-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Create_an_Eco_Garden-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> especially if fertilizer is applied in excess, or when plants are not actively growing. Climate-friendly gardeners may choose instead to use <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation#Root_nodule_symbioses" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing</a> plants which will add nitrogen to the soil without increasing nitrous oxide emissions.</p>
