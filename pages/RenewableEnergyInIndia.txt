    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b>Renewable energy in India</b> comes under the purview of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_New_and_Renewable_Energy" title="Ministry of New and Renewable Energy">Ministry of New and Renewable Energy</a>. India was the first country in the world to set up a ministry of <a href="/wiki/Non-conventional_energy" title="Non-conventional energy" class="mw-redirect">non-conventional energy</a> resources, in early 1980s. India's cumulative grid interactive or grid tied renewable energy capacity (excluding large hydro) has reached 33.8 GW,<sup id="cite_ref-mnre2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mnre2-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> of which 66% comes from wind, while solar PV contributed nearly 4.59% along with biomass and small hydro power of the renewable energy installed capacity in India.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The development of <b>wind power in India</b> began in the 1990s, and has significantly increased in the last few years. Although a relative newcomer to the wind industry compared with <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> or the US, domestic policy support for <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> has led India to become the country with the fifth largest installed wind power capacity in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-WWEA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWEA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> As of December 2013 the installed capacity of wind power in India was 20149.50 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts" class="mw-redirect">MW</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-mnre2_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mnre2-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> mainly spread across <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> (7162.18 MW), <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a> (3021.85 MW), <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> (3174.58 MW), <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a> (2135.50 MW), <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> (2684.65 MW), <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a> (386.00 MW), <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a> (447.65 MW), <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> (35.10 MW), <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> (1.10 MW), other states (3.20 MW)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It is estimated that 6,000 MW of additional wind power capacity will be installed in India by 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-power_by_2012_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-power_by_2012-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Wind power accounts for 6% of India's total installed power capacity, and it generates 1.6% of the country's power.<sup id="cite_ref-People_And_Planet_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-People_And_Planet-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In its 12th Five Year Plan (2012-2017), the Indian Government has set a target of adding 18.5 GW of renewable energy sources to the generation mix out of which 11 GW is Wind Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Windpower1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Windpower1-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>Indian Wind Energy Alliance (IWEA) is the apex body for the wind energy industry in India. It was launched in December 2014 and Mr. <a href="/wiki/Sumant_Sinha" title="Sumant Sinha">Sumant Sinha</a> is the chairman of IWEA. <sup id="cite_ref-IWEA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWEA-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">Solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_India" title="Solar power in India">Solar power in India</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png/220px-Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png" width="220" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png/330px-Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png/440px-Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png 2x" data-file-width="799" data-file-height="951"></a>

         <a href="/wiki/File:Solar_Resource_Map_of_India.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://solargis.info/doc/48">Solar Resource Map</a> of India

     <p>India is densely populated and has high solar <a href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation" class="mw-redirect">insolation</a>, an ideal combination for using <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> in India. Much of the country does not have an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a>, so one of the first applications of solar power has been for water pumping, to begin replacing India's four to five million diesel powered <a href="/wiki/Water_pump" title="Water pump" class="mw-redirect">water pumps</a>, each consuming about 3.5&nbsp;kilowatts, and off-grid lighting. Some large projects have been proposed, and a 35,000&nbsp;km² area of the <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">Thar Desert</a> has been set aside for <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> projects, sufficient to generate 700 to 2,100&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt" class="mw-redirect">gigawatts</a>.</p> 
     <p>The Indian Solar Loan Programme, supported by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> has won the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Energy_Globe" title="Energy Globe" class="mw-redirect">Energy Globe</a> World award for Sustainability for helping to establish a consumer financing program for solar home power systems. Over the span of three years more than 16,000 solar home systems have been financed through 2,000 bank branches, particularly in rural areas of South India where the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_grid" title="Electricity grid" class="mw-redirect">electricity grid</a> does not yet extend.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ind_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ind-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Launched in 2003, the Indian Solar Loan Programme was a four-year partnership between UNEP, the UNEP Risoe Centre, and two of India's largest banks, the Canara Bank and Syndicate Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-ind_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ind-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Announced in November 2009, the Government of India proposed to launch its <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru_National_Solar_Mission" title="Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission">Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission</a> under the National Action Plan on Climate Change with plans to generate 1,000&nbsp;MW of power by 2013 and up to 20,000&nbsp;MW grid-based solar power, 2,000&nbsp;MW of off-grid solar power and cover 20 million sq metres with collectors by the end of the final phase of the mission in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The Mission aims to achieve grid parity (electricity delivered at the same cost and quality as that delivered on the grid) by 2020. Achieving this target would establish India as a global leader in solar power generation.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_to_energy">Waste to energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Waste to energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Every year, about 55 million tonnes of municipal solid waste (MSW) and 38 billion litres of sewage are generated in the urban areas of India. In addition, large quantities of solid and liquid wastes are generated by industries. Waste generation in India is expected to increase rapidly in the future. As more people migrate to urban areas and as incomes increase, consumption levels are likely to rise, as are rates of waste generation. It is estimated that the amount of waste generated in India will increase at a per capita rate of approximately 1-1.33% annually. This has significant impacts on the amount of land that is and will be needed for disposal, economic costs of collecting and transporting waste, and the environmental consequences of increased MSW generation levels. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.eai.in/ref/ae/wte/wte.html">Waste Energy In India</a><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>India has had a long involvement with <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> technologies. Waste water treatment plants in the country have been established which produce renewable energy from sewage gas, however there is significant un-tapped potential.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Also wastes from the distillery sector are on some sites converted into biogas to run in a <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engine</a> to generate onsite power.</p> 
     <p><b>Types of Waste</b> Waste can be broadly classified into</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Urban_Waste" title="Urban Waste">Urban Waste</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">Industrial waste</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a> Waste</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Biomedical_waste" title="Biomedical waste">Biomedical waste</a></li> 
     </ul>
