     <p>An <b>oil</b> is any neutral, <a href="/wiki/Polarity_(chemistry)" title="Polarity (chemistry)" class="mw-redirect">nonpolar</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> that is a <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> at ambient temperatures and is both hydrophobic (immiscible with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, literally "water fearing") and lipophilic (miscible with other oils, literally "fat loving"). Oils have a high carbon and hydrogen content and are usually flammable and <a href="/wiki/Slip_(vehicle_dynamics)" title="Slip (vehicle dynamics)">slippery</a>.</p> 
     <p>The general definition of oil includes classes of chemical compounds that may be otherwise unrelated in structure, properties, and uses. Oils may be <a href="/wiki/Animal_fats" title="Animal fats" class="mw-redirect">animal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Petrochemistry" title="Petrochemistry">petrochemical</a> in origin, and may be <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> or non-volatile.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> They are used for food, fuel, <a href="/wiki/Lubrication" title="Lubrication">lubrication</a>, and the manufacture of paints, plastics, and other materials. Specially prepared oils are used in some religious ceremonies as purifying agents.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2> 
     <p>First attested in English 1176, the word <i>oil</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>oile</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>oleum</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> which in turn comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">·ºîŒªŒ±ŒπŒøŒΩ</span> (<i>elaion</i>), "olive oil, oil"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and that from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">·ºêŒªŒ±ŒØŒ±</span> (<i>elaia</i>), "<a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> tree", "olive fruit".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The earliest attested forms of the word are the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a> <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">êÄÅêÄ®êÄ∫</span>, <i>e-ra-wo</i> and <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">êÄÅêÅâêÄ∫</span>, <i>e-rai-wo</i>, written in the <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> syllabic script.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_oils">Organic oils</span></h3> 
     <p>Organic oils are produced in remarkable diversity by plants, animals, and other organisms through natural <a href="/wiki/Metabolic" title="Metabolic" class="mw-redirect">metabolic</a> processes. <i><a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">Lipid</a></i> is the scientific term for the <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> and similar chemicals often found in the oils produced by living things, while oil refers to an overall mixture of chemicals. Organic oils may also contain chemicals other than lipids, including <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxes</a> (class of compounds with oil-like properties that are solid at common temperatures) and <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a>.</p> 
     <p>Lipids can be classified by the way that they are made by an organism, their chemical structure and their limited <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in water compared to oils. They have a high <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> content and are considerably lacking in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> compared to other organic compounds and minerals; they tend to be relatively <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">nonpolar</a> molecules, but may include both polar and nonpolar regions as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> and steroids.<sup id="cite_ref-alberts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_oils">Mineral oils</span></h3> 
     <p>Crude oil, or <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, and its refined components, collectively termed <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochemicals" title="Petrochemicals" class="mw-redirect">petrochemicals</a></i>, are crucial resources in the modern economy. Crude oil originates from ancient <a href="/wiki/Fossilized" title="Fossilized" class="mw-redirect">fossilized</a> <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material" class="mw-redirect">organic materials</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Geochemical" title="Geochemical" class="mw-redirect">geochemical</a> processes convert into oil.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The name "mineral oil" is a <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">misnomer</a>, in that minerals are not the source of the oil‚Äîancient plants and animals are. Mineral oil is organic. However, it is classified as "mineral oil" instead of as "organic oil" because its organic origin is remote (and was unknown at the time of its discovery), and because it is obtained in the vicinity of rocks, underground traps, and sands. <i><a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">Mineral oil</a></i> also refers to several specific distillates of crude oil.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Cooking_oil" title="Cooking oil">Cooking oil</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Several edible vegetable and animal oils, and also <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, are used for various purposes in cooking and food preparation. In particular, many foods are fried in oil much hotter than boiling water. Oils are also used for flavoring and for modifying the texture of foods (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Stir_frying" title="Stir frying">Stir Fry</a>).</p> 
     <p>Cooking oils are derived either from animal fat, as <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lard" title="Lard">lard</a> and other types, or plant oils from the <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_oil" title="Corn oil">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_oil" title="Sunflower oil">sunflower</a> and many other species.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmetics">Cosmetics</span></h3> 
     <p>Oils are applied to hair to give it a lustrous look, to prevent tangles and roughness and to stabilize the hair to promote growth. See <a href="/wiki/Hair_conditioner" title="Hair conditioner">hair conditioner</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h3> 
     <p>Oil has been used throughout history as a religious medium. It is often considered a spiritually purifying agent and is used for <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">anointing</a> purposes. As a particular example, <a href="/wiki/Holy_anointing_oil" title="Holy anointing oil">holy anointing oil</a> has been an important ritual liquid for <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span></h3> 
     <p>Color <a href="/wiki/Pigments" title="Pigments" class="mw-redirect">pigments</a> are easily <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspended</a> in oil, making it suitable as a supporting medium for <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">paints</a>. The oldest known extant oil paintings date from 650 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_transfer">Heat transfer</span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Transformer_oil" title="Transformer oil">Transformer oil</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Oils are used as coolants in <a href="/wiki/Oil_cooling" title="Oil cooling">oil cooling</a>, for instance in electric <a href="/wiki/Transformer#Coolant" title="Transformer">transformers</a>. Heat transfer oils are used both as <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolants</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Oil_cooling" title="Oil cooling">oil cooling</a>), for heating (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Oil_heater" title="Oil heater">oil heaters</a>) and in other applications of heat transfer.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lubrication">Lubrication</span></h3> 
     <p>Given that they are non-polar, oils do not easily adhere to other substances. This makes them useful as <a href="/wiki/Lubricants" title="Lubricants" class="mw-redirect">lubricants</a> for various engineering purposes. Mineral oils are more commonly used as machine lubricants than biological oils are. <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">Whale oil</a> is preferred for lubricating clocks, because it does not evaporate, leaving dust, although its use was banned in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>It is a long-running myth that <a href="/wiki/Spermaceti" title="Spermaceti">spermaceti</a> from whales has still been used in NASA projects such as the Hubble Telescope and the Voyager probe because of its extremely low freezing temperature. Spermaceti is not actually an oil, but a mixture mostly of wax esters, and there is no evidence that NASA has used whale oil.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel">Fuel</span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg/170px-Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg/255px-Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg/340px-Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1114"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Mobil_1_motor_oil.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Synthetic motor oil
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Some oils <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">burn</a> in liquid or <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> form, generating <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> which can be used directly or converted into other forms of energy such as electricity or mechanical work. To obtain many fuel oils, <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil" class="mw-redirect">crude oil</a> is pumped from the ground and is shipped via <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tanker</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a>. There, it is converted from crude oil to <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a> (petrodiesel), <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> (and other short-chain <a href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes" class="mw-redirect">alkanes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oils</a> (heaviest of commercial fuels, used in ships/furnaces), <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> (petrol), <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> (historically), and <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied petroleum gas</a>. A 42-gallon barrel (U.S.) of crude oil produces approximately 10 gallons of diesel, 4 gallons of jet fuel, 19 gallons of gasoline, 7 gallons of other products, 3 gallons split between heavy fuel oil and liquified petroleum gases,<sup id="cite_ref-EIA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and 2 gallons of heating oil. The total production of a barrel of crude into various products results in an increase to 45 gallons.<sup id="cite_ref-EIA_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Not all oils used as fuels are mineral oils, see <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil_fuel" title="Vegetable oil fuel">vegetable oil fuel</a>.</p> 
     <p>In the 18th and 19th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a> was commonly used for lamps, which was replaced with natural gas and then electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_feedstock">Chemical feedstock</span></h3> 
     <p>Crude oil can be refined into a wide variety of component <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect">hydrocarbons</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochemicals" title="Petrochemicals" class="mw-redirect">Petrochemicals</a></i> are the refined components of <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil" class="mw-redirect">crude oil</a> and the chemical products made from them. They are used as <a href="/wiki/Detergents" title="Detergents" class="mw-redirect">detergents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers" class="mw-redirect">fertilizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicines" title="Medicines" class="mw-redirect">medicines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paints" title="Paints" class="mw-redirect">paints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastics" title="Plastics" class="mw-redirect">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">synthetic fibers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a>.</p> 
     <p>Organic oils are another important chemical feedstock, especially in <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a>.</p>
