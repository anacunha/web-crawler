     <p>An <b>earth structure</b> is a building or other structure made largely from <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>. Since soil is a widely available material, it has been used in construction since prehistoric times. It may be combined with other materials, compressed and/or baked to add strength. Soil is still an economical material for many applications, and may have low environmental impact both during and after construction.</p> 
     <p>Earth structure materials may be as simple as <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">mud</a>, or mud mixed with straw to make <a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">cob</a>. Sturdy dwellings may be also built from <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">sod</a> or turf. Soil may be stabilized by the addition of lime or cement, and may be compacted into <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a>. Construction is faster with pre-formed <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">mudbricks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compressed_earth_block" title="Compressed earth block">compressed earth blocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthbag_construction" title="Earthbag construction">earthbags</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">fired clay bricks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Types of earth structure include <a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">earth shelters</a>, where a dwelling is wholly or partly embedded in the ground or encased in soil. Native American <a href="/wiki/Earth_lodge" title="Earth lodge">earth lodges</a> are examples. <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">Wattle and daub</a> houses use a "wattle" of poles interwoven with sticks to provide stability for mud walls. <a href="/wiki/Sod_house" title="Sod house">Sod houses</a> were built on the northwest coast of Europe, and later by European settlers on the North American prairies. Adobe or mud-brick buildings are built around the world and include houses, apartment buildings, mosques and churches. <a href="/wiki/Fujian_Tulou" title="Fujian Tulou">Fujian Tulous</a> are large fortified rammed earth buildings in southeastern China that shelter as many as 80 families. Other types of earth structure include mounds and pyramids used for religious purposes, <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanically_stabilized_earth" title="Mechanically stabilized earth">mechanically stabilized earth</a> retaining walls, forts, trenches and <a href="/wiki/Embankment_dam" title="Embankment dam">embankment dams</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil">Soil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Soil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:SoilTexture_USDA.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SoilTexture_USDA.png/220px-SoilTexture_USDA.png" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SoilTexture_USDA.png/330px-SoilTexture_USDA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/SoilTexture_USDA.png/440px-SoilTexture_USDA.png 2x" data-file-width="2368" data-file-height="2272"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:SoilTexture_USDA.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Soil_type" title="Soil type">Soil types</a> by clay, silt and sand composition as used by the 
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA</a>
       </div> 
      </div>

     <p>Soil is created from rock that has been chemically or physically weathered, transported, deposited and precipitated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUSDA1974112-113_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUSDA1974112-113-3"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Soil particles include <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>. Sand particles are the largest at 2 to 0.05 millimetres (0.0787 to 0.0020&nbsp;in) in diameter and clay the smallest at less than 0.002 millimetres (7.9<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−5</sup>&nbsp;in) in diameter.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Both sand and silt are mostly inert rock particles, including <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShahShroff200322_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShahShroff200322-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Clays typically are <a href="/wiki/Phyllosilicate" title="Phyllosilicate" class="mw-redirect">phyllosilicate</a> minerals with a sheet-like structure.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The very small clay particles interact with each other physically and chemically. Even a small proportion of clay affects the physical properties of the soil much more than might be expected.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShahShroff200322_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShahShroff200322-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Clays such as <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a> do not expand or contract when wetted or dried, and are useful for brick-making. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Smectite" title="Smectite" class="mw-redirect">smectites</a>, expand or contract considerably when wet or dry, and are not suitable for building.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoil_Composition_and_Formation.2C_SCDNR-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">Loam</a> is a mix of sand, silt and clay in which none predominates. Soils are given different names depending on the relative proportions of sand, silt and clay such as "Silt Loam", "Clay Loam" and "Silty Clay".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBallNorton2002219_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallNorton2002219-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Loam construction, the subject of this article, referred to as adobe construction when it uses unfired clay bricks, is an ancient building technology. It was used in the early civilizations of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus" class="mw-redirect">Indus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow</a> river valleys, in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. As of 2005 about 1.5 billion people lived in houses built of loam.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20051_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20051-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In recent years, interest in loam construction has revived in the developed world. It is seen as a way to minimize use of fossil fuels and pollution, particularly carbon dioxide, during manufacture, and to create a comfortable living environment through the high mass and high absorption of the material.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20052_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20052-9"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The two main technologies are stamped or rammed earth, clay or loam, called <i>pise de terre</i> in French, and <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a>, typically using sun-dried bricks made of a mud and straw mixture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20052_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKochKochSeidl20052-9"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>c<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

         <a href="/wiki/File:Mud_houses_are_commonly_found_in_various_regions_of_Afghanistan..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Mud houses in 
        <a href="/wiki/Kandahar" title="Kandahar">Kandahar</a>, Afghanistan
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Earth usually requires some sort of processing for use in construction. It may be combined with water to make <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">mud</a>, straw may be added, some form of stabilizing material such as lime or cement may be used to harden the earth, and the earth may be compacted to increase strength.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993290_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993290-11"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mud">Mud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Coursed mud construction is one of the oldest approaches to building walls. Moist mud is formed by hand to make the base of a wall, and allowed to dry. More mud is added and allowed to dry to form successive courses until the wall is complete. With puddled mud, a hand-made mud form is filled with wetter mud and allowed to dry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECornerstones_Community_Partnerships200672_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornerstones_Community_Partnerships200672-12"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In Iran, puddled mud walls are called <i>chine</i> construction. Each course is about 18 to 24 inches (460 to 610&nbsp;mm) thick, and about 18 to 24 inches (460 to 610&nbsp;mm) high. Typically the technique is used for garden walls but not for house construction, presumably because of concern about the strength of walls made in this way.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry1984110_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry1984110-13"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A drawback to the approach is that a lot of time can be spent waiting for each course to dry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844-14"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Another technique, used in areas where wood is plentiful, is to build a wood frame house and to infill it with mud, primarily to provide insulation. In parts of England a similar technique was used with cob.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECornerstones_Community_Partnerships200672_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornerstones_Community_Partnerships200672-12"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cob">Cob</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cob">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg/220px-Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg/330px-Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg/440px-Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Cob_wall_in_Harwell_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1483279.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Cob wall in 
        <a href="/wiki/Harwell,_Oxfordshire" title="Harwell, Oxfordshire">Harwell, Oxfordshire</a>, England, hundreds of years old, thatched to protect it from water
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">Cob</a>, sometimes referred to as "monolithic adobe",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114-15"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_building" title="Natural building">natural building</a> material made from soil that includes clay, sand or small stones and an organic material such as <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>. Cob walls are usually built up in courses, have no mortar joints and need 30% or more clay in the soil. Cob can be used as in-fill in post-and-beam buildings, but is often used for load bearing walls, and can bear up to two stories. A cob wall should be at least 16 inches (410&nbsp;mm) thick, and the ratio of width to height should be no more than one to ten.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114-15"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It will typically be plastered inside and out with a mix of lime, soil and sand. Cob is fireproof, and its thermal mass helps stabilize indoor temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEICAEN2004114-15"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Tests have shown that cob has some resistance to seismic activity. However, building codes in the developed world may not recognize cob as an approved material.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoodnow2007_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodnow2007-16"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sod_or_turf">Sod or turf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sod or turf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Sod_House_Detail_(4245555122).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg/220px-Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg/330px-Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg/440px-Sod_House_Detail_%284245555122%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Sod_House_Detail_(4245555122).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Sod bricks in a house wall
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Cut <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">sod</a> bricks, called <i>terrone</i> in Spanish, can be used to make tough and durable walls. The sod is cut from soil that has a heavy mat of grass roots, which may be found in river bottom lands. It is stood on edge to dry before being used in construction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844-14"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> European settlers on the North American Prairies found that the sod least likely to deteriorate due to freezing or rain came from dried sloughs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERollings-Magnusson201228_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERollings-Magnusson201228-17"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Turf was once extensively used for the walls of houses in Ireland, Scotland and Iceland, where some turf houses may still be found. A turf house may last fifty years or longer if well-maintained in a cold climate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerge2009232_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerge2009232-18"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Icelanders find that the best quality turf is the <i>Strengur</i>, the top 5 centimetres (2.0&nbsp;in) of the grass turf.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerge2009233_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerge2009233-19"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stabilized_earth">Stabilized earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stabilized earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Clay is usually hard and strong when dry, but becomes very soft when it absorbs water. The dry clay helps hold an earth wall together, but if the wall is directly exposed to rain, or to water leaking down from the roof, it may become saturated and collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20074_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20074-20"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Earth may be "stabilized" to make it more weather resistant. The practice of stabilizing earth by adding burnt lime is centuries old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETeterLiuKent19641_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETeterLiuKent19641-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen" class="mw-redirect">bitumen</a> may also be added to earth intended for construction which adds strength, although the stabilized earth is not as strong as fired clay or concrete.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETeterLiuKent19641_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETeterLiuKent19641-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Mixtures of cement and lime, or <a href="/wiki/Pozzolana" title="Pozzolana">pozzolana</a> and lime, may also be used for stabilization.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20075_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20075-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Preferably the sand content of the soil will be 65% – 75%. Soils with low clay content, or with no more than 15% non-expansive clay, are suitable for stabilized earth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20076_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20076-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The clay percentage may be reduced by adding sand, if available.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20078_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20078-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> If there is more than 15% clay it may take more than 10% cement to stabilize the soil, which adds to the cost.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20076_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20076-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> If earth contains little clay and holds 10% or more cement, it is in effect concrete. Cement is not particularly environmentally friendly, since the manufacturing process generates large amounts of carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeable2012_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeable2012-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Low-density stabilized earth will be porous and weak. The earth must therefore be compacted either by a machine that makes blocks or within the wall using the "rammed earth" technique.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish20075_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish20075-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rammed_earth">Rammed earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rammed earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg/220px-Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg/330px-Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg/440px-Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Alte_Schule_Bonbaden_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Old school built of rammed earth in 1836–37 in 
        <a href="/wiki/Bonbaden" title="Bonbaden" class="mw-redirect">Bonbaden</a>, Hesse, Germany
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Rammed earth is a technique for building walls using natural raw materials such as earth, <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>. A rammed earth wall is built by placing damp soil in a temporary form. The soil is manually or mechanically compacted and then the form is removed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFlemingHonourPevsner1966_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlemingHonourPevsner1966-26"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Rammed earth is generally made without much water, and so does not need much time to dry as the building rises. It is susceptible to moisture, so must be laid on a course that stops rising dampness, must be roofed or covered to keep out water from above, and may need protection through some sort of plaster, paint or sheathing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeable2012_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeable2012-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In China, rammed earth walls were built by the <a href="/wiki/Longshan_culture" title="Longshan culture">Longshan people</a> in 2600–1900 BC, during the period when cities first appeared in the region. Thick sloping walls made of rammed earth became a characteristic of traditional Buddhist monasteries throughout the Himalayas and became very common in northern Indian areas such as <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOutlook200838_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOutlook200838-27"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The technique spread to the Middle East, and to North Africa, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> was built of rammed earth. From there the technology was brought to Europe by the Romans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERael200917_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERael200917-28"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Rammed earth structures may be long lasting. Most of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> was made from rammed earth, as was the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Granada" title="Kingdom of Granada" class="mw-redirect">Kingdom of Granada</a>. In Northern Europe there are rammed earth buildings up to seven stories high and two hundred years old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKeable2012_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeable2012-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete">Concrete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Concrete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Romans made durable concrete strong enough for load-bearing walls.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnell200427-28_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnell200427-28-29"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Roman concrete contains a rubble of broken bricks and rocks set in mortar. The mortar included lime and <i>pozzolana</i>, a volcanic material that contributed significantly to its strength.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELancaster20053_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELancaster20053-30"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Roman concrete structures such as the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a>, completed in 80 AD, still stand.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnell200428_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnell200428-31"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Their longevity may be explained by the fact that the builders used a relatively dry mix of mortar and aggregate and compacted it by pounding it down to eliminate air pockets.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnell200429_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnell200429-32"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Although derived from earth products, concrete structures would not usually be considered earth structures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERael20099_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERael20099-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_units">Building units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Building units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mud_brick_or_adobe_brick">Mud brick or adobe brick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mud brick or adobe brick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">Mudbricks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">Adobe</a> bricks are preformed modular masonry units of sun-dried mud that were invented at different times in different parts of the world as civilization developed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19843_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19843-33"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Construction with bricks avoids the delays while each course of puddled mud dries. Wall murals show that adobe production techniques were highly advanced in Egypt by 2500 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19844-14"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Adobe construction is common throughout much of Africa today.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElleh199847_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElleh199847-34"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Adobe bricks are traditionally made from sand and clay mixed with water to a plastic consistency, with straw or grass as a binder.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELookTiller200449_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELookTiller200449-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>d<span>]</span></a></sup> The mud is prepared, placed in wooden forms, tamped and leveled, and then turned out of the mold to dry for several days. The bricks are then stood on end to air-cure for a month or more.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELookTiller200449_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELookTiller200449-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the southwest United States and Mexico adobe buildings had massive walls and were rarely more than two stories high. Adobe mission churches were never more than about 35 feet (11&nbsp;m).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELookTiller200450_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELookTiller200450-37"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Since adobe surfaces are fragile, coatings are used to protect them. These coatings, periodically renewed, have included mud plaster, lime plaster, whitewash<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>e<span>]</span></a></sup> or stucco.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELookTiller200451_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELookTiller200451-38"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Adobe walls were historically made by laying the bricks with mud mortar, which swells and shrinks at the same rate as the bricks when wetted or dried, heated or cooled. Modern adobe may be stabilized with cement and bonded with cement mortars, but cement mortars will cause unstabilized adobe bricks to deteriorate due to the different rates of thermal expansion and contraction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELookTiller200450_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELookTiller200450-37"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressed_earth_block">Compressed earth block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Compressed earth block">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Cebhomes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Compressed_earth_block" title="Compressed earth block">Compressed earth block</a> housing being built in

       </div> 
      </div>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Compressed_earth_block" title="Compressed earth block">Compressed earth blocks</a> (CEB) were traditionally made by using a stick to ram soil into a wooden mold. Today they are usually made from subsoil compressed in a hand-operated or powered machine. In the developing world, manual machines can be a cost-effective solution for making uniform building blocks, while the more complex and expensive motorized machines are less likely to be appropriate. Although labor-intensive, CEB construction avoids the cost of buying and transporting materials.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002138_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002138-40"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Block-making machines may form blocks that have interlocking shapes to reduce the requirement for mortar. The block may have holes or grooves so rods such as bamboo can be inserted to improve earthquake resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002139_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002139-41"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Suitable earth must be used, with enough clay to hold the block together and resist erosion, but not too much expansive clay.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002140_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002140-42"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> When the block has been made from stabilized earth, which contains cement, the concrete must be given perhaps three weeks to cure. During this time the blocks should be stacked and kept from drying out by sprinkling water over them. This may be a problem in hot, dry climates where water is scarce. Closely stacking the blocks and covering them with a polythene sheet may help reduce water loss.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagadish200724_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagadish200724-43"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthbags">Earthbags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Earthbags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Earthbag_construction" title="Earthbag construction">Earthbag construction</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_building" title="Natural building">natural building</a> technique that has evolved from historic military construction techniques for <a href="/wiki/Bunker" title="Bunker">bunkers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149-44"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Local subsoil of almost any composition can be used, although an adobe mix would be preferable. The soil is moistened so it will compact into a stable structure and packed into woven polypropylene or burlap sacks. Polypropylene sacks are better than burlap since they are both cheaper and more durable.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149-44"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The bags are laid in courses, with barbed wire between each course to prevent slipping. Each course is tamped after it is laid.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalkins2008162_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalkins2008162-45"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The structure is thus similar to a rammed earth structure, and may be extremely strong.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002149-44"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Earthbags may be used to make dome-shaped buildings, and may also be used for retaining walls.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002152_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennedyWanekSmith2002152-46"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fired_clay_brick">Fired clay brick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fired clay brick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg/220px-English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg/330px-English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg/440px-English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:English_Bond_-_geograph.org.uk_-_702506.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> English bond bricks from 1454 at the Old College in 
        <a href="/wiki/Tattershall" title="Tattershall">Tattershall</a>, Lincolnshire, England
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The technique of firing clay <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">bricks</a> in a kiln dates to about 3500 BC. Fired bricks were being used to build durable masonry across Europe, Asia and North Africa by 1200 BC and still remain an important building material.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngham2010163_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngham2010163-47"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Modern fired clay bricks are formed from clays or shales, shaped and then fired in a kiln for 8–12 hours at a temperature of 900–1150&nbsp;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngham2010164_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngham2010164-48"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>f<span>]</span></a></sup> The result is a <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> that is mainly composed of silica and alumina, with other ingredients such as quartz sand. The porosity of the brick depends on the materials and on the firing temperature and duration. The bricks may vary in color depending on the amount of iron and calcium carbonate in the materials used, and the amount of oxygen in the kiln.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngham2010164_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngham2010164-48"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Bricks may decay due to crystallization of salts on the brick or in its pores, from frost action and from acidic gases.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngham2010166_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngham2010166-51"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Bricks are laid in courses bonded with mortar, a combination of Portland cement, lime and sand.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKreh199772_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKreh199772-52"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> A wall that is two bricks thick will include stretcher bricks with their long, narrow side exposed and header bricks crossing from side to side. There are various <a href="/wiki/Brickwork" title="Brickwork">brickwork</a> "bonds", or patterns of stretchers and headers, including the English, Dutch and Flemish bonds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELloyd192981_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd192981-53"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_structure_examples">Earth structure examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Earth structure examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_sheltering">Earth sheltering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Earth sheltering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG/220px-Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG/330px-Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG/440px-Iceland_Keldur_Earth_covered_homes.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption">

        <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_turf_house" title="Icelandic turf house">Turf houses</a> in 
        <a href="/wiki/Keldur" title="Keldur">Keldur</a>,

     <p><a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">Earth sheltering</a> has been used for thousands of years to make energy-efficient dwellings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19873_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19873-54"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> There are various configurations. At one extreme, an earth sheltered dwelling is completely underground, with perhaps an open courtyard to provide air and light. An <a href="/wiki/Earth_house" title="Earth house">earth house</a> may be set into a slope, with windows or door openings in one or more of its sides, or the building may be on ground level, but with earth mounded against the walls, and perhaps with an earth roof.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19878_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19878-55"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Pit houses made by <a href="/wiki/Hohokam" title="Hohokam">Hohokam</a> farmers between 100 and 900 AD, in what is now the southwest of the USA, were bermed structures, partially embedded in south-facing slopes. Their successful design was used for hundreds of years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19842_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcHenry19842-56"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Matmata,_Tunisia" title="Matmata, Tunisia">Matmata, Tunisia</a>, most of the ancient homes were built 12 metres (39&nbsp;ft) below ground level, and surrounded courtyards about 12 metres (39&nbsp;ft) square.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19874_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19874-57"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>g<span>]</span></a></sup> The homes were reached through tunnels. Other examples of subterranean, semi-subterranean or cliff-based dwellings in both hot and cold climates are found in Turkey, northern China and the Himalayas, and the southwest USA.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19874_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19874-57"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> A number of Buddhist monasteries built from earth and other materials into cliff sides or caves in Himalayan areas such as <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and northern India are often perilously placed. Starting in the 1970s, interest in the technique has revived in developed countries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19873_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19873-54"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> By setting an earth house into the ground, the house will be cooler in the warm season and warmer in the cool season.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19878_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerGrondzik19878-55"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_American_earth_lodge">Native American earth lodge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Native American earth lodge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>An <a href="/wiki/Earth_lodge" title="Earth lodge">earth lodge</a> is a circular building made by some of the <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> of North America. They have wood post and beam construction and are dome-shaped.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEarth_Lodge.2C_Random_House_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEarth_Lodge.2C_Random_House-60"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> A typical structure would have four or more central posts planted in the ground and connected at the top by cross beams. The smoke hole would be left open in the center. Around the central structure there was a larger ring of shorter posts, also connected by cross beams. Rafters radiated from the central cross beams to the outside cross beams, and then split planks or beams formed the slanting or vertical side walls.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENabokov1989126_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENabokov1989126-61"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The structure was covered by sticks and brush or grass, covered in turn by a heavy layer of earth or <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">sod</a>. Some groups plastered the whole structure with mud, which dried to form a shell.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENabokov1989126_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENabokov1989126-61"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wattle_and_daub">Wattle and daub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Wattle and daub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">Wattle and daub</a> is an old building technique in which vines or smaller sticks are interwoven between upright poles, and then mud mixed with straw and grass is plastered over the wall.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungFowler200046_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungFowler200046-62"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The technique is found around the world, from the Nile Delta to Japan, where bamboo was used to make the wattle.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERacusinMcArleton2012252_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERacusinMcArleton2012252-63"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a>, now in Illinois, USA, wattle and daub houses were built with the floor lowered by 1 to 3 feet (0.30 to 0.91&nbsp;m) below the ground. A variant of the technique is called <i>bajareque</i> in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungFowler200046_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungFowler200046-62"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> In prehistoric Britain simple circular wattle and daub shelters were built wherever adequate clay was available.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESunshine20065_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESunshine20065-64"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Wattle and daub is still found as the panels in timber-framed buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESunshine2006Back_cover_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESunshine2006Back_cover-65"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Generally the walls are not structural, and in interior use the technique in the developed world was replaced by lath and plaster, and then by <a href="/wiki/Gypsum_wallboard" title="Gypsum wallboard" class="mw-redirect">gypsum wallboard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERacusinMcArleton2012252_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERacusinMcArleton2012252-63"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prairie_sod_house">Prairie sod house</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Prairie sod house">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg/220px-Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg/330px-Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg/440px-Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2936" data-file-height="2235"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Omer_Kem%27s_sod_house.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Omer_Madison_Kem" title="Omer Madison Kem">Omer Madison Kem</a>, (later, Representative to the United States Congress) in front of his sod house in Nebraska (1886)
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>European pioneer farmers in the prairies of North America, where there is no wood for construction, often made their first home in a dug-out cave in the side of a hill or ravine, with a covering over the entrance. When they had time, they would build a <a href="/wiki/Sod_house" title="Sod house">sod house</a>. The farmer would use a plow to cut the sod into bricks 1 by 2 feet (0.30 by 0.61&nbsp;m), which were then piled up to form the walls.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite1991228_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite1991228-66"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The sod strips were piled grass-side down, staggered in the same way as brickwork, in three side-by-side rows, resulting in a wall over 3 feet (0.91&nbsp;m) thick. The sod wall was built around door and window frames, and the corners of the wall were secured by rods driven vertically through them. The roof was made with poles or brush, covered with prairie grass, and then sealed with a layer of sod.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorterfield200439_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorterfield200439-67"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Sod houses were strong and often lasted many years, but they were damp and dirty unless the interior walls were plastered.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite1991228_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite1991228-66"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The roofs tended to leak, and sometimes collapsed in a rainstorm.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorterfield200439_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorterfield200439-67"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mud_brick_buildings">Mud brick buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mud brick buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>There are innumerable examples of mud brick or adobe building around the world. The walled city of <a href="/wiki/Shibam" title="Shibam">Shibam</a> in Yemen, designated a World Heritage Site in 1982, is known for its ten-story unreinforced mud-brick buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJerome2006144_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJerome2006144-68"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Djinguereber_Mosque" title="Djinguereber Mosque">Djinguereber Mosque</a> of <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>, Mali, was first built at the start of the 14th century AD (8th century AH) from round mud bricks and a stone-mud misture, and was rebuilt several times afterwards, steadily growing in size.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESliwoski200770_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESliwoski200770-69"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Further south in Mali, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_Djenn%C3%A9" title="Great Mosque of Djenné">Great Mosque</a> of <a href="/wiki/Djenn%C3%A9" title="Djenné">Djenné</a>, a dramatic example of Sahel mudbrick architecture. was built in 1907, based on the design of an earlier Great Mosque first built on the site in 1280. Mudbrick requires maintenance, and the fundamentalist ruler <a href="/wiki/Seku_Amadu" title="Seku Amadu">Seku Amadu</a> had let the previous mosque collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHam2009498_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHam2009498-70"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Casa_Grande_Ruins_National_Monument" title="Casa Grande Ruins National Monument">Casa Grande Ruins</a>, now a national monument in Arizona protected by a modern roof, is a massive four-story adobe structure built by Hohokam people between 1200 and 1450 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERael200913_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERael200913-71"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> The first European to record the great house was a Jesuit priest, Father <a href="/wiki/Eusebio_Kino" title="Eusebio Kino">Eusebio Kino</a>, who visited the site in 1694. At that time it had long been abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHouk19966_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouk19966-72"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> By the time a temporary roof was installed in 1903 the adobe building had been standing empty and unmaintained for hundreds of years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHouk19969_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouk19969-73"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Huaca_de_la_Luna" title="Huaca de la Luna">Huaca de la Luna</a> in what is now northern Peru is a large adobe temple built by the <a href="/wiki/Moche_culture" title="Moche culture">Moche people</a>. The building went through a series of construction phases, growing eventually to a height of about 32 metres (105&nbsp;ft), with three main platforms, four plazas and many smaller rooms and enclosures. The walls were covered by striking multi-colored murals and freizes; those visible today date from about 400–610 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJackson200822_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJackson200822-74"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed"> 
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 162px"> 
       <div style="width: 162px">

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toulous">Toulous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Toulous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Snail_pit_tulou.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Snail_pit_tulou.jpg/220px-Snail_pit_tulou.jpg" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Snail_pit_tulou.jpg/330px-Snail_pit_tulou.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Snail_pit_tulou.jpg/440px-Snail_pit_tulou.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1820" data-file-height="1267"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Snail_pit_tulou.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Tianluokeng_Tulou_cluster" title="Tianluokeng Tulou cluster">Tianluokeng Tulou cluster</a> in 
        <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> province, China
       </div>

     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Fujian_Tulou" title="Fujian Tulou">Fujian Tulou</a> is a type of rural dwelling of the <a href="/wiki/Hakka_people" title="Hakka people">Hakka people</a> in the mountainous areas in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a>, China.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeedham1971133.E2.80.93134_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeedham1971133.E2.80.93134-75"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> They were mostly built between the 13th and the 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFujian_Tulou.2C_UNESCO_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFujian_Tulou.2C_UNESCO-76"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> A tulou is a large, enclosed and fortified earth building, rectangular or circular, with very thick load-bearing <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a> walls between three and five stories high. A toulou might house up to 80 families. Smaller interior buildings are often enclosed by these huge peripheral walls which can contain halls, storehouses, wells and living areas. The structure resembles a small fortified city.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEarthen_Houses_.28Tulou.29.2C_Fujian_Province_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEarthen_Houses_.28Tulou.29.2C_Fujian_Province-77"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> The walls are formed by compacting earth mixed with stone, bamboo, wood and other readily available materials, and are to 6 feet (1.8&nbsp;m) thick. The result is a well-lit, well-ventilated, windproof and earthquake-proof building that is warm in winter and cool in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEarthen_Houses_.28Tulou.29.2C_Fujian_Province_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEarthen_Houses_.28Tulou.29.2C_Fujian_Province-77"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mounds_and_pyramids">Mounds and pyramids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mounds and pyramids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg/220px-Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg/330px-Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg/440px-Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2576" data-file-height="1932"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_ziggurat_at_Ali_Air_Base_Iraq_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Ziggurat at 
        <a href="/wiki/Ali_Air_Base" title="Ali Air Base">Ali Air Base</a> in Iraq
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Ziggurat" title="Ziggurat">Ziggurats</a> were elevated temples constructed by the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerians</a> between the end of the 4th millennium BC and the 2nd millennium BC, rising in a series of terraces to a temple up to 200 feet (61&nbsp;m) above ground level. The <a href="/wiki/Ziggurat_of_Ur" title="Ziggurat of Ur">Ziggurat of Ur</a> contained about three million bricks, none more than 15 inches (380&nbsp;mm) in length, so construction would have been a huge project.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForrest19696_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForrest19696-78"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The largest ziggurat was in Babylon, and is thought by some to be the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Babel" title="Tower of Babel">Tower of Babel</a> mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. It was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> and only the foundations remain, but originally it stood 300 feet (91&nbsp;m) high on a base about 660 feet (200&nbsp;m) square.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaiella200517_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaiella200517-79"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Sun-dried bricks were used for the interior and kiln-fired bricks for the facing. The bricks were held together by clay or bitumen.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaiella200518_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaiella200518-80"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Many pre-Columbian Native American societies of ancient North America built large <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramidal</a> earth structures known as <a href="/wiki/Platform_mound" title="Platform mound">platform mounds</a>. Among the largest and best-known of these structures is <a href="/wiki/Monks_Mound" title="Monks Mound">Monks Mound</a> at the site of <a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a> in what became Illinois, completed around 1100 AD, which has a base larger than that of the Great Pyramid at Giza. Many of the mounds underwent multiple episodes of mound construction at periodic intervals, some becoming quite large. They are believed to have played a central role in the mound-building peoples' religious life and documented uses include semi-public <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chief</a>'s house platforms, public <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a> platforms, <a href="/wiki/Mortuary" title="Mortuary" class="mw-redirect">mortuary</a> platforms, <a href="/wiki/Charnel_house" title="Charnel house">charnel house</a> platforms, <a href="/wiki/Earth_lodge" title="Earth lodge">earth lodge</a>/town house platforms, residence platforms, square ground and rotunda platforms, and dance platforms.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindauerBlitz1997169ff_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindauerBlitz1997169ff-81"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFogelson2004741_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFogelson2004741-82"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The 207 feet (63&nbsp;m) <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_of_the_Sun" title="Pyramid of the Sun">Pyramid of the Sun</a> in <a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a>, Mexico, was started in 100 AD. The stone-faced structure contains two million tons of rammed earth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERael200917_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERael200917-28"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthworks">Earthworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Earthworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Retaining_wall,_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg/220px-Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg/330px-Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg/440px-Retaining_wall%2C_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Retaining_wall,_Ramsden_Wood_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1268429.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Retaining wall near 
        <a href="/wiki/Todmorden" title="Todmorden">Todmorden</a>, West Yorkshire, England
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">Earthworks</a> are engineering works created through moving or processing quantities of soil or unformed rock. The material may be moved to another location and formed into a desired shape for a purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMerrittLoftinRicketts199513.1_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMerrittLoftinRicketts199513.1-83"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">Levees</a>, embankments and dams are types of earthwork. A levee, floodbank or stopbank is an elongated natural ridge or artificially constructed <a href="/wiki/Fill_dirt" title="Fill dirt">dirt fill</a> wall that regulates water levels. It is usually earthen and often runs parallel to the course of a river in its <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a> or along low-lying coastlines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetroski20067.E2.80.9311_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetroski20067.E2.80.9311-84"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Mechanically_stabilized_earth" title="Mechanically stabilized earth">Mechanically stabilized earth</a> (MSE) retaining walls may be used for embankments.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994131_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994131-85"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> MSE walls combine a concrete leveling pad, wall facing panels, coping, soil reinforcement and select backfill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasse20003_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasse20003-86"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> A variety of designs of wall facing panels may be used.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasse20003_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasse20003-86"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> After the leveling pad has been laid and the first row of panels has been placed and braced, the first layer of earth backfill is brought in behind the wall and compacted. The first set of reinforcements is then laid over the earth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasse200015_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasse200015-87"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> The reinforcements, which may be tensioned polymer or galvanized metal strips or grids, are attached to the facing panels.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasse20002_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasse20002-88"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> This process is repeated with successive layers of panels, earth and reinforcements. The panels are thus tied into the earth embankment to make a stable structure with balanced stresses.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasse200016-17_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasse200016-17-89"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Although construction using the basic principles of MSE has a long history, MSE was developed in its current form in the 1960s. The reinforcing elements used can vary but include <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geosynthetic" title="Geosynthetic" class="mw-redirect">geosynthetics</a>. The term MSE is usually used in the USA to distinguish it from "Reinforced Earth", a trade name of the Reinforced Earth Company, but elsewhere Reinforced Soil is the generally accepted term.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994131_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994131-85"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> MSE construction is relatively fast and inexpensive, and although labor-intensive, it does not demand high levels of skill. It is therefore suitable for developing as well as developed countries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994132-133_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlfaroBalasubramaniamBergado1994132-133-90"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forts_and_trenches">Forts and trenches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Forts and trenches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright">

     <p>Earth has been used to construct fortifications for thousands of years, including strongholds and walls, often protected by ditches. Aerial photography in Europe has revealed traces of earth fortifications from the Roman era, and later medieval times.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBourgeoisMeganck2005323_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourgeoisMeganck2005323-91"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Offa%27s_Dyke" title="Offa's Dyke">Offa's Dyke</a> is a huge earthwork that stretches along the disputed border between England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams200931_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams200931-92"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> Little is known about the period or the builder, King <a href="/wiki/Offa_of_Mercia" title="Offa of Mercia">Offa of Mercia</a>, who died in 796 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams200932_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams200932-93"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> An early timber and earth fortification might later be succeeded by a brick or stone structure on the same site.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2013118_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPluskowski2013118-94"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Trenches were used by besieging forces to approach a fortification while protected from missiles. Sappers would build "saps", or trenches, that zig-zagged towards the fortress being attacked. They piled the excavated dirt to make a protective wall or gabion. The combined trench depth and gabion height might be 8 to 10 feet (2.4 to 3.0&nbsp;m). Sometimes the sap was a tunnel, dug several feet below the surface. Sappers were highly skilled and highly paid due to the extreme danger of their work.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENolan2008417_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENolan2008417-95"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> (1861−1865) trenches were used for defensive positions throughout the struggle, but played an increasingly important role in the campaigns of the last two years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHess2011xiv_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHess2011xiv-96"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Military earthworks perhaps culminated in the vast network of trenches built during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> (1914−1918) that stretched from <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> to the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> by the end of 1914.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton20106_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton20106-97"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> The two lines of trenches faced each other, manned by soldiers living in appalling conditions of cold, damp and filth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamilton20107_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton20107-98"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> Conditions were worst in the Allied trenches. The Germans were more willing to accept the trenches as long-term positions, and used concrete blocks to build secure shelters deep underground, often with electrical lighting and heating.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHorne2011214_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHorne2011214-99"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embankment_dams">Embankment dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Embankment dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:MicaDam.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MicaDam.JPG/220px-MicaDam.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MicaDam.JPG/330px-MicaDam.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MicaDam.JPG/440px-MicaDam.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:MicaDam.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The 
        <a href="/wiki/Mica_Dam" title="Mica Dam">Mica Dam</a> in 
        <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>An <a href="/wiki/Embankment_dam" title="Embankment dam">embankment dam</a> is a massive artificial <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">water barrier</a>. It is typically created by the emplacement and <a href="/wiki/Soil_compaction" title="Soil compaction">compaction</a> of a complex semi-<a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plastic</a> mound of various compositions of soil, sand, clay and/or rock. It has a semi-permanent waterproof natural covering for its surface, and a dense, waterproof core. This makes such a dam impervious to surface or <a href="/wiki/Seepage#Permeability_and_seepage" title="Seepage" class="mw-redirect">seepage</a> <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDam_Basics.2C_PBS_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDam_Basics.2C_PBS-100"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> The force of the impoundment creates a downward thrust upon the mass of the dam, greatly increasing the weight of the dam on its foundation. This added force effectively seals and makes waterproof the underlying foundation of the dam, at the interface between the dam and its <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream bed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmbankment_dam:_forces.2C_PBS_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmbankment_dam:_forces.2C_PBS-101"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> Such a dam is composed of fragmented independent material particles. The friction and interaction of particles binds the particles together into a stable mass rather than by the use of a cementing substance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham1997_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham1997-102"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Syncrude_Tailings_Dam" title="Syncrude Tailings Dam">Syncrude Mildred Lake Tailings Dyke</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada, is an embankment dam about 18 kilometres (11&nbsp;mi) long and from 40 to 88 metres (131 to 289&nbsp;ft) high. By volume of fill, as of 2001 it was believed to be the largest earth structure in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgenstern200158_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgenstern200158-103"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
