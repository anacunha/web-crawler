     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, the <b>thermal efficiency</b> (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/1/7a19ba3fdf776ed7bf92a998cb72996c.png">) is a <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless</a> performance measure of a device that uses <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>, such as an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerator</a> for example. For a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">power cycle</a>, thermal efficiency indicates the extent to which the energy added by heat is converted to net work output. In the case of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump_and_refrigeration_cycle" title="Heat pump and refrigeration cycle">refrigeration or heat pump cycle</a>, thermal efficiency indicates the extent to which the energy added by work is converted to net heat output.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg/220px-Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg.png" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg/330px-Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg/440px-Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="340" data-file-height="280"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Efficiency_diagram_by_Zureks.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Output (mechanical) energy is always lower than input energy
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In general, <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy conversion efficiency</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> between the useful output of a <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_machine" title="Energy conversion machine" class="mw-redirect">device</a> and the input, in <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> terms. For thermal efficiency, the input, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q_{in} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/c/f1c4c7336615eee1ba6b49f07e1d20bf.png">, to the device is <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, or the heat-content of a fuel that is consumed. The desired output is mechanical <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a>, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W_{out} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/b/88b978f23ccaa794ce4ae75f256ff19d.png">, or heat, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q_{out} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/b/3/2b3236d379d388053d4ad8f7b3c70690.png">, or possibly both. Because the input heat normally has a real financial cost, a memorable, generic definition of thermal efficiency is<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \equiv \frac{\text{output}}{\text{input}}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/f/7/1f7933757faeb8ac4cf4137a6462bcf1.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>From the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a>, the energy output cannot exceed the input, so</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="0 \le \eta_{th} \le 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/6/b/36bb9ae83beaa2db287d93c1e7f95eb0.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>When expressed as a percentage, the thermal efficiency must be between 0% and 100%. Efficiency is typically less than 100% because there are inefficiencies such as friction and heat loss that convert the energy into alternative forms. For example, a typical gasoline automobile engine operates at around 25% efficiency, and a large coal-fueled electrical generating plant peaks at about 46%. <a href="/wiki/W%C3%A4rtsil%C3%A4-Sulzer_RTA96-C" title="Wärtsilä-Sulzer RTA96-C">The largest diesel engine in the world</a> peaks at 51.7%. In a <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> plant, thermal efficiencies are approaching 60%.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Such a real-world value may be used as a <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_merit" title="Figure of merit">figure of merit</a> for the device.</p> 
     <p>For engines where a fuel is burned there are two types of thermal efficiency: indicated thermal efficiency and brake thermal efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This efficiency is only appropriate when comparing similar types or similar devices.</p> 
     <p>For other systems the specifics of the calculations of efficiency vary but the non dimensional input is still the same. Efficiency = Output energy / input energy</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_engines">Heat engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Heat engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Heat engines transform <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>, or heat, <i>Q<sub>in</sub></i> into <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a>, <i>W<sub>out</sub></i>. They cannot do this task perfectly, so some of the input heat energy is not converted into work, but is dissipated as <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> <i>Q<sub>out</sub></i> into the environment</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q_{in} = W_{out} + Q_{out} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/a/2/fa2495adbf5d88f15501f8e3c3f02357.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The thermal efficiency of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> is the percentage of heat energy that is transformed into <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a>. Thermal efficiency is defined as</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \equiv \frac{W_{out}}{Q_{in}} = 1 - \frac{Q_{out}}{Q_{in}}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/6/f/96f572638b7a5d99ea7baf5ee2dc8e0e.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The efficiency of even the best heat engines is low; usually below 50% and often far below. So the energy lost to the environment by heat engines is a major waste of energy resources. Since a large fraction of the fuels produced worldwide go to powering heat engines, perhaps up to half of the useful energy produced worldwide is wasted in engine inefficiency, although modern <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_recycling" title="Energy recycling">energy recycling</a> schemes are beginning to use this heat for other purposes. This inefficiency can be attributed to three causes. There is an overall theoretical limit to the efficiency of any heat engine due to temperature, called the Carnot efficiency. Second, specific types of engines have lower limits on their efficiency due to the inherent <a href="/wiki/Irreversibility" title="Irreversibility" class="mw-redirect">irreversibility</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Engine_cycle" title="Engine cycle" class="mw-redirect">engine cycle</a> they use. Thirdly, the nonideal behavior of real engines, such as mechanical <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> and losses in the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> process causes further efficiency losses.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnot_efficiency">Carnot efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carnot efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> puts a fundamental limit on the thermal efficiency of all heat engines. Even an ideal, frictionless engine can't convert anywhere near 100% of its input heat into work. The limiting factors are the temperature at which the heat enters the engine, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/8/f789d11dded01d1e74f6704a3e584523.png">, and the temperature of the environment into which the engine exhausts its waste heat, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/e/56e080b47f1da865f94fed757bb80777.png">, measured in an absolute scale, such as the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rankine_scale" title="Rankine scale">Rankine</a> scale. From <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">Carnot's theorem</a>, for any engine working between these two temperatures:<sup id="cite_ref-Holman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holman-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \le 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/c/3/2c38c2bf9b428765f846f836e7a81786.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>This limiting value is called the <b>Carnot cycle efficiency</b> because it is the efficiency of an unattainable, ideal, <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible</a> engine cycle called the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a>. No device converting heat into mechanical energy, regardless of its construction, can exceed this efficiency.</p> 
     <p>Examples of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/8/f789d11dded01d1e74f6704a3e584523.png"> are the temperature of hot steam entering the turbine of a <a href="/wiki/Steam_power_plant" title="Steam power plant" class="mw-redirect">steam power plant</a>, or the temperature at which the fuel burns in an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>. <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/e/56e080b47f1da865f94fed757bb80777.png"> is usually the ambient temperature where the engine is located, or the temperature of a lake or river that waste heat is discharged into. For example, if an automobile engine burns gasoline at a temperature of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H = 816^\circ \text{C} = 1500^\circ \text{F}  = 1089 \text{K}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/3/a/13aabd067d5daa302251ee7d768d5572.png"> and the ambient temperature is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C = 21^\circ \text{C} = 70^\circ \text{F} = 294 \text{K}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/5/a/25a440150b5a3fec866843ae5bc1e36d.png">, then its maximum possible efficiency is:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \le 1 - \frac{294 K}{1089 K} = 73.0\%" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/8/698d3003f5795b707d03a18fb440dcb7.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>It can be seen that since <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/e/56e080b47f1da865f94fed757bb80777.png"> is fixed by the environment, the only way for a designer to increase the Carnot efficiency of an engine is to increase <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/8/f789d11dded01d1e74f6704a3e584523.png">, the temperature at which the heat is added to the engine. The efficiency of ordinary heat engines also generally increases with <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>, and advanced structural materials that allow engines to operate at higher temperatures is an active area of research.</p> 
     <p>Due to the other causes detailed below, practical engines have efficiencies far below the Carnot limit. For example, the average automobile engine is less than 35% efficient.</p> 
     <p>Carnot's theorem applies to thermodynamic cycles, where thermal energy is converted to mechanical work. Devices that convert a fuel's chemical energy directly into electrical work, such as <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells" class="mw-redirect">fuel cells</a>, can exceed the Carnot efficiency. <sup id="cite_ref-Sharma_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharma-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Winterbone_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winterbone-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_cycle_efficiency">Engine cycle efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engine cycle efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Carnot cycle is <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible</a> and thus represents the upper limit on efficiency of an engine cycle. Practical engine cycles are irreversible and thus have inherently lower efficiency than the Carnot efficiency when operated between the same temperatures <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/8/f789d11dded01d1e74f6704a3e584523.png"> and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/e/56e080b47f1da865f94fed757bb80777.png">. One of the factors determining efficiency is how heat is added to the working fluid in the cycle, and how it is removed. The Carnot cycle achieves maximum efficiency because all the heat is added to the working fluid at the maximum temperature <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/8/f789d11dded01d1e74f6704a3e584523.png">, and removed at the minimum temperature <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/e/56e080b47f1da865f94fed757bb80777.png">. In contrast, in an internal combustion engine, the temperature of the fuel-air mixture in the cylinder is nowhere near its peak temperature as the fuel starts to burn, and only reaches the peak temperature as all the fuel is consumed, so the average temperature at which heat is added is lower, reducing efficiency.</p> 
     <p>An important parameter in the efficiency of combustion engines is the <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_ratio" title="Specific heat ratio" class="mw-redirect">specific heat ratio</a> of the air-fuel mixture, <i>γ</i>. This varies somewhat with the fuel, but is generally close to the air value of 1.4. This standard value is usually used in the engine cycle equations below, and when this approximation is made the cycle is called an <i>air-standard cycle</i>.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Otto cycle: automobiles</b> The <a href="/wiki/Otto_cycle" title="Otto cycle">Otto cycle</a> is the name for the cycle used in spark-ignition <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> such as gasoline and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel" title="Hydrogen fuel">hydrogen fueled</a> <a href="/wiki/Automobile_engine" title="Automobile engine" class="mw-redirect">automobile engines</a>. Its theoretical efficiency depends on the <a href="/wiki/Compression_ratio" title="Compression ratio">compression ratio</a> <i>r</i> of the engine and the <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_ratio" title="Specific heat ratio" class="mw-redirect">specific heat ratio</a> <i>γ</i> of the gas in the combustion chamber.<sup id="cite_ref-Holman_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holman-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:558</sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/e/3/3e3f680750305fb7f4ebb1e4372138a4.png">
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       Thus, the efficiency increases with the compression ratio. However the compression ratio of Otto cycle engines is limited by the need to prevent the uncontrolled combustion known as 
       <a href="/wiki/Engine_knocking" title="Engine knocking">knocking</a>. Modern engines have compression ratios in the range 8 to 11, resulting in ideal cycle efficiencies of 56% to 61%.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Diesel cycle: trucks and trains</b> In the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_cycle" title="Diesel cycle">Diesel cycle</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel truck and train engines</a>, the fuel is ignited by compression in the cylinder. The efficiency of the Diesel cycle is dependent on <i>r</i> and <i>γ</i> like the Otto cycle, and also by the <i>cutoff ratio</i>, <i>r<sub>c</sub></i>, which is the ratio of the cylinder volume at the beginning and end of the combustion process:<sup id="cite_ref-Holman_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holman-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} = 1-\frac{r^{1-\gamma}(r_c^\gamma - 1)}{\gamma(r_c - 1)} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/e/3/7e3a07a81cc004a2d9b3f1f748ee65c4.png">
      </dd> 
      <dd>
       The Diesel cycle is less efficient than the Otto cycle when using the same compression ratio. However, practical Diesel engines are 30% - 35% more efficient than gasoline engines.
       <sup id="cite_ref-FEG_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FEG-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> This is because, since the fuel is not introduced to the combustion chamber until it is required for ignition, the compression ratio is not limited by the need to avoid knocking, so higher ratios are used than in spark ignition engines.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Rankine cycle: steam power plants</b> The <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> is the cycle used in steam turbine power plants. The overwhelming majority of the world's electric power is produced with this cycle. Since the cycle's working fluid, water, changes from liquid to vapor and back during the cycle, their efficiencies depend on the thermodynamic properties of water. The thermal efficiency of modern steam turbine plants with reheat cycles can reach 47%, and in <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> plants, in which a steam turbine is powered by exhaust heat from a gas turbine, it can approach 60%.<sup id="cite_ref-Holman_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holman-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><b>Brayton cycle: gas turbines and jet engines</b> The <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> is the cycle used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>. It consists of a compressor that increases pressure of the incoming air, then fuel is continuously added to the flow and burned, and the hot exhaust gasses are expanded in a turbine. The efficiency depends largely on the ratio of the pressure inside the combustion chamber <i>p<sub>2</sub></i> to the pressure outside <i>p<sub>1</sub></i><sup id="cite_ref-Holman_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holman-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} = 1 - \bigg(\frac{p_2}{p_1}\bigg)^\frac{1-\gamma}{\gamma} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/6/7/46795afb8340ec7738a84e5aa1f1e175.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_inefficiencies">Other inefficiencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other inefficiencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The above efficiency formulas are based on simple idealized mathematical models of engines, with no friction and working fluids that obey simple thermodynamic rules called the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>. Real engines have many departures from ideal behavior that waste energy, reducing actual efficiencies far below the theoretical values given above. Examples are:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> of moving parts</li> 
      <li>inefficient combustion</li> 
      <li>heat loss from the combustion chamber</li> 
      <li>departure of the working fluid from the thermodynamic properties of an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a></li> 
      <li>aerodynamic drag of air moving through the engine</li> 
      <li>energy used by auxiliary equipment like oil and water pumps</li> 
      <li>inefficient compressors and turbines</li> 
      <li>imperfect valve timing</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Another source of inefficiency is that engines must be optimized for other goals besides efficiency, such as low <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. The requirements for vehicle engines are particularly stringent: they must be designed for low <a href="/wiki/Emissions_reduction" title="Emissions reduction" class="mw-redirect">emissions</a>, adequate acceleration, fast starting, light weight, low noise, etc. These require compromises in design (such as altered <a href="/wiki/Valve_timing" title="Valve timing">valve timing</a> to reduce emissions) that reduce efficiency. The average automobile engine is only about 35% efficient, and must also be kept idling at stoplights, wasting an additional 17% of the energy, resulting in an overall efficiency of 18%.<sup id="cite_ref-FEG_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FEG-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Large stationary <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electric generating plants</a> have fewer of these competing requirements as well as more efficient Rankine cycles, so they are significantly more efficient than vehicle engines, around 50% Therefore, replacing <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion" title="Internal combustion" class="mw-redirect">internal combustion</a> vehicles with <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a>, which run on a <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> that is charged with electricity generated by burning fuel in a power plant, has the theoretical potential to increase the thermal efficiency of energy use in transportation, thus decreasing the demand for fossil fuels.</p> 
     <p>When comparing different heat engines as sources of power, such as electric power or the power to run vehicles, the engine efficiency alone is only one factor. To give a meaningful comparison, the overall efficiency of the entire energy supply chain from the fuel source to the consumer must be considered. Although the heat wasted by heat engines is usually the largest source of inefficiency, factors such as the energy cost of fuel refining and transportation, and energy loss in electrical transmission lines to transport it, may offset the advantage of a more efficient heat engine.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_conversion">Energy conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Energy conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>For a device that <a href="/wiki/Energy_transformation" title="Energy transformation">converts energy</a> from another form into thermal energy (such as an electric heater, boiler, or furnace), the thermal efficiency is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{th} \equiv \frac{Q_{out}}{Q_{in}}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/3/6832b40fae02972f7ab7473bf091b53d.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where the <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/9/f09564c9ca56850d4cd6b3319e541aee.png"> quantities are heat-equivalent values.</p> 
     <p>So, for a boiler that produces 210&nbsp;kW (or 700,000 BTU/h) output for each 300&nbsp;kW (or 1,000,000 BTU/h) heat-equivalent input, its thermal efficiency is 210/300 = 0.70, or 70%. This means that 30% of the energy is lost to the environment.</p> 
     <p>An <a href="/wiki/Electric_resistance_heater" title="Electric resistance heater" class="mw-redirect">electric resistance heater</a> has a thermal efficiency close to 100%.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> When comparing heating units, such as a highly efficient electric resistance heater to an 80% efficient natural gas-fueled furnace, an <a href="/wiki/Engineering_economics" title="Engineering economics">economic analysis</a> is needed to determine the most cost-effective choice.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_fuel_heating_value">Effects of fuel heating value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects of fuel heating value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value" class="mw-redirect">Heating value</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The <b>heating value</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> is the amount of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> released during an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>) and is a characteristic of each substance. It is measured in units of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> per unit of the substance, usually <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, such as: kJ/kg, <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>.</p> 
     <p>The heating value for <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a> is expressed as the HHV, LHV, or GHV to distinguish treatment of the heat of phase changes:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value" class="mw-redirect">Higher heating value</a></b> (<b>HHV</b>) is determined by bringing all the products of combustion back to the original pre-combustion temperature, and in particular condensing any vapor produced. This is the same as the thermodynamic <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">heat of combustion</a>.</li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value" class="mw-redirect">Lower heating value</a></b> (<b>LHV</b>) (or <i>net calorific value</i>) is determined by subtracting the <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization" class="mw-redirect">heat of vaporization</a> of the water vapor from the higher heating value. The energy required to vaporize the water therefore is not realized as heat.</li> 
      <li><b>Gross heating value</b> accounts for water in the exhaust leaving as vapor, and includes liquid water in the fuel prior to combustion. This value is important for fuels like <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, which will usually contain some amount of water prior to burning.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Which definition of heating value is being used significantly affects any quoted efficiency. Not stating whether an efficiency is HHV or LHV renders such numbers very misleading.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_pumps_and_refrigerators">Heat pumps and refrigerators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heat pumps and refrigerators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">Heat pumps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioner" title="Air conditioner" class="mw-redirect">air conditioners</a> use work to move heat from a colder to a warmer place, so their function is the opposite of a heat engine. The work energy (W<sub>in</sub>) that is applied to them is converted into heat, and the sum of this energy and the heat energy that is moved from the cold reservoir (Q<sub>C</sub>) is equal to the total heat energy added to the hot reservoir (Q<sub>H</sub>)</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q_H = Q_C + W_{in} \," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/1/d/81d04a2f48e23f6199ebf47e8a29eceb.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Their efficiency is measured by a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">coefficient of performance</a> (COP). Heat pumps are measured by the efficiency with which they add heat to the hot reservoir, COP<sub>heating</sub>; refrigerators and air conditioners by the efficiency with which they remove heat from the cold interior, COP<sub>cooling</sub>:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{heating}} \equiv \frac{Q_H}{W_{in}}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/d/7/fd7295da6a5c4e2f49a55a3ddaa210a3.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{cooling}} \equiv \frac{Q_C}{W_{in}}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/f/e/6feb1a52dca120471439af839841f036.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The reason for not using the term 'efficiency' is that the coefficient of performance can often be greater than 100%. Since these devices are moving heat, not creating it, the amount of heat they move can be greater than the input work. Therefore, heat pumps can be a more efficient way of heating than simply converting the input work into heat, as in an electric heater or furnace.</p> 
     <p>Since they are heat engines, these devices are also limited by <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">Carnot's theorem</a>. The limiting value of the Carnot 'efficiency' for these processes, with the equality theoretically achievable only with an ideal 'reversible' cycle, is:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{heating}}  \le \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/8/f186338536ed2de35eadf1392c755fa0.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{cooling}}  \le \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/3/833f29767dedfd87d50cf87c423d4fd0.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The same device used between the same temperatures is more efficient when considered as a heat pump than when considered as a refrigerator:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{heating}} - \mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{cooling}} = 1\," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/9/e/59e7271550fccaf79b79b29e24cf9560.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>This is because when heating, the work used to run the device is converted to heat and adds to the desired effect, whereas if the desired effect is cooling the heat resulting from the input work is just an unwanted byproduct.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">Energy efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The 'thermal efficiency' is sometimes called the <b>energy efficiency</b>. In the United States, in everyday usage the <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_energy_efficiency_ratio" title="Seasonal energy efficiency ratio">SEER</a> is the more common measure of energy efficiency for cooling devices, as well as for heat pumps when in their heating mode. For energy-conversion heating devices their peak steady-state thermal efficiency is often stated, e.g., 'this furnace is 90% efficient', but a more detailed measure of seasonal energy effectiveness is the <a href="/wiki/Annual_fuel_utilization_efficiency" title="Annual fuel utilization efficiency">Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency</a> (AFUE).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency_of_heat_exchangers">Energy efficiency of heat exchangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency of heat exchangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A counter flow heat exchanger is generally 100% efficient in transferring heat energy from one circuit to the other, albeit at a slight loss in temperature. However, for a more complete picture of heat exchanger efficiency, exergetic considerations must be taken into account.</p>
