     <p><b>Efficient energy use</b>, sometimes simply called <b>energy efficiency</b>, is the goal to reduce the amount of energy required to provide products and services. For example, <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulating a home</a> allows a building to use less heating and cooling energy to achieve and maintain a comfortable temperature. Installing <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lights" title="Fluorescent lights" class="mw-redirect">fluorescent lights</a>, <a href="/wiki/LED_lights" title="LED lights" class="mw-redirect">LED lights</a> or natural <a href="/wiki/Skylight_(window)" title="Skylight (window)" class="mw-redirect">skylights</a> reduces the amount of energy required to attain the same level of illumination compared with using traditional <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulbs" title="Incandescent light bulbs" class="mw-redirect">incandescent light bulbs</a>. Improvements in energy efficiency are generally achieved by adopting a more efficient technology or production process<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or by application of commonly accepted methods to reduce energy losses.</p> 
     <p>There are many motivations to improve energy efficiency. Reducing energy use reduces energy costs and may result in a financial cost saving to consumers if the energy savings offset any additional costs of implementing an energy efficient technology. Reducing energy use is also seen as a solution to the problem of reducing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions. According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>, improved energy efficiency in <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficient_buildings" title="Energy efficient buildings" class="mw-redirect">buildings</a>, industrial processes and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transportation" title="Sustainable transportation" class="mw-redirect">transportation</a> could reduce the world's energy needs in 2050 by one third, and help control global emissions of greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Energy efficiency and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> are said to be the <i>twin pillars</i> of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a> policy<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and are high priorities in the sustainable <a href="/wiki/Energy_hierarchy" title="Energy hierarchy">energy hierarchy</a>. In many countries energy efficiency is also seen to have a national security benefit because it can be used to reduce the level of energy imports from foreign countries and may slow down the rate at which domestic energy resources are depleted.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Domestic_energy_consumption" title="Domestic energy consumption">Domestic energy consumption</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Energy efficiency has proved to be a cost-effective strategy for building economies without necessarily increasing <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">energy consumption</a>. For example, the state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> began implementing energy-efficiency measures in the mid-1970s, including building code and appliance standards with strict efficiency requirements. During the following years, California's energy consumption has remained approximately flat on a per capita basis while national U.S. consumption doubled.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As part of its strategy, California implemented a "loading order" for new energy resources that puts energy efficiency first, renewable electricity supplies second, and new fossil-fired power plants last.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Lovins' <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Institute" title="Rocky Mountain Institute">Rocky Mountain Institute</a> points out that in industrial settings, "there are abundant opportunities to save 70% to 90% of the energy and cost for lighting, fan, and pump systems; 50% for electric motors; and 60% in areas such as heating, cooling, office equipment, and appliances." In general, up to 75% of the electricity used in the U.S. today could be saved with efficiency measures that cost less than the electricity itself. The same holds true for home-owners, leaky ducts have remained an invisible energy culprit for years. In fact, researchers at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> and their consortium, Residential Energy Efficient Distribution Systems (REEDS) have found that duct efficiency may be as low as 50-70%. The US Department of Energy has stated that there is potential for energy saving in the magnitude of 90 Billion kWh by increasing home energy efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Other studies have emphasized this. A report published in 2006 by the McKinsey Global Institute, asserted that "there are sufficient economically viable opportunities for energy-productivity improvements that could keep global energy-demand growth at less than 1 percent per annum"—less than half of the 2.2 percent average growth anticipated through 2020 in a business-as-usual scenario.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT292006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT292006-7">[7]</a></sup> Energy productivity, which measures the output and quality of goods and services per unit of energy input, can come from either reducing the amount of energy required to produce something, or from increasing the quantity or quality of goods and services from the same amount of energy.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Climate_Change_Talks_2007" title="Vienna Climate Change Talks 2007" class="mw-redirect">Vienna Climate Change Talks 2007</a> Report, under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC), clearly shows "that energy efficiency can achieve real emission reductions at low cost."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appliances">Appliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Green_computing" title="Green computing">green computing</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_lamp" title="Solar lamp">solar lamp</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_saving_lamp" title="Energy saving lamp">energy saving lamp</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Power_usage_effectiveness" title="Power usage effectiveness">power usage effectiveness</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Modern appliances, such as, <a href="/wiki/Freezers" title="Freezers" class="mw-redirect">freezers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oven" title="Oven">ovens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoves" title="Stoves" class="mw-redirect">stoves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dishwashers" title="Dishwashers" class="mw-redirect">dishwashers</a>, and clothes washers and dryers, use significantly less energy than older appliances. Installing a clothesline will significantly reduce one's energy consumption as their dryer will be used less. Current energy efficient refrigerators, for example, use 40 percent less energy than conventional models did in 2001. Following this, if all households in Europe changed their more than ten-year-old appliances into new ones, 20 billion kWh of electricity would be saved annually, hence reducing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by almost 18 billion kg.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the US, the corresponding figures would be 17 billion kWh of electricity and 27,000,000,000&nbsp;lb (1.2<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup>&nbsp;kg) CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to a 2009 study from McKinsey &amp; Company the replacement of old appliances is one of the most efficient global measures to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Modern power management systems also reduce energy usage by idle appliances by turning them off or putting them into a low-energy mode after a certain time. Many countries identify energy-efficient appliances using <a href="/wiki/Energy_input_labeling" title="Energy input labeling">energy input labeling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-app_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-app-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The impact of energy efficiency on peak demand depends on when the appliance is used. For example, an air conditioner uses more energy during the afternoon when it is hot. Therefore, an energy efficient air conditioner will have a larger impact on peak demand than off-peak demand. An energy efficient dishwasher, on the other hand, uses more energy during the late evening when people do their dishes. This appliance may have little to no impact on peak demand.</p>

     <p>A building’s location and surroundings play a key role in regulating its temperature and illumination. For example, trees, landscaping, and hills can provide shade and block wind. In cooler climates, designing northern hemisphere buildings with south facing windows and southern hemisphere buildings with north facing windows increases the amount of sun (ultimately heat energy) entering the building, minimizing energy use, by maximizing <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_heating" title="Passive solar heating" class="mw-redirect">passive solar heating</a>. Tight building design, including energy-efficient windows, well-sealed doors, and additional thermal insulation of walls, basement slabs, and foundations can reduce heat loss by 25 to 50 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-app_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-app-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Dark roofs may become up to 39 C° (70 F°) hotter than the most reflective white surfaces. They transmit some of this additional heat inside the building. US Studies have shown that lightly colored roofs use 40 percent less energy for cooling than buildings with darker roofs. White roof systems save more energy in sunnier climates. Advanced electronic heating and cooling systems can moderate energy consumption and improve the comfort of people in the building.<sup id="cite_ref-app_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-app-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Proper placement of windows and skylights as well as the use of architectural features that reflect light into a building can reduce the need for artificial lighting. Increased use of natural and task lighting has been shown by one study to increase productivity in schools and offices.<sup id="cite_ref-app_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-app-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lights" title="Compact fluorescent lights" class="mw-redirect">Compact fluorescent lights</a> use two-thirds less energy and may last 6 to 10 times longer than <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulbs" title="Incandescent light bulbs" class="mw-redirect">incandescent light bulbs</a>. Newer fluorescent lights produce a natural light, and in most applications they are cost effective, despite their higher initial cost, with payback periods as low as a few months.</p>

     <p>Effective energy-efficient building design can include the use of low cost Passive Infra Reds (PIRs) to switch-off lighting when areas are unnoccupied such as toilets, corridors or even office areas out-of-hours. In addition, lux levels can be monitored using daylight sensors linked to the building's lighting scheme to switch on/off or dim the lighting to pre-defined levels to take into account the natural light and thus reduce consumption. Building Management Systems (BMS) link all of this together in one centralised computer to control the whole building's lighting and power requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In an analysis that integrates a residential bottom-up simulation with an economic multi-sector model, it has been shown that variable heat gains caused by insulation and air-conditioning efficiency can have load-shifting effects that are not uniform on the electricity load. The study also highlighted the impact of higher household efficiency on the power generation capacity choices that are made by the power sector.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The choice of which space heating or cooling technology to use in buildings can have a significant impact on energy use and efficiency. For example, replacing an older 50% efficient <a href="/wiki/Furnace#Household_furnaces" title="Furnace">natural gas furnace</a> with a new 95% efficient one will dramatically reduce energy use, carbon emissions, and winter natural gas bills. <a href="/wiki/Ground_source_heat_pump" title="Ground source heat pump" class="mw-redirect">Ground source heat pumps</a> can be even more energy efficient and cost effective. These systems use pumps and compressors to move refrigerant fluid around a thermodynamic cycle in order to "pump" heat against its natural flow from hot to cold, for the purpose of transferring heat into a building from the large thermal reservoir contained within the nearby ground. The end result is that heat pumps typically use four times less electrical energy to deliver an equivalent amount of heat than a direct electrical heater does. Another advantage of a ground source heat pump is that it can be reversed in summertime and operate to cool the air by transferring heat from the building to the ground. The disadvantage of ground source heat pumps is their high initial capital cost, but this is typically recouped within five to ten years as a result of lower energy use.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">Smart meters</a> are slowly being adopted by the commercial sector to highlight to staff and for internal monitoring purposes the building's energy usage in a dynamic presentable format. The use of Power Quality Analysers can be introduced into an existing building to assess usage, harmonic distortion, peaks, swells and interruptions amongst others to ultimately make the building more energy-efficient. Often such meters communicate by using <a href="/wiki/Wireless_sensor_networks" title="Wireless sensor networks" class="mw-redirect">wireless sensor networks</a>.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Green_Building_XML" title="Green Building XML">Green Building XML</a> (gbXML) is an emerging schema, a subset of the <a href="/wiki/Building_Information_Modeling" title="Building Information Modeling" class="mw-redirect">Building Information Modeling</a> efforts, focused on green building design and operation. gbXML is used as input in several energy simulation engines. But with the development of modern computer technology, a large number of <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">building energy simulation</a> tools are available on the market. When choosing which simulation tool to use in a project, the user must consider the tool's accuracy and reliability, considering the building information they have at hand, which will serve as input for the tool. Yezioro, Dong and Leite<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> developed an artificial intelligence approach towards assessing building performance simulation results and found that more detailed simulation tools have the best simulation performance in terms of heating and cooling electricity consumption within 3% of mean absolute error.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) is a rating system organized by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a> (USGBC) to promote environmental responsibility in building design. They currently offer four levels of certification for existing buildings (LEED-EBOM) and new construction (LEED-NC) based on a building's compliance with the following criteria: Sustainable Sites, Water Efficiency, Energy and Atmosphere, Materials and Resources, Indoor Environmental Quality, and Innovation in Design.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2013, USGBC developed the LEED Dynamic Plaque, a tool to track building performance against LEED metrics and a potential path to recertification. The following year, the council collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">Honeywell</a> to pull data on energy and water use, as well as indoor air quality from a BAS to automatically update the plaque, providing a near-real-time view of performance. The USGBC office in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> is one of the first buildings to feature the live-updating LEED Dynamic Plaque.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Deep_energy_retrofit" title="Deep energy retrofit">deep energy retrofit</a> is a whole-building analysis and construction process that uses to achieve much larger energy savings than conventional <a href="/wiki/Green_retrofit" title="Green retrofit">energy retrofits</a>. Deep energy retrofits can be applied to both residential and non-residential (“commercial”) buildings. A deep energy retrofit typically results in energy savings of 30 percent or more, perhaps spread over several years, and may significantly improve the building value.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> has undergone a deep energy retrofit process that was completed in 2013. The project team, consisting of representatives from <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Controls" title="Johnson Controls">Johnson Controls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Institute" title="Rocky Mountain Institute">Rocky Mountain Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Climate_Initiative" title="Clinton Climate Initiative" class="mw-redirect">Clinton Climate Initiative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jones_Lang_LaSalle" title="Jones Lang LaSalle" class="mw-redirect">Jones Lang LaSalle</a> will have achieved an annual energy use reduction of 38% and $4.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-ESB_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESB-21">[21]</a></sup> For example, the 6,500 windows were remanufactured onsite into <a href="/w/index.php?title=Superwindows&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Superwindows (page does not exist)">superwindows</a> which block heat but pass light. <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">Air conditioning</a> operating costs on hot days were reduced and this saved $17 million of the project's capital cost immediately, partly funding other retrofitting.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Receiving a gold <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED)</a> rating in September 2011, the Empire State Building is the tallest LEED certified building in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-inhabitat1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inhabitat1-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_City-County_Building" title="Indianapolis City-County Building">Indianapolis City-County Building</a> recently underwent a deep energy retrofit process, which has achieved an annual energy reduction of 46% and $750,000 annual energy saving.</p> 
     <p>Energy retrofits, including deep, and other types undertaken in residential, commercial or industrial locations are generally supported through various forms of financing or incentives. Incentives include pre-packaged rebates where the buyer/user may not even be aware that the item being used has been rebated or "bought down". "Upstream" or "Midstream" buy downs are common for efficient lighting products. Other rebates are more explicit and transparent to the end user through the use of formal applications. In addition to rebates, which may be offered through government or utility programs, governments sometimes offer tax incentives for energy efficiency projects. Some entities offer rebate and payment guidance and facilitation services that enable energy end use customers tap into rebate and incentive programs.</p>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian_national_government" title="Australian national government" class="mw-redirect">Australian national government</a> is actively leading the country in efforts to increase their energy efficiency, mainly through the government’s <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Industry_and_Science" title="Department of Industry and Science">Department of Industry and Science</a>. In July 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Australian_Governments" title="Council of Australian Governments">Council of Australian Governments</a>, which represents the individual states and territories of Australia, agreed to a National Strategy on Energy Efficiency (NSEE).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>This is a ten-year plan accelerate the implementation of a nationwide adoption of energy efficient practices and a preparation for the country’s transformation into a <a href="/wiki/Low_carbon" title="Low carbon" class="mw-redirect">low carbon</a> future. There are several different areas of energy use addressed within the NSEE. But, the chapter devoted to the approach on energy efficiency that is to be adopted on a national level stresses four points in achieving stated levels of energy efficiency. They are:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>To help households and businesses transition to a low carbon future</li> 
      <li>To streamline the adoption of efficient energy</li> 
      <li>To make buildings more energy efficient</li> 
      <li>For governments to work in partnership and lead the way to energy efficiency</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The overriding agreement that governs this strategy is the National Partnership Agreement on Energy Efficiency<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>This document also explains the role of both the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">commonwealth</a> and the individual states and territories in the NSEE, as well provides for the creation of benchmarks and measurement devices which will transparently show the nation’s progress in relation to the stated goals, and addresses the need for funding of the strategy in order to enable it to move forward.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Industries use a large amount of energy to power a diverse range of manufacturing and resource extraction processes. Many industrial processes require large amounts of heat and mechanical power, most of which is delivered as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum fuels</a> and as <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. In addition some industries generate fuel from waste products that can be used to provide additional energy.</p> 
     <p>Because industrial processes are so diverse it is impossible to describe the multitude of possible opportunities for energy efficiency in industry. Many depend on the specific technologies and processes in use at each industrial facility. There are, however, a number of processes and energy services that are widely used in many industries.</p> 
     <p>Various industries generate <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> and electricity for subsequent use within their facilities. When electricity is generated, the heat that is produced as a by-product can be captured and used for process steam, heating or other industrial purposes. Conventional electricity generation is about 30% efficient, whereas combined heat and power (also called <a href="/wiki/Co-generation" title="Co-generation" class="mw-redirect">co-generation</a>) converts up to 90 percent of the fuel into usable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-indust_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indust-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Advanced boilers and furnaces can operate at higher temperatures while burning less fuel. These technologies are more efficient and produce fewer pollutants.<sup id="cite_ref-indust_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indust-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Over 45 percent of the fuel used by US manufacturers is burnt to make steam. The typical industrial facility can reduce this energy usage 20 percent (according to the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" title="US Department of Energy" class="mw-redirect">US Department of Energy</a>) by insulating steam and condensate return lines, stopping steam leakage, and maintaining steam traps.<sup id="cite_ref-indust_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indust-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Electric_motors" title="Electric motors" class="mw-redirect">Electric motors</a> usually run at a constant speed, but a <a href="/wiki/Variable_speed_drives" title="Variable speed drives" class="mw-redirect">variable speed drive</a> allows the motor’s energy output to match the required load. This achieves energy savings ranging from 3 to 60 percent, depending on how the motor is used. Motor coils made of <a href="/wiki/Superconducting" title="Superconducting" class="mw-redirect">superconducting</a> materials can also reduce energy losses.<sup id="cite_ref-indust_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indust-25">[25]</a></sup> Motors may also benefit from <a href="/wiki/Voltage_optimisation" title="Voltage optimisation">voltage optimisation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Industry uses a large number of <a href="/wiki/Pumps" title="Pumps" class="mw-redirect">pumps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compressors" title="Compressors" class="mw-redirect">compressors</a> of all shapes and sizes and in a wide variety of applications. The efficiency of pumps and compressors depends on many factors but often improvements can be made by implementing better <a href="/wiki/Process_control" title="Process control">process control</a> and better maintenance practices. Compressors are commonly used to provide <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a> which is used for sand blasting, painting, and other power tools. According to the US Department of Energy, optimizing compressed air systems by installing variable speed drives, along with preventive maintenance to detect and fix air leaks, can improve energy efficiency 20 to 50 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-indust_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indust-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

     <p>The estimated energy efficiency for an automobile is 280 Passenger-Mile/10<sup>6</sup> Btu.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> There are several ways to enhance a vehicle's energy efficiency. Using improved <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> to minimize drag can increase vehicle <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>. Reducing vehicle weight can also improve fuel economy, which is why <a href="/wiki/Composite_materials" title="Composite materials" class="mw-redirect">composite materials</a> are widely used in car bodies.</p> 
     <p>More advanced tires, with decreased tire to road friction and rolling resistance, can save gasoline. Fuel economy can be improved by up to 3.3% by keeping tires inflated to the correct pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Replacing a clogged air filter can improve a cars fuel consumption by as much as 10 percent on older vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On newer vehicles (1980s and up) with fuel-injected, computer-controlled engines, a clogged air filter has no effect on mpg but replacing it may improve acceleration by 6-11 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">Turbochargers</a> can increase fuel efficiency by allowing a smaller displacement engine. The 'Engine of the year 2011' is a Fiat 500 engine equipped with an MHI turbocharger. "Compared with a 1.2-liter 8v engine, the new 85 HP turbo has 23% more power and a 30% better performance index. The performance of the two-cylinder is not only equivalent to a 1.4-liter 16v engine, but fuel consumption is 30% lower."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Energy-efficient vehicles may reach twice the fuel efficiency of the average automobile. Cutting-edge designs, such as the diesel <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_Bionic" title="Mercedes-Benz Bionic">Mercedes-Benz Bionic</a> concept vehicle have achieved a fuel efficiency as high as 84 miles per US gallon (2.8&nbsp;L/100&nbsp;km; 101&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>), four times the current conventional automotive average.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The mainstream trend in automotive efficiency is the rise of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> (all@electric or hybrid electric). Hybrids, like the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a>, use <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking" class="mw-redirect">regenerative braking</a> to recapture energy that would dissipate in normal cars; the effect is especially pronounced in city driving.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">Plug-in hybrids</a> also have increased battery capacity, which makes it possible to drive for limited distances without burning any gasoline; in this case, energy efficiency is dictated by whatever process (such as coal-burning, hydroelectric, or renewable source) created the power. Plug-ins can typically drive for around 40 miles (64&nbsp;km) purely on electricity without recharging; if the battery runs low, a gas engine kicks in allowing for extended range. Finally, all-electric cars are also growing in popularity; the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">Tesla Model S</a> sedan is the only high-performance all-electric car currently on the market.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Street_lighting">Street lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Street lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Cities are exploring more efficient energy use, reducing <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street light</a> power consumption by dimming lights during off-peak hours and switching to LED lamps.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Cities around the globe light up millions of streets with 300 million lights.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">

     <p>There are several ways to reduce energy usage in air transportation, from modifications to the planes themselves, to how air traffic is managed. As in cars, turbochargers are an effective way to reduce energy consumption; however, instead of allowing for the use of a smaller-displacement engine, turbochargers in jet turbines operate by compressing the thinner air at higher altitudes. This allows the engine to operate as if it were at sea-level pressures while taking advantage of the reduced drag on the aircraft at higher altitudes.</p> 
     <p>Air traffic management systems are another way to increase the efficiency of not just the aircraft but the airline industry as a whole. New technology allows for superior automation of takeoff, landing, and collision avoidance, as well as within airports, from simple things like HVAC and lighting to more complex tasks such as security and scanning.</p>

     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">Energy conservation</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright">

       </div>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">Energy conservation</a> is broader than energy efficiency in including active efforts to decrease energy consumption, for example through <a href="/wiki/Behaviour_change" title="Behaviour change" class="mw-redirect">behaviour change</a>, in addition to using energy more efficiently. Examples of conservation without efficiency improvements are heating a room less in winter, using the car less, air-drying your clothes instead of using the dryer, or enabling energy saving modes on a computer. As with other definitions, the boundary between efficient energy use and energy conservation can be fuzzy, but both are important in environmental and economic terms.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This is especially the case when actions are directed at the saving of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels" class="mw-redirect">fossil fuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Energy conservation is a challenge requiring policy programmes, technological development and behavior change to go hand in hand. Many energy <a href="/wiki/Intermediary" title="Intermediary">intermediary</a> organisations, for example governmental or non-governmental organisations on local, regional, or national level, are working on often publicly funded programmes or projects to meet this challenge.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Psychologists have also engaged with the issue of energy conservation and have provided guidelines for realizing behavior change to reduce energy consumption while taking technological and policy considerations into account.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The National Renewable Energy Laboratory maintains a comprehensive list of apps useful for energy efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Commercial property managers that plan and manage energy efficiency projects generally use a software platform to perform energy audits and to collaborate with contractors to understand their full range of options. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://apps1.eere.energy.gov/buildings/tools_directory/software.cfm/ID=622/pagename=alpha_list_sub">Department of Energy (DOE) Software Directory</a> describes EnergyActio software, a cloud based platform designed for this purpose.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_energy">Sustainable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sustainable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">Sustainable energy</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Energy efficiency and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> are said to be the “twin pillars” of a sustainable <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy" title="Energy policy">energy policy</a>. Both strategies must be developed concurrently in order to stabilize and reduce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="Carbon dioxide emissions" class="mw-redirect">carbon dioxide emissions</a>. Efficient energy use is essential to slowing the energy demand growth so that rising <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy" class="mw-redirect">clean energy</a> supplies can make deep cuts in fossil fuel use. If energy use grows too rapidly, renewable energy development will chase a receding target. Likewise, unless clean energy supplies come online rapidly, slowing demand growth will only begin to reduce total carbon emissions; a reduction in the carbon content of energy sources is also needed. A sustainable energy economy thus requires major commitments to both efficiency and renewables.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebound_effect">Rebound effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Efficient_energy_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rebound effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">Rebound effect (conservation)</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Jevons%27_paradox" title="Jevons' paradox" class="mw-redirect">Jevons' paradox</a>
     </div> 
     <p>If the demand for energy services remains constant, improving energy efficiency will reduce energy consumption and carbon emissions. However, many efficiency improvements do not reduce energy consumption by the amount predicted by simple engineering models. This is because they make energy services cheaper, and so consumption of those services increases. For example, since fuel efficient vehicles make travel cheaper, consumers may choose to drive farther, thereby offsetting some of the potential energy savings. Similarly, an extensive historical analysis of technological efficiency improvements has conclusively shown that energy efficiency improvements were almost always outpaced by economic growth, resulting in a net increase in resource use and associated pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> These are examples of the direct <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-direct_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-direct-42">[42]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Estimates of the size of the rebound effect range from roughly 5% to 40%.<sup id="cite_ref-Greening_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greening-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The rebound effect is likely to be less than 30% at the household level and may be closer to 10% for transport.<sup id="cite_ref-direct_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-direct-42">[42]</a></sup> A rebound effect of 30% implies that improvements in energy efficiency should achieve 70% of the reduction in energy consumption projected using engineering models. The rebound effect may be particularly large for lighting, because in contrast to tasks like transport there is effectively no upper limit on how much light could be considered useful.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In fact, it appears that lighting has accounted for about 0.7% of GDP across many societies and hundreds of years, implying a rebound effect of 100%.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
