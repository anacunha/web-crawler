     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_of_measurement">Units of measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Units of measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Efficiency can be expressed in terms of:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>distance per vehicle per unit fuel volume e.g. km/L or <a href="/wiki/Mile_per_gallon" title="Mile per gallon" class="mw-redirect">miles per gallon (US or imperial)</a></li> 
      <li>distance per vehicle per unit fuel mass e.g. km/kg)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>volume of fuel (or total energy) consumed per unit distance per vehicle e.g. L/100&nbsp;km or MJ/100&nbsp;km</li> 
      <li>volume of fuel (or total energy) consumed per unit distance per passenger e.g. L/(100&nbsp;passenger·km)</li> 
      <li>volume of fuel (or total energy) consumed per unit distance per unit mass of <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a> transported e.g. L/100&nbsp;kg·km or MJ/t·km.</li> 
      <li>electricity used per unit distance e.g. <a href="/wiki/KW%C2%B7h" title="KW·h" class="mw-redirect">kW·h</a>/100&nbsp;km or <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_gallon_equivalent" title="Miles per gallon equivalent" class="mw-redirect">miles per gallon equivalent (mpg-e)</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_types">Transportation types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transportation types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>For <a href="/wiki/Freight" title="Freight" class="mw-redirect">freight</a> transport, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ship_transport" title="Ship transport">ship transport</a> are generally much more efficient than <a href="/wiki/Truck_driver" title="Truck driver">trucking</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_freight" title="Air freight" class="mw-redirect">air freight</a> is much less efficient.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking">Walking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Walking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A 68&nbsp;kg (150&nbsp;lb) person <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a> at 4&nbsp;km/h (2.5&nbsp;mph) requires approximately 210 kilocalories (880&nbsp;kJ) of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> per hour, which is equivalent to 4.55&nbsp;km/MJ.<sup id="cite_ref-brianmac_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brianmac-6">[6]</a></sup> 1 US gallon (3.8&nbsp;L) of gasoline contains about 114,000 <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">British thermal units</a> (120&nbsp;MJ)<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-7">[7]</a></sup> of energy, so this is approximately equivalent to 360 miles per US gallon (0.65&nbsp;L/100&nbsp;km).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Velomobile">Velomobile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Velomobile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Waw020.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Waw020.jpg/220px-Waw020.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Waw020.jpg/330px-Waw020.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Waw020.jpg/440px-Waw020.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="375"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Waw020.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Velomobile
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Velomobile" title="Velomobile">Velomobile</a>
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Velomobile" title="Velomobile">Velomobiles</a> seem to have the highest energy efficiency in personal transportation. At a speed of 50&nbsp;km/h (31&nbsp;mph) the WAW manufacturer claims they need only 0.5&nbsp;kW·h of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> per 100&nbsp;km to transport the passenger, which is around 1/5 (20%) of a normal <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a>, and 1/50 (2%) of an average fossil fuel or electric car. This corresponds to 4700 miles per US gallon (2000&nbsp;km/L or 0.05 L/100&nbsp;km).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other sources give a figure of 1/3.4 (29.5%) of the energy efficiency of a normal bicycle.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycling">Bicycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bicycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A relatively light and slow vehicle with low-friction tires and an efficient <a href="/wiki/Chain_drive" title="Chain drive">chain-driven</a> <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">drivetrain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a> is one of the most energy-efficient forms of transport. A 64&nbsp;kg (140&nbsp;lb) cyclist riding at 16&nbsp;km/h (10&nbsp;mph) requires about half the energy per unit distance of walking: 43&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">kcal</a>/mi, 27&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">kcal</a>/km or 3.1&nbsp;kW·h (11&nbsp;MJ) per 100&nbsp;km.<sup id="cite_ref-brianmac_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brianmac-6">[6]</a></sup> This converts to about 732&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (0.321&nbsp;L/100&nbsp;km; 879&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-Wolfram_Alpha_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfram_Alpha-10">[10]</a></sup> This figure depends on the speed and mass of the rider: greater speeds give higher air drag and heavier riders consume more energy per unit distance.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Motorized_bicycle" title="Motorized bicycle">motorized bicycle</a> such as the Velosolex allows human power and the assistance of a 49&nbsp;cm<sup>3</sup> (3.0&nbsp;cu&nbsp;in) engine, giving a range of 160&nbsp;to 200&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (1.5–1.2&nbsp;L/100&nbsp;km; 190–240&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Electric pedal-assisted bikes run on as little as 1.0&nbsp;kW·h (3.6&nbsp;MJ) per 100&nbsp;km,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> while maintaining speeds in excess of 30&nbsp;km/h (19&nbsp;mph).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These best-case figures rely on a human doing 70% of the work, with around 3.6&nbsp;MJ (1.0&nbsp;kW·h) per 100&nbsp;km coming from the motor.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_power">Human power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Including the human energy dramatically changes the efficiency of cycles quoted above. As with walking, this would include the increase in food consumption due to caloric efficiency of human muscle and cardiovascular efficiency. However, only the rise in food consumption above the diet of a non-cyclist should be considered.</p> 
     <p>To be thorough, a comparison must also consider the energy costs of producing, transporting and packaging of fuel (food or fossil fuel), the energy incurred in disposing of exhaust waste, and the energy costs of manufacturing the vehicle. This last can be significant given that walking requires little or no special equipment, while automobiles, for example, require a great deal of energy to produce and have relatively short lifespans. In addition, any comparison of electric vehicles and liquid-fuelled vehicles must include the fuel consumed in the power station to generate the electricity. In the UK for instance the efficiency of the electricity generation and distribution system is around 0.40.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automobiles">Automobiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Automobiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">Fuel economy in automobiles</a>
     </div> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy-maximizing_behaviors" title="Fuel economy-maximizing behaviors" class="mw-redirect">Fuel economy-maximizing behaviors</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Automobile <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a> is most commonly expressed in terms of the volume of fuel consumed per one hundred kilometres (L/100&nbsp;km), but in some countries (including the USA, UK and India) it is more commonly expressed in terms of the distance per volume fuel consumed (km/L or miles per US or imperial gallon). This is complicated by the different energy content of fuels such as petrol and diesel. The Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) states that the energy content of unleaded gasoline is 115,000 British thermal unit (BTU) per US gallon (32&nbsp;MJ/L) compared to 130,500 BTU per US gallon (36.4&nbsp;MJ/L) for diesel.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A second important consideration is the energy costs of producing energy. Bio-fuels, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, for instance, have significant energy inputs in their production. Because of this, the 50-70% efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a> has to be combined with the vehicle efficiency to yield net efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-eere_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eere-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A third consideration to take into account is the occupancy rate of the vehicle. As the number of passengers per vehicle increases the consumption per unit distance per vehicle increases. However this increase is slight compared to the reduction in consumption per unit distance per passenger. We can compare, for instance, the estimated average occupancy rate of about 1.3 passengers per car in the San Francisco Bay Area<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> to the 2006 UK estimated average of 1.58.<sup id="cite_ref-ukdft_carocc_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukdft_carocc-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Finally, vehicle energy efficiency calculations would be misleading without factoring the energy cost of producing the vehicle itself. This initial energy cost can of course be depreciated over the life of the vehicle to calculate an average energy efficiency over its effective life span. In other words, vehicles that take a lot of energy to produce and are used for relatively short periods will require a great deal more energy over their effective lifespan than those that do not, and are therefore much less energy efficient than they may otherwise seem. Compare, for example, walking, which requires no special equipment at all, and an automobile, produced in and shipped from another country, and made from parts manufactured around the world from raw materials and minerals mined and processed elsewhere again, and used for a limited number of years.</p> 
     <p>It has been researched that driving practices and vehicles can be modified to improve their energy efficiency by about 15%.<sup id="cite_ref-Beusen_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beusen-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-transport_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transport-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

     <p>A principal determinant of energy consumption in aircraft is <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>, which must be opposed by thrust for the aircraft to progress.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Induced_drag" title="Induced drag" class="mw-redirect">Drag</a> is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> required for flight,<sup id="cite_ref-Capehart_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capehart-28">[28]</a></sup> which is equal to the weight of the aircraft. As induced drag increases with weight, mass reduction, with improvements in engine efficiency and reductions in <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a>, has been a principal source of efficiency gains in aircraft, with a rule-of-thumb being that a 1% weight reduction corresponds to around a 0.75% reduction in fuel consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Capehart_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capehart-28">[28]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Flight altitude affects engine efficiency. Jet-engine efficiency increases at altitude up to the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, the temperature minimum of the atmosphere; at lower temperatures, the <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine#Efficiency" title="Heat engine">Carnot efficiency</a> is higher.<sup id="cite_ref-Capehart_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capehart-28">[28]</a></sup> Jet engine efficiency is also increased at high speeds, but above about Mach 0.85 the airframe aerodynamic losses increase faster.</li> 
      <li>Compressibility effects: beginning at <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">transonic</a> speeds of around Mach 0.85, shockwaves form increasing drag.</li> 
      <li>For supersonic flight, it is difficult to achieve a <a href="/wiki/Lift_to_drag_ratio" title="Lift to drag ratio" class="mw-redirect">lift to drag ratio</a> greater than 5, and fuel consumption is increased in proportion.</li> 
     </ul>

     <p>Passenger airplanes averaged 4.8 l/100&nbsp;km per passenger (1.4 MJ/passenger-km) (49 passenger-miles per gallon) in 1998.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Note that on average 20% of seats are left unoccupied. Jet aircraft efficiencies are improving: Between 1960 and 2000 there was a 55% overall fuel efficiency gain (if one were to exclude the inefficient and limited fleet of the DH Comet 4 and to consider the Boeing 707 as the base case).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Most of the improvements in efficiency were gained in the first decade when jet craft first came into widespread commercial use. Compared to advanced piston engine airliners of the 1950s, current jet airliners are only marginally more efficient per passenger-mile.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Between 1971 and 1998 the fleet-average annual improvement per available seat-kilometre was estimated at 2.4%. <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> the <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_transport" title="Supersonic transport">supersonic transport</a> managed about 17 passenger-miles to the Imperial gallon; similar to a business jet, but much worse than a subsonic turbofan aircraft. <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> states a fuel rate consumption of their <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">A380</a> at less than 3 l/100&nbsp;km per passenger (78 passenger-miles per US gallon).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>As over 80% of the fully laden take-off weight of a modern aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a> is craft and fuel, there remains considerable room for future improvements in fuel efficiency.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The mass of an aircraft can be reduced by using light-weight materials such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber" class="mw-redirect">carbon fiber</a> and other composite plastics. Expensive materials may be used, if the reduction of mass justifies the price of materials through improved fuel efficiency. The improvements achieved in fuel efficiency by mass reduction, reduces the amount of fuel that needs to be carried. This further reduces the mass of the aircraft and therefore enables further gains in fuel efficiency. For example, the Airbus A380 design includes multiple light-weight materials.</p> 
     <p>Airbus has showcased <a href="/wiki/Wingtip_device" title="Wingtip device">wingtip devices</a> (sharklets or winglets) that can achieve 3.5 percent reduction in fuel consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There are wingtip devices on the Airbus A380. Further developed Minix winglets have been said to offer 6 percent reduction in fuel consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-gizmag_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gizmag-36">[36]</a></sup> Winglets at the tip of an aircraft wing, can be retrofitted to any airplane, and smooths out the wing-tip vortex, reducing the aircraft's wing drag.<sup id="cite_ref-gizmag_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gizmag-36">[36]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>NASA and Boeing are conducting tests on a 500&nbsp;lb (230&nbsp;kg) "<a href="/wiki/Blended_wing_body" title="Blended wing body">blended wing</a>" aircraft. This design allows for greater fuel efficiency since the whole craft produces lift, not just the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The blended wing body (BWB) concept offers advantages in structural, aerodynamic and operating efficiencies over today's more conventional fuselage-and-wing designs. These features translate into greater range, fuel economy, reliability and life cycle savings, as well as lower manufacturing costs.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> NASA has created a cruise efficient STOL (CESTOL) concept.</p> 
     <p>Fraunhofer Institute for Manufacturing Engineering and Applied Materials Research (IFAM) have researched a <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">shark</a> skin imitating paint that would reduce drag through a riblet effect.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Aircraft are a major potential application for new technologies such as aluminium <a href="/wiki/Metal_foam" title="Metal foam">metal foam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> such as the shark skin imitating paint.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">Propfan</a> propulsors are a more fuel efficient technology than <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprops</a>, but turboprops have an optimum speed below about 450&nbsp;mph (700&nbsp;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This speed is less than used with jets by major airlines today. However, the decrease in speed reduces drag. With the current <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated.">dated info</span></a></i>]</sup> high price for <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> and the emphasis on engine/airframe efficiency to reduce emissions, there is renewed interest in the propfan concept for jetliners that might come into service beyond the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787" title="Boeing 787" class="mw-redirect">Boeing 787</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A350" title="Airbus A350" class="mw-redirect">Airbus A350</a>XWB. For instance, Airbus has patented aircraft designs with twin rear-mounted counter-rotating propfans.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> NASA has conducted an Advanced Turboprop Project (ATP), where they researched a variable pitch propfan that produced less noise and achieved high speeds.</p> 
     <p>Related to fuel efficiency is the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">impact of aviation emissions on climate</a>.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_aircraft">Small aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Small aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Motor-gliders made of composite materials can reach an extremely low fuel consumption for cross-country flights, using thermal air currents and winds.</li> 
      <li>At 160&nbsp;km/h, a diesel powered two-seater Dieselis burns 6 liters of fuel per hour, 1.9 liters per 100 passenger km.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>at 220&nbsp;km/h, a four-seater 100&nbsp;hp <a href="/wiki/Dyn%27A%C3%A9ro_MCR4S" title="Dyn'Aéro MCR4S">MCR-4S</a> burns 20 liters of gas per hour, 2.2 liters per 100 passenger km.</li> 
      <li>Under continuous motorised flight at 225&nbsp;km/h, a Pipistrel Sinus burns 11 liters of fuel per flight hour. Carrying 2 people aboard, it operates at 2.4 liters per 100 passenger km.</li> 
      <li>Ultralight aircraft Tecnam P92 Echo Classic at cruise speed of 185&nbsp;km/h burns 17 liters of fuel per flight hour, 4.6 liters per 100 passenger km (2 people).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Other modern ultralight aircraft have increased efficiency; Tecnam P2002 Sierra RG at cruise speed of 237&nbsp;km/h burns 17 liters of fuel per flight hour, 3.6 liters per 100 passenger km (2 people).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Two-seater and four-seater flying at 250&nbsp;km/h with old generation engines can burn 25 to 40 liters per flight hour, 3 to 5 liters per 100 passenger km.</li> 
      <li>The <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-76" title="Sikorsky S-76">Sikorsky S-76</a>C++ twin turbine helicopter gets about 1.65&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (143&nbsp;L/100&nbsp;km; 1.98&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>) at 140 knots (260&nbsp;km/h; 160&nbsp;mph) and carries 12 for about 19.8 passenger-miles per gallon (11.9 L per 100 passenger km).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen_Elizabeth">Queen Elizabeth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Queen Elizabeth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Cunard_Line" title="Cunard Line">Cunard</a> states that the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Queen_Elizabeth_2" title="RMS Queen Elizabeth 2" class="mw-redirect">RMS Queen Elizabeth 2</a> travels 49.5 feet per imperial gallon of diesel oil (3.32&nbsp;m/l or 41.2&nbsp;ft/US&nbsp;gal), and that it has a passenger capacity of 1777.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Thus carrying 1777 passengers we can calculate an efficiency of 16.7 passenger miles per imperial gallon (16.9&nbsp;l/100&nbsp;p·km or 13.9&nbsp;p·mpg<sub>–US</sub>).</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Emma_Maersk">Emma Maersk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Emma Maersk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Emma_Maersk" title="Emma Maersk" class="mw-redirect">Emma Maersk</a> uses a <a href="/wiki/W%C3%A4rtsil%C3%A4-Sulzer_RTA96-C" title="Wärtsilä-Sulzer RTA96-C">Wärtsilä-Sulzer RTA96-C</a>, which consumes 163&nbsp;g/kW·h and 13,000&nbsp;kg/h. If it carries 13,000 containers then 1&nbsp;kg fuel transports one container for one hour over a distance of 45&nbsp;km. The ship takes 18 days from Tanjung (Singapore) to Rotterdam (Netherlands), 11 from Tanjung to Suez, and 7 from Suez to Rotterdam,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> which is roughly 430 hours, and has 80&nbsp;MW, +30&nbsp;MW. 18 days at a mean speed of 25 knots (46&nbsp;km/h) gives a total distance of 10,800 nautical miles (20,000&nbsp;km).</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sailboats">Sailboats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sailboats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboat</a>, much like a solar car, can locomote without consuming any fuel. A sail boat such as a Dinghy using just wind power requires no input energy in terms of fuel. However some manual energy is required by the crew to steer the boat and adjust the sails using ropes. In addition energy will be needed for demands other than propulsion, such as cooking, heating or lighting. The fuel efficiency of a single-occupancy boat is highly dependent on the size of its engine, the speed at which it travels, and its displacement. Due to the high viscosity of water, with a single passenger, the equivalent energy efficiency will be lower than in a car, train, or plane.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trains">Trains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Trains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Trains can be an efficient means of transport for freight and passengers. Efficiency varies significantly with passenger loads, and losses incurred in electricity generation and supply (for electrified systems),<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and, importantly, end-to-end delivery, where stations are not the originating final destinations of a journey.</p> 
     <p>Actual consumption depends on gradients, maximum speeds, loading and stopping patterns. Data produced for the European MEET project (Methodologies for Estimating Air Pollutant Emissions) illustrate the different consumption patterns over several track sections. The results show the consumption for a German ICE high-speed train varied from around 19&nbsp;to 33&nbsp;kW·h/km (68–119&nbsp;MJ/km; 31–53&nbsp;kW·h/mi). The data also reflects the weight of the train per passenger. For example, <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> double-deck Duplex trains use lightweight materials, which keep axle loads down and reduce damage to track and also save energy.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Considering only the energy spent to move the train, and taking as example the urban area of <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, train seems to be on average 20 times more efficient than automobile for transportation of passengers, if we consider energy spent per passenger-km.<sup id="cite_ref-train_in_Lisbon_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-train_in_Lisbon-60">[60]</a></sup> Considering an automobile which has a consumptions of around 6&nbsp;l/100&nbsp;km (47&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>; 39&nbsp;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub>) of gasoline, the fact the on average cars in Europe have an occupation ratio of around 1.2 passengers per automobile and that one litre of gasoline <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">amounts for about 8826 Wh</a>, one gets on average 441&nbsp;Wh (1,590&nbsp;kJ) per passenger-km. On the other hand, a modern urban train with an average occupation of 20% of total capacity, which has a consumption of about 8.5&nbsp;kW·h/km (31&nbsp;MJ/km; 13.7&nbsp;kW·h/mi), one gets 21.5 Wh per passenger-km, 20 times less than the automobile.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Braking_losses">Braking losses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Braking losses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Stopping is a considerable source of inefficiency. Modern electric trains like the <i><a href="/wiki/Shinkansen" title="Shinkansen">Shinkansen</a></i> (the <i>Bullet Train</i>) use <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking" class="mw-redirect">regenerative braking</a> to return current into the <a href="/wiki/Overhead_lines" title="Overhead lines" class="mw-redirect">catenary</a> while they brake. A Siemens study indicated that regenerative braking might recover 41.6% of the total energy consumed. The Passenger Rail (Urban and Intercity) and Scheduled Intercity and All Charter Bus Industries Technological and Operational Improvements – FINAL REPORT states that "Commuter operations can dissipate more than half of their total traction energy in braking for stops." and that "We estimate <a href="/wiki/Head-end_power" title="Head-end power">head-end power</a> to be 35 percent (but it could possibly be as high as 45 percent) of total energy consumed by commuter railways."<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Having to accelerate and decelerate a heavy train load of people at every stop is inefficient despite <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking" class="mw-redirect">regenerative braking</a> which can recover typically around 20% of the energy wasted in braking. Weight is a determinant of braking losses.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_transport_comparisons">International transport comparisons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: International transport comparisons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_Public_transport">UK Public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: UK Public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Rail and bus are generally required to serve 'off peak' and rural services, which by their nature have lower loads than city bus routes and inter city train lines. Moreover, due to their 'walk on' ticketing it is much harder to match daily demand and passenger numbers. As a consequence, the overall load factor on UK railways is 35% or 90 people per train:<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Conversely, Air services work on point-to-point networks between large population centres and are 'pre-book' in nature. Using <a href="/wiki/Yield_management" title="Yield management">yield management</a> overall loads can be raised to around 70-90%. However, recently intercity train operators have been using similar techniques, with loads reaching typically 71% overall for <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> services in France and a similar figure for the UK's <a href="/wiki/Virgin_trains" title="Virgin trains" class="mw-redirect">Virgin trains</a> services.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>For emissions, the electricity generating source needs to be taken into account.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_transportation">Canadian transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_efficiency_in_transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Canadian transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Natural Resources Canada's Office of Energy Efficiency publishes annual statistics regarding the efficiency of the entire Canadian fleet. For researchers, these fuel consumption estimates are more realistic than the fuel consumption ratings of new vehicles, as they represent the real world driving conditions, including extreme weather and traffic. The annual report is called Energy Efficiency Trends Analysis. There are dozens of tables illustrating trends in energy consumption expressed in energy per passenger km (passengers) or energy per tonne km (freight).<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Comparing fuel efficiency in transportation has several challenges:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>All transportation methods require a different infrastructure. The building, repairing and renovation of this must also be considered, and heavy usage of infrastructure will require earlier maintenance, which will also be energy consuming. Road transport requires an extensive network of well-maintained paved roads and trains require railroads and stations along the whole length of the track. Meanwhile, ships and aircraft only require a more limited amount of harbours or airports, without any en route infrastructure except for the occasional navigation beacon.</li> 
      <li>All transportation also requires storage, inspection, preparation and boarding areas. The size of the vehicle and supporting items including security, parking areas, especially when required due to distance from densely populated areas, baggage removal and boarding aids such as ramps, gangplanks or jet bridges should be taken into account for total area used by each for of transportation.</li> 
      <li>Some types of transportation can take shorter routes, thereby (partly) offsetting an increase in energy consumption per unit of distance traveled. For example, a trip from the UK to Italy would take a boat an enormous detour via the Strait of Gibraltar, while cars need to travel much less distance, a train can cross through the channel tunnel, and aircraft can (theoretically; in reality it's going from good to better) take by the shortest route or, in case of favorable winds, a more efficient route.</li> 
      <li>While aircraft ideally can travel the shortest distance, they do require takeoff and landing with specific directions related to wind conditions and airport runway locations. These require course corrections during takeoff and landing. Additionally, there are cases where aircraft require circling due to congestion at airports. Where other forms of transportation can stop and idle when there is congestion airplanes cannot do this and can occasionally travel a farther distance than strictly necessary.</li> 
      <li>There is a distinction between vehicle <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_gallon_of_gasoline_equivalent" title="Miles per gallon of gasoline equivalent" class="mw-redirect">MPGe</a> and passenger MPGe. Most of these entries cite passenger MPGe even if not explicitly stated. It is important not to compare energy figures that relate to dissimilar journeys. An airline jet cannot be used for an urban commute so when comparing aircraft with cars the car figures must take this into account.</li> 
      <li>There is currently no agreed method of comparing <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> efficiency to <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>) vehicle efficiency, but typical emissions and thermal energy consumption can be compared. Vehicle speed is also an important parameter, and a peer-reviewed evaluation which convolves these criteria may be found at <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://benthamopen.com/contents/pdf/TOEFJ/TOEFJ-1-11.pdf">http://benthamopen.com/contents/pdf/TOEFJ/TOEFJ-1-11.pdf</a></li> 
      <li>If the issue is rapid investment in new electric mass transit it is important to use emissions associated with the most polluting fuel because increased demand for electricity increases the use of polluting fuel used in generation for the immediate future, as well as low emissions fuels in the case of some countries.</li> 
      <li>Systems that re-use vehicles like trains and buses can't be directly compared to vehicles that get parked at their destination. They use energy to return (less full) for more passengers and must sometimes run on schedules and routes with little patronage. These factors greatly affect overall system efficiencies. The energy costs of accumulating load need to be included. In the case of most mass transit distributing and accumulating load over many stops means that passenger kilometres are inherently a small proportion of vehicle kilometres see Transport Energy Metrics, Lessons from the west Coast Main line Modernisation and figures for London Underground in transport statistics for Great Britain 2003. Lessons from the west coast mainline modernisation suggest that long passenger rail should operate at less than 40% capacity utilisation and for London underground the figure is probably less than 15%.</li> 
      <li>Most cars run at less than full capacity, with the usual average load being between 1 and 2. Cars are also subject to inefficiencies because of congestion and the need to negotiate road junctions. The impact of transport road building to reduce congestion should always be considered as should the improving efficiency of cars see <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://webarchive.nationalarchives.gov.uk/20071204132715/http://www.hm-treasury.gov.uk/media/9/5/pbr_csr07_king840.pdf">http://webarchive.nationalarchives.gov.uk/20071204132715/http://www.hm-treasury.gov.uk/media/9/5/pbr_csr07_king840.pdf</a></li> 
      <li>Vehicles are not isolated systems. They usually form a part of larger systems whose design inherently determines energy consumption. Judging the value of transport systems by comparing the performance of their vehicles alone can be misleading. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">metro</a> systems may have a poor energy efficiency per <i>passenger kilometre</i>, but their high throughput and low physical footprint makes the existence of high <a href="/wiki/Urban_density" title="Urban density">urban population densities</a> viable. Total energy consumption per capita declines sharply as population density increases, since journeys become shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>See also Logistics and Transport Focus (the Journal of the Charter Institute of Transport) vol 9 number 10 through volume 10 number 6 for a series of articles debating the general issues of fuel efficiency in transportation in the context of impact on climate change.</li> 
     </ul>
