     <p><b>Scandinavia</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Historical_region" title="Historical region" class="mw-redirect">historical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural-linguistic</a> region in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> characterized by a common ethno-cultural <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic</a> heritage and mutually intelligible <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a>.</p> 
     <p>In English, Scandinavia usually refers to the three <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy#Modern_constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">kingdoms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> are sometimes included. Modern Norway and Sweden proper<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup> , and the north-west part of Finland, are situated on the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a>, whereas modern Denmark consists of <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Danish_islands" title="Danish islands" class="mw-redirect">Danish islands</a>.</p> 
     <p>The term <i>Scandinavia</i> is usually used as a cultural term, but in English usage, it is occasionally confused with the geographical term <i><a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a></i>, which took its name from the cultural-linguistic concept.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostergard_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostergard-3"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The name Scandinavia originally referred vaguely to the formerly Danish, now Swedish, region <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a>. The terms <i>Scandinavia</i> and <i>Scandinavian</i> entered usage in the late 18th century as terms for the three Scandinavian countries, their Germanic majority peoples and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_languages" title="Scandinavian languages" class="mw-redirect">associated language</a> and culture, being introduced by the early linguistic and cultural <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Scandinavist movement</a>. In foreign usage, the term <i>Scandinavia</i> is sometimes incorrectly taken to also include Iceland, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and Finland, because of their historical association with the Scandinavian countries and the Scandinavian peoples and languages. However, this broader group of countries is officially and commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-4"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The southern and by far most populous regions of Scandinavia have a <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climate</a>. Scandinavia extends north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>, but has relatively mild weather for its latitude due to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. Much of the Scandinavian mountains have an <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine tundra</a> climate. There are many lakes and <a href="/wiki/Moraines" title="Moraines" class="mw-redirect">moraines</a>, legacies of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, which ended about ten millennia ago.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish languages</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> and are known as the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">Scandinavian languages</a>—all of which are considered <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a> with one another, although Danish is considered much closer to Norwegian. <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, sometimes referred to as insular Scandinavian languages, are intelligible in continental Scandinavian languages only to a very limited extent. <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami languages</a> and several minority languages spoken in Western Russia are related to each other, but are entirely unrelated to the Scandinavian languages.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>c<span>]</span></a></sup> They do, however, include several words that have been adopted during the history from the neighboring languages, just as Swedish, spoken in Sweden today, has borrowed from Finnish.</p> 
     <p>The vast majority of the population of Scandinavia are Scandinavians, descended from several <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">(North) Germanic tribes</a> who originally inhabited the southern part of Scandinavia, who spoke a <a href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language" class="mw-redirect">Germanic language</a> that evolved into <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> and who were known as <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking" class="mw-redirect">Vikings</a> are popularly associated with Norse culture. The <a href="/wiki/Icelanders" title="Icelanders">Icelanders</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroese_people" title="Faroese people" class="mw-redirect">Faroese</a> are to a significant extent, but not exclusively, descended from peoples retrospectively known as Scandinavians. A small minority of <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami people</a> live in the extreme north of Scandinavia.</p>

     <p>In English, Scandinavia usually refers to Denmark, Norway, and Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-4"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Tourist" title="Tourist" class="mw-redirect">tourist</a>-oriented sources argue for the inclusion of Finland and Iceland,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> though that broader region is usually known by the countries concerned as <i>Norden</i> (Finnish <i>Pohjoismaat</i>, Icelandic <i>Norðurlöndin</i>, Faroese <i>Norðurlond</i>), or the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nordic_Council_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nordic_Council-11"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>The use of the name Scandinavia as a convenient general term for the three kingdoms of Denmark, Norway and Sweden is fairly recent; according to some historians, it was adopted and introduced in the eighteenth century, at a time when the ideas about a common heritage started to appear and develop into early literary and linguistic <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Scandinavism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostergard_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostergard-3"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Before this time, the term <i>Scandinavia</i> was familiar mainly to classical scholars through <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s writings, and was used vaguely for Scania and the southern region of the peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostergard_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostergard-3"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>As a political term, "Scandinavia" was first used by students agitating for <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Pan-Scandinavianism</a> in the 1830s.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostergard_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostergard-3"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The popular usage of the term in Sweden, Denmark and Norway as a unifying concept became established in the nineteenth century through poems such as <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>'s "I am a Scandinavian" of 1839. After a visit to Sweden, Andersen became a supporter of early political Scandinavism. In a letter describing the poem to a friend, he wrote: "All at once I understood how related the Swedes, the Danes and the Norwegians are, and with this feeling I wrote the poem immediately after my return: 'We are one people, we are called Scandinavians!'"<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The clearest example of the use of the term "Scandinavia" as a political and societal construct is the unique position of Finland, based largely on parts of modern-day Finland being one of the four <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_Sweden" title="Lands of Sweden">historical lands</a> in the Swedish kingdom for hundreds of years, thus to much of the world associating Finland with all of Scandinavia. But the creation of a Finnish identity is unique in the region in that it was formed in relation to two different imperial models, the Swedish<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and the Russian,<sup id="cite_ref-redescriptions_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-redescriptions-14"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> as described by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4" title="University of Jyväskylä">University of Jyväskylä</a> based editorial board of the Finnish journal <i>Yearbook of Political Thought and Conceptual History</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The term is often defined according to the conventions of the cultures that lay claim to the term in their own use.<sup id="cite_ref-olwig_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olwig-17"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> When a speaker wants to explicitly include Finland alongside Scandinavia-proper, the geographic terms <a href="/wiki/Fenno-Scandinavia" title="Fenno-Scandinavia" class="mw-redirect">Fenno-Scandinavia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a> are sometimes used in English, although these terms are hardly if at all used within Scandinavia. More precisely, and subject to no dispute, is that Finland is included in the broader term 'Nordic countries'.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Societal_and_tourism_promotional_organizations">Societal and tourism promotional organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Societal and tourism promotional organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Various promotional agencies of the Nordic countries in the United States (such as <a href="/wiki/The_American-Scandinavian_Foundation" title="The American-Scandinavian Foundation">The American-Scandinavian Foundation</a>, established in 1910 by the <a href="/wiki/Danish_American" title="Danish American" class="mw-redirect">Danish American</a> industrialist Niels Poulsen) serve to promote market and tourism interests in the region. Today, the five Nordic heads of state act as the organization's patrons and according to the official statement by the organization, its mission is "to promote the Nordic region as a whole while increasing the visibility of Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway and Sweden in New York City and the United States."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The official tourist boards of Scandinavia sometimes cooperate under one umbrella, such as the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Tourist_Board" title="Scandinavian Tourist Board">Scandinavian Tourist Board</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The cooperation was introduced for the Asian market in 1986, when the Swedish national tourist board joined the Danish national tourist board to coordinate intergovernmental promotion of the two countries. Norway's government entered one year later. All five Nordic governments participate in the joint promotional efforts in the United States through the Scandinavian Tourist Board of North America.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_Nordic_countries_vs._Scandinavia">Use of <i>Nordic countries</i> vs. <i>Scandinavia</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use of Nordic countries vs. Scandinavia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      For more details on this terminology, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.
     </div> 
     <p>While the term <i>Scandinavia</i> is commonly used for Denmark, Norway and Sweden, the term <i>the Nordic countries</i> is used unambiguously for Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Iceland, including their associated territories (<a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, the Faroe Islands, and the Åland Islands).<sup id="cite_ref-saetre_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saetre-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Scandinavia can thus be considered a subset of the Nordic countries. Furthermore, the term <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a> refers to Scandinavia, Finland and <a href="/wiki/Karelia" title="Karelia">Karelia</a>, excluding Denmark and overseas territories; however, the usage of this term is restricted to <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, when speaking of the <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandian_Shield" title="Fennoscandian Shield" class="mw-redirect">Fennoscandian Shield</a> (Baltic Shield).</p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> has applied for membership in the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Council" title="Nordic Council">Nordic Council</a>, referring to its cultural heritage and close linguistic links to Finland, and historical connections to both Denmark and Sweden, although normally Estonia is regarded as one of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_countries" title="Baltic countries" class="mw-redirect">Baltic countries</a>. It is similar to the situation of Finland around the 1920s as Finland was considered to be one of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" title="Baltic States" class="mw-redirect">Baltic States</a> as well,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as it too had emerged from Russian domination along with the other three countries under similar circumstances. While Finnish and Estonian are <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a>, Latvian and Lithuanian are <a href="/wiki/Baltic_languages" title="Baltic languages">Baltic languages</a>.</p> 
     <p>It should be noted that whereas the term "Scandinavia" is relatively straightforward as traditionally relating to the three kingdoms of Denmark, Norway and Sweden there exists some ambiguity as regards the ethnic aspect of the concept in the modern era. Traditionally, the term refers specifically to the majority peoples of Denmark, Norway and Sweden, their states, their Germanic languages and their culture. In the modern era, the term will often include minority peoples such as the <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Me%C3%A4nkieli_dialects" title="Meänkieli dialects">Meänkieli</a> speakers in a political and to some extent cultural sense, as they are citizens of Scandinavian countries and speak Scandinavian languages either as their first or second language. However, Scandinavian is still also seen as an ethnic term for the Germanic majority peoples of Scandinavia, and as such, the inclusion of Sami and Finnish speakers can be seen as controversial within these groups.</p>

     <p>Scandinavia and <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> (<i>Skåne</i>, the southernmost province of Sweden) are considered to have the same etymology. Both terms are thought to go back to the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound</a> *<i>Skaðin-awjō</i>, which appears later in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> as <i>Scedenig</i> and in <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> as <i>Skáney</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The earliest identified source for the name Scandinavia is <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i>, dated to the first century A.D.</p> 
     <p>Various references to the region can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procopius" title="Procopius">Procopius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">Jordanes</a>, usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Scandza" title="Scandza">Scandza</a>. It is believed that the name used by Pliny may be of <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">West Germanic</a> origin, originally denoting Scania.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> According to some scholars, the Germanic stem can be reconstructed as *<i>Skaðan-</i> meaning "danger" or "damage" (English <i>scathing</i>, German <i>Schaden</i>, Dutch <i>schade</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Helle_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helle-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The second segment of the name has been reconstructed as <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/awj%C5%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/awjō">*<i>awjō</i></a>, meaning "land on the water" or "island". The name Scandinavia would then mean "dangerous island", which is considered to refer to the treacherous sandbanks surrounding Scania.<sup id="cite_ref-Helle_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helle-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Skan%C3%B6r-Falsterbo" title="Skanör-Falsterbo" class="mw-redirect">Skanör</a> in Scania, with its long Falsterbo reef, has the same stem (<i>skan</i>) combined with -<i>ör</i>, which means "sandbanks".</p> 
     <p>In the reconstructed Germanic root *<i>Skaðin-awjō</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Edh" title="Edh" class="mw-redirect">edh</a> represented in Latin by t or d), the first segment is sometimes considered more uncertain than the second segment. The American Heritage Dictionary<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> derives the second segment from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <i>*akwa-</i>, "water", in the sense of "watery land".</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> goddess name <i><a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skaði">Skaði</a></i>, along with <i>Sca(n)dinavia</i> and <i>Skáney</i>, may be related to <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> <i>skadus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sceadu</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> <i>scado</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>scato</i> (meaning "shadow"). Scholar John McKinnell comments that this etymology suggests that the goddess Skaði may have once been a personification of the geographical region of Scandinavia or associated with the underworld.<sup id="cite_ref-MCKINNELL63_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCKINNELL63-26"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pliny_the_Elder.27s_descriptions">Pliny the Elder's descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pliny the Elder's descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Pliny's descriptions of <i>Scatinavia</i> and surrounding areas are not always easy to decipher. Writing in the capacity of a Roman admiral, he introduces the northern region by declaring to his Roman readers that there are 23 islands "<i>Romanis armis cognitae</i>" ("known to Roman arms") in this area. According to Pliny, the "<i>clarissima</i>" ("most famous") of the region's islands is <i>Scatinavia</i>, of unknown size. There live the <a href="/wiki/Hilleviones" title="Hilleviones">Hilleviones</a>. The belief that Scandinavia was an island became widespread among classical authors during the first century and dominated descriptions of Scandinavia in classical texts during the centuries that followed.</p> 
     <p>Pliny begins his description of the route to <i>Scatinavia</i> by referring to the mountain of Saevo (<i>mons Saevo ibi</i>), the Codanus Bay (<i>Codanus sinus</i>) and the Cimbrian promontory.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The geographical features have been identified in various ways; by some scholars "Saevo" is thought to be the mountainous <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> coast at the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Skagerrak" title="Skagerrak">Skagerrak</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cimbri" title="Cimbri">Cimbrian</a> peninsula is thought to be <a href="/wiki/Skagen" title="Skagen">Skagen</a>, the north tip of <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>, Denmark. As described, Saevo and Scatinavia can also be the same place.</p> 
     <p>Pliny mentions Scandinavia one more time: in Book VIII he says that the animal called <i>achlis</i> (given in the accusative, <i>achlin</i>, which is not Latin), was born on the island of Scandinavia.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The animal grazes, has a big upper lip and some mythical attributes.</p> 
     <p>The name "<a href="/wiki/Scandia" title="Scandia" class="mw-redirect">Scandia</a>", later used as a synonym for Scandinavia, also appears in Pliny's Naturalis Historia, but is used for a group of Northern European islands which he locates north of <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>. "Scandia" thus does not appear to be denoting the island Scadinavia in Pliny's text. The idea that "<i>Scadinavia</i>" may have been one of the "<i>Scandiae</i>" islands was instead introduced by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> (c. 90 – c. 168&nbsp;AD), a mathematician, geographer and astrologer of Roman Egypt. He used the name "<i>Skandia</i>" for the biggest, most easterly of the three "<i>Scandiai</i>" islands, which according to him were all located east of <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Helle_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helle-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Neither Pliny's nor Ptolemy's lists of Scandinavian tribes include the <a href="/wiki/Suiones" title="Suiones" class="mw-redirect">Suiones</a> mentioned by Tacitus. Some early Swedish scholars of the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Hyperborean" title="Hyperborean" class="mw-redirect">Hyperborean</a> school<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> and of the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Romantic_nationalism" title="Romantic nationalism">romantic nationalism</a> period proceeded to synthesize the different versions by inserting references to the Suiones, arguing that they must have been referred to in the original texts and obscured over time by spelling mistakes or various alterations.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_reconstruction">Germanic reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Germanic reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Latin names in Pliny's text gave rise to different forms in medieval Germanic texts. In Jordanes' history of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> (AD 551) the form <i><a href="/wiki/Scandza" title="Scandza">Scandza</a></i> is the name used for their original home, separated by sea from the land of Europe (chapter 1, 4).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Where Jordanes meant to locate this quasi-legendary island is still a hotly debated issue, both in scholarly discussions and in the <a href="/wiki/Nationalistic" title="Nationalistic" class="mw-redirect">nationalistic</a> discourse of various European countries.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The form <i>Scadinavia</i> as the original home of the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Langobards</a> appears in <a href="/wiki/Paulus_Diaconus" title="Paulus Diaconus" class="mw-redirect">Paulus Diaconus</a>' <i>Historia Langobardorum</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> in other versions of <i>Historia Langobardorum</i> appear the forms <i>Scadan</i>, <i>Scandanan</i>, <i>Scadanan</i> and <i>Scatenauge</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Frankish sources used <i>Sconaowe</i> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelweard_(historian)" title="Æthelweard (historian)">Aethelweard</a>, an Anglo-Saxon historian, used <i>Scani</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-North_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North-38"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a>, the forms <i>Scedenige</i> and <i>Scedeland</i> are used, while the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfredian</a> translation of <a href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" title="Paulus Orosius" class="mw-redirect">Orosius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wulfstan_of_Hedeby" title="Wulfstan of Hedeby">Wulfstan</a>'s travel accounts used the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>Sconeg</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-North_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North-38"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sami_etymology">Sami etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sami etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:This_is_the_center_of_the_sami_town_Kautokeino.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        <a href="/wiki/Kautokeino" title="Kautokeino">Kautokeino</a>, the main 
        <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami</a> city in

       </div>

     <p>The earliest <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami</a> <a href="/wiki/Yoik" title="Yoik" class="mw-redirect">yoik</a> texts written down refer to the world as <i>Skadesi-suolo</i> (north-Sami) and <i>Skađsuâl</i> (east-Sami), meaning "<a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skaði">Skaði</a>'s island" (Svennung 1963). Svennung considers the Sami name to have been introduced as a <a href="/wiki/Loan_word" title="Loan word" class="mw-redirect">loan word</a> from the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Svennung_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svennung-39"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skaði">Skaði</a>" is the <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giant</a> stepmother of <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>. It has been suggested that Skaði to some extent is modeled on a Sami woman. The name for Skade's father <a href="/wiki/Thjazi" title="Thjazi" class="mw-redirect">Thjazi</a> is known in Sami as <i>Čáhci</i>, "the waterman", and her son with Odin, <a href="/wiki/Saeming" title="Saeming" class="mw-redirect">Saeming</a>, can be interpreted as a descendent of <i>Saam</i> the Sami population (Mundel 2000),<sup id="cite_ref-Mundel_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mundel-40"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> (Steinsland 1991).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Older joik texts give evidence of the old Sami belief about living on an island and state that the wolf is known as <i>suolu gievra</i>, meaning "the strong one on the island." The Sami <a href="/wiki/Place_name" title="Place name" class="mw-redirect">place name</a> <i>Sulliidčielbma</i> means "the island's threshold" and <i><a href="/wiki/Saariselk%C3%A4" title="Saariselkä">Suoločielgi</a></i> means "the island's back."</p> 
     <p>In recent <a href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum" class="mw-redirect">substrate</a> studies, Sami linguists have examined the initial cluster sk- in words used in Sami and concluded that sk- is a <a href="/wiki/Phonotactic" title="Phonotactic" class="mw-redirect">phonotactic</a> structure of alien origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikio_2004_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikio_2004-42"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_etymologies">Other etymologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other etymologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Another possibility is that all or part of the segments of the name came from the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> people inhabiting the region.<sup id="cite_ref-Giorgio_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgio-43"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In modernity, Scandinavia is a peninsula, but between approximately 10,300 and 9,500 years ago, the southern part of Scandinavia was an island separated from the northern peninsula, with water exiting the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> through the area where <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> is now located.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> scholars have presented the idea that the segment <i>sk</i> that appears in *Ska∂inaujàin is connected to the name for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Euzko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Euzko (page does not exist)">Euzko</a> peoples, akin to Basques, that populated <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> Europe. According to some of these intellects, Scandinavian people share particular <a href="/wiki/Genetic_marker" title="Genetic marker">genetic markers</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people" class="mw-redirect">Basque people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Giorgio_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgio-43"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <div role="note" class="hatnote">

      <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Denmark" title="Geography of Denmark">Geography of Denmark</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Finland" title="Geography of Finland">Geography of Finland</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Iceland" title="Geography of Iceland">Geography of Iceland</a>,

     <p>The geography of Scandinavia is extremely varied. Notable are the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norwegian_fjords" title="List of Norwegian fjords">Norwegian fjords</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Mountains" title="Scandinavian Mountains">Scandinavian Mountains</a>, the flat, low areas in Denmark, and the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a> of Sweden and Norway. Sweden has many lakes and moraines, legacies of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">ice age</a>.</p> 
     <p>The climate varies from north to south and from west to east; a marine west coast climate (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#GROUP_C:_Temperate.2Fmesothermal_climates" title="Köppen climate classification">Cfb</a>) typical of western Europe dominates in Denmark, southernmost part of Sweden and along the west coast of Norway reaching north to 65°N, with <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a> giving more mm/year <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)" class="mw-redirect">precipitation</a> (&lt;5000&nbsp;mm) in some areas in western Norway. The central part – from <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> – has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a> (Dfb), which gradually gives way to <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subarctic climate</a> (Dfc) further north and cool marine west coast climate (Cfc) along the northwestern coast. A small area along the northern coast east of the <a href="/wiki/North_Cape,_Norway" title="North Cape, Norway" class="mw-redirect">North Cape</a> has tundra climate (Et) as a result of a lack of summer warmth. The Scandinavian Mountains block the mild and moist air coming from the southwest, thus northern Sweden and the <a href="/wiki/Finnmarksvidda" title="Finnmarksvidda">Finnmarksvidda</a> plateau in Norway receive little precipitation and have cold winters. Large areas in the Scandinavian mountains have <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine tundra</a> climate.</p> 
     <p>The warmest temperature ever recorded in Scandinavia is 38.0&nbsp;°C in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A5lilla" title="Målilla">Målilla</a> (Sweden).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The coldest temperature ever recorded is −52.6&nbsp;°C in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vuoggatj%C3%A5lme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vuoggatjålme (page does not exist)">Vuoggatjålme</a> (Sweden).<sup id="cite_ref-smhi.se_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smhi.se-46"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> The coldest month was February 1985 in Vittangi (Sweden) with a mean of −27.2&nbsp;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-smhi.se_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smhi.se-46"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Southwesterly winds further warmed by <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">foehn wind</a> can give warm temperatures in narrow Norwegian fjords in winter; <a href="/wiki/Tafjord" title="Tafjord">Tafjord</a> has recorded 17.9&nbsp;°C in January and <a href="/wiki/Sunndal" title="Sunndal">Sunndal</a> 18.9&nbsp;°C in February.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages_in_Scandinavia">Languages in Scandinavia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Languages in Scandinavia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main articles: 
      <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami languages</a>, 
      <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Scandoromani" title="Scandoromani" class="mw-redirect">Scandoromani</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Two language groups have coexisted on the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_peninsula" title="Scandinavian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Scandinavian peninsula</a> since prehistory—the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a> (Scandinavian languages) and the <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The majority languages on the peninsula, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, are today, along with <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, classified as Continental Scandinavian.<sup id="cite_ref-Heine_2006_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heine_2006-48"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The North Germanic languages of Scandinavia are traditionally divided into an <a href="/wiki/East_Scandinavian" title="East Scandinavian" class="mw-redirect">East Scandinavian</a> branch (<a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>) and a <a href="/wiki/West_Scandinavian" title="West Scandinavian" class="mw-redirect">West Scandinavian</a> branch (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> but because of changes appearing in the languages since 1600, the East Scandinavian and West Scandinavian branches are now usually reconfigured into Insular Scandinavian (<i>ö-nordisk</i>/<i>øy-nordisk</i>) featuring <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> and Continental Scandinavian (<i>Skandinavisk</i>), comprising Danish, Norwegian, and Swedish.<sup id="cite_ref-Heine_2006_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heine_2006-48"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> The modern division is based on the degree of mutual comprehensibility between the languages in the two branches.<sup id="cite_ref-Torp_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Torp-52"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Note that <i>skandinavisk(a)</i> may also refer to a way of speaking one Scandinavian language in a way intended to be more easily understood by speakers of the other Scandinavian languages, like the Danish saying the beginning of a number in Swedish to Swedish people.</p> 
     <p>Apart from Sami and the languages of minority groups speaking a variant of the majority language of a neighboring state, the following minority languages in Scandinavia are protected under the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a>: <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">Romani Chib, Romanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandoromani" title="Scandoromani" class="mw-redirect">Romani</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Scandinavian_languages">Continental Scandinavian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Continental Scandinavian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Danish_language#Dialects" title="Danish language">dialects of Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_dialects" title="Norwegian dialects">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_dialects" title="Swedish dialects">Sweden</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> and are mutually intelligible. The populations of the Scandinavian countries, with a Scandinavian mother tongue, can—at least with some training—understand each other's <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard languages</a> as they appear in print and are heard on radio and television. The reason Danish, Swedish and the two official written versions of Norwegian (<i>Nynorsk</i> and <i>Bokmål</i>) are traditionally viewed as different languages, rather than dialects of one common language, is that each is a well established standard language in its respective country. They are related to, but not mutually intelligible with, the other North Germanic languages, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a>, which are descended from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language" class="mw-redirect">Old West Norse</a>. Danish, Swedish and Norwegian have, since medieval times, been influenced to varying degrees by <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> and standard German. A substantial amount of that influence was a by-product of the economic activity generated by the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>.</p> 
     <p>Norwegians are accustomed to variation, and may perceive Danish and Swedish only as slightly more distant dialects. This is because they have two official written standards, in addition to the habit of strongly holding on to local dialects. The people of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, Sweden and <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, Denmark, have the greatest difficulty in understanding other Scandinavian languages.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In the Faroe Islands and Iceland, learning <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> is mandatory. This causes Faroese people as well as Icelandic people to become bilingual in two very distinct North Germanic languages, making it relatively easy for them to understand the other two Mainland Scandinavian languages.,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Scandinavian languages are (as a language family) entirely unrelated to <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami languages</a>, which as <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a> are distantly related to <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>. Owing to the close proximity, there is still a great deal of borrowing from the Swedish and Norwegian languages in the Finnish, Estonian, and Sami languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Sapmi_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sapmi-56"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The long history of linguistic influence of Swedish on Finnish is also due to the fact that Finnish, the language of the majority in Finland, was treated as a minority language while Finland was part of Sweden. Finnish-speakers had to learn Swedish in order to advance to higher positions.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Swedish spoken in today's Finland includes a lot of words that are borrowed from Finnish, whereas the written language remains closer to that of Sweden.</p> 
     <p>Although Iceland was under the political control of Denmark until a much later date (1918), very little influence and borrowing from Danish has occurred in the Icelandic language.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Icelandic remained the preferred language among the ruling classes in Iceland; Danish was not used for official communications, most of the royal officials were of Icelandic descent and the language of the church and law courts remained Icelandic.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sami_languages">Sami languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sami languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">Sami languages</a> are indigenous minority languages in Scandinavia.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> They belong to <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">their own</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic language family</a> and are unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a> other than by limited grammatical (particularly lexical) characteristics resulting from prolonged contact.<sup id="cite_ref-Sapmi_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sapmi-56"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Sami is divided into several languages or dialects.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Consonant gradation is a feature in both Finnish and northern Sami dialects, but it is not present in south Sami, which is considered to have a different language history. According to the Sami Information Centre of the <a href="/wiki/Sami_Parliament" title="Sami Parliament" class="mw-redirect">Sami Parliament</a> in Sweden, southern Sami may have originated in an earlier migration from the south into the Scandinavian peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-Sapmi_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sapmi-56"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland_and_Scandinavia">Finland and Scandinavia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Finland and Scandinavia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Finland is officially bilingual, with Finnish and Swedish having mostly the same status at national level. Finland's majority population are <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finns</a>, whose mother tongue is either Finnish (approximately 95%), Swedish or both; the Swedish speaking minority lives mainly on the coast from the city of Porvoo, in the Gulf of Finland, to the city of Kokkola, up in the Bothnian Bay. The Åland Islands, an autonomous province of Finland, situated in the Baltic Sea between Finland and Sweden, is entirely Swedish speaking. Children are taught the other official language at school; for Swedish-speakers, this is Finnish (usually from the 3rd grade), and for Finnish-speakers, Swedish (usually from the 3rd, 5th or 7th grade).</p> 
     <p>Finnish speakers constitute a <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">language minority</a> in Sweden, Norway and Russian Federation. There are also languages derived from Finnish, having evolved separately, known as <a href="/wiki/Me%C3%A4nkieli" title="Meänkieli" class="mw-redirect">Meänkieli</a> in Sweden and <a href="/wiki/Kven_language" title="Kven language">Kven</a> in Norway.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>During a period of <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a> and state formation in the 10th–13th centuries, numerous <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Petty_kingdoms" title="Petty kingdoms" class="mw-redirect">petty kingdoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiefdoms" title="Chiefdoms" class="mw-redirect">chiefdoms</a> were unified into three kingdoms:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Denmark, forged from the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_Denmark" title="Lands of Denmark">Lands of Denmark</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zealand_(Denmark)" title="Zealand (Denmark)" class="mw-redirect">Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A5neland" title="Skåneland">Scania (Skåneland)</a> on the Scandinavian Peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The island <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a> in modern-day Sweden was initially also part of the Danish realm.)</li> 
      <li>Sweden, forged from the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_Sweden" title="Lands of Sweden">Lands of Sweden</a> on the Scandinavian Peninsula (excluding the provinces <a href="/wiki/Bohusl%C3%A4n" title="Bohuslän">Bohuslän</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4rjedalen" title="Härjedalen">Härjedalen</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4mtland" title="Jämtland">Jämtland</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%84lvdalen_Municipality" title="Älvdalen Municipality">Idre &amp; Särna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halland" title="Halland">Halland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blekinge" title="Blekinge">Blekinge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> of modern-day Sweden)</li> 
      <li>Norway (including <a href="/wiki/Bohusl%C3%A4n" title="Bohuslän">Bohuslän</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4rjedalen" title="Härjedalen">Härjedalen</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4mtland" title="Jämtland">Jämtland</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%84lvdalen_Municipality" title="Älvdalen Municipality">Idre &amp; Särna</a> on the Scandinavian Peninsula, and its island colonies Iceland, Greenland, Faroe Islands, <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>.)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>In the 1645 <a href="/wiki/Second_Treaty_of_Br%C3%B6msebro_(1645)" title="Second Treaty of Brömsebro (1645)">Treaty of Brömsebro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">Denmark–Norway</a> ceded the Norwegian provinces of Jämtland, Härjedalen and Idre &amp; Särna, as well as the Baltic Sea islands of Gotland and <a href="/wiki/Saaremaa" title="Saaremaa">Ösel</a> (in Estonia) to Sweden. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Roskilde" title="Treaty of Roskilde">Treaty of Roskilde</a>, signed in 1658, forced Denmark–Norway to cede the Danish provinces Scania, Blekinge, Halland, <a href="/wiki/Bornholm" title="Bornholm">Bornholm</a> and the Norwegian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Bohusl%C3%A4n" title="Bohuslän">Båhuslen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Trøndelag">Trøndelag</a> to Sweden. The 1660 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Copenhagen_(1660)" title="Treaty of Copenhagen (1660)">Treaty of Copenhagen</a> forced Sweden to return Bornholm and Trøndelag to Denmark–Norway, and to give up its recent claims to the island <a href="/wiki/Funen" title="Funen">Funen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Expanding North and East, today's Finland, as well as parts of Estonia and Russia, were under Scandinavian rule for significant periods of time. Scandinavia has, despite many wars over the years since the formation of the three kingdoms, been politically and culturally close.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavian_unions">Scandinavian unions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Scandinavian unions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The three Scandinavian kingdoms joined in 1387 in the <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">Kalmar Union</a> under Queen <a href="/wiki/Margaret_I_of_Denmark" title="Margaret I of Denmark">Margaret I of Denmark</a>. Sweden left the union in 1523 under King <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Vasa" title="Gustav Vasa" class="mw-redirect">Gustav Vasa</a>. In the aftermath of Sweden's secession from the Kalmar Union, civil war broke out in Denmark and Norway. The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> followed. When things had settled, the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" title="Privy Council" class="mw-redirect">Privy Council</a> was abolished—it assembled for the last time in 1537. A <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a>, entered into by the kingdoms of Denmark and Norway in 1536, lasted until 1814. Three sovereign <a href="/wiki/Successor_states" title="Successor states" class="mw-redirect">successor states</a> have subsequently emerged from this unequal union: Denmark, Norway and Iceland.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">Denmark–Norway</a> as a historiographical name refers to the former political union consisting of the kingdoms of Denmark and Norway, including the Norwegian dependencies of Iceland, Greenland and the Faroe Islands. The corresponding adjective and <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> is <a href="/wiki/Dano-Norwegian_(disambiguation)" title="Dano-Norwegian (disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig">Dano-Norwegian</a>. During Danish rule, Norway kept its separate laws, coinage and army, as well as some institutions such as a royal <a href="/wiki/Chancellor" title="Chancellor">chancellor</a>. Norway's old royal line had died out with the death of <a href="/wiki/Olav_IV" title="Olav IV" class="mw-redirect">Olav IV</a><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> in 1387, but Norway's remaining a <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_Kingdom_of_Norway" title="Hereditary Kingdom of Norway">hereditary kingdom</a> became an important factor for the <a href="/wiki/Oldenburg" title="Oldenburg" class="mw-redirect">Oldenburg</a> dynasty of Denmark–Norway in its struggles to win elections as kings of Denmark.</p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Kiel" title="Treaty of Kiel">Treaty of Kiel</a> (14 January 1814) formally dissolved the Dano-Norwegian union and ceded the territory of Norway proper to the King of Sweden, but Denmark retained Norway's overseas possessions. However, widespread Norwegian resistance to the prospect of a union with Sweden induced the governor of Norway, crown prince Christian Frederick (later <a href="/wiki/Christian_VIII_of_Denmark" title="Christian VIII of Denmark">Christian VIII of Denmark</a>), to call a constituent assembly at <a href="/wiki/Eidsvoll" title="Eidsvoll">Eidsvoll</a> in April 1814. The assembly drew up a liberal constitution and elected Christian Frederick to the throne of Norway. Following a Swedish invasion during the summer, the peace conditions of the <a href="/wiki/Convention_of_Moss" title="Convention of Moss">Convention of Moss</a> (14 August 1814) specified that king Christian Frederik had to resign, but Norway would keep its independence and its constitution within a personal union with Sweden. Christian Frederik formally abdicated on 10 August 1814 and returned to Denmark. The Norwegian parliament <a href="/wiki/Storting" title="Storting">Storting</a> elected king <a href="/wiki/Charles_XIII" title="Charles XIII" class="mw-redirect">Charles XIII</a> of Sweden as king of Norway on 4 November.</p> 
     <p>The Storting <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_union_between_Norway_and_Sweden" title="Dissolution of the union between Norway and Sweden">dissolved</a> the <a href="/wiki/Union_between_Sweden_and_Norway" title="Union between Sweden and Norway">union between Sweden and Norway</a> in 1905, after which the Norwegians elected Prince Charles of Denmark as king of Norway: he reigned as <a href="/wiki/Haakon_VII" title="Haakon VII" class="mw-redirect">Haakon VII</a>.</p>

     <p>The modern use of the term <i>Scandinavia</i> has been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Scandinavism</a> (the Scandinavist political movement), which was active in the middle of the nineteenth century, mainly between the <a href="/wiki/First_Schleswig_War" title="First Schleswig War">First Schleswig War</a> (1848–1850), in which Sweden and Norway contributed with considerable military force, and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Schleswig_War" title="Second Schleswig War">Second Schleswig War</a> (1864). In 1864, the Swedish parliament denounced the promises of military support made to Denmark by <a href="/wiki/Charles_XV_of_Sweden" title="Charles XV of Sweden">Charles XV of Sweden</a>. The members of the Swedish parliament were wary of joining an alliance against Prussia, the rising German power.</p> 
     <p>The Swedish king also proposed a unification of Denmark, Norway and Sweden into a single united kingdom. The background for the proposal was the tumultuous events during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_wars" title="Napoleonic wars" class="mw-redirect">Napoleonic wars</a> in the beginning of the century. This war resulted in Finland (formerly the eastern third of Sweden) becoming the Russian <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland" title="Grand Duchy of Finland">Grand Duchy of Finland</a> in 1809 and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> (<i>de jure</i> in union with Denmark since 1387, although <i>de facto</i> treated as a province) becoming independent in 1814, but thereafter swiftly forced to accept a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> with Sweden. The dependent territories Iceland, the Faroe Islands and Greenland, historically part of Norway, remained with Denmark in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Kiel" title="Treaty of Kiel">Treaty of Kiel</a>. Sweden and Norway were thus united under the Swedish monarch, but Finland's inclusion in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> excluded any possibility for a political union between Finland and any of the other Nordic countries.</p> 
     <p>The end of the Scandinavian political movement came when Denmark was denied the military support promised from Sweden and Norway to annex the (Danish) <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">Duchy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig" title="Schleswig" class="mw-redirect">Schleswig</a>, which together with the (German) Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a> had been in <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> with Denmark. The Second war of Schleswig followed in 1864, a brief but disastrous war between Denmark and <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> (supported by Austria). <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a> was conquered by Prussia, and after Prussia's success in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> a Prussian-led <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> was created, and a new <a href="/wiki/Power_(international)" title="Power (international)" class="mw-redirect">power-balance</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_sea_countries" title="Baltic sea countries" class="mw-redirect">Baltic sea countries</a> was established.</p> 
     <p>Even if a Scandinavian political union never came about at this point, there was a <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Monetary_Union" title="Scandinavian Monetary Union">Scandinavian Monetary Union</a> established in 1873, lasting until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
