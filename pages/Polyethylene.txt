     <p><b>Polyethylene</b> (abbreviated <b>PE</b>) or <b>polyethene</b> (<a href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC" class="mw-redirect">IUPAC</a> name <b>polyethene</b> or <b>poly(methylene)</b>) is the most common <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>. The annual global production is around 80 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its primary use is in <a href="/wiki/Packaging" title="Packaging" class="mw-redirect">packaging</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plastic_bag" title="Plastic bag">plastic bags</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic_film" title="Plastic film">plastic films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomembranes" title="Geomembranes" class="mw-redirect">geomembranes</a>, containers including <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottles</a>, etc.). Many kinds of polyethylene are known, with most having the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>)<sub><i>n</i></sub>. Thus, PE is usually a mixture of similar <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> with various values of <i>n</i>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Polyethylene was first synthesized by the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_Pechmann" title="Hans von Pechmann">Hans von Pechmann</a>, who prepared it by accident in 1898 while investigating <a href="/wiki/Diazomethane" title="Diazomethane">diazomethane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> When his colleagues <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Bamberger" title="Eugen Bamberger">Eugen Bamberger</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friedrich_Tschirner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Friedrich Tschirner (page does not exist)">Friedrich Tschirner</a> characterized the white, waxy substance that he had created, they recognized that it contained long –CH<sub>2</sub>– chains and termed it <i>polymethylene</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:First_polythene_pillbox.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/First_polythene_pillbox.JPG/220px-First_polythene_pillbox.JPG" width="220" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/First_polythene_pillbox.JPG/330px-First_polythene_pillbox.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/First_polythene_pillbox.JPG/440px-First_polythene_pillbox.JPG 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="595"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:First_polythene_pillbox.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A 
        <a href="/wiki/Pill_organizer" title="Pill organizer">pill box</a> presented to a technician at ICI in 1936 made from the first pound of polyethylene
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The first industrially practical polyethylene synthesis (diazomethane is a notoriously unstable substance that is generally avoided in industrial application) was discovered in 1933 by Eric Fawcett and Reginald Gibson, again by accident, at the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemical Industries</a> (ICI) works in <a href="/wiki/Northwich" title="Northwich">Northwich</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Upon applying extremely high pressure (several hundred atmospheres) to a mixture of ethylene and <a href="/wiki/Benzaldehyde" title="Benzaldehyde">benzaldehyde</a> they again produced a white, waxy, material. Because the reaction had been initiated by trace <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> contamination in their apparatus, the experiment was, at first, difficult to reproduce. It was not until 1935 that another ICI chemist, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Perrin" title="Michael Perrin">Michael Perrin</a>, developed this accident into a reproducible high-pressure synthesis for polyethylene that became the basis for industrial LDPE production beginning in 1939. Because polyethylene was found to have very low-loss properties at very high frequency radio waves, commercial distribution in Britain was suspended on the outbreak of World War II, secrecy imposed and the new process was used to produce insulation for UHF and SHF <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cables</a> of <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> sets. During World War II, further research was done on the ICI process and in 1944 Bakelite Corporation at Sabine, Texas, and Du Pont at Charleston, West Virginia, began large scale commercial production under license from ICI.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The breakthrough landmark in the commercial production of polyethylene began with the development of <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst" class="mw-redirect">catalyst</a> that promote the polymerization at mild temperatures and pressures. The first of these was a <a href="/wiki/Chromium_trioxide" title="Chromium trioxide">chromium trioxide</a>–based catalyst discovered in 1951 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Banks_(chemist)" title="Robert Banks (chemist)">Robert Banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._Paul_Hogan" title="J. Paul Hogan">J. Paul Hogan</a> at <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Petroleum" title="Phillips Petroleum" class="mw-redirect">Phillips Petroleum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1953 the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ziegler" title="Karl Ziegler">Karl Ziegler</a> developed a catalytic system based on <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> and organoaluminium compounds that worked at even milder conditions than the Phillips catalyst. The Phillips catalyst is less expensive and easier to work with, however, and both methods are heavily used industrially. By the end of the 1950s both the Phillips- and <a href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalyst" title="Ziegler-Natta catalyst" class="mw-redirect">Ziegler</a>-type catalysts were being used for HDPE production. In the 1970s, the Ziegler system was improved by the incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_chloride" title="Magnesium chloride">magnesium chloride</a>. Catalytic systems based on soluble catalysts, the <a href="/wiki/Metallocene" title="Metallocene">metallocenes</a>, were reported in 1976 by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kaminsky" title="Walter Kaminsky">Walter Kaminsky</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hansj%C3%B6rg_Sinn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hansjörg Sinn (page does not exist)">Hansjörg Sinn</a>. The Ziegler- and metallocene-based catalysts families have proven to be very flexible at copolymerizing ethylene with other <a href="/wiki/Olefin" title="Olefin" class="mw-redirect">olefins</a> and have become the basis for the wide range of polyethylene <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a> available today, including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Very_low_density_polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Very low density polyethylene (page does not exist)">very low density polyethylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linear_low-density_polyethylene" title="Linear low-density polyethylene">linear low-density polyethylene</a>. Such resins, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene#UHMWfiber" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">UHMWPE fibers</a>, have (as of 2005) begun to replace <a href="/wiki/Aramid" title="Aramid">aramids</a> in many high-strength applications.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The properties of polyethylene can be divided into mechanical, chemical, electrical, optical and thermal properties.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_properties">Mechanical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Polyethylene is of low strength, <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a> and rigidity, but has a high <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impact_strength" title="Impact strength" class="mw-redirect">impact strength</a> as well as low friction. It shows strong <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a> under persistent force, which can be reduced by addition of short fibers. It feels waxy when touched.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_properties">Thermal properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermal properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The usefulness of polyethylene is limited by its softening point of 80&nbsp;°C (176&nbsp;°F) (HDPE, types of low crystalline softens earlier). For common commercial grades of medium- and high-density polyethylene the melting point is typically in the range 120 to 180&nbsp;°C (248 to 356&nbsp;°F). The melting point for average, commercial, low-density polyethylene is typically 105 to 115&nbsp;°C (221 to 239&nbsp;°F). These temperatures vary strongly with the type of polyethylene.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Polyethylene consists of nonpolar, saturated, high molecular weight hydrocarbons. Therefore, its chemical behavior is similar to <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">paraffin</a>. The individual macromolecules are not <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalently</a> linked. Because of their symmetric molecular structure, they tend to crystallize; overall polyethylene is <a href="/wiki/Crystallization_of_polymers" title="Crystallization of polymers">partially crystalline</a>. Higher <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystallinity</a> increase <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and mechanical and chemical stability.</p> 
     <p>Most <a href="/wiki/LDPE" title="LDPE" class="mw-redirect">LDPE</a>, <a href="/wiki/MDPE" title="MDPE" class="mw-redirect">MDPE</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HDPE" title="HDPE" class="mw-redirect">HDPE</a> grades have excellent chemical resistance, meaning they are not attacked by strong acids or strong bases, and are resistant to gentle oxidants and reducing agents. Crystalline samples do not dissolve at room temperature. Polyethylene (other than cross-linked polyethylene) usually can be dissolved at elevated temperatures in <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic hydrocarbons</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xylene" title="Xylene">xylene</a>, or in chlorinated solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethane" title="Trichloroethane">trichloroethane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trichlorobenzene" title="Trichlorobenzene">trichlorobenzene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Polyethylene absorbs almost no <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. The gas and water vapor permeability (only polar gases) is lower than for most plastics; <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flavorings" title="Flavorings" class="mw-redirect">flavorings</a> on the other hand can pass it easily.</p> 
     <p>PE can become brittle when exposed to sunlight, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a> is usually used as a UV stabilizer.</p> 
     <p>Polyethylene burns slowly with a blue flame having a yellow tip and gives off an odour of paraffin (similar to <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candle</a> flame). The material continues burning on removal of the flame source and produces a drip.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Polyethylene can not be imprinted or stuck together without pretreatment.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_Properties">Electrical Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Electrical Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Polyethylene is a good <a href="/wiki/Electrical_insulator" title="Electrical insulator" class="mw-redirect">electrical insulator</a>. It offers good tracking resistance, however, it becomes easily electrostatically charged (which can be reduced by additions of graphite, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a> or antistatic agents).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_Properties">Optical Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Optical Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Depending on thermal history and film thickness PE can vary between almost clear (<a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">transparent</a>), milky-opaque (<a href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent" class="mw-redirect">translucent</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a>. PE-LD thereby owns the largest, PE-LLD slightly lower and PE-HD the least transparency. Transparency is reduced by <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">crystallites</a>, if they are larger than the wavelength of light.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_process">Manufacturing process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manufacturing process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monomer">Monomer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Monomer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The ingredient or <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> is <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (<a href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC" class="mw-redirect">IUPAC</a> name ethene), a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> with the formula C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>, which can be viewed as a pair of <a href="/wiki/Methylene_group" title="Methylene group">methylene groups</a> (=<span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 2</span></span>) connected to each other. Because the compound is highly reactive, the ethylene must be of high purity. Typical specifications are &lt;5 ppm for water, oxygen, and other <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. Acceptable contaminants include N<sub>2</sub>, ethane (common precursor to ethylene), and methane. Ethylene is usually produced from petrochemical sources, but also is generated by dehydration of ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymerization">Polymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Polymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Ethylene is a rather stable molecule that polymerizes only upon contact with catalysts. The conversion is highly <a href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic" class="mw-redirect">exothermic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coordination_polymerization" title="Coordination polymerization">Coordination polymerization</a> is the most pervasive technology, which means that metal chlorides or metal oxides are used. The most common catalysts consist of <a href="/wiki/Titanium(III)_chloride" title="Titanium(III) chloride">titanium(III) chloride</a>, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalyst" title="Ziegler-Natta catalyst" class="mw-redirect">Ziegler-Natta catalysts</a>. Another common catalyst is the <a href="/wiki/Phillips_catalyst" title="Phillips catalyst">Phillips catalyst</a>, prepared by depositing <a href="/wiki/Chromium_trioxide" title="Chromium trioxide">chromium(VI) oxide</a> on silica.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Polythylene can be produced through <a href="/wiki/Radical_polymerization" title="Radical polymerization">radical polymerization</a>, but this route has only limited utility and typically requires high-pressure apparatus.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joining">Joining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Joining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Commonly used methods for joining polyethylene parts together include:<sup id="cite_ref-pdl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pdl-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Hot_gas_welding" title="Hot gas welding" class="mw-redirect">Hot gas welding</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Fasteners" title="Fasteners" class="mw-redirect">Fastening</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared_welding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Infrared welding (page does not exist)">Infrared welding</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Laser_welding" title="Laser welding" class="mw-redirect">Laser welding</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_welding" title="Ultrasonic welding">Ultrasonic welding</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Heat_seal" title="Heat seal" class="mw-redirect">Heat sealing</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Heat_fusion" title="Heat fusion">Heat fusion</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">Adhesives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> are rarely used because polyethylene is <a href="/wiki/Nonpolar" title="Nonpolar" class="mw-redirect">nonpolar</a> and has a high resistance to solvents. <a href="/wiki/Pressure-sensitive_adhesive" title="Pressure-sensitive adhesive">Pressure-sensitive adhesives</a> (PSA) are feasible if the surface is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flame_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Flame treatment (page does not exist)">flame treated</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corona_treatment" title="Corona treatment">corona treated</a>. Commonly used adhesives include:<sup id="cite_ref-pdl_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pdl-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Dispersion of solvent-type PSAs</li> 
      <li>Polyurethane contact adhesives</li> 
      <li>Two-part <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane_adhesive" title="Polyurethane adhesive" class="mw-redirect">polyurethane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Epoxy_adhesive" title="Epoxy adhesive" class="mw-redirect">epoxy adhesives</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Vinyl_acetate" title="Vinyl acetate">Vinyl acetate</a> <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymer</a> <a href="/wiki/Hot_melt_adhesive" title="Hot melt adhesive" class="mw-redirect">hot melt adhesives</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Polyethylene is classified by its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Branching_(chemistry)" title="Branching (chemistry)" class="mw-redirect">branching</a>. Its mechanical properties depend significantly on variables such as the extent and type of branching, the crystal structure, and the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight" class="mw-redirect">molecular weight</a>. There are several types of polyethylene:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene</a> (UHMWPE)</li> 
      <li>Ultra-low-molecular-weight polyethylene (ULMWPE or PE-WAX)</li> 
      <li>High-molecular-weight polyethylene (HMWPE)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/High-density_polyethylene" title="High-density polyethylene">High-density polyethylene</a> (HDPE)</li> 
      <li>High-density cross-linked polyethylene (HDXLPE)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Cross-linked_polyethylene" title="Cross-linked polyethylene">Cross-linked polyethylene</a> (PEX or XLPE)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Medium-density_polyethylene" title="Medium-density polyethylene">Medium-density polyethylene</a> (MDPE)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Linear_low-density_polyethylene" title="Linear low-density polyethylene">Linear low-density polyethylene</a> (LLDPE)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Low-density_polyethylene" title="Low-density polyethylene">Low-density polyethylene</a> (LDPE)</li> 
      <li>Very-low-density polyethylene (VLDPE)</li> 
      <li>Chlorinated polyethylene (CPE)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>With regard to sold volumes, the most important polyethylene grades are HDPE, LLDPE, and LDPE.</p>

       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>UHMWPE is polyethylene with a molecular weight numbering in the millions, usually between 3.1 and 5.67 million. The high molecular weight makes it a very <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">tough</a> material, but results in less efficient packing of the chains into the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a> as evidenced by densities of less than high density polyethylene (for example, 0.930–0.935&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>). UHMWPE can be made through any catalyst technology, although Ziegler catalysts are most common. Because of its outstanding toughness and its cut, wear, and excellent chemical resistance, UHMWPE is used in a diverse range of applications. These include can- and <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottle</a>-handling machine parts, moving parts on weaving machines, bearings, gears, artificial joints, edge protection on ice rinks, and butchers' chopping boards. It is commonly used for the construction of articular portions of <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">implants</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Hip_replacement" title="Hip replacement">hip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knee_replacement" title="Knee replacement">knee replacements</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene#UHMWfiber" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">fiber</a>, it competes with <a href="/wiki/Aramid" title="Aramid">aramid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bulletproof_vests" title="Bulletproof vests" class="mw-redirect">bulletproof vests</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-density_polyethylene_.28HDPE.29">High-density polyethylene (HDPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: High-density polyethylene (HDPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/High-density_polyethylene" title="High-density polyethylene">High-density polyethylene</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg/220px-Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg/330px-Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg/440px-Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4142" data-file-height="2912"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Hdpe_pipe_installation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/High-density_polyethylene" title="High-density polyethylene">HDPE</a> pipe installation in storm drain project in 
        <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>HDPE is defined by a density of greater or equal to 0.941&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>. HDPE has a low degree of branching. The mostly linear molecules pack together well, so intermolecular forces are stronger than in highly branched polymers. HDPE can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>/silica catalysts, <a href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalyst" title="Ziegler-Natta catalyst" class="mw-redirect">Ziegler-Natta catalysts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metallocene" title="Metallocene">metallocene</a> catalysts; by choosing catalysts and reaction conditions, the small amount of branching that does occur can be controlled. These catalysts prefer the formation of <a href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals" class="mw-redirect">free radicals</a> at the ends of the growing polyethylene molecules. They cause new ethylene monomers to add to the ends of the molecules, rather than along the middle, causing the growth of a linear chain.</p> 
     <p>HDPE has high tensile strength. It is used in products and packaging such as milk jugs, detergent bottles, butter tubs, garbage containers, and water pipes. One-third of all toys are manufactured from HDPE. In 2007, the global HDPE consumption reached a volume of more than 30 million tons.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-linked_polyethylene_.28PEX_or_XLPE.29">Cross-linked polyethylene (PEX or XLPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cross-linked polyethylene (PEX or XLPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Cross-linked_polyethylene" title="Cross-linked polyethylene">Cross-linked polyethylene</a>
     </div> 
     <p>PEX is a medium- to high-density polyethylene containing <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">cross-link</a> bonds introduced into the polymer structure, changing the thermoplastic into a <a href="/wiki/Thermoset" title="Thermoset" class="mw-redirect">thermoset</a>. The high-temperature properties of the polymer are improved, its flow is reduced, and its chemical resistance is enhanced. PEX is used in some potable-water plumbing systems because tubes made of the material can be expanded to fit over a metal nipple and it will slowly return to its original shape, forming a permanent, water-tight connection.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium-density_polyethylene_.28MDPE.29">Medium-density polyethylene (MDPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Medium-density polyethylene (MDPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Medium-density_polyethylene" title="Medium-density polyethylene">Medium-density polyethylene</a>
     </div> 
     <p>MDPE is defined by a density range of 0.926–0.940&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>. MDPE can be produced by chromium/silica catalysts, Ziegler-Natta catalysts, or metallocene catalysts. MDPE has good shock and drop resistance properties. It also is less notch-sensitive than HDPE; stress-cracking resistance is better than HDPE. MDPE is typically used in gas pipes and fittings, sacks, shrink film, packaging film, carrier bags, and screw closures.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_low-density_polyethylene_.28LLDPE.29">Linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Linear_low-density_polyethylene" title="Linear low-density polyethylene">Linear low-density polyethylene</a>
     </div> 
     <p>LLDPE is defined by a density range of 0.915–0.925&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>. LLDPE is a substantially linear polymer with significant numbers of short branches, commonly made by <a href="/wiki/Copolymerization" title="Copolymerization" class="mw-redirect">copolymerization</a> of ethylene with short-chain <a href="/wiki/Alpha-olefin" title="Alpha-olefin">alpha-olefins</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/1-butene" title="1-butene" class="mw-redirect">1-butene</a>, <a href="/wiki/1-hexene" title="1-hexene" class="mw-redirect">1-hexene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1-octene" title="1-octene" class="mw-redirect">1-octene</a>). LLDPE has higher tensile strength than LDPE, and it exhibits higher impact and <a href="/wiki/Puncture_resistance" title="Puncture resistance">puncture resistance</a> than LDPE. Lower thickness (gauge) films can be blown, compared with LDPE, with better environmental stress-cracking resistance, but is not as easy to process. LLDPE is used in packaging, particularly film for bags and sheets. Lower thickness may be used compared to LDPE. It is used for cable coverings, toys, lids, buckets, containers, and pipe. While other applications are available, LLDPE is used predominantly in film applications due to its toughness, flexibility, and relative transparency. Product examples range from agricultural films, Saran wrap, and bubble wrap, to multilayer and composite films. In 2013, the world LLDPE market reached a volume of US$40 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-density_polyethylene_.28LDPE.29">Low-density polyethylene (LDPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Low-density polyethylene (LDPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Low-density_polyethylene" title="Low-density polyethylene">Low-density polyethylene</a>
     </div> 
     <p>LDPE is defined by a density range of 0.910–0.940&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>. LDPE has a high degree of short- and long-chain branching, which means that the chains do not pack into the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a> as well. It has, therefore, less strong intermolecular forces as the <a href="/wiki/Instantaneous-dipole_induced-dipole_attraction" title="Instantaneous-dipole induced-dipole attraction" class="mw-redirect">instantaneous-dipole induced-dipole attraction</a> is less. This results in a lower <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength" class="mw-redirect">tensile strength</a> and increased <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a>. LDPE is created by <a href="/wiki/Radical_polymerization" title="Radical polymerization">free-radical polymerization</a>. The high degree of branching with long chains gives molten LDPE unique and desirable flow properties. LDPE is used for both rigid containers and plastic film applications such as plastic bags and film wrap. In 2013, the global LDPE market had a volume of almost US$33 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The radical polymerization process used to make LDPE does not include a catalyst that "supervises" the radical sites on the growing PE chains. (In HDPE synthesis, the radical sites are at the ends of the PE chains, because the catalyst stabilizes their formation at the ends.) Secondary <a href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical" class="mw-redirect">radicals</a> (in the middle of a chain) are more stable than primary radicals (at the end of the chain), and tertiary radicals (at a branch point) are more stable yet. Each time an ethylene monomer is added, it creates a primary radical, but often these will rearrange to form more stable secondary or tertiary radicals. Addition of ethylene monomers to the secondary or tertiary sites creates branching.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very-low-density_polyethylene_.28VLDPE.29">Very-low-density polyethylene (VLDPE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Very-low-density polyethylene (VLDPE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>VLDPE is defined by a density range of 0.880–0.915&nbsp;g/cm<sup>3</sup>. VLDPE is a substantially linear polymer with high levels of short-chain branches, commonly made by copolymerization of ethylene with short-chain alpha-olefins (for example, 1-butene, 1-hexene and 1-octene). VLDPE is most commonly produced using metallocene catalysts due to the greater co-monomer incorporation exhibited by these catalysts. VLDPEs are used for hose and tubing, ice and frozen food bags, food packaging and stretch wrap as well as impact modifiers when blended with other polymers.</p> 
     <p>Recently, much research activity has focused on the nature and distribution of long chain branches in polyethylene. In HDPE, a relatively small number of these branches, perhaps one in 100 or 1,000 branches per backbone carbon, can significantly affect the <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheological</a> properties of the polymer.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copolymers">Copolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Copolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In addition to <a href="/wiki/Copolymerization" title="Copolymerization" class="mw-redirect">copolymerization</a> with alpha-olefins, ethylene can also be copolymerized with a wide range of other monomers and ionic composition that creates ionized free radicals. Common examples include <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_acetate" title="Vinyl acetate">vinyl acetate</a> (the resulting product is <a href="/wiki/Ethylene-vinyl_acetate" title="Ethylene-vinyl acetate">ethylene-vinyl acetate</a> copolymer, or EVA, widely used in athletic-shoe sole foams) and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Acrylate" title="Acrylate">acrylates</a>. Applications of <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_resin" title="Acrylic resin">acrylic</a> <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymer</a> include packaging and sporting goods, and <a href="/wiki/Superplasticizer" title="Superplasticizer">superplasticizer</a>, used for cement production.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_structure_of_different_PE_types">Molecular structure of different PE types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Molecular structure of different PE types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The diverse material behavior of different types of polyethylene can be explained by their molecular structure. Molecular weight and crystallinity are having the biggest impact, the crystallinity in turn depends on molecular weight and degree of branching. The less the polymer chains are branched, and the smaller the molecular weight, the higher the crystallinity of polyethylene. The crystallinity is between 35% (PE-LD/PE-LLD) and 80% (PE-HD). Within crystallites polyethylene has a density of 1.0&nbsp;g·cm<sup>−3</sup>, in the amorphous regions of 0.86&nbsp;g·cm<sup>−3</sup>.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegrading_plastics">Biodegrading plastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Biodegrading plastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>One of the main problems of polyethylene is that without special treatment it is not readily biodegradable, and thus accumulates. In Japan, getting rid of plastics in an environmentally friendly way was the major problem discussed until the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">Fukushima disaster</a> in 2011. It was listed as a $90 billion market for solutions. Since 2008, Japan has rapidly increased the recycling of plastics, but still has a large amount of plastic wrapping which goes to waste.<sup id="cite_ref-prideaux2007_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prideaux2007-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In May 2008, Daniel Burd, a 16-year-old Canadian, won the <a href="/wiki/Canada-Wide_Science_Fair" title="Canada-Wide Science Fair">Canada-Wide Science Fair</a> in Ottawa after discovering that <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_fluorescens" title="Pseudomonas fluorescens">Pseudomonas fluorescens</a></i>, with the help of <i><a href="/wiki/Sphingomonas" title="Sphingomonas">Sphingomonas</a></i>, can degrade over 40% of the weight of plastic bags in less than three months.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The thermophilic bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Brevibacillus_borstelensis" title="Brevibacillus borstelensis">Brevibacillus borstelensis</a></i> (strain 707) was isolated from a soil sample and found to use low-density polyethylene as a sole carbon source; when incubated together at 50&nbsp;°C. Biodegradation increased with time exposed to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2010, a Japanese researcher, Akinori Ito, released the prototype of a machine which creates oil from polyethylene using a small, self-contained vapor distillation process.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><i>Acinetobacter</i> sp. 351 can degrade lower molecular-weight PE oligomers. When PE is subjected to thermo- and photo-oxidization, products including alkanes, alkenes, ketones, aldehydes, alcohols, carboxylic acid, keto-acids, dicarboxylic acids, lactones, and esters are released.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2014, a Chinese researcher discovered that <a href="/wiki/Indian_mealmoth" title="Indian mealmoth">Indian mealmoth</a> larvae could metabolize polyethylene from observing that plastic bags at his home had small holes in them. Deducing that the hungry larvae must have digested the plastic somehow, he and his team analyzed their gut bacteria and found a few that could use plastic as their only carbon source. Not only could the bacteria from the guts of the <i>Plodia interpunctella</i> moth larvae metabolize polyethylene, they degraded it significantly, dropping its tensile strength by 50%, its mass by 10% and the molecular weights of its polymeric chains by 13%.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemically_modified_polyethylene">Chemically modified polyethylene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Chemically modified polyethylene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Polyethylene may either be modified in the polymerization by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a> or non-polar <a href="/wiki/Comonomer" title="Comonomer">comonomers</a> or after polymerization through polymer-analogous reactions. Common polymer-analogous reactions are in case of polyethylene <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">crosslinking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorination_reaction" title="Chlorination reaction" class="mw-redirect">chlorination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfochlorination" title="Sulfochlorination" class="mw-redirect">sulfochlorination</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-polar_ethylene_copolymers">Non-polar ethylene copolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Non-polar ethylene copolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B1-olefins">α-olefins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: α-olefins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In the low pressure process <a href="/wiki/Alpha-olefin" title="Alpha-olefin">α-olefins</a> (eg. <a href="/wiki/1-Butene" title="1-Butene">1-butene</a> or <a href="/wiki/1-Hexene" title="1-Hexene">1-hexene</a>) may be added, which are incorporated in the polymer chain during polymerization. These copolymers introduce short side chains, thus <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystallinity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> are reduced. As explained above, mechanical and thermal properties are changed thereby. In particular, PE-LLD is produced this way.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Metallocene_polyethylene_.28PE-MC.29">Metallocene polyethylene (PE-MC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Metallocene polyethylene (PE-MC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Metallocene polyethylene (PE-M) is prepared by means of <a href="/wiki/Metallocene_catalysts" title="Metallocene catalysts" class="mw-redirect">metallocene catalysts</a>, usually including copolymers (z. B. ethene / hexene). Metallocene polyethylene has a relatively narrow <a href="/wiki/Molecular_weight_distribution" title="Molecular weight distribution" class="mw-redirect">molecular weight distribution</a>, exceptionally high toughness, excellent optical properties and a uniform comonomer content. Because of the narrow molecular weight distribution it behaves less pseudoplastic (especially under larger shear rates). Metallocene polyethylene has a low proportion of low molecular weight (extractable) components and a low welding and sealing temperature. Thus, it is particularly suitable for the food industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:238</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vasile_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vasile-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:19</sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyethylene_with_multimodal_molecular_weight_distribution">Polyethylene with multimodal molecular weight distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Polyethylene with multimodal molecular weight distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Polyethylene with multimodal molecular weight distribution consists of several polymer fractions, which are homogeneously mixed. Such polyethylene types offer extremely high stiffness, toughness, strength, stress crack resistance and an increased crack propagation resistance. They consist of equal proportions higher and lower molekularerer polymer fractions. The lower molecular weight units crystallize easier and relax faster. The higher molecular weight fractions form linking molecules between crystallites, thereby increasing toughness and stress crack resistance. Polyethylene with multimodal molecular weight distribution can be prepared either in two-stage reactors, by catalysts with two different active centers on a carrier or by blending in extruders.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:238</sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclic_olefin_copolymers_.28COC.29">Cyclic olefin copolymers (COC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Cyclic olefin copolymers (COC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Cyclic_olefin_copolymer" title="Cyclic olefin copolymer">Cyclic olefin copolymers</a> are prepared by copolymerization of ethene and <a href="/wiki/Cycloolefin" title="Cycloolefin" class="mw-redirect">cycloolefins</a> (usually <a href="/wiki/Norbornene" title="Norbornene">norbornene</a>) produced by using metallocene catalysts. The resulting polymers are amorphous polymers and particularly transparent and heat resistant.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:239</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vasile_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vasile-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_ethylene_copolymers">Polar ethylene copolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Polar ethylene copolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The basic compounds used as polar comonomers are vinyl alcohol (<a href="/wiki/Vinyl_alcohol" title="Vinyl alcohol">Ethenol</a>, an unsaturated alcohol), acrylic acid (<a href="/wiki/Propenoic_acid" title="Propenoic acid" class="mw-redirect">propenoic acid</a>, an unsaturated acid) and <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> containing one of the two compounds.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethylene_copolymers_with_unsaturated_alcohols">Ethylene copolymers with unsaturated alcohols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Ethylene copolymers with unsaturated alcohols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Ethylene/vinyl alcohol copolymer (EVOH) is (formally) a copolymer of PE and vinyl alcohol (ethenol), which is prepared by (partial) hydrolysis of ethylene-vinyl acetate copolymer (as vinyl alcohol itself is not stable). However, typically EVOH has a higher comonomer content than the VAC commonly used.<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:239</sup></p> 
     <p>EVOH is used in multilayer films for packaging as a barrier layer (barrier plastic). As EVOH is hygroscopic (water-attracting), it absorbs water from the environment, whereby it loses its barrier effect. Therefore, it must be used as a core layer surrounded by other plastics (like LDPE, PP, PA or PET). EVOH is also used as a coating agent against corrosion at street lights, traffic light poles and noise protection walls.<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:239</sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethylene.2Facrylic_acid_copolymers_.28EAA.29">Ethylene/acrylic acid copolymers (EAA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Ethylene/acrylic acid copolymers (EAA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Copolymer of ethylene and unsaturated carboxylic acids (such as acrylic acid) are characterized by good adhesion to different materials, by resistance to stress cracking and high flexibility.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> However, they are more sensitive to heat and oxidation than ethylene homopolymers. Ethylene/acrylic acid copolymers are used as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adhesion_promoter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Adhesion promoter (page does not exist)">adhesion promoters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>If salts of an unsaturated carboxylic acid are present in the polymer, thermo-reversible ion networks are formed, they are called <a href="/wiki/Ionomer" title="Ionomer">ionomers</a>. Ionomers are highly transparent thermoplastics which are characterized by high adhesion to metals, high abrasion resistance and high water absorption.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethylene_copolymers_with_unsaturated_esters">Ethylene copolymers with unsaturated esters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Ethylene copolymers with unsaturated esters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>If unsaturated esters are copolymerized with ethylene, either the alcohol moiety may be in the polymer backbone (as it is the case in ethylene-vinyl acetate copolymer) or of the acid moiety (e. g. in ethylene-ethyl acrylate copolymer). <a href="/wiki/Ethylene-vinyl_acetate" title="Ethylene-vinyl acetate">Ethylene-vinyl acetate</a> copolymers are prepared similarly to LD-PE by high pressure polymerization. The proportion of comonomer has a decisive influence on the behaviour of the polymer.</p> 
     <p>The density decreases up to a comonomer share of 10% because of the disturbed crystal formation. With higher proportions it approaches to the one of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate" title="Polyvinyl acetate">polyvinyl acetate</a> (1.17 g/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:235</sup> Due to decreasing crystallinity ethylene vinyl acetate copolymers are getting softer with increasing comonomer content. The polar side groups change the chemical properties significantly (compared to polyethylene):<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:224</sup> weather resistance, adhesiveness and weldability rise with comonomer content, while the chemical resistance decreases. Also mechanical properties are changed: stress cracking resistance and toughness in the cold rise, whereas yield stress and heat resistance decrease. With a very high proportion of comonomers (about 50%) rubbery thermoplastics are produced (<a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic_elastomer" title="Thermoplastic elastomer">thermoplastic elastomers</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:235</sup></p> 
     <p>Ethylene-ethyl acrylate copolymers behave similarly to ethylene-vinyl acetate copolymers.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:240</sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crosslinking">Crosslinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Crosslinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Various methods can used to prepare <a href="/wiki/Cross-linked_polyethylene" title="Cross-linked polyethylene">cross-linked polyethylene</a> (PE-X) from thermoplastic polyethylene (PE-LD, PE-LLD or PE-HD). By crosslinking low-temperature impact strength, <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_resistance" title="Abrasion resistance" class="mw-redirect">abrasion resistance</a> and environmental stress cracking resistance can be increased significantly, whereas <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a> and rigidity are somewhat reduced. PE-X does not melt anymore (analogous to elastomers) and is thermally resistant (over longer periods of up to 120&nbsp;°C, for short periods without mechanical load up to 250&nbsp;°C). With increasing crosslinking density also the maximum shear modulus increases (even at higher temperatures).<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Saechtling_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saechtling-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> PE-X has significantly enhanced properties compared with ordinary PE.<sup id="cite_ref-Koltzenburg_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koltzenburg-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> As PE-X is infusible, always the final pre-products or the mold part are cross-linked.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>PE-X is used as insulating material for medium and <a href="/wiki/High_voltage" title="High voltage">high voltage</a> <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">cable insulation</a>, for hot water pipes and molded parts in electrical engineering, plant engineering and in automotive industry.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_crosslinking">Types of crosslinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Types of crosslinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>A basic distinction is made between peroxide crosslinking (PE-Xa), silane crosslinking (PE-Xb), electron beam crosslinking (PE-Xc) and azo crosslinking (PE-Xd).<sup id="cite_ref-Saechtling_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saechtling-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chlorination_and_sulfochlorination">Chlorination and sulfochlorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Chlorination and sulfochlorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorinated_Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chlorinated Polyethylene (page does not exist)">Chlorinated Polyethylene</a> (PE-C) is an inexpensive material having a chlorine content from 34 to 44%. It is used in blends with <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> because the soft, rubbery chloropolyethylene is embedded in the PVC matrix, thereby increasing the <a href="/wiki/Impact_resistance" title="Impact resistance" class="mw-redirect">impact resistance</a>. In addition, it also increases the weather resistance. Furthermore, it is used for softening PVC foils, without risking the migrate of plasticizers. Chlorinated polyethylene can be crosslinked peroxidically to form an elastomer which is used in cable and rubber industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Domininghaus_25-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domininghaus-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> When chlorinated polyethylene is added to other polyolefins, it reduces the flammability.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaiser_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaiser-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:245</sup></p> 
     <p>Chlorosulfonated PE (CSM) is used as starting material for ozone resistant synthetic rubber.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio-based_polyethylene">Bio-based polyethylene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Bio-based polyethylene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">

     <p><a href="/wiki/Braskem" title="Braskem">Braskem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Tsusho_Corporation" title="Toyota Tsusho Corporation" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Tsusho Corporation</a> started joint marketing activities to produce polyethylene from <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>. Braskem will build a new facility at their existing industrial unit in Triunfo, RS, Brazil with an annual production capacity of 200,000 short tons (180,000,000&nbsp;kg), and will produce high-density and low-density polyethylene from <a href="/wiki/Bioethanol" title="Bioethanol" class="mw-redirect">bioethanol</a> derived from sugarcane.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Polyethylene can also be made from other feedstocks, including <a href="/wiki/Wheat_grain" title="Wheat grain" class="mw-redirect">wheat grain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>. Retrieved from cane sugar, i.e. plant biomass renewable feedstock, Brazil is the first country to develop the product.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_and_general_description_of_the_process">Nomenclature and general description of the process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Nomenclature and general description of the process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The name polyethylene comes from the ingredient and not the resulting chemical compound, which contains no double bonds. The scientific name <i>polyethene</i> is systematically derived from the scientific name of the monomer.<sup id="cite_ref-guide_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kahovec_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahovec-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The alkene monomer converts to a long, sometimes <i>very</i> long, alkane in the polymerization process.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahovec_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahovec-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In certain circumstances it is useful to use a structure-based nomenclature; in such cases <a href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC" class="mw-redirect">IUPAC</a> recommends poly(methylene) (poly(methanediyl) is a non-preferred alternative).<sup id="cite_ref-guide_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The difference in names between the two systems is due to the <i>opening up</i> of the monomer's double bond upon polymerization.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The name is abbreviated to <b>PE</b>. In a similar manner <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> are shortened to PP and PS, respectively. In the United Kingdom the polymer is commonly called <b>polythene</b>, from the ICI <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trade name</a>, although this is not recognized scientifically.</p>
