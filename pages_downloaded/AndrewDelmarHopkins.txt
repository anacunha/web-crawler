   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Andrew Delmar Hopkins</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b>Andrew Delmar Hopkins</b> (August 20, 1857 – September 22, 1948) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist" class="mw-redirect">entomologist</a> of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Though self-taught, his scientific understanding of forest entomology was exceptional. He received an honorary doctorate from West Virginia University and in 1902 he went to work for the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Agriculture" title="US Department of Agriculture" class="mw-redirect">US Department of Agriculture</a>. He was subsequently named head of the newly created Division of Forest Insect Investigations. He became a specialist in the bark beetle family <a href="/wiki/Scolytidae" title="Scolytidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolytidae</a>, especially the genus <a href="/wiki/Dendroctonus" title="Dendroctonus">Dendroctonus</a>, species of which are the most destructive insects in coniferous forests of North America. His taxonomic monographs on these beetles are classics. He proposed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Bioclimatics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Law of Bioclimatics (page does not exist)">Law of Bioclimatics</a> and he also developed the <a href="/wiki/Hopkins_Notes_and_Records_System" title="Hopkins Notes and Records System">Hopkins Notes and Records System</a>, a system he brought into the federal government when he first came to work for the Division of Entomology in the late 1890s. Hopkins’ research is one of the cornerstones of entomology on the North American continent and he is often referred to as the “father of North American forest entomology.”</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_History">Life History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A.D. Hopkins was born in Jackson County, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Jackson_County,_West_Virginia" title="Jackson County, West Virginia">Jackson County</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>). His took an interest in nature from childhood, and by the age of twelve, he was collecting insects. This curiosity would eventually be channeled into an intense desire to study forest insects and the changes they effected in woodlands. Yet even though Hopkins was seminal in developing the scope of forest entomology in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, his only formal education was in the county schools of West Virginia. After his schooling and at the age of 17, he took the reins of his grandfather's farm. His work organizing one of the first farm cooperatives during that period brought him to the attention of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Virginia_Agricultural_Experiment_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="West Virginia Agricultural Experiment Station (page does not exist)">West Virginia Agricultural Experiment Station</a>. The State was looking for an entomologist and Hopkins volunteered, making it clear that the pay was not important to him, and asking only that he be allowed to work from his home. To alleviate the Station's concern that he wasn't professionally trained, he sent the director the manuscript he'd written about a local agricultural pest complete with his original drawings.<sup id="cite_ref-Furniss1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furniss1-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>His work gradually shifted from farming to scientific research. He started his investigations for the Station by determining which insects were causing the most damage in West Virginia. He traveled throughout the state, gathering the data he needed by interviewing farmers. On one of those trips, he and a co-worker discovered a major bark beetle outbreak in the eastern highlands, on <a href="/wiki/Cheat_Mountain" title="Cheat Mountain">Cheat Mountain</a>. The beetles, along with the <a href="/wiki/Hessian_fly" title="Hessian fly">Hessian fly</a>'s effects on wheat, then became the main focus of his early work. Both helped build his reputation, given the scientific understanding they brought to entomology.</p> 
     <p>Hopkins also taught Economic Entomology at the University of West Virginia, where he'd received an honorary doctorate. Through the 1890s and into the 20th century, he would acquire ever-greater expertise in his chosen field. That expertise increased his stature in the scientific community. His increased status meant that he was given the opportunity to excel in his professional role as other entomologists elaborated on his work. He eventually left his position as vice-director of the West Virginia Agricultural Experiment Station to resume his work with the US Department of Agriculture, where he stayed for the rest of his career.</p> 
     <p>He had originally worked for the Department as a special agent, with most of his work in the Western United States studying the impact of bark beetles on Western conifers. By 1904 after his return, he rose to become head of the newly formed Division of Forest Insects (Bureau of Entomology) in <a href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C." class="mw-redirect">Washington D.C.</a>. Hopkins retired in 1923, and he returned home to his farm in <a href="/wiki/Wood_County,_West_Virginia" title="Wood County, West Virginia">Wood County, West Virginia</a>, where he'd started his career He continued developing his ideas on <a href="/wiki/Bioclimatics" title="Bioclimatics" class="mw-redirect">bioclimatics</a>, and the Department of Agriculture established a field station which was given the title "the Kanawha Farms Intercontinental Base Station for Bioclimatic Research." He also took up plant breeding once again, an avocation that had first gotten him noticed during his earliest days with the farm cooperative he'd helped start. He died from unknown causes in 1948.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements">Achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>During his lifetime, he was a member of many scientific organizations and held leadership positions in many of them. He was a fellow of the American Academy for the Advancement of Science, having become a member in 1893, and an emeritus member in 1938, a fellow of the Entomological Society of America, a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Economic_Entomologists" title="American Association of Economic Entomologists" class="mw-redirect">American Association of Economic Entomologists</a> (vice-president in 1900 and president in 1902), first president of the West Virginia Academy of Sciences, president of the Entomological Society of Washington, president in 1920 of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Society_of_Washington" title="Biological Society of Washington">Biological Society of Washington</a>, vice president of the Washington Academy of the Sciences, life member of the American Meteorological Society, honorary member of the Society of Economic Biologists of England, and member of the Cosmos Club. Andrew was elected to the Washington Biologists’ Field Club in 1904 and ended his membership in 1912.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_Study">Areas of Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Areas of Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioclimatics">Bioclimatics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bioclimatics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>During his time as an entomologist, Andrew Delmar Hopkins’ personal study also included bioclimatics. Hopkins described bioclimatics as the attempt to correlate phenological phenomena of plants and animals with the various elements which make up the climate of the region. In 1889, Hopkins formulated the relationship of elevation, latitude and longitude to seasonal events such as the coming of spring. The relationship was coined the “Hopkins Law of Bioclimatics”. Many horticulturalists and other plant enthusiasts use this law to determine a planting schedule.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_Time_Law">Spring Time Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spring Time Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>While living in West Virginia in 1889, A.D. Hopkins discovered that spring advances one day for every 15 minutes of latitude northward, 1.25&nbsp;days for each degree of longitude westward, and one day for every 100 feet higher in elevation. Thus, for a site located 15 minutes of latitude north, one degree west and 200 meters higher than another chosen site, spring should arrive 4.25&nbsp;days later. For example, according to the Hopkins Law, flowers should bloom, trees should bud, and geese should appear around 11&nbsp;days later in Omaha, Neb. than in Kansas City, Kansas. The calculation is expressed in the appendix.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bark_Beetle_Research">Bark Beetle Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bark Beetle Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Many of Hopkins early research consisted of qualitative observations of the beetle family. Hopkins most famous bark beetle observation was coined the Hopkins' host-selection principle (HHSP), which refers to the observation that many adult insects demonstrate a preference for the host species on which they themselves developed as larvae. However, the practicality of HHSP has been debated significantly since its first proposal in 1916. Many modern scientists have even discounted the evidence that supports HHSP as speculation. Although he has written dozens of well renowned publications, Hopkins most popular on bark beetles include the “Catalog of West Virginia Scolytidae (Bark Beetles) and Their Enemies" and "Catalog of West Virginia Forest and Shade Tree Insects."</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Delmar_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The diversity and volume of publications that Hopkins produced is unmatched by any other entomologist. Hopkins’ breakthrough research on North American beetles and quantifying Hopkins Law are still highly regarded in today’s scientific community.</p>

     <p>Appendix</p> 
     <p><b>Example Calculations using Hopkins Time Law</b></p> 
     <p>If you were to travel from <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Kansas" title="Kansas City, Kansas">Kansas City</a> (1000&nbsp;ft elevation, 39.11 degrees N latitude, -94.63 longitude) to <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a> (1034&nbsp;feet elevation, 41.26 degrees N latitude, -95.93 longitude), the delay in the coming of spring would be…</p> 
     <p>[((1034&nbsp;ft - 1000&nbsp;ft)/100&nbsp;ft * 1.25&nbsp;days) + ((41.26 degrees - 39.11 degrees)* 4&nbsp;days) + ((-95.93 degrees - -94.63 degrees ) * 1.25&nbsp;days)] =</p> 
     <p>(34&nbsp;ft /100&nbsp;ft * 1.25&nbsp;days) + (2.15 degrees * 4&nbsp;days) + (1.3 degrees * 1.25&nbsp;days) = 8.5&nbsp;days + 2.6875&nbsp;days + 1.625&nbsp;days = 10.65 Days</p>
