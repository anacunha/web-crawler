   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Window film</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
      Jump to: 
     <a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 
     <a href="#p-search">search</a> 
    </div> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      "Tinted windows" redirects here. For the musical group, see 
      <a href="/wiki/Tinted_Windows_(band)" title="Tinted Windows (band)">Tinted Windows (band)</a>.
     </div> 
     <p><b>Window film</b> is a thin film or laminate that can be installed to the interior of glass surfaces in <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile" class="mw-redirect">automobiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boats" title="Boats" class="mw-redirect">boats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">homes</a> and buildings. It is usually made from <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> due to its clarity, tensile strength, dimensional stability; and ability to accept a variety of surface-applied or embedded treatments.</p> 
     <p>Window film can be generically described by the components it contains for performance and/or aesthetics; i.e. dyed, metallized, ceramic - or by its intended use. Uses can be roughly divided into automotive/marine where surfaces are often curved, and architectural where flat glass is usual. Window film is normally applied by professional service companies but there are also D.I.Y kits widely available.</p> 
     <p>The International Window Film Association (www.iwfa.com), founded in 1991 provides unbranded information about window films.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>There are many different grades, shades, colours, and thicknesses of available window films built to offer solutions to a variety of challenges. Window films are a retrofit upgrade for existing glass that can be used to address problems inherent to glazing, including:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Heat and glare reduction</li> 
      <li>Thermal insulation</li> 
      <li>UV filtration</li> 
      <li>Safety and security</li> 
      <li>Privacy</li> 
      <li>Decoration, Signage and branding</li> 
      <li>Protection from <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Window films are an extremely cost-effective method to reduce heating and cooling costs in existing buildings by reducing the amount of heat transfer through glazing.<sup id="cite_ref-Energy_Saving_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Saving-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_properties">Primary properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Primary properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg/250px-CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg" width="250" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg/375px-CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg/500px-CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2920" data-file-height="2598"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:CIT_Building_Herndon_Va.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The colorful film on these windows gives the building a unique appearance while providing privacy and insulation from the sun.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Heat rejection films are normally applied to the interior of flat glass windows to reduce the amount of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV) <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> entering windows. Such films are usually dyed or metalized (which can be transparent to visible light) to convert incoming <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation" class="mw-redirect">solar radiation</a> to infrared radiation, which is then rejected back through the glass to the exterior.<sup id="cite_ref-solar_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solar-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Modern window film technology has created ceramic window films which are non-metallic and do not contain dyes that can result in discoloration. The ceramic and metallic window films usually cost 10–15 percent more than regular window film but can reduce energy transmission by as much as 80 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-DOE_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Ceramic window films cost slightly more but provide a substantial increase in blocking UV rays and ability to control heat transfer.</p> 
     <p>To keep the sun's heat out of the house, a low-emissivity coating should be applied to the outside pane of <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">glazed windows</a>. If the windows are designed to provide heat energy in the winter and keep heat inside the house (typical of cold climates), the low-emissivity coating should be applied to the inside pane of glazed windows. Such films also reduce the amount of visible and ultraviolet radiation entering a window, and are often applied to reduce fading of the contents of a room. Silvered film may also be employed to the same end. Spectrally selective films act by blocking certain wavelengths of the sun's infrared radiation and reject heat without reducing natural light.</p> 
     <p>Security films are applied to glass so when the glass is broken it holds together, preventing dangerous shards from flying about, or to make it more difficult for an intruder to gain entry. Typically applied to commercial glass, these films are made of heavy-gauge plastic and are intended to maintain the integrity of glass when subject to heavy <a href="/wiki/Impact_force" title="Impact force" class="mw-redirect">impact</a>. The most robust security films are capable of preventing fragmentation and the production of hazardous glass <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shard" class="extiw" title="wikt:shard">shards</a> from forces such as <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bomb</a> blasts. Some companies have even experimented with bullet ballistics and multiple layers of security film.<sup id="cite_ref-FEMA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FEMA-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Another key application for security window films (safety window films) is on large areas of "flat glass" such as storefront windows, sliding glass doors, and larger windows that are prone to hurricane damage. These security films, if applied properly, can also provide protection for vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-IWFA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWFA-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> These security films are often tinted and can be up to 400 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometers</a> (µm) thick, compared to less than 50&nbsp;µm for ordinary tint films. If anchored correctly, they can also provide protection for architectural glazing in the event of an explosion.<sup id="cite_ref-IWFA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWFA-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A layer of film (of 100&nbsp;µm thickness or greater) can prevent the ejection of <a href="/wiki/Spall" title="Spall">spall</a> when a projectile impacts on its surface, which otherwise creates small dagger-like shards of glass that can cause injury.</p> 
     <p>Switchable films can be switched from opaque to clear by a safe voltage under 36V AC. In its opaque state, it can be perfectly used as a projection screen that is viewable from both sides. 3G switchable film also has UV (100%) and IR (94%) blocking functions and security function.</p> 
     <p>Graphic design films are generally colored vinyl or frosted. Frosted finish films closely resemble <a href="/wiki/Sandblasting" title="Sandblasting" class="mw-redirect">sandblasted</a> or <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">acid-etched</a> glass, while vinyl films are available in a range of colors. Both types of films are commonly used in commercial applications.</p> 
     <p>Privacy films reduce visibility through the glass. Privacy film for flat-glass commercial and residential applications may be silvered, offering an unimpeded view from the low-light side but virtually no view from the high-light side. It may also be frosted, rendering the window <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)" class="mw-redirect">translucent</a> but not transparent. Privacy films for automobiles are available in <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradients</a> of darkness, with the darker tints commonly known as "limo tint."</p> 
     <p>Correctly applied mirror film can create <a href="/wiki/One-way_mirror" title="One-way mirror">one-way mirrors</a>. Note that in order to be effective the light differential must be from 6 to 10 times greater to maintain the intended performance. For example, the buildings in a city, during the daytime are difficult to see into, at night however, they can be seen in with ease and if a light is on in the interior space it is easily seen along with the contents.</p> 
     <p>Other benefits include protection for passengers in the rear of automobiles, protection from UV rays (which have some <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet#Harmful_effects" title="Ultraviolet">harmful effects</a>), cooling for pets, greater <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a>, reduced chance of theft (because valuables are less visible), reduced <a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">glare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflection</a> on <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display" class="mw-redirect">liquid crystal display</a> (LCD) screens, and protection for those who have conditions involving <a href="/wiki/Photosensitivity" title="Photosensitivity">photosensitivity</a> or skin sensitivity, such as <a href="/wiki/Lupus_erythematosus" title="Lupus erythematosus">lupus</a> (SLE). Window film is also considered more effective and practical than stick-on blinds.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UV_filtering_to_reduce_fading">UV filtering to reduce fading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UV filtering to reduce fading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Protection from UV light can be accomplished by film that addresses only the UV spectrum. As UV is one of the main sources of fading,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> it can prolong the life of fixtures and fittings. Basic colorless clear films reject (absorbs) some UV radiation up to 380&nbsp;nm, and depending on the manufacturer, UV inhibitors are added to the polyester, with specialist films offering protection to 400&nbsp;nm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Tinted filmes are necessary for protection to 500&nbsp;nm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Window films filter out over 99% of UV rays to 380&nbsp;nm, reducing the main factor in fading. However, additional factors such as solar heat, visible light, humidity, and presence or absence of chemical vapors also contribute to fading. Therefore, the greatest protection from fading is offered by tinted or metallised window films that reject large levels of heat and visible light, in addition to UV.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_of_window_film">Selection of window film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selection of window film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Not all films are suitable for all glass. The absorptance of the glass and the film, the size of the pane, the thickness of the glass, the construction of the window should be considered in terms of single pane, insulated glass, treated (with a low-emissivity coating), laminated or toughened.</p> 
     <p>Advice on the appropriate selection of film for the glass is vital to ensure that the glass does not crack as a result of thermal stress. However, it is possible that a pane of glass may break subsequent to the application of an appropriate film, because the pane has been damaged during the glazing of the window, or as a result of movement of the building or other physical stresses that are not apparent at the time of the application of the film.</p> 
     <p>Glass may crack subsequent to the application of sign writing or if heavy, thermally efficient drapes are hung close to the glass, particularly if the edges of the glass are damaged.</p> 
     <p>The chances of glass breakage occurring subsequent to the application of film or sign writing or the hanging of drapes, are very small. However breakage can occur and it is impossible to predict the edge condition of the glass without removing it from the frame – an approach that is not practical.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_properties">Thermal properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thermal properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">Heating and cooling</a> systems have energy costs, and window films can used to increase <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">building insulation</a>. Modern windows with <a href="/wiki/Solar_heat_gain" title="Solar heat gain" class="mw-redirect">low-solar-gain</a> glass do not need retrofit films, but since most of the existing windows do not have a low <a href="/wiki/Solar_heat_gain" title="Solar heat gain" class="mw-redirect">solar heat gain</a>, window films make a lot of sense as a retrofit energy improvement.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> They can provide thermal comfort and are one of the most cost-competitive and environmentally-friendly energy improvements, especially in hot and mixed climates.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unintended_features">Unintended features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unintended features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Under certain conditions, window film will exhibit <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescence</a>. This phenomenon usually occurs at night, when the source of illumination within a building is fluorescent lighting.</p> 
     <p>The amount of iridescence may vary from almost imperceptible to very visible. It most frequently occurs when the film is constructed with scratch resistance protection.</p> 
     <p>When iridescence does occur in window film, the best way to stop it is to prevent the fluorescent light from illuminating the film or to use an alternative type of light.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_use">Building use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Building use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Window tints can be used in applications like shopfronts windows, office block windows, and house windows. This is often done to increase privacy, and decrease heating and cooling costs. Window tints are used in some <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficient_buildings" title="Energy efficient buildings" class="mw-redirect">energy efficient buildings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Window films are also used to apply <a href="/wiki/See-through_graphics" title="See-through graphics">See-through graphics</a> to glass and other transparent surfaces, to provide advertising, branding, signage and decoration. See-through graphics are most commonly applied using a perforated window film with a graphic visible from one side but not the other side, which remains see-though. <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_wrap_advertising" title="Vehicle wrap advertising" class="mw-redirect">Vehicle wraps</a> on buses are perhaps the widest application of See-through graphics.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification_and_standards">Certification and standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Certification and standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The thicker window films known as <a href="/wiki/Safety_and_security_window_film" title="Safety and security window film">safety and security window film</a> are designed to perform under extreme conditions, and as such there are specific standard criteria these films should meet, such as American standards <a href="/wiki/American_National_Standards_Institute" title="American National Standards Institute">ANSI</a> Z.97, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Product_Safety_Commission" title="Consumer Product Safety Commission" class="mw-redirect">CPSC</a> 16 CFR 1201, Cat II (400&nbsp;ft-lb), and the <a href="/wiki/British_Standards" title="British Standards">British Standards</a> BS 6206 (Class A, B, C). The <a href="/wiki/European_Committee_for_Standardization" title="European Committee for Standardization">European Committee for Standardization</a> offer the EN12600 standard Classification of Resistance of Glazing to Impact. Often, building codes dictate that a film must have a report verifying that it has met at least one of these standards.</p> 
     <p>Solar window film is usually subject to less critical testing. However, standards are in place to maintain a level of quality in the industry. The ANSI Standards ASTM E903 and ASTM D1044-93 relate to the solar/UV transmission properties and abrasion resistance, respectively. The larger window film manufacturers use these standards to guarantee the quality of their raw materials and finished products.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations_for_automotive_use">Regulations for automotive use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Regulations for automotive use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>Automobile window tinting reduces the "Visible Light Transmission" (VLT) through car windows. This can be problematic at night, or when <a href="/wiki/Driving" title="Driving">motorists</a> must be able to see through the windows of other vehicles in order to spot hazards. Police also may want to be able to identify the passengers in a vehicle. Therefore, in many <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a>, there are laws to restrict the darkness of tinting.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Factory_Tint">Factory Tint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Factory Tint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>"Factory tint", done at the time of manufacture is generally not an applied film, but instead is done by dying the inside of the glass with a darkened pigment; an electrical process known as "deep dipping." The pigment gives the glass a tint, but doesn't provide UV ray protection or heat rejection like most window films do. The average VLT of factory tint is between 15%-26%.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_country">Regulation by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regulation by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <ul> 
      <li>In the U.S., <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">each state</a> has different limits of allowable tint as measured on the front side window.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> These range from a low VLT of 24% in Washington,<sup id="cite_ref-RCW_Tint_Limit_and_Exceptions_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCW_Tint_Limit_and_Exceptions-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> to 88% in California,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IWFA_State_Tint_Laws_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWFA_State_Tint_Laws-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> with less stringent restriction being common for windows in the rear of the vehicle. Full details of glazing and sticker laws can be found at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Motor_Carrier_Safety_Administration" title="Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration">Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</a> website.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In Canada, automobile tinting laws are set at the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provincial level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In the United Kingdom, regulations set forth by the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_and_Operator_Services_Agency" title="Vehicle and Operator Services Agency">Vehicle and Operator Services Agency</a> under the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Road_Vehicles_(Construction_and_Use)_Regulations_1986&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Road Vehicles (Construction and Use) Regulations 1986 (page does not exist)">Road Vehicles (Construction and Use) Regulations 1986</a> provide that all front windows in front of the B post can have no less than 70 percent VLT. Windscreens are only allowed tinting outside the sweep of the windscreen wipers to a maximum of six inches from the top of the windscreen.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Law enforcement in the United Kingdom">British police</a> are increasingly cracking down on illegal tinting on vehicles although window tints are not checked on the annual MoT Test.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, window tinting limits are specified in GOST 5727-88. The limit is 75% for the front windshield (a darker stripe no wider than 10&nbsp;cm is allowed at the very top) and 70% for the front side windows. Tinting of back windows is not regulated. In practice, however, <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribes</a> are often used to pass inspection or avoid road police tickets even with illegal tint.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, any kind of window tint is illegal. An exception is made for factory tint in vehicles purchased before 1 April 2006. Also exempt are vehicles of cash collectors, KGB and certain officials.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, window tinting is permitted on rear windows and rear windshield only, with no limits on films graduation. A professional installer certificate is also required, and it's necessary to keep visible the film's brand and homologation number on the windows.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, Australia; the <a href="/wiki/Roads_and_Traffic_Authority" title="Roads and Traffic Authority" class="mw-redirect">Roads and Traffic Authority</a> permits VLT of 35% on all windows. Tint on the windscreen – a band across the top is permitted with a depth of no more than 10% of the depth of the windscreen. An exception to the side &amp; rear window tinting regulations applies to cars tinted before 1 August 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>; the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Transport_Agency" title="New Zealand Transport Agency" class="mw-redirect">New Zealand Transport Agency</a> stipulates that private motor vehicles must have at least 35% VLT after film has been applied. Side and rear windows; including the windows next to the driver; are allowed full coverage. The front windshield may have an anti-glare band no lower than the extended sun visors. There is no concise regulation for a sunroof. There is an exception for factory tinted glass, which may be any VLT level. Also, commercial goods vehicles are allowed any level of VLT on windows behind the driver, provided the driver has adequate rear vision via side mirrors.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>; The Malta Transport Authority Permits VLT of 70% on Front Side windows. Tint on the windscreen – a band across the top is permitted with a depth of no more than 100mm of the depth of the windscreen. 30% on the rear and side windows.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>; The rules governing tinted glass are defined in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://khcaa.org/wordpress/?p=187">The Motor Vehicles Act 1988</a>, which states that the front and rear windshield should be 70% VLT and all other windows are limited at 50% VLT. On 27 April 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_India" title="Supreme Court of India">Supreme Court of India</a> ordered all black films to be removed.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ul>
