     <p><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a> has earned a respected place in the related fields of <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Building_construction" title="Building construction" class="mw-redirect">building construction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">interior design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> From <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a> to <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">homes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Office" title="Office">offices</a>, copper is used for a variety of architectural elements, including <a href="/wiki/Roofing" title="Roofing" class="mw-redirect">roofs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flashing_(weatherproofing)" title="Flashing (weatherproofing)">flashings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain_gutter" title="Rain gutter">gutters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downspout" title="Downspout">downspouts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">domes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaults</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper_cladding" title="Copper cladding">wall cladding</a>, and building <a href="/wiki/Expansion_joint" title="Expansion joint">expansion joints</a>.</p> 
     <p>The history of <b>copper in architecture</b> can be linked to its durability, <a href="/wiki/Corrosion_resistance" title="Corrosion resistance" class="mw-redirect">corrosion resistance</a>, prestigious appearance, and ability to form complex shapes.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> For centuries, craftsmen and designers utilized these attributes to build aesthetically pleasing and long-lasting building systems.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For the past quarter century, copper has been designed into a much wider range of buildings, incorporating new styles, varieties of colors, and different shapes and textures.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper clad walls are a modern design element in both indoor and outdoor environments.</p> 
     <p>Some of the world's most distinguished modern architects have relied on copper. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>, who specified copper materials in all of his building projects;<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Michael Graves, an AIA Gold Medalist who designed over 350 buildings worldwide;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a>, who designed pre-patinated clad copper for the <a href="/wiki/NEMO_(museum)" title="NEMO (museum)">NEMO</a>-Metropolis Museum of Science in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Malcolm Holzman, whose patinated copper shingles at the WCCO Television Communications Centre made the facility an architectural standout in Minneaoplis;<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and Marianne Dahlbäck and Göran Månsson, who designed the <a href="/wiki/Vasa_Museum" title="Vasa Museum">Vasa Museum</a>, a prominent feature of Stockholm’s skyline, with 12,000-square metres copper cladding.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Architect <a href="/wiki/Frank_O._Gehry" title="Frank O. Gehry" class="mw-redirect">Frank O. Gehry</a>’s enormous copper fish sculpture atop the <a href="/wiki/La_Vila_Ol%C3%ADmpica_del_Poblenou" title="La Vila Olímpica del Poblenou">Vila Olimpica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> is an example of the artistic use of copper.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper’s most famous trait is its display from a bright metallic colour to iridescent brown to near black and finally to a greenish verdigris <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a>. Architects describe the array of browns as russet, chocolate, plum, mahogany, and ebony.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The metal’s distinctive green patina has long been coveted by architects and designers.</p> 
     <p>This article describes practical and aesthetic benefits of copper in architecture as well as its use in exterior applications, interior design elements, and green buildings.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Copper has played a role in architecture for thousands of years. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, massive doors to the temple of <a href="/wiki/Amen-Re" title="Amen-Re" class="mw-redirect">Amen-Re</a> at <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">Karnak</a> were clad with copper. In the 3rd Century B.C., copper roof shingles were installed atop of the Lowa Maha Paya Temple in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> And the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> used copper as roof covering for the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in 27&nbsp;B.C.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Centuries later, copper and its alloys were integral in <a href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval" class="mw-redirect">medieval</a> architecture. The doors of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Nativity" title="Church of the Nativity">Church of the Nativity</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a> (6th century) are covered with plates of bronze, cut out in patterns. Those of <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, of the 8th and 9th century, are wrought in bronze. Bronze doors on the Aachen Cathedral in Germany date back to about 800 A.D. Bronze baptistery doors at the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Florence" title="Cathedral of Florence" class="mw-redirect">Cathedral of Florence</a> were completed in 1423 A.D. by <a href="/wiki/Ghiberti" title="Ghiberti" class="mw-redirect">Ghiberti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The copper roof of <a href="/wiki/Hildesheim_Cathedral" title="Hildesheim Cathedral">Hildesheim Cathedral</a>, installed in 1280 A.D., survives to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> And the roof at <a href="/wiki/Kronborg" title="Kronborg">Kronborg</a>, one of northern Europe's most important <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> castles that was immortalized as <a href="/wiki/Elsinore_Castle" title="Elsinore Castle" class="mw-redirect">Elsinore Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect">Shakespeare</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a>, was installed in 1585 A.D.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The copper on the tower was renovated in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For years, copper was reserved mainly for public institutions, such as churches, government buildings, and universities. Copper roofs are often one of the most architecturally distinguishable features of these structures.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2006_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2006-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Today, architectural copper is used in <a href="/wiki/Roofing" title="Roofing" class="mw-redirect">roofing</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Flashing_(weatherproofing)" title="Flashing (weatherproofing)">flashings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coping_(architecture)" title="Coping (architecture)">copings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain_gutter" title="Rain gutter">rain gutters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downspout" title="Downspout">downspouts</a>, building <a href="/wiki/Expansion_joint" title="Expansion joint">expansion joints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper_cladding" title="Copper cladding">wall cladding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">domes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaults</a>, and various other design elements. Simultaneously, the metal has evolved from a weather barrier and exterior design element into indoor building environments where it is changing the way commercial and residential interiors are decorated.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the 21st century, the use of copper continues to evolve in the indoor environment. Its recently proven <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_properties_of_copper" title="Antimicrobial properties of copper">antimicrobial properties</a> reduce pathogenic <a href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial" class="mw-redirect">bacterial</a> loads on such products as <a href="/wiki/Handrail" title="Handrail">handrails</a>, bedrails, bathroom fixtures, <a href="/wiki/Counter_top" title="Counter top" class="mw-redirect">counter tops</a>, etc. These <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces" title="Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces">antimicrobial copper</a>-based products are now being incorporated into public facilities (<a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nursing_home" title="Nursing home" class="mw-redirect">nursing homes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass_transit" title="Mass transit" class="mw-redirect">mass transit</a> facilities) as well as in residential buildings because of the public health benefits. <i>(For main article, see: <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces" title="Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces">Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces</a>.)</i></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrosion_resistance">Corrosion resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Corrosion resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>As an architectural metal, copper provides excellent <a href="/wiki/Corrosion_resistance" title="Corrosion resistance" class="mw-redirect">corrosion resistance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper surfaces form tough oxide-sulfate <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a> coatings that protect underlying copper surfaces and resist corrosion for a very long time.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper corrodes at negligible rates in unpolluted air, water, de-aerated non-oxidizing acids, and when exposed to <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">saline</a> solutions, <a href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline" class="mw-redirect">alkaline</a> solutions, and <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemical" title="Organic chemical" class="mw-redirect">organic chemicals</a>. Copper roofing in rural atmospheres corrodes at rates of less than 0.4&nbsp;mm in 200 years.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Unlike most other metals, copper does not suffer from underside corrosion that can cause premature failures in roofing. With a copper roof, supporting substrates and structures usually fail long before the copper on the roof.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Architectural copper is, however, susceptible to corrosive attack under certain conditions. <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_acid" title="Oxidizing acid">Oxidizing acids</a>, oxidizing heavy-metal salts, <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, and some sulfur and ammonium compounds can expedite copper corrosion. Precipitation in areas with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> less than 5.5 may corrode copper, possibly before a patina or protective oxide film has the time to form. Acidic precipitation, known as <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, is due to emissions from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> combustion, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_manufacturing" title="Chemical manufacturing" class="mw-redirect">chemical manufacturing</a>, or other processes that release <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Erosion_corrosion" title="Erosion corrosion">Erosion corrosion</a> may occur when acidic water from a non-copper roof that does not neutralise the acidity, such as tile, slate, wood, or asphalt, falls on a small area of copper. Line corrosion can occur if the drip edge of an inert roofing material rests directly on copper. A solution to this may be to raise the lower edge of the shingles with a cant strip, or to provide a replaceable reinforcing strip between the shingles and the copper.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Proper water-shedding design and detailing, which reduces the dwell time of acidic water on metal surfaces, can prevent the majority of atmospheric corrosion problems.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Durability.2FLong-life">Durability/Long-life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Durability/Long-life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Copper roofs are extremely durable in most environments. They have performed well for over 700 years, primarily because of the protective patina that forms on copper surfaces. Tests conducted on 18th Century copper roofs in Europe showed that, in theory, they could last for one thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_thermal_movement">Low thermal movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Low thermal movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Properly designed copper roofs minimize movements due to thermal changes. Copper’s low <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a>, 40% less than zinc and lead, helps to prevent deterioration and failure. Also, copper’s high <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> ensures that it will not <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a> or stretch as some other metals do.</p>

     <p>On small <a href="/wiki/Gable_roof" title="Gable roof" class="mw-redirect">gable roofs</a>, thermal movement is relatively minor and usually is not an issue. On wide-span buildings over 60&nbsp;meters and when long panels are used, an allowance for thermal expansion may be necessary. This enables the roof to "float" over supporting substructures while remaining secure.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_maintenance">Low maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Low maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Copper does not require cleaning or maintenance. It is particularly suited for areas that are difficult or dangerous to access after installation.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightweight">Lightweight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lightweight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>When used as a fully supported roof covering, copper is half the weight (including substrate) of lead and only a quarter of tiled roofs. This generally provides savings in supporting structure and materials costs. Copper cladding offers additional opportunities to reduce the weight of copper structures <i>(For more details, see: <a href="/wiki/Copper_cladding" title="Copper cladding">Copper cladding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_architecture#Applications.23Wall_cladding" title="Copper in architecture">Wall cladding</a>).</i></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">Ventilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ventilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Copper does not require complex <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> measures. It is suitable for both unventilated ‘warm’ and ventilated ‘cold’ roof constructions.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_frequency_shielding">Radio frequency shielding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Radio frequency shielding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Sensitive electronic equipment are vulnerable to interference and unauthorized <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a>. These products also require protection from <a href="/wiki/High_voltage" title="High voltage">high voltages</a>. <a href="/wiki/RF_shielding" title="RF shielding" class="mw-redirect">Radio frequency (RF) shielding</a> can address these issues by reducing the transmission of <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> from one space to another.</p> 
     <p>Copper is an excellent material for RF shielding because it absorbs <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_wave" title="Magnetic wave" class="mw-redirect">magnetic waves</a>. Other useful properties for RF shielding is that copper has a high electrical conductivity, is ductile, malleable, and solders easily.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>RF shielding enclosures filter a range of frequencies for specific conditions. Properly designed and constructed copper enclosures satisfy most RF shielding needs, from computer and electrical switching rooms to hospital <a href="/wiki/CAT-scan" title="CAT-scan" class="mw-redirect">CAT-scan</a> and <a href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI" class="mw-redirect">MRI</a> facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Special attention needs to be addressed regarding potential shield penetrations, such as doors, vents, and cables.</p> 
     <p>A shield can be effective against one type of electromagnetic field but not against another. For example, a copper foil or screen RF shield will be minimally effective against power frequency magnetic fields. A power frequency magnetic shield could offer little reduction of radio frequency fields. The same is true for different RF frequencies. A simple large-mesh screen shield can work well for lower frequencies, but can be ineffective for microwaves.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightning_protection">Lightning protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lightning protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Lightning_strike" title="Lightning strike">Lightning strike</a> protection minimizes damage to buildings during lightning terminations. This is usually accomplished by providing multiple interconnected pathways of low electrical impedance to the ground.</p> 
     <p>Copper and its alloys are the most common materials used in residential lightning protections, however in industrial, chemically corrosive environments, the copper may need to be clad in tin.<sup id="cite_ref-copper1995_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper1995-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper effectively facilitates the transmission of lightning energy to the ground because of its excellent <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity" class="mw-redirect">electrical conductivity</a>. Also, it bends easily compared to other conductor materials.</p> 
     <p>When copper roofing, gutters, and rain leaders are electrically bonded to an earth termination facility, a pathway of low electrical impedance to ground is provided, however without dedicated conduction pathways to concentrate the discharge channel, a disperse energized surface may not be the most desirable.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copper2_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper2-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because copper has a higher electrical conductivity than aluminium and its impedance during a lightning termination is less, copper allows for the use of less cross-sectional surface area per linear length, in its woven wires pathway than does aluminum. Also, aluminium cannot be used in poured concrete or for any component underground due to its <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">galvanic properties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>To be effective, lightning protection systems generally maximize the surface area contact between the conductors and the earth through a ground grid of varying designs. To supplement grounding grids in low-conductivity earth, such as sand or rock, long, hollow copper tubes filled with metallic salts are available. These salts leach through holes in the tube, making the surrounding soil more conductive as well as increasing the overall surface area which decreases effective resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-copper1995_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper1995-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper roofs may be used as part of a lightning protection scheme where the copper <a href="/wiki/Skin_effect" title="Skin effect">skin</a>, gutters and rainwater pipes can be linked and bonded to an earth termination facility. The thickness of copper specified for roofing materials is usually adequate for lightning protection.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> A dedicated lightning protection system may be recommended to adequate lightning protection with an installed copper roof system. The system would include air terminals and intercepting conductors on the roof, a system of ground electrodes, and a system of down-conductors connecting the roof and ground components. It is recommended that the copper roof be bonded to the system of conductors. Bonding ensures that the conductors and roof remain at equipotential and reduce side flashing and possible roof damage.<sup id="cite_ref-copper2_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper2-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wide_range_of_finishes">Wide range of finishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wide range of finishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>It is sometimes desirable to chemically alter the surface of copper or copper alloys to create a different color. The most common colors produced are brown or <a href="/wiki/Statuary" title="Statuary" class="mw-redirect">statuary</a> finishes for <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> and green or patina finishes for <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Mechanical surface treatments, chemical coloring, and coatings are described elsewhere in this article at: <i><a href="/wiki/Copper_in_architecture#Finishes" title="Copper in architecture">Finishes</a>.</i></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_continuity">Design continuity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Design continuity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Architects often look to architectural copper for continuity in design elements. For example, a copper roofing system may be designed with copper flashings, weatherings, vents, gutters, and downpipes. Cover details may include <a href="/wiki/Cornice" title="Cornice">cornices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molding_(decorative)" title="Molding (decorative)">moldings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finial" title="Finial">finials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>With the growing use of vertical cladding, vertical and roofing surfaces can run into each other so that complete continuity of material and performance is maintained. <a href="/wiki/Rain_screen" title="Rain screen" class="mw-redirect">Rain screens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curtain_wall_(architecture)" title="Curtain wall (architecture)">curtain walling</a> (often linked with <a href="/wiki/Transom_(architectural)" title="Transom (architectural)">transoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mullion" title="Mullion">mullions</a>) are also gaining popularity in modern architectural design.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept2_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept2-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antimicrobial">Antimicrobial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Antimicrobial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces" title="Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces">Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Extensive worldwide tests have proved that uncoated copper and copper alloys (e.g., brass, bronze, copper nickel, copper-nickel-zinc) have strong intrinsic antimicrobial properties with efficacies against a wide range of disease-resistant <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">molds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi" class="mw-redirect">fungi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> After years of testing, the U.S. approved the registration of over 300 different copper alloys (copper, brasses, bronzes, copper-nickels, and nickel-silvers) as antimicrobial materials. These developments are creating markets for antimicrobial copper and copper alloys in interior architecture. To meet the design needs for building surfaces, structures, fixtures, and components, antimicrobial copper-based products are available in a wide range of colors, finishes, and mechanical properties.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper handrails, counter tops, hallways, doors, push plates, kitchens, and bathrooms are just some of the antimicrobial products approved for hospitals, airports, offices, schools, and army barracks to kill harmful bacteria. See: <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces#Approved_products" title="Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces">a list of products approved in the U.S</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>While a universally accepted definition of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> remains elusive, the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">Brundtland Commission</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> defined sustainable development as development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Sustainability, the long-term maintenance of responsibility, requires the reconciliation of environmental, social equity and economic demands. These "three pillars" of sustainability encompass the responsible management of resource use. Also, it can mean that we can use a resource which won't cease to be abundant despite increasing intake.</p> 
     <p>Copper is a sustainable material. Its durability offers long service with little maintenance. Its high electrical and thermal energy efficiencies reduce the waste of electrical energy. Its antimicrobial properties destroy pathogenic microorganisms that cause disease. And its high scrap value and ability to be continuously recycled without any loss in performance ensure its responsible management as a valuable resource.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_inventory" title="Life cycle inventory" class="mw-redirect">Life cycle inventory</a> (LCI) information on copper tube, sheet, and wire products, using <a href="/wiki/ISO_standards" title="ISO standards" class="mw-redirect">ISO standards</a> and covering the mining and primary copper production sectors (i.e., smelting and refining) is available.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Used in <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessments" title="Life cycle assessments" class="mw-redirect">life cycle assessments</a> (LCAs), particularly in the building and construction sector, LCI datasets assist manufacturers of copper-containing products with compliance and voluntary improvement initiatives. They also support policy makers in the development of environmental guidelines and regulations with the aim of fostering <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.</p> 
     <p>The long lifetime of copper roofing and cladding has a significant positive effect on whole life assessments of copper versus other materials in terms of embodied energy consumption (i.e., the total energy consumed during every phase of each lifecycle in MJ/m<sup>2</sup>), CO<sub>2</sub> generation, and cost.</p>

     <p>Recyclability is a key factor of a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable" class="mw-redirect">sustainable</a> material. It reduces the need to mine new <a href="/wiki/Resources" title="Resources" class="mw-redirect">resources</a> and requires less energy than <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>. Copper and its alloys are virtually 100% <a href="/wiki/Recyclable" title="Recyclable" class="mw-redirect">recyclable</a><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and can be recycled infinitely without any loss of quality (i.e., copper does not degrade (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Downcycle" title="Downcycle" class="mw-redirect">downcycle</a>) after each recycling loop as do most non-metallic materials, if they are recyclable at all). Copper retains much of its primary metal value: premium-grade scrap normally contains at least 95% of the value of primary metal from newly mined <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a>. Scrap values for competing materials range from about 60% down to 0%. And copper recycling requires only around 20% of the energy needed to extract and process primary metal.</p> 
     <p>Currently, around 40% of Europe’s annual copper demand<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> and about 55% of copper used in architecture<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> come from recycled sources. New copper coil and sheet often have 75%-100% recycled content.</p> 
     <p>By 1985, more copper was recycled than the total amount of copper that was consumed in 1950. This is due to the relative ease of reusing processing waste and salvaging copper from products after their useful life.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_effectiveness">Cost effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cost effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Performance, maintenance, service life, and recovery costs from recycling are factors that determine the cost effectiveness of building components. While copper’s initial cost is higher than some other architectural metals, it usually does not need to be replaced during the life of a building. Due to its durability, low maintenance, and ultimate <a href="/wiki/Salvage_value" title="Salvage value" class="mw-redirect">salvage value</a>, the additional cost for copper may be insignificant over the life of a roofing system.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper roofing is considerably less expensive than <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slate</a>, or hand-made <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> tiles. Its costs are comparable with <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum" class="mw-redirect">aluminum</a> and even some clay and <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> tiles when considering overall roofing costs (including structure).<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some studies indicate that copper is a more cost-effective material on a life cycle basis than other roof materials with a lifetime of 30 years or more.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> A European study comparing roofing costs of copper with other metals, concrete and clay tiles, slate, and <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen" class="mw-redirect">bitumen</a> found that in the medium to long-term (for lives of 60 to 80 years and 100 years and over), copper and stainless steel were the most cost effective roofing materials of all materials examined.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Installation techniques such as prefabrication, in-situ machine forming, mechanized seaming, and the long-strip system help to reduce the installation costs of copper roofing. By lowering installation costs, these techniques permit designers to specify copper into a wider array of building types, not just large prestigious projects as had been common in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since scrap copper retains much of its primary value, copper’s life cycle costs are reduced when accounting for its salvage value. For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_architecture#Benefits.23Recyclability" title="Copper in architecture">Recyclability</a> section in this article.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_vs._alloyed_copper">Pure vs. alloyed copper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pure vs. alloyed copper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><b>Pure copper.</b> Unlike other metals, copper is frequently used in its pure (99.9% Cu) unalloyed form for sheet and strip applications in roofing, exterior cladding, and flashing.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">Tempering</a> is a heat treatment technique used to increase the toughness of metals. Tempers determine the <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a> of the metal, and therefore how well it forms and will hold its shape without additional support.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In the U.S., copper is available in six tempers: 060 soft, 1/8 hard cold rolled, 1/4 cold rolled high yield, half hard, three quarter hard, and hard.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> In the U.K., only three designations exist: soft, half-hard, and hard.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper and its alloys are defined in the U.S. in Standard Designations for Copper and Copper Alloys by ASTM; in Europe by BS EN 1172: 1997 - 'Copper and Copper Alloys in Europe’; and in the U.K. by the British Standard Code of Practice CP143: Part12: 1970.</p> 
     <p>Cold rolled copper temper is by far the most popular in building construction in the U.S. It is less malleable than soft copper but is far stronger.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept2_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept2-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Cold rolled 1/8-hard tempered copper is often recommended for roofing and flashing installations. Roof sheets with higher tempers may be specified for certain applications.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Soft tempered copper is extremely malleable and offers far less resistance than cold rolled copper to the stresses induced by expansion and contraction. It is used for intricate ornamental work and where extreme forming is required, such as in complicated thru-wall flashing conditions.</p> 
     <p>The major use for high-yield copper is in flashing products, where malleability and strength are both important.</p> 
     <p>The thickness of sheet and strip copper is measured by its weight in ounces per square foot. Thicknesses commonly used in construction in the U.S. are between 12ounces and 48&nbsp;ounces. Since the industry often uses gauge numbers or actual thicknesses for sheet metal or other building materials, it is necessary to convert between the different measurement systems.</p> 
     <p>In Europe, phosphorus de-oxidized non-arsenical copper is used with the designation C106. The copper is rolled to thicknesses ranging between 0.5 and 1.0&nbsp;millimeters (1.5 - 3.0&nbsp;millimeters for curtain walling) but a 0.6 - 0.7&nbsp;millimeters thickness is usually used for roofing.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Alloyed copper.</b> Copper alloys, such as brass and bronze, are also used in residential and commercial building structures.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Variations in color stem primarily from differences in the alloy chemical composition.</p>

     <p>In practice, the term ‘bronze’ may be used for a variety of copper alloys with little or no tin if they resemble true bronzes in color.</p> 
     <p>Further information on architectural copper alloys is available.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_criteria">Selection criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Selection criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The criteria by which copper and copper alloys are selected for architectural projects include color, strength, hardness, resistance to fatigue and corrosion, electrical and thermal conductivity, and ease of fabrication.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Appropriate thicknesses and tempers for specific applications are essential; substitutions can lead to inadequate performance.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Architectural copper is generally used in sheet and strip. Strip is 24-inches or less in width, while sheet is over 24-inches in width, up to 48-inches in width by 96- or 120-inches long, plus in coil form.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_Considerations">Structural Considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Structural Considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Structural considerations play an important role in the proper design of copper applications. The primary concern is about thermal effects: movement and stresses related to temperature variations. Thermal effects can be accommodated by preventing movement and resisting cumulative stresses or by allowing movement at predetermined locations, thereby relieving anticipated thermal stresses.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

        <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> has 80 metric tons of copper sheeting.

     <p><a href="/wiki/Wind_resistance" title="Wind resistance" class="mw-redirect">Wind resistance</a> is an important structural consideration. <a href="/wiki/Underwriters_Laboratories" title="Underwriters Laboratories" class="mw-redirect">Underwriters Laboratories</a> (UL) conducted a series of tests on copper roof systems. A copper standing-seam roof with 10-foot x 10-foot test panels was subjected to the UL 580, Uplift Resistance Test Protocol. The copper system did not exhibit unusual deformation, the cleats did not loosen from the structural deck, and the system passed UL 580 requirements. UL-90 designation was granted.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated8_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated8-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joining">Joining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Joining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Copper and its alloys are readily joined by mechanical techniques, such as crimping, staking, riveting, and bolting; or by bonding techniques, such as <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">soldering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazing" title="Brazing">brazing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>. Selection of the best joining technique is determined by service requirements, joint configuration, thickness of components, and alloy composition.</p> 
     <p><b>Soldering</b> is the preferred joining method where strong, watertight joints are required, such as for internal gutters, roofing, and flashing applications.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A soldered seam joins two pieces of copper into a cohesive unit that expands and contracts as one piece. Well-soldered seams are often stronger than the original base material and provide many years of service.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Mechanical fasteners,</b> such as screws, bolts, and rivets, are often used to strengthen the joints and seams. Continuous, long runs of soldered seams can cause stress fractures and should therefore be avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-copper3_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper3-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Common 50-50 tin-lead bar solder is often used for uncoated copper; 60-40 tin-lead solder is used for lead-coated copper.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications2000_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications2000-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Many lead-free solders are also acceptable.</p> 
     <p><b>Adhesives</b> can be used in certain applications. Relatively thin sheet alloys can be bonded to plywood or certain types of foam which act as rigid insulation.</p> 
     <p><b>Brazing</b> is the preferred method for joining pipe and tube copper alloys. Copper metal sections are joined with a non-ferrous filler material with a melting point above 800 degrees Fahrenheit but below the melting point of the base metals. Blind or concealed joints are recommended since the color match of silver filler material is fair to poor.</p> 
     <p><b>Welding</b> is a process where pieces of copper are effectively melted together, either by flame, electricity, or high pressure. With increasing availability of modern TIG welding equipment, welding of even light-gauge copper decorative elements is gaining acceptance.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Instructional_video" title="Instructional video" class="mw-redirect">Instructional videos</a> are available regarding fluxing and <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">soldering</a> techniques; how to make flat seam solder joints, double-lock standing seams, lap seams, soldering vertical sheet copper lap seams, and stitches (including the butterfly stitch); as well as copper <a href="/wiki/Tinning" title="Tinning">tinning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bending" title="Bending">bending</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flare_fitting" title="Flare fitting">flaring</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazing" title="Brazing">brazing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sealants">Sealants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sealants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Sealants are an alternative to solder where additional strength is not required. In most cases, sealants should not be necessary with a properly designed copper installation. They are at best a relatively short-term solution requiring frequent maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Regardless, sealant-filled joints have been used successfully as a secondary waterproofing measure for standing seam and batten seam roofing applications where low-sloped roofs are less than three inches per foot. Sealants can also be used in joints that are primarily designed to accommodate thermal movement of the copper.</p> 
     <p>The sealants used should be tested by the manufacturer and designated as compatible for use with copper.</p> 
     <p>In general, <a href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">butyl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polysulfide" title="Polysulfide">polysulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>, and other inorganic or <a href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber" class="mw-redirect">rubber</a>-based sealants are reasonably compatible with copper. <a href="/wiki/Acrylate_polymer" title="Acrylate polymer">Acrylic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitrile</a>-based sealants actively corrode copper. <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">Silicone</a> sealants are somewhat successful with copper but their suitability should be verified before application.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Galvanic_corrosion">Galvanic corrosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Galvanic corrosion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg/220px-Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg/330px-Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg/440px-Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="2400"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Minneapolis_City_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">Galvanic corrosion</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical" title="Electrochemical" class="mw-redirect">electrochemical</a> process whereby one metal <a href="/wiki/Corrode" title="Corrode" class="mw-redirect">corrodes</a> preferentially to another when both metals are in electrical contact with each other in the presence of an <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>, such as moisture and salts. This is because the dissimilar metals have different <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">electrode potentials</a>. The potential difference between the dissimilar metals is the driving force for the accelerated attack on the metal with the lower galvanic number (i.e., the anode). Over time, the anode metal dissolves into the electrolyte.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copper4_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper4-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Metals are ranked according to galvanic numbers as a qualitative measure of their nobility. These numbers qualify the resistance to corrosion of any metal when in contact with other metals.<sup id="cite_ref-copper3_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper3-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> A larger difference in the galvanic number between two metals in contact with each other indicates a greater potential for corrosion. The galvanic numbers of the most common metals used in construction are ranked as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> 1. aluminum; 2. zinc; 3. steel; 4. iron; 5. stainless steel - active; 6. tin; 7. lead; 8. copper; 9. stainless steel - passive.</p> 
     <p>Galvanic corrosion is a primary concern with metal roof maintenance. Marine environments present an additional concern due to the higher concentration of salts in the air and water.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper is one of the most noble metals. It will not be harmed by contact with other metals but it will cause corrosion to some other metals if contacted directly. The principal metals of concern regarding direct contact with copper are aluminum, light-gauge steel, and zinc. Aluminum and steel flashings and galvanized steel fasteners should not be used with copper. Runoff from a copper roof corrodes aluminum and steel guttering.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> It is not necessary to isolate copper from lead, tin or many stainless steels under most circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-copper5_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper5-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>When it is not possible to avoid contact, an effective method of material separation is required.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications2000_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications2000-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> If paints or coatings are used for isolation, they should be compatible with both metals. <a href="/wiki/Bituminous" title="Bituminous" class="mw-redirect">Bituminous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chromate" title="Zinc chromate">zinc chromate</a> primers can be used between copper and aluminum. Bituminous, zinc chromate, or a red lead primer can be effective in separating copper from iron and other ferrous metals. Taping or gasketing with non-absorptive materials or sealants are effective in separating copper from all other metals. In areas with severe exposure, lead or similar gasketing materials should be used, except between copper and aluminum. Water draining from copper surfaces should be prevented from exposure to aluminum and galvanized steel as traces of copper salts may accelerate corrosion.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copper5_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper5-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> In some cases, anodizing may protect thicker aluminum, such as aluminum window system mullions.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_patinas">Natural patinas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Natural patinas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Copper goes through a natural <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a> process that forms a unique protective <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a> on the metal. The surface of the metal undergoes a series of color changes: from iridescent/salmon pinks to oranges and reds interspersed with brassy yellows, blues, greens and purples. As the oxide thickens, these colors are replaced by russet and chocolate browns, dull slate grays or blacks, and finally to a light-green or blue-green.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper’s patination process is complex. It starts immediately on exposure to the environment with the initial formation of <a href="/wiki/Copper_oxide" title="Copper oxide">copper oxide</a> conversion films that are noticeable within six months. Weathering may be uneven at first but the film becomes even after about nine months.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Within the first few years, cuprous and cupric <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> conversion films darken the surface to browns and then dull slate gray or dull black. Continued weathering transforms the sulfide films to <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, which are the notable blue-green or gray-green patinas.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG/220px-Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG/330px-Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG/440px-Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Dachfl%C3%A4che_Stadthalle_Heidelberg.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Copper roofs in 
        <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, Germany.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The rate of patination conversion is dependent upon copper’s exposure to moisture, salt, and acidity from acid-forming pollutants. In marine climates, the entire patination process can take seven to nine years.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In industrial environments, patina formation reaches its final stage in about fifteen to twenty-five years. In clean rural atmospheres with low concentrations of airborne sulfur dioxide, the final stage may take ten to thirty years to develop.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> In arid environments, a patina may not form at all if the moisture is insufficient. Where patination does take place in arid environments, it may mature to an ebony or nut brown. In all environments except the coastal areas, patination takes longer for vertical surfaces due to more rapid water runoff.</p> 
     <p>The copper patinas are very thin: just 0.05080–0.07620&nbsp;millimeters in thickness. Yet, they are highly adherent to the underlying copper metal. The initial and intermediate oxide and sulfide patina films are not particularly corrosion resistant. The final sulfate patina is a particularly durable layer that is highly resistant to all forms of atmospheric corrosion and protects the underlying metal against further weathering. As patination progresses and the durable sulfate layer forms, the rate of corrosion decreases, averaging between 0.0001–0.0003&nbsp;millimeters per year. For a 0.6&nbsp;millimeter-thick sheet, this equates to less than 5% corrosion over a period of 100 years.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> Further information is available on copper patination.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copper3_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper3-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finishes">Finishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Finishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Copper and its alloys can be ‘finished’ to embark a particular look, feel, and/or color. Finishes include mechanical surface treatments, chemical coloring, and coatings. These are described here.</p> 
     <p><b>Mechanical surface treatments.</b> Several types of mechanical surface treatments exist. Mill finishes are derived by normal production processes, such as rolling, extrusion, or casting. "Buffeted" finishes impart bright mirror-like appearances after grinding, polishing, and buffing. "Directional textured" finishes provide a smooth, velvety satin sheen in a continuous pattern of fine nearly parallel scratches. "Non-directional textured matte" finishes achieve a rough texture, primarily on castings, as spray sand or metal shot are applied under high pressure. And "patterned" finishes, made by pressing a copper alloy sheet between two rolls, produce a textured and embossed look.</p> 
     <p><b>Chemically induced patina.</b> Architects sometimes request a particular patina color at installation. Factory-applied chemically induced pre-patination systems can produce a wide range of colored finishes similar to natural patination. Pre-patinated copper is particularly useful in repairs when there is need to provide close color matches to old copper roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Pre-patination is also considered in some modern building materials, such as vertical cladding, soffits, and gutters where patination is desired but would not ordinarily occur.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A variation of chemical patinas on copper can be seen on this website: <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.rachiele.com/rustic-patinas.aspx">http://www.rachiele.com/rustic-patinas.aspx</a></p> 
     <p>Chemical coloring is an art involving craftsmanship and experience. Coloring techniques depend upon time, temperature, surface preparation, humidity and other variables.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Pre-patinated copper sheets are produced by fabricators under controlled environments using patented chemical processes. Green patina finishes are primarily developed using acid chloride or acid sulfate. Treatments with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sal_ammoniac" title="Sal ammoniac">sal ammoniac</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cuprous_chloride" title="Cuprous chloride" class="mw-redirect">cuprous chloride</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate" title="Ammonium sulfate">ammonium sulfate</a> are somewhat successful.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1994_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1994-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bronze_sculpture" title="Bronze sculpture">Statuary</a> finishes can be produced in light, medium, and dark brown, depending on the concentration and number of coloring applications. One advantage is that the treatment disguises surface markings on bright mill finish copper and can advance the natural patination process.<sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because of the number of variables involved, chemically induced patinas are prone to problems such as a lack of adhesion, excessive staining of adjacent materials, and inability to achieve reasonable color uniformity over large surface areas. Chemical patination applied in the field is not recommended due to variations in temperature, humidity, and chemical requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> Warranties are prudent when purchasing pre-patinated copper for architectural projects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Useful techniques and recipes for coloring copper, brass, yellow-brass, bronze, cast bronze, gilding metal, along with various physical and chemical textural finishes are available.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Coatings.</b> Clear coatings preserve the natural color, warmth and metallic tone of copper alloys. However, particularly on exterior applications, they introduce maintenance into what is naturally a maintenance-free material. They are organic chemicals that are dry at ambient temperatures or require heat for curing or solvent evaporation. Examples of clear organic coatings include <a href="/wiki/Alkyd" title="Alkyd">alkyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acrylate_polymer" title="Acrylate polymer">acrylic</a>, cellulose acetate butyrate, <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">urethane</a>. Further details are available.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Oils and waxes exclude moisture from copper surfaces and simultaneously enhance their appearance by bringing out a rich luster and depth of color. Oiling is typically used to prolong the time exposed copper remains in a brown to black tone. It will not keep copper shiny on an exterior installation. Oils and waxes offer short-term protection for exterior applications and longer-term protection for interior applications.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Oiling predominates for roofing and flashing work. The most popular oils are Lemon Oil, U.S.P., Lemon Grass Oil, Native E.I., paraffin oils, <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Castor_oil" title="Castor oil">castor oil</a>. On copper roofing or flashing, reapplication as infrequently as once every three years can effectively retard patina formation. In arid climates, the maximum span between oilings may be extended to from three to five years.</p> 
     <p>Waxing is generally reserved for architectural components subject to close inspection and/or traffic. Mixes considered satisfactory include <a href="/wiki/Carnauba_wax" title="Carnauba wax">Carnauba wax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_turpentine" title="Wood turpentine" class="mw-redirect">wood turpentine</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Beeswax" title="Beeswax">beeswax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_turpentine" title="Wood turpentine" class="mw-redirect">wood turpentine</a>, or paste waxes.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1994_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1994-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Opaque paint coatings are used primarily for work applied over copper when substrate integrity and longevity are desired but a specific color other than the naturally occurring copper hues is required.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><br> Zinc-tin coatings are an alternative to lead coatings since they have approximately the same appearance and workability.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">Vitreous enamel</a> coatings are used primarily for art work over copper.</p> 
     <p>More details on copper finishes are available.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Copper-Architecture.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Copper-Architecture.jpg/220px-Copper-Architecture.jpg" width="220" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Copper-Architecture.jpg/330px-Copper-Architecture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Copper-Architecture.jpg/440px-Copper-Architecture.jpg 2x" data-file-width="539" data-file-height="611"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Copper-Architecture.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Example of architectural copper work, showing copper dome with copper finial, winding spiral copper roof awning, and a copper railing.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Craftsmen and designers utilize copper’s inherent benefits to build aesthetically pleasing and long-lasting building systems. From <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a> to <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> and from homes to offices, copper is used in many products: low-sloped and pitched roofs, <a href="/wiki/Soffit" title="Soffit">soffits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fascia_(architecture)" title="Fascia (architecture)">fascias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flashing_(weatherproofing)" title="Flashing (weatherproofing)">flashings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain_gutter" title="Rain gutter">gutters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downspout" title="Downspout">downspouts</a>, building expansion joints, <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">domes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaults</a>. Copper is also used to clad walls and other surfaces in the exterior and interior environment.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copperconcept1_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copperconcept1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roofing">Roofing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Roofing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Copper offers a unique character and durability as a roofing material. Its appearance can complement any style of building, from traditional to modern. Its warmth and beauty make it a desirable material for many architects. Copper also satisfies demands of architects and building owners regarding lifetime cost, ease of fabrication, low maintenance, and environmental friendliness.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG/220px-Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG/330px-Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG/440px-Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2853" data-file-height="2188"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Curoofhecksherjeh.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> New copper roof installation.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The installation of copper roofing is a craft requiring experienced installers. Its <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malleability" title="Malleability" class="mw-redirect">malleability</a> make it a compatible material to form over irregular roof structures. It is easy to hammer or work into watertight designs without caulk or gaskets.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> Domes and other curved roof shapes are readily handled with copper.</p> 
     <p>When properly designed and installed, a copper roof provides an economical, long-term roofing solution. Tests on European copper roofs from the 18th century showed that, in theory, copper roofs can last one thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Another advantage of copper roofing systems is that they are relatively easy to repair. For small pits or cracks, affected areas can be cleaned and filled with <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">solder</a>. For larger areas, patches can be cut and soldered into place. For major areas, the affected copper can be cut out and replaced using a flat locked soldered seam.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_26-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper roofs can be designed to meet or surpass other materials in terms of energy savings. A vented copper roof assembly at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratories" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratories" class="mw-redirect">Oak Ridge National Laboratories</a> (U.S.) substantially reduced heat gain versus stone-coated steel shingle (SR246E90) or <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_shingle" title="Asphalt shingle">asphalt shingle</a> (SR093E89), resulting in lower energy costs.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Types of copper roofs include:<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Standing seam roofing</b> is composed of preformed or field-formed pans. The pans run parallel to the slope of the roof and are joined to adjacent pans with double-locked standing seams. Copper cleats locked into these seams secure the roofing to the deck.</p> 
     <p><b>Batten seam roofing</b> consists of copper pans running parallel to the roof slope, separated by wood battens. Battens are covered with copper caps that are loose-locked into adjacent pans to help to secure the roofing. Cleats attached to the battens secure the roofing pans. Transverse seams are required to join ends of preformed pans.</p> 
     <p><b>Horizontal seam roofs,</b> also called the Bermuda style, consist of copper pans where the long dimension runs horizontally across a roof, attached to horizontal wood nailers. A step is used at each nailer to allow adjacent pans to lock effectively. The height and spacing of the steps enable different appearances.</p> 
     <p>A common design for a <b>chevron roof</b> is based on a batten seam construction to which auxiliary battens are attached. With proper design, decorative battens can have almost any shape or size and run in any direction.</p> 
     <p><b>Flat locked and soldered seam roofing</b> systems are typically used on flat or low-pitched roofs. They are also used on curved surfaces such as domes and barrel vaults.</p> 
     <p><b>Flat seam</b> unsoldered copper roofing is a shingle-like option for high slope applications.</p> 
     <p><b>Mansard roofs</b> are used on vertical or nearly vertical surfaces. For the most part, these roofs are based on standing seam or batten seam construction.</p> 
     <p><b>Long-pan systems</b> (pans and seam lengths greater than 10-feet) accommodate the cumulative expansion stress over long spans of copper sheets. These installations can be complicated due to the length of roof pan versus seam length, cleat design and spacing, and the physical expansion characteristics of copper sheets. This expansion must be accommodated by fixing the pan at one end (which accumulates the expansion at the loose end) or by fixing the center of the pan (which accumulates half of the expansion at both free ends).<sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition to panels, copper roof tiles can add uniqueness to a roofing system. They can be used on any roof shape and in all types of climates.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flashing">Flashing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Flashing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>While most modern construction materials are fairly resistant to moisture penetration, many joints between masonry units, panels, and architectural features are not. The effects of natural movement due to settlement, expansion, and contraction may eventually lead to leaks.</p> 
     <p>Copper is an excellent material for <a href="/wiki/Flashing_(weatherproofing)" title="Flashing (weatherproofing)">flashing</a> because of its malleability, strength, solderability, workability, high resistance to the caustic effects of mortars and hostile environments, and long service life. This enables a roof to be built without weak points. Since flashing is expensive to replace if it fails, copper’s long life is a major cost advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-copper4_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper4-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Cold rolled 1/8"-hard temper copper is recommended for most flashing applications. This material offers more resistance than soft copper to the stresses of expansion and contraction. Soft copper can be specified where extreme forming is required, such as in complicated roof shapes. Thermal movement in flashings is prevented or is permitted only at predetermined locations.<sup id="cite_ref-specifications2000_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications2000-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Flashing installed incorrectly can promote line corrosion and shorten the life of valley flashing, especially in acidic environments. The risk is most prevalent at the leading edge of shingles where the shingle edges rest on the copper flashing.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated8_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated8-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-specifications1998_58-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-specifications1998-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Through-wall flashing diverts moisture that has entered the wall before it can cause damage. Counterflashing diverts water to the base flashing, which, in turn, diverts it to other materials.</p> 
     <p>Various types of copper flashings and copings exist. Diagramatic explanations are available.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gutters_and_downspouts">Gutters and downspouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Gutters and downspouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Copper-Gutter-System.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Copper-Gutter-System.png/220px-Copper-Gutter-System.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Copper-Gutter-System.png/330px-Copper-Gutter-System.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Copper-Gutter-System.png 2x" data-file-width="390" data-file-height="293"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Copper-Gutter-System.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Copper gutter system showing half round copper gutters, radius copper guttering, copper leader head, round copper downspout pipe, decorative copper gutter hangers.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Leaking <a href="/wiki/Rain_gutter" title="Rain gutter">gutters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downspout" title="Downspout">downspouts</a> can cause serious damage to a building's interior and exterior. Copper is a good choice for gutters and downspouts because it makes strong leak-proof joints. Gutters and downspouts made with copper are expected to outlast other metal materials and plastics. Even in corrosion-prone <a href="/wiki/Seacoast" title="Seacoast" class="mw-redirect">seacoast</a> environments or in areas with <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>, copper gutters and downspouts can provide 50 years or more of service.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Downspouts can be plain or corrugated, round or rectangular. Sixteen- or twenty-ounce cold rolled copper is typically used. Decorative designs are also available.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg/220px-Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg/330px-Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg/440px-Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="334"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Gargoyle_rain_spout.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Gargoyle rain spout roof scupper combination fashioned from pure copper.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Hung copper gutters are supported by brass- or copper brackets or hangers, or by brass straps. Copper gutter linings are often built into wood framed supporting structures. Scuppers are used to provide an outlet through parapet walls or gravel stops on flat and built-up roofs to allow drainage of excess water. They can be used in conjunction with gutters and downspouts to divert water flow to the desired location. Copper roof sumps are generally used for draining small roof areas such as canopies. Roof sump drains are not recommended for general roof drainage systems.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:LG_Leaderhead_045.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/LG_Leaderhead_045.gif/220px-LG_Leaderhead_045.gif" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/LG_Leaderhead_045.gif/330px-LG_Leaderhead_045.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/LG_Leaderhead_045.gif 2x" data-file-width="430" data-file-height="447"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:LG_Leaderhead_045.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Custom-made copper leader head.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>One of the disadvantages of copper is its propensity to stain light-colored building materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> or <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2002_17-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2002-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Green staining is particularly visible on light-colored surfaces. Lead-coated copper can result in a black or gray stain that may blend well with lighter building materials. Staining can be reduced by collecting runoff in gutters and directing it away from the building via downspouts or by designing drip edges to help reduce the amount of copper laden moisture that comes into contact with material below. Coating the adjacent surface of the porous material with a clear <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> sealant also reduces staining. Staining may not develop in areas of rapid run-off due to the short dwell time of water on the copper.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wall_cladding">Wall cladding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Wall cladding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Copper_cladding" title="Copper cladding">Copper cladding</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Copper cladding has become popular in modern architecture. The technology enables architects to incorporate visually desirable features into their designs, such as embossed or shaped-metal cladding.</p> 
     <p>Cladding enables structures to be made with much less weight than solid copper. Four millimeter-thick composites weigh 2.08&nbsp;pounds per square foot, only 35% as much as solid copper of the same thickness.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper cladding is used in building exteriors and indoor environments. On building exteriors, copper cladding sheets, shingles, and pre-fabricated panels shield buildings from the elements, acting as first line of defenses against wind, dust, and water. The cladding is lightweight, durable, and corrosion resistant, which is particularly important for large buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup> Common interior applications include <a href="/wiki/Lobby_(room)" title="Lobby (room)">lobby</a> walls, <a href="/wiki/Soffit" title="Soffit">soffits</a>, column facings, and interior walls of <a href="/wiki/Elevator_cab" title="Elevator cab" class="mw-redirect">elevator cabs</a>.</p> 
     <p>Copper cladding can be cut, routed, sawed, filed, drilled, screwed, welded, and curved to form complex shapes. A variety of finishes and colors are available.</p> 
     <p>Flat, circular, and unusually shaped walls can be covered with copper cladding. Most are field-formed from sheet material. They can also be pre-manufactured. In addition, engineered systems such as insulated panels, non-insulated honeycomb panels, copper screen panels, and structural wall claddings are available. Horizontal copper siding provides a relatively flat appearance with fine horizontal lines. Beveled copper panels have depth for heavy-shadowed effects. Flat siding has minimal shadows. Structural panels are designed to be attached directly to a wall structure without the use of a continuous substrate. Diagonal flat lock panels are used on curved surfaces, such as domes, spires and vaults. Horizontal flat lock panels are basically identical to flat seam roofing applied on a vertical surface. Copper screen panels are a lightweight finish screen that can be perforated or have shaped openings to function as sun or decorative screens. A copper alloy curtain wall is a non-structural outer building covering that keeps out weather.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup> Composite copper cladding is made by attaching copper sheeting to both sides of rigid <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> sheet.</p>

     <p>Several different copper facade cladding systems are available:</p> 
     <p><b>Seaming technique.</b> This is a vertical or horizontal classical cladding construction used in copper roof and façade designs. Available in sheets and strips, the cladding is fixed with clips. Since water tightness may not be a concern on vertical surfaces, angle standing seams are often sufficient. Double lock standing seams are often not necessary. Links to photographs of horozontal and vertical standing and flat lock seams at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Debrecen" title="University of Debrecen">University of Debrecen</a>'s Copper Gateway in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a><sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> and of pre-oxidized copper clad seamed facades at the Hotel Crowne Plaza Milano, in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup> are available.</p> 
     <p><b>System shingles.</b> Shingles are pre-manufactured rectangular or square flat tiles for roofs, walls, and individual building components. They have 180<sup>0</sup> folds along all four borders – two folds towards the external side and two towards the internal side. The shingles are interlocked during installation. The fastening is hidden with stainless steel or copper clips on wood sheeting or trapezoidal panels. Machine notching and folding ensures that the shingles have uniform dimensions. Links to pictoral examples of copper shingles in an exterior<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> and interior<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup> environment are available.</p> 
     <p><b>Panels.</b> Panels are sheets of pre-profiled copper with lengths up to 4–5&nbsp;meters and standard widths up to 500&nbsp;mm. They are two-sided cladding elements that can be with or without an end base. Assembly is performed using the tongue and groove principle or by overlapping. Panels can be assembled vertically, horizontally, or diagonally. There are three basic forms: tongue and groove panels laid vertically as level surface facade cladding; tongue and groove panels laid horizontally as level surface facade cladding; and custom panels laid in different directions with visible or masked fastening, flush against the surface or overlapping. Links to representative photographs of golden-colored<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> and patinated-green panels<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup> are available.</p> 
     <p><b>System cassettes.</b> This is a rigid rectangular ventilated wall system consisting of curved or flat metal panels mounted and secured to a supporting structure. All four borders are pre-folded at the factory. Folded edges on every side allow large sheet metal parts to lie even with the cladding surface. Fixing is usually by riveting, screwing, or by using angle brackets or bolt hooks to fix the cassettes directly to the substrate. System cassettes are pre-profiled to meet specific architectural requirements. Links to representative photographs of cassette cladding are available.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><b>Profiled sheets.</b> Profiled sheets are well suited for covering large cladding surfaces without joints because of their regular, unimposing profiles. Available in a wide variety of shapes, they are well-suited for new flat roofs, façade and pitched roofs, and renovation work. Profiles available include: sinusoidal wave corrugated profiles; trapezoidal profiles with various geometries; and custom profiles with special geometry and edges. They can be pre-manufactured and specified with embossed patterns or other designs.</p> 
     <p><b>Special shapes.</b> Special shaped façades are available to impart desired visual effects. Perforated metal sheets are available with a variety of shapes (round, square, oblong, etc.) and arrangements (rectangular, diagonal, parallel width, staggered, etc.). They can be designed to create subtle patterns, ‘super graphics,’ and text. Mesh and textile structures are also available. Links to photographs of special-shaped cladded buildings are available.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_expansion_joints">Building expansion joints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Building expansion joints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Designing for the movement of building components due to temperature, loads, and settlement is an important part of architectural detailing. Building expansion joints provide barriers to the exterior and cover spaces between components. Copper is an excellent material for expansion joints because it is easy to form and lasts a long time. Details regarding roof conditions, roof edges, floors, are available.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>Copper aesthetically enhances interior wall systems, ceilings, fixtures, furniture, and hardware by evoking an atmosphere of warmth, tranquility, and calm. Regarding performance advantages, it is lightweight, fire resistant, durable, workable, and non-organic (it does not off-gas). Typical copper-based interiors include panels, <a href="/wiki/Roof_shingle" title="Roof shingle">shingles</a>, screens, <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(art)" title="Ornament (art)">ornaments</a>, fixtures, and other decorative enhancements.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since copper surfaces kill <a href="/wiki/Pathogenic_microbe" title="Pathogenic microbe" class="mw-redirect">pathogenic microbes</a>, architects who design public facilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mass_transit" title="Mass transit" class="mw-redirect">mass transit</a> facilities, look to copper products as a <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In recent years, copper <a href="/wiki/Countertop" title="Countertop">countertops</a>, range hoods, <a href="/wiki/Sink" title="Sink">sinks</a>, handles, <a href="/wiki/Doorknob" title="Doorknob" class="mw-redirect">doorknobs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faucet" title="Faucet" class="mw-redirect">faucets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">furniture</a> embellishments have become trendy – both for their appearance as well as for their <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">antimicrobial</a> properties. <i>(See main article: <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces" title="Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces">Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces</a>).</i></p> 
     <p>Copper is joined in indoor environments by butt welding, soldering, rivets, nails, screws, bolting, standing seams, lap seams (with and without fasteners), flat seams, bolted flanges, splines, flush laps, and batten seams.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_buildings">Green buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_in_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Green buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable" class="mw-redirect">Sustainable</a> materials are key elements of <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green buildings</a>. Some benefits of sustainable materials include durability, long life, recyclability, and energy and thermal efficiency. Copper ranks highly in all of these categories.</p> 
     <p>Copper is one of nature’s most efficient thermal and electrical conductors, which helps to conserve energy. Because of its high thermal conductivity, it is used extensively in building <a href="/wiki/Heating_system" title="Heating system">heating systems</a>, direct exchange <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pumps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> and hot water equipment. Its high electrical conductivity increases the efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_energy_efficient_motors" title="Copper in energy efficient motors" class="mw-redirect">electrical motors</a>, fans, and appliances, making a building’s operation more cost effective with less energy and environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because copper has a better thermal conductivity rating than usual façade and roofing materials, it is well-suited to solar thermal façade systems. The first commercial application of a fully integrated solar thermal copper façade system was installed at the Pori Public Swimming Complex in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. The installation is an urban example of sustainability and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" title="Carbon emissions" class="mw-redirect">carbon emissions</a> reduction. The solar façade works in conjunction with roof collectors and is supplemented by roof-mounted <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect">photovoltaics</a> that provide 120,000 kWh of heat, an amount of energy equivalent to that used annually by six average family houses in cold-climate Finland.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>One standard in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Green_Building_Council" title="United States Green Building Council" class="mw-redirect">United States Green Building Council</a> (USGBC)’s <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> rating system (<a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a>) requires that newly constructed buildings include materials containing pre- and post-consumer recycled content. Most copper products used in construction (except electrical materials that require highly refined virgin copper) contain a large percentage of recycled content. See: <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_architecture#Recycling" title="Copper in architecture">Copper in architecture#Recycling</a>.</p>

     <p>Award programs highlight copper architecture installations in Canada and the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119"><span>[</span>119<span>]</span></a></sup> and in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120"><span>[</span>120<span>]</span></a></sup> An International Copper and the Home Competition also exists.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121"><span>[</span>121<span>]</span></a></sup> Judged by architecture and copper industry experts, criteria for the awards programs include copper in building design, craft of copper installation, excellence in innovation, and historic renovation.</p>
