     <p><b>Geothermal energy</b> is <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> generated and stored in the Earth. Thermal energy is the energy that determines the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of matter. The geothermal energy of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> originates from the original formation of the planet and from <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of materials (in currently uncertain<sup id="cite_ref-Dye_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dye-1">[1]</a></sup> but possibly roughly equal<sup id="cite_ref-Gando_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gando-2">[2]</a></sup> proportions). The <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal gradient</a>, which is the difference in temperature between the core of the planet and its surface, drives a continuous <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">conduction of thermal energy</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> from the core to the surface. The adjective <i>geothermal</i> originates from the Greek roots <i>γη (ge)</i>, meaning earth, and <i>θερμος (thermos)</i>, meaning hot.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_internal_heat_budget" title="Earth's internal heat budget">Earth's internal heat</a> is thermal energy generated from radioactive decay and continual heat loss from Earth's formation.<sup id="cite_ref-turcotte_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turcotte-3">[3]</a></sup> Temperatures at the <a href="/wiki/Core%E2%80%93mantle_boundary" title="Core–mantle boundary">core–mantle boundary</a> may reach over 4000&nbsp;°C (7,200&nbsp;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The high temperature and pressure in Earth's interior cause some rock to melt and solid <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> to behave plastically, resulting in portions of <a href="/wiki/Mantle_convection" title="Mantle convection">mantle convecting</a> upward since it is lighter than the surrounding rock. Rock and water is heated in the crust, sometimes up to 370&nbsp;°C (700&nbsp;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>From <a href="/wiki/Hot_springs" title="Hot springs" class="mw-redirect">hot springs</a>, geothermal energy has been used for bathing since <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> times and for <a href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating" class="mw-redirect">space heating</a> since ancient Roman times, but it is now better known for <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a>. Worldwide, 11,700 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts" class="mw-redirect">megawatts</a> (MW) of geothermal power is online in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> An additional 28 gigawatts of direct <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a> capacity is installed for district heating, space heating, spas, industrial processes, desalination and agricultural applications in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Geothermal power is cost effective, reliable, sustainable, and environmentally friendly,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but has historically been limited to areas near <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate_boundaries" title="Tectonic plate boundaries" class="mw-redirect">tectonic plate boundaries</a>. Recent technological advances have dramatically expanded the range and size of viable resources, especially for applications such as home heating, opening a potential for widespread exploitation. Geothermal wells release greenhouse gases trapped deep within the earth, but these emissions are much lower per energy unit than those of fossil fuels. As a result, geothermal power has the potential to help mitigate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> if widely deployed in place of fossil fuels.</p> 
     <p>The Earth's geothermal resources are theoretically more than adequate to supply humanity's energy needs, but only a very small fraction may be profitably exploited. Drilling and exploration for deep resources is very expensive. Forecasts for the future of geothermal power depend on assumptions about technology, energy prices, subsidies, and interest rates. Pilot programs like EWEB's customer opt in Green Power Program <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> show that customers would be willing to pay a little more for a renewable energy source like geothermal. But as a result of government assisted research and industry experience, the cost of generating geothermal power has decreased by 25% over the past two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2001, geothermal energy costs between two and ten US cents per kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Oldest_geothermal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/220px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/330px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/440px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Oldest_geothermal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The oldest known pool fed by a hot spring, built in the 
        <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> in the 3rd century BCE.
       </div>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">Hot springs</a> have been used for bathing at least since <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> times<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The oldest known spa is a stone pool on China's Lisan mountain built in the <a href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" title="Qin Dynasty" class="mw-redirect">Qin Dynasty</a> in the 3rd century BC, at the same site where the Huaqing Chi palace was later built. In the first century AD, Romans conquered <i><a href="/wiki/Aquae_Sulis" title="Aquae Sulis">Aquae Sulis</a></i>, now <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath, Somerset</a>, England, and used the hot springs there to feed <a href="/wiki/Thermae" title="Thermae">public baths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">underfloor heating</a>. The admission fees for these baths probably represent the first commercial use of geothermal power. The world's oldest geothermal district heating system in <a href="/wiki/Chaudes-Aigues" title="Chaudes-Aigues">Chaudes-Aigues</a>, France, has been operating since the 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-13">[13]</a></sup> The earliest industrial exploitation began in 1827 with the use of geyser steam to extract <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> from <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_mud" title="Volcanic mud" class="mw-redirect">volcanic mud</a> in <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a>, Italy.</p> 
     <p>In 1892, America's first <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> system in <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a> was powered directly by geothermal energy, and was copied in <a href="/wiki/Klamath_Falls,_Oregon" title="Klamath Falls, Oregon">Klamath Falls, Oregon</a> in 1900. A deep geothermal well was used to heat greenhouses in Boise in 1926, and geysers were used to heat greenhouses in Iceland and <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> at about the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-Dickson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-14">[14]</a></sup> Charlie Lieb developed the first <a href="/wiki/Downhole_heat_exchanger" title="Downhole heat exchanger">downhole heat exchanger</a> in 1930 to heat his house. Steam and hot water from geysers began heating homes in Iceland starting in 1943.</p>

     <p>In the 20th century, demand for electricity led to the consideration of geothermal power as a generating source. Prince <a href="/wiki/Piero_Ginori_Conti" title="Piero Ginori Conti">Piero Ginori Conti</a> tested the first geothermal power generator on 4 July 1904, at the same Larderello dry steam field where geothermal acid extraction began. It successfully lit four light bulbs.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Later, in 1911, the world's first commercial geothermal power plant was built there. It was the world's only industrial producer of geothermal electricity until New Zealand built a plant in 1958. In 2012, it produced some 594 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">Lord Kelvin</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a> in 1852, and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Zoelly" title="Heinrich Zoelly">Heinrich Zoelly</a> had patented the idea of using it to draw heat from the ground in 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-zogg_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zogg-18">[18]</a></sup> But it was not until the late 1940s that the geothermal heat pump was successfully implemented. The earliest one was probably Robert C. Webber's home-made 2.2&nbsp;kW direct-exchange system, but sources disagree as to the exact timeline of his invention.<sup id="cite_ref-zogg_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zogg-18">[18]</a></sup> J. Donald Kroeker designed the first commercial geothermal heat pump to heat the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Building_(Portland,_Oregon)" title="Commonwealth Building (Portland, Oregon)">Commonwealth Building (Portland, Oregon)</a> and demonstrated it in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomquist_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomquist-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Professor Carl Nielsen of <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a> built the first residential open loop version in his home in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The technology became popular in Sweden as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, and has been growing slowly in worldwide acceptance since then. The 1979 development of <a href="/wiki/Polybutylene" title="Polybutylene">polybutylene</a> pipe greatly augmented the heat pump’s economic viability.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomquist_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomquist-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 1960, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric" title="Pacific Gas and Electric" class="mw-redirect">Pacific Gas and Electric</a> began operation of the first successful geothermal electric power plant in the United States at The Geysers in California.<sup id="cite_ref-100years_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100years-22">[22]</a></sup> The original turbine lasted for more than 30&nbsp;years and produced 11&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt" class="mw-redirect">MW</a> net power.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Binary_cycle_power_plant" title="Binary cycle power plant" class="mw-redirect">binary cycle power plant</a> was first demonstrated in 1967 in the <a href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR" class="mw-redirect">USSR</a> and later introduced to the US in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-100years_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100years-22">[22]</a></sup> This technology allows the generation of electricity from much lower temperature resources than previously. In 2006, a binary cycle plant in <a href="/wiki/Chena_Hot_Springs,_Alaska" title="Chena Hot Springs, Alaska">Chena Hot Springs, Alaska</a>, came on-line, producing electricity from a record low fluid temperature of 57&nbsp;°C (135&nbsp;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Chena_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chena-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity">Electricity</span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_electricity" title="Geothermal electricity">Geothermal electricity</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The International Geothermal Association (IGA) has reported that 10,715 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts" class="mw-redirect">megawatts</a> (MW) of geothermal power in 24 countries is online, which was expected to generate 67,246 GWh of electricity in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-gea2010_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gea2010-26">[26]</a></sup> This represents a 20% increase in online capacity since 2005. IGA projects growth to 18,500 MW by 2015, due to the projects presently under consideration, often in areas previously assumed to have little exploitable resource.<sup id="cite_ref-gea2010_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gea2010-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy_in_the_United_States" title="Geothermal energy in the United States">United States</a> led the world in geothermal electricity production with 3,086 MW of installed capacity from 77 power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-geap7_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geap7-27">[27]</a></sup> The largest group of geothermal <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant" class="mw-redirect">power plants</a> in the world is located at <a href="/wiki/The_Geysers" title="The Geysers">The Geysers</a>, a geothermal field in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Khan_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khan-28">[28]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> is the second highest producer, with 1,904 MW of capacity online. Geothermal power makes up approximately 27% of Philippine electricity generation.<sup id="cite_ref-geap7_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geap7-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Geothermal electric plants were traditionally built exclusively on the edges of tectonic plates where high temperature geothermal resources are available near the surface. The development of <a href="/wiki/Binary_cycle_power_plant" title="Binary cycle power plant" class="mw-redirect">binary cycle power plants</a> and improvements in drilling and extraction technology enable <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_geothermal_systems" title="Enhanced geothermal systems" class="mw-redirect">enhanced geothermal systems</a> over a much greater geographical range.<sup id="cite_ref-INEL_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INEL-30">[30]</a></sup> Demonstration projects are operational in <a href="/wiki/Landau-Pfalz" title="Landau-Pfalz" class="mw-redirect">Landau-Pfalz</a>, Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Soultz-sous-For%C3%AAts" title="Soultz-sous-Forêts">Soultz-sous-Forêts</a>, France, while an earlier effort in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, Switzerland was shut down <a href="/wiki/Induced_seismicity_in_Basel" title="Induced seismicity in Basel">after it triggered earthquakes</a>. Other demonstration projects are under construction in <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_Australia" title="Geothermal power in Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America" class="mw-redirect">United States of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> of geothermal electric plants is low, around 10–23%, because geothermal fluids do not reach the high temperatures of steam from boilers. The laws of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> limits the efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Cycle_efficiency" title="Cycle efficiency" class="mw-redirect">heat engines</a> in extracting useful energy. Exhaust heat is wasted, unless it can be used directly and locally, for example in greenhouses, timber mills, and district heating. System efficiency does not materially affect operational costs as it would for plants that use fuel, but it does affect return on the capital used to build the plant. In order to produce more energy than the pumps consume, electricity generation requires relatively hot fields and specialized heat cycles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Because geothermal power does not rely on variable sources of energy, unlike, for example, wind or solar, its <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a> can be quite large – up to 96% has been demonstrated.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The global average was 73% in 2005.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2> 
     <p>Geothermal energy comes in either <i>vapor-dominated</i> or <i>liquid-dominated</i> forms. <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Geysers" title="The Geysers">The Geysers</a> are vapor-dominated. Vapor-dominated sites offer temperatures from 240 to 300&nbsp;°C that produce superheated steam.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid-dominated_plants">Liquid-dominated plants</span></h3> 
     <p>Liquid-dominated reservoirs (LDRs) were more common with temperatures greater than 200&nbsp;°C (392&nbsp;°F) and are found near young volcanoes surrounding the Pacific Ocean and in rift zones and hot spots. <i>Flash plants</i> are the common way to generate electricity from these sources. Pumps are generally not required, powered instead when the water turns to steam. Most wells generate 2-10MWe. Steam is separated from liquid via cyclone separators, while the liquid is returned to the reservoir for reheating/reuse. As of 2013, the largest liquid system is <a href="/wiki/Cerro_Prieto" title="Cerro Prieto">Cerro Prieto</a> in Mexico, which generates 750 MWe from temperatures reaching 350&nbsp;°C (662&nbsp;°F). The <a href="/wiki/Salton_Sea" title="Salton Sea">Salton Sea</a> field in Southern California offers the potential of generating 2000 MWe.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Lower temperature LDRs (120–200&nbsp;°C) require pumping. They are common in extensional terrains, where heating takes place via deep circulation along faults, such as in the Western US and Turkey. Water passes through a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> binary plant. The water vaporizes an organic working fluid that drives a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>. These binary plants originated in the Soviet Union in the late 1960s and predominate in new US plants. Binary plants have no emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eere_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eere-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_energy">Thermal energy</span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main articles: 
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">Geothermal heating</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">geothermal heat pump</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Lower temperature sources produce the energy equivalent of 100M BBL per year. Sources with temperatures of 30–150&nbsp;°C are used without conversion to electricity as <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries" class="mw-redirect">fisheries</a>, mineral recovery, industrial process heating and bathing in 75 countries. Heat pumps extract energy from shallow sources at 10–20&nbsp;°C in 43 countries for use in space heating and cooling. Home heating is the fastest-growing means of exploiting geothermal energy, with global annual growth rate of 30% in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and 20% in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eere_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eere-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Approximately 270&nbsp;petajoules&nbsp;(PJ) of geothermal heating was used in 2004. More than half went for space heating, and another third for heated pools. The remainder supported industrial and agricultural applications. Global installed capacity was 28&nbsp;GW, but capacity factors tend to be low (30% on average) since heat is mostly needed in winter. Some 88&nbsp;PJ for space heating was extracted by an estimated 1.3&nbsp;million <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">geothermal heat pumps</a> with a total capacity of 15 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Heat for these purposes may also be extracted from co-generation at a <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_electrical_plant" title="Geothermal electrical plant" class="mw-redirect">geothermal electrical plant</a>.</p> 
     <p>Heating is cost-effective at many more sites than electricity generation. At natural hot springs or <a href="/wiki/Geysers" title="Geysers" class="mw-redirect">geysers</a>, water can be piped directly into <a href="/wiki/Radiators" title="Radiators" class="mw-redirect">radiators</a>. In hot, dry ground, <a href="/wiki/Earth_tubes" title="Earth tubes" class="mw-redirect">earth tubes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Downhole_heat_exchanger" title="Downhole heat exchanger">downhole heat exchangers</a> can collect the heat. However, even in areas where the ground is colder than room temperature, heat can often be extracted with a geothermal heat pump more cost-effectively and cleanly than by conventional furnaces.<sup id="cite_ref-strategic_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strategic-35">[35]</a></sup> These devices draw on much shallower and colder resources than traditional geothermal techniques. They frequently combine functions, including <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">seasonal thermal energy storage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> collection, and electric heating. Heat pumps can be used for space heating essentially anywhere.</p> 
     <p>Iceland is the world leader in direct applications. Some 92.5% of its homes are heated with geothermal energy, saving Iceland over $100 million annually in avoided oil imports. <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> has the world's biggest district heating system. Once known as the most polluted city in the world, it is now one of the cleanest.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_geothermal">Enhanced geothermal</span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_geothermal_system" title="Enhanced geothermal system">Enhanced geothermal system</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) actively inject water into wells to be heated and pumped back out. The water is injected under high pressure to expand existing rock fissures to enable the water to freely flow in and out. The technique was adapted from oil and gas extraction techniques. However, the geologic formations are deeper and no toxic chemicals are used, reducing the possibility of environmental damage. Drillers can employ <a href="/wiki/Directional_drilling" title="Directional drilling">directional drilling</a> to expand the size of the reservoir.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Small-scale EGS have been installed in the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Graben" title="Rhine Graben" class="mw-redirect">Rhine Graben</a> at Soultz-sous-Forêts in France and at Landau and Insheim in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-sci2013_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span></h2> 
     <p>Geothermal power requires no fuel (except for pumps), and is therefore immune to fuel cost fluctuations. However, capital costs are significant. Drilling accounts for over half the costs, and exploration of deep resources entails significant risks. A typical well doublet (extraction and injection wells) in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> can support 4.5 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt" class="mw-redirect">megawatts</a> (MW) and costs about $10&nbsp;million to drill, with a 20% failure rate.<sup id="cite_ref-econ101_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ101-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png/220px-Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png/330px-Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png/440px-Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png 2x" data-file-width="1750" data-file-height="894"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Sonoma_Plant_at_The_Geysers_4778.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A power plant at The Geysers
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In total, electrical plant construction and well drilling cost about €2–5&nbsp;million&nbsp; per&nbsp;MW of electrical capacity, while the <a href="/wiki/Levelised_energy_cost" title="Levelised energy cost" class="mw-redirect">break–even</a> price is 0.04–0.10&nbsp;€ per&nbsp;kW·h.<sup id="cite_ref-Bertani_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertani-15">[15]</a></sup> Enhanced geothermal systems tend to be on the high side of these ranges, with capital costs above $4&nbsp;million per&nbsp;MW and break–even above $0.054 per&nbsp;kW·h in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Direct heating applications can use much shallower wells with lower temperatures, so smaller systems with lower costs and risks are feasible. Residential geothermal heat pumps with a capacity of 10&nbsp;kilowatt (kW) are routinely installed for around $1–3,000 per&nbsp;kilowatt. District heating systems may benefit from economies of scale if demand is geographically dense, as in cities and greenhouses, but otherwise piping installation dominates capital costs. The capital cost of one such district heating system in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> was estimated at somewhat over 1&nbsp;million&nbsp;€ per&nbsp;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Direct systems of any size are much simpler than electric generators and have lower maintenance costs per&nbsp;kW·h, but they must consume electricity to run pumps and compressors. Some governments subsidize geothermal projects.</p> 
     <p>Geothermal power is highly scalable: from a rural village to an entire city.<sup id="cite_ref-small_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-small-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The most developed geothermal field in the United States is <a href="/wiki/The_Geysers" title="The Geysers">The Geysers</a> in Northern California.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Geothermal projects have several stages of development. Each phase has associated risks. At the early stages of reconnaissance and geophysical surveys, many projects are cancelled, making that phase unsuitable for traditional lending. Projects moving forward from the identification, exploration and exploratory drilling often trade equity for financing.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources">Resources</span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:EGS_diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/EGS_diagram.svg/220px-EGS_diagram.svg.png" width="220" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/EGS_diagram.svg/330px-EGS_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/EGS_diagram.svg/440px-EGS_diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="744" data-file-height="1052"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:EGS_diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <b>Enhanced geothermal system</b> 1:Reservoir 2:Pump&nbsp;house 3:Heat&nbsp;exchanger 4:Turbine&nbsp;hall 5:Production&nbsp;well 6:Injection&nbsp;well 7:Hot water to district heating 8:Porous&nbsp;sediments 9:Observation&nbsp;well 10:Crystalline&nbsp;bedrock
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The Earth's internal thermal energy flows to the surface by conduction at a rate of 44.2 <a href="/wiki/Terawatts" title="Terawatts" class="mw-redirect">terawatts</a> (TW),<sup id="cite_ref-pollack_et_al_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pollack_et_al-43">[43]</a></sup> and is replenished by radioactive decay of minerals at a rate of 30&nbsp;TW.<sup id="cite_ref-sustainability_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sustainability-44">[44]</a></sup> These power rates are more than double humanity’s current energy consumption from all primary sources, but most of this energy flow is not recoverable. In addition to the internal heat flows, the top layer of the surface to a depth of 10 meters (33&nbsp;ft) is heated by solar energy during the summer, and releases that energy and cools during the winter.</p> 
     <p>Outside of the seasonal variations, the <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal gradient</a> of temperatures through the crust is 25–30&nbsp;°C (77–86&nbsp;°F) per kilometer of depth in most of the world. The conductive heat <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> averages 0.1&nbsp;MW/km<sup>2</sup>. These values are much higher near tectonic plate boundaries where the crust is thinner. They may be further augmented by fluid circulation, either through <a href="/wiki/Magma_conduit" title="Magma conduit" class="mw-redirect">magma conduits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_springs" title="Hot springs" class="mw-redirect">hot springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_circulation" title="Hydrothermal circulation">hydrothermal circulation</a> or a combination of these.</p> 
     <p>A geothermal heat pump can extract enough heat from shallow ground anywhere in the world to provide home heating, but industrial applications need the higher temperatures of deep resources.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-13">[13]</a></sup> The thermal efficiency and profitability of electricity generation is particularly sensitive to temperature. The most demanding applications receive the greatest benefit from a high natural heat flux, ideally from using a <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot spring</a>. The next best option is to drill a well into a hot <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>. If no adequate aquifer is available, an artificial one may be built by injecting water to <a href="/wiki/Hydraulically_fracture" title="Hydraulically fracture" class="mw-redirect">hydraulically fracture</a> the bedrock. This last approach is called <a href="/wiki/Hot_dry_rock_geothermal_energy" title="Hot dry rock geothermal energy">hot dry rock geothermal energy</a> in Europe, or <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_geothermal_systems" title="Enhanced geothermal systems" class="mw-redirect">enhanced geothermal systems</a> in North America. Much greater potential may be available from this approach than from conventional tapping of natural aquifers.<sup id="cite_ref-INEL_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INEL-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Estimates of the potential for electricity generation from geothermal energy vary sixfold, from <span style="white-space:nowrap">.035<span style="margin-left:0.2em">to</span><span style="margin-left:0.2em">2</span><span style="margin-left:0.2em">TW</span></span> depending on the scale of investments.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">[7]</a></sup> Upper estimates of geothermal resources assume enhanced geothermal wells as deep as 10 kilometres (6&nbsp;mi), whereas existing geothermal wells are rarely more than 3 kilometres (2&nbsp;mi) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">[7]</a></sup> Wells of this depth are now common in the petroleum industry. The deepest research well in the world, the <a href="/wiki/Kola_superdeep_borehole" title="Kola superdeep borehole" class="mw-redirect">Kola superdeep borehole</a>, is 12 kilometres (7&nbsp;mi) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p> 
     <div style="clear:both;"></div> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2> 
     <p>According to the Geothermal Energy Association (GEA) installed geothermal capacity in the United States grew by 5%, or 147.05 MW, ce the last annual survey in March 2012. This increase came from seven geothermal projects that began production in 2012. GEA also revised its 2011 estimate of installed capacity upward by 128 MW, bringing current installed U.S. geothermal capacity to 3,386 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_Engineering_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Myanmar Engineering Society (page does not exist)">Myanmar Engineering Society</a> has identified at least 39 locations capable of geothermal power production and some of these hydrothermal reservoirs lie quite close to <a href="/wiki/Yangon" title="Yangon">Yangon</a> which is a significant underutilized resource.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewability_and_sustainability">Renewability and sustainability</span></h2> 
     <p>Geothermal power is considered to be renewable because any projected heat extraction is small compared to the Earth's heat content. The Earth has an internal heat content of <a href="/wiki/1_E31_J" title="1 E31 J" class="mw-redirect">10<sup>31</sup>&nbsp;joules</a> (3·10<sup>15</sup>&nbsp;TW·hr), approximately 100 billion times current (2010) worldwide annual energy consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">[7]</a></sup> About 20% of this is residual heat from <a href="/wiki/Planetary_accretion" title="Planetary accretion" class="mw-redirect">planetary accretion</a>, and the remainder is attributed to higher radioactive decay rates that existed in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-turcotte_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turcotte-3">[3]</a></sup> Natural heat flows are not in equilibrium, and the planet is slowly cooling down on geologic timescales. Human extraction taps a minute fraction of the natural outflow, often without accelerating it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="When would tapping heat not accelerate the cooling? Minutely, yes, but still. (May 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Geothermal power is also considered to be sustainable thanks to its power to sustain the Earth’s intricate ecosystems. By using geothermal sources of energy present generations of humans will not endanger the capability of future generations to use their own resources to the same amount that those energy sources are presently used.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Further, due to its low emissions geothermal energy is considered to have excellent potential for mitigation of global warming.</p> 
     <p>Even though geothermal power is globally sustainable, extraction must still be monitored to avoid local depletion.<sup id="cite_ref-sustainability_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sustainability-44">[44]</a></sup> Over the course of decades, individual wells draw down local temperatures and water levels until a new equilibrium is reached with natural flows. The three oldest sites, at <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wairakei" title="Wairakei">Wairakei</a>, and the Geysers have experienced reduced output because of local depletion. Heat and water, in uncertain proportions, were extracted faster than they were replenished. If production is reduced and water is reinjected, these wells could theoretically recover their full potential. Such mitigation strategies have already been implemented at some sites. The long-term sustainability of geothermal energy has been demonstrated at the Lardarello field in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> since 1913, at the Wairakei field in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> since 1958,<sup id="cite_ref-Wairakei_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wairakei-49">[49]</a></sup> and at The Geysers field in California since 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-300years_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-300years-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Falling electricity production may be boosted through drilling additional supply boreholes, as at <a href="/wiki/Poihipi_Power_Station" title="Poihipi Power Station">Poihipi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohaaki_Power_Station" title="Ohaaki Power Station">Ohaaki</a>.      <p>Fluids drawn from the deep earth carry a mixture of gases, notably <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 2</span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 2</span>S</span>), <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 4</span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 3</span></span>). These pollutants contribute to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, and noxious smells if released. Existing geothermal electric plants emit an average of 122 kilograms (269&nbsp;lb) of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br> 2</span></span> per megawatt-hour (MW·h) of electricity, a small fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Emission_intensity" title="Emission intensity">emission intensity</a> of conventional fossil fuel plants.<sup id="cite_ref-CO2_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CO2-51">[51]</a></sup> Plants that experience high levels of acids and volatile chemicals are usually equipped with emission-control systems to reduce the exhaust.</p> 
     <p>In addition to dissolved gases, hot water from geothermal sources may hold in solution trace amounts of toxic elements such as <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-toxic_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toxic-52">[52]</a></sup> These chemicals precipitate as the water cools, and can cause environmental damage if released. The modern practice of injecting cooled geothermal fluids back into the Earth to stimulate production has the side benefit of reducing this environmental risk.</p> 
     <p>Direct geothermal heating systems contain pumps and compressors, which may consume energy from a polluting source. This <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_load" title="Parasitic load">parasitic load</a> is normally a fraction of the heat output, so it is always less polluting than electric heating. However, if the electricity is produced by burning fossil fuels, then the net emissions of geothermal heating may be comparable to directly burning the fuel for heat. For example, a geothermal heat pump powered by electricity from a <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> plant would produce about as much pollution as a natural gas <a href="/wiki/Condensing_furnace" title="Condensing furnace" class="mw-redirect">condensing furnace</a> of the same size.<sup id="cite_ref-strategic_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strategic-35">[35]</a></sup> Therefore, the environmental value of direct geothermal heating applications is highly dependent on the emissions intensity of the neighboring electric grid.</p> 
     <p>Plant construction can adversely affect land stability. <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">Subsidence</a> has occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Wairakei_field" title="Wairakei field" class="mw-redirect">Wairakei field</a> in New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-13">[13]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Staufen_im_Breisgau" title="Staufen im Breisgau">Staufen im Breisgau</a>, Germany, <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">tectonic uplift</a> occurred instead, due to a previously isolated <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a> layer coming in contact with water and turning into gypsum, doubling its volume.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_geothermal_systems" title="Enhanced geothermal systems" class="mw-redirect">Enhanced geothermal systems</a> can trigger <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a>. The project in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> was suspended because more than 10,000 seismic events measuring up to 3.4 on the <a href="/wiki/Richter_Scale" title="Richter Scale" class="mw-redirect">Richter Scale</a> occurred over the first 6 days of water injection.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Geothermal has minimal land and freshwater requirements. Geothermal plants use 3.5 square kilometres (1.4&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) per&nbsp;gigawatt of electrical production (not capacity) versus 32 square kilometres (12&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) and 12 square kilometres (4.6&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) for <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> facilities and <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-13">[13]</a></sup> They use 20 litres (5.3&nbsp;US&nbsp;gal) of freshwater per MW·h versus over 1,000 litres (260&nbsp;US&nbsp;gal) per&nbsp;MW·h for nuclear, coal, or oil.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-13">[13]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_frameworks">Legal frameworks</span></h2> 
     <p>Some of the legal issues raised by geothermal energy resources include questions of ownership and allocation of the resource, the grant of exploration permits, exploitation rights, royalties, and the extent to which geothermal energy issues have been recognized in existing planning and environmental laws. Other questions concern overlap between geothermal and mineral or petroleum tenements. Broader issues concern the extent to which the legal framework for encouragement of renewable energy assists in encouraging geothermal industry innovation and development.</p>
