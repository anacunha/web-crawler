     <p>An <b>electrical grid</b> is an interconnected network for delivering <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> from suppliers to consumers. It consists of generating stations that produce electrical power, high-voltage transmission lines that carry power from distant sources to demand centers, and distribution lines that connect individual customers.<sup id="cite_ref-Overview_of_Electrical_Power_System_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_Electrical_Power_System-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">Power stations</a> may be located near a fuel source, at a dam site, or to take advantage of renewable energy sources, and are often located away from heavily populated areas. They are usually quite large to take advantage of the <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>. The electric power which is generated is stepped up to a higher voltage at which it connects to the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric power transmission</a> network.</p> 
     <p>The bulk power transmission network will move the power long distances, sometimes across international boundaries, until it reaches its wholesale customer (usually the company that owns the local <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">electric power distribution</a> network).</p> 
     <p>On arrival at a substation, the power will be stepped down from a transmission level voltage to a distribution level voltage. As it exits the substation, it enters the distribution wiring. Finally, upon arrival at the service location, the power is stepped down again from the distribution voltage to the required service voltage(s).</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Early electric energy was produced near the device or service requiring that energy. In the 1880s, electricity competed with steam, hydraulics, and especially <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>. It was first produced on customer’s premises but later evolved into <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> plants that enjoyed <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>. In the industrialized world cities had networks of piped gas, used for lighting. But gas lamps since produced poor light, wasted heat, made rooms hot and smoky, and gave off <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>. In the 1880s electric lighting soon became advantageous compared to gas lighting.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">Electric utility</a> companies took advantage of economies of scale and moved to centralized power generation, distribution, and system management.<sup id="cite_ref-Distributed_Generation_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Distributed_Generation-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> With long distance power transmission it became possible to interconnect stations to balance loads and improve load factors.</p> 
     <p>In the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Merz" title="Charles Merz" class="mw-redirect">Charles Merz</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Merz_%26_McLellan" title="Merz &amp; McLellan">Merz &amp; McLellan</a> consulting partnership, built the <a href="/wiki/Neptune_Bank_Power_Station" title="Neptune Bank Power Station">Neptune Bank Power Station</a> near <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in 1901,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and by 1912 had developed into the largest integrated power system in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Merz was appointed head of a Parliamentary Committee and his findings led to the Williamson Report of 1918, which in turn created the Electricity Supply Bill of 1919. The bill was the first step towards an integrated electricity system. The Electricity (Supply) Act of 1926 led to the setting up of the National Grid.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Board" title="Central Electricity Board">Central Electricity Board</a> standardised the nation's electricity supply and established the first synchronised AC grid, running at 132 kilovolts and 50 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hertz</a>. This started operating as a national system, the <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_(UK)" title="National Grid (UK)" class="mw-redirect">National Grid</a>, in 1938.</p> 
     <p>In the United States in the 1920s, utilities formed joint-operations to share peak load coverage and backup power. In 1934, with the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act" class="mw-redirect">Public Utility Holding Company Act</a> (USA), electric utilities were recognized as <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a> of importance and were given outlined restrictions and regulatory oversight of their operations. The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_1992" title="Energy Policy Act of 1992">Energy Policy Act of 1992</a> required transmission line owners to allow electric generation companies open access to their network<sup id="cite_ref-Distributed_Generation_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Distributed_Generation-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Electric_Power_Planning_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Power_Planning-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and led to a restructuring of how the electric industry operated in an effort to create competition in power generation. No longer were electric utilities built as vertical monopolies, where generation, transmission and distribution were handled by a single company. Now, the three stages could be split among various companies, in an effort to provide fair accessibility to high voltage transmission.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_Electric_Power_Systems_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Electric_Power_Systems-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:21</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act of 2005</a> allowed incentives and loan guarantees for alternative energy production and advance innovative technologies that avoided greenhouse emissions.</p> 
     <p>In France, <a href="/wiki/Electrification" title="Electrification">electrification</a> began in the 1900s, with 700 <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">communes</a> in 1919, 36528 in 1938. At the same time, the nearby networks began to interconnect: Paris in 1907 at 12kV, the Pyrénées in 1923 at 150 kV, and finally almost all of the country interconnected in 1938 at 220 kV. By 1946, the grid is the world's most dense. That year that state nationalized the industry, by uniting the private companies as <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lectricit%C3%A9_de_France" title="Électricité de France">Électricité de France</a>. The frequency was standardized at 50&nbsp;Hz, and the 225kV network replaces 110 and 120. From 1956, household current is standardized at 220 / 380V, replacing the previous 127/220V. During the 1970s, the 400kV network, the new European standard, is implemented.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_of_distribution_grids">Structure of distribution grids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure of distribution grids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>The structure, or "<a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">topology</a>" of a grid can vary depending on the constraints of budget, requirements for system reliability, and the load and generation characteristics. The physical layout is often forced by what land is available and its geology. Distribution networks are divided into two types, radial or network.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The cheapest and simplest topology for a distribution or transmission grid is a <i>radial</i> structure. This is a <i>tree</i> shape where power from a large supply radiates out into progressively lower voltage lines until the destination homes and businesses are reached.</p> 
     <p>Most transmission grids offer the reliability that more complex <i>mesh networks</i> provide. The expense of mesh topologies restrict their application to transmission and medium voltage distribution grids. Redundancy allows line failures to occur and power is simply rerouted while workmen repair the damaged and deactivated line.</p> 
     <p>Other topologies used are <i>looped</i> systems found in Europe and <i>tied ring</i> networks.</p>

     <p>In cities and towns of North America, the grid tends to follow the classic <i>radially fed</i> design. A substation receives its power from the transmission network, the power is stepped down with a transformer and sent to a <a href="/wiki/Busbar" title="Busbar">bus</a> from which feeders fan out in all directions across the countryside. These feeders carry <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">three-phase power</a>, and tend to follow the major streets near the substation. As the distance from the substation grows, the fanout continues as smaller laterals spread out to cover areas missed by the feeders. This tree-like structure grows outward from the substation, but for reliability reasons, usually contains at least one unused backup connection to a nearby substation. This connection can be enabled in case of an emergency, so that a portion of a substation's service territory can be alternatively fed by another substation.</p>

     <p>Transmission networks are more complex with redundant pathways. For example, see the map of the United States' (right) high-voltage transmission network.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_synchronous_grid" title="Wide area synchronous grid">wide area synchronous grid</a> or "interconnection" is a group of distribution areas all operating with <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) frequencies synchronized (so that peaks occur at the same time). This allows transmission of AC power throughout the area, connecting a large number of electricity generators and consumers and potentially enabling more efficient electricity markets and redundant generation. Interconnection maps are shown of North America (right) and Europe (below left).</p> 
     <p>In a synchronous grid all the generators run not only at the same <a href="/wiki/Utility_frequency" title="Utility frequency">frequency</a> but also at the same <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>, each generator maintained by a local <a href="/wiki/Governor_(device)" title="Governor (device)">governor</a> that regulates the driving torque by controlling the steam supply to the turbine driving it. Generation and consumption must be balanced across the entire grid, because energy is consumed almost instantaneously as it is produced. Energy is stored in the immediate short term by the rotational kinetic energy of the generators.</p> 
     <p>A large failure in one part of the grid - unless quickly compensated for - can cause current to re-route itself to flow from the remaining generators to consumers over transmission lines of insufficient capacity, causing further failures. One downside to a widely connected grid is thus the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">cascading failure</a> and widespread <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outage</a>. A central authority is usually designated to facilitate communication and develop protocols to maintain a stable grid. For example, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Electric_Reliability_Corporation" title="North American Electric Reliability Corporation">North American Electric Reliability Corporation</a> gained binding powers in the United States in 2006, and has advisory powers in the applicable parts of Canada and Mexico. The U.S. government has also designated <a href="/wiki/National_Interest_Electric_Transmission_Corridor" title="National Interest Electric Transmission Corridor">National Interest Electric Transmission Corridors</a>, where it believes transmission bottlenecks have developed.</p> 
     <p>Some areas, for example rural communities in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, do not operate on a large grid, relying instead on local diesel generators.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">High-voltage direct current</a> lines or <a href="/wiki/Variable_frequency_transformer" title="Variable frequency transformer" class="mw-redirect">variable frequency transformers</a> can be used to connect two alternating current interconnection networks which are not synchronized with each other. This provides the benefit of interconnection without the need to synchronize an even wider area. For example, compare the wide area synchronous grid map of Europe (above left) with the map of HVDC lines (below right).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redundancy_and_defining_.22grid.22">Redundancy and defining "grid"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Redundancy and defining &quot;grid&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A town is only said to have achieved <a href="/wiki/Grid_connection" title="Grid connection">grid connection</a> when it is connected to several <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(engineering)" title="Redundancy (engineering)">redundant</a> sources, generally involving long-distance transmission.</p> 
     <p>This redundancy is limited. Existing national or regional grids simply provide the interconnection of facilities to utilize whatever redundancy is available. The exact stage of development at which the supply structure becomes a <i>grid</i> is arbitrary. Similarly, the term <i>national grid</i> is something of an <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronism</a> in many parts of the world, as transmission cables now frequently cross national boundaries. The terms <i><a href="/wiki/Distribution_grid" title="Distribution grid" class="mw-redirect">distribution grid</a></i> for local connections and <i><a href="/wiki/Transmission_grid" title="Transmission grid" class="mw-redirect">transmission grid</a></i> for long-distance transmissions are therefore preferred, but <i>national grid</i> is often still used for the overall structure.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interconnected_Grid">Interconnected Grid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interconnected Grid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Electric utilities across regions are many times interconnected for improved economy and reliability. Interconnections allow for <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, allowing energy to be purchased from large, efficient sources. Utilities can draw power from generator reserves from a different region in order to ensure continuing, reliable power and diversify their loads. Interconnection also allows regions to have access to cheap bulk energy by receiving power from different sources. For example, one region may be producing cheap hydro power during high water seasons, but in low water seasons, another area may be producing cheaper power through wind, allowing both regions to access cheaper energy sources from one another during different times of the year. Neighboring utilities also help others to maintain the overall system frequency and also help manage tie transfers between utility regions.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_Electric_Power_Systems_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Electric_Power_Systems-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aging_Infrastructure">Aging Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aging Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Despite the novel institutional arrangements and network designs of the electrical grid, its power delivery infrastructures suffer aging across the developed world. Four contributing factors to the current state of the electric grid and its consequences are:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>Aging power equipment – older equipment has higher <a href="/wiki/Failure_rate" title="Failure rate">failure rates</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">customer interruption rates</a> affecting the economy and society; also, older assets and facilities lead to higher inspection <a href="/wiki/Maintenance,_repair,_and_operations" title="Maintenance, repair, and operations">maintenance</a> costs and further <a href="/wiki/Maintenance,_repair,_and_operations" title="Maintenance, repair, and operations">repair</a>/<a href="/wiki/Renovation" title="Renovation">restoration</a> costs.</li> 
      <li>Obsolete system layout – older areas require serious additional <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">substation sites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Right-of-way_(transportation)" title="Right-of-way (transportation)">rights-of-way</a> that cannot be obtained in current area and are forced to use existing, insufficient facilities.</li> 
      <li>Outdated engineering – traditional tools for <a href="/wiki/Power_delivery" title="Power delivery" class="mw-redirect">power delivery</a> planning and engineering are ineffective in addressing current problems of aged equipment, obsolete system layouts, and modern deregulated loading levels.</li> 
      <li>Old cultural value – <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/operation" class="extiw" title="wikt:operation">operating</a> of system using concepts and procedures that worked in vertically integrated industry exacerbate the problem under a deregulated industry <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
     </ol> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_trends">Modern trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>As the 21st century progresses, the electric utility industry seeks to take advantage of novel approaches to meet growing energy demand. Utilities are under pressure to evolve their classic topologies to accommodate <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">distributed generation</a>. As generation becomes more common from rooftop solar and wind generators, the differences between distribution and transmission grids will continue to blur. Also, <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">demand response</a> is a grid management technique where retail or wholesale customers are requested either electronically or manually to reduce their load. Currently, transmission grid operators use demand response to request load reduction from major energy users such as industrial plants.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>With everything interconnected, and open competition occurring in a free market economy, it starts to make sense to allow and even encourage <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">distributed generation</a> (DG). Smaller generators, usually not owned by the utility, can be brought on-line to help supply the need for power. The smaller generation facility might be a home-owner with excess power from their solar panel or wind turbine. It might be a small office with a diesel generator. These resources can be brought on-line either at the utility's behest, or by owner of the generation in an effort to sell electricity. Many small generators are allowed to sell electricity back to the grid for the same price they would pay to buy it. Furthermore, numerous efforts are underway to develop a "<a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a>". In the U.S., the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act of 2005</a> and Title XIII of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Independence_and_Security_Act_of_2007" title="Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007">Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007</a> are providing funding to encourage smart grid development. The hope is to enable utilities to better predict their needs, and in some cases involve consumers in some form of time-of-use based tariff. Funds have also been allocated to develop more robust energy control technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Various planned and proposed systems to dramatically increase transmission capacity are known as <a href="/wiki/Super_grid" title="Super grid">super, or mega grids</a>. The promised benefits include enabling the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> industry to sell electricity to distant markets, the ability to increase usage of intermittent energy sources by balancing them across vast geological regions, and the removal of congestion that prevents electricity markets from flourishing. Local opposition to siting new lines and the significant cost of these projects are major obstacles to super grids. One study for a European super grid estimates that as much as 750 GW of extra transmission capacity would be required- capacity that would be accommodated in increments of 5 GW HVDC lines. A recent proposal by Transcanada priced a 1,600-km, 3-GW HVDC line at $3 billion USD and would require a corridor wide. In India, a recent 6 GW, 1,850-km proposal was priced at $790 million and would require a wide right of way. With 750 GW of new HVDC transmission capacity required for a European super grid, the land and money needed for new transmission lines would be considerable.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_trends">Future trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Future trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The electrical grid is expected to evolve to a new grid paradigm: the <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a>, an enhancement of the 20th century electrical grid. The traditional electrical grids are generally used to carry power from a few central generators to a large number of users or customers. In contrast, the new emerging smart grid uses two-way ﬂows of electricity and information to create an automated and distributed advanced energy delivery network.</p> 
     <p>Many research projects have been conducted to explore the concept of smart grid. According to a newest survey on smart grid,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> the research is mainly focused on three systems in smart grid- the infrastructure system, the management system, and the protection system.</p> 
     <p>The infrastructure system is the energy, information, and communication infrastructure underlying of the smart grid that supports 1) advanced <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a>, delivery, and consumption; 2) advanced information metering, monitoring, and management; and 3) advanced communication technologies. In the transition from the conventional power grid to smart grid, we will replace a physical infrastructure with a digital one. The needs and changes present the power industry with one of the biggest challenges it has ever faced.</p> 
     <p>A smart grid would allow the power industry to observe and control parts of the system at higher resolution in time and space.<sup id="cite_ref-Introduction_to_Electric_Power_Systems_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Introduction_to_Electric_Power_Systems-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> It would allow for customers to obtain cheaper, greener, less intrusive, more reliable and higher quality power from the grid. The legacy grid did not allow for real time information to be relayed from the grid, so one of the main purposes of the smart grid would be to allow real time information to be received and sent from and to various parts of the grid to make operation as efficient and seamless as possible. It would allow us to manage logistics of the grid and view consequences that arise from its operation on a time scale with high resolution; from high-frequency switching devices on a microsecond scale, to wind and solar output variations on a minute scale, to the future effects of the carbon emissions generated by power production on a decade scale.</p> 
     <p>The management system is the subsystem in smart grid that provides advanced management and control services. Most of the existing works aim to improve energy efﬁciency, demand proﬁle, utility, cost, and emission, based on the infrastructure by using <a href="/wiki/Optimization" title="Optimization" class="mw-redirect">optimization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>. Within the advanced infrastructure framework of smart grid, more and more new management services and applications are expected to emerge and eventually revolutionize consumers' daily lives.</p> 
     <p>The protection system is the subsystem in smart grid that provides advanced grid reliability analysis, failure protection, and security and privacy protection services. We must note that the advanced infrastructure used in smart grid on one hand empowers us to realize more powerful mechanisms to defend against attacks and handle failures, but on the other hand, opens up many new vulnerabilities. For example, <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> pointed out that the major benefit provided by smart grid, the ability to get richer data to and from customer <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">smart meters</a> and other electric devices, is also its Achilles' heel from a privacy viewpoint. The obvious privacy concern is that the energy use information stored at the meter acts as an information rich side channel. This information could be mined and retrieved by interested parties to reveal personal information such as individual's habits, behaviors, activities, and even beliefs.</p>
