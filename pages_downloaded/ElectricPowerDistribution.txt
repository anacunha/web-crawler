     <p>An <b>electric power distribution system</b> is the final stage in the <a href="/wiki/Power_delivery" title="Power delivery" class="mw-redirect">delivery</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>; it carries electricity from the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission system</a> to individual consumers. Distribution substations connect to the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission system</a> and lower the transmission voltage to medium <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> ranging between 2&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Kilovolt" title="Kilovolt" class="mw-redirect">kV</a> and 35&nbsp;kV with the use of <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>. <i>Primary</i> distribution lines carry this medium voltage power to <a href="/wiki/Distribution_transformer" title="Distribution transformer">distribution transformers</a> located near the customer's premises. Distribution transformers again lower the voltage to the <a href="/wiki/Utilization_voltage" title="Utilization voltage" class="mw-redirect">utilization voltage</a> of household appliances and typically feed several customers through <i>secondary</i> distribution lines at this voltage. Commercial and residential customers are connected to the secondary distribution lines through <a href="/wiki/Service_drop" title="Service drop">service drops</a>. Customers demanding a much larger amount of power may be connected directly to the primary distribution level or the <a href="/wiki/Subtransmission" title="Subtransmission" class="mw-redirect">subtransmission</a> level.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_electric_power_transmission" title="History of electric power transmission">History of electric power transmission</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/220px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png" width="220" height="329" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/330px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png/440px-Brush_Company_arc_light_madison_square_new_york_1882.png 2x" data-file-width="1246" data-file-height="1861"></a>

     <p>Electric power distribution only became necessary in the 1880s when electricity started being generated at <a href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations" class="mw-redirect">power stations</a>. Before that electricity was usually generated where it was used. The first power distribution systems installed in European and US cites were used to supply lighting: <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lighting</a> running on very high voltage (usually higher than 3000 volt) <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) or <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> (DC), and <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp" class="mw-redirect">incandescent lighting</a> running on low voltage (100 volt) direct current.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Both were supplanting <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a> systems, with arc lighting taking over large area/street lighting, and incandescent lighting replacing gas for business and residential lighting.</p> 
     <p>Due to the high voltages used in arc lighting, a single generating station could supply a long string of lights, up to 7-mile (11&nbsp;km) long circuits,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> since the capacity of a wire is proportional to the square of the current traveling on it, each doubling of the voltage would allow the same size cable to transmit the same amount of power four times the distance. Direct current indoor incandescent lighting systems (for example the first Edison <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_Station" title="Pearl Street Station">Pearl Street Station</a> installed in 1882), had difficulty supplying customers more than a mile away due to the low 110 volt system being used throughout the system, from the generators to the final use. The Edison DC system needed thick copper conductor cables, and the generating plants needed to be within about 1.5 miles (2.4&nbsp;km) of the farthest customer to avoid excessively large and expensive conductors.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_the_AC_transformer">Introduction of the AC transformer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Introduction of the AC transformer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Trying to deliver electricity long distance at high voltage and then reducing it to a fractional voltage for indoor lighting became a recognized engineering roadblock to electric power distribution with many, not very satisfactory, solutions tested by lighting companies. The mid-1880s saw a breakthrough with the development of functional AC <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a> that allowed the voltage to be "stepped up" to much higher transmission voltages and then dropped down to a lower end user voltage. With much cheaper transmission costs and the greater <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a> of having large generating plants supply whole cities and regions, the use of AC spread rapidly.</p> 
     <p>In the US the competition between direct current and alternating current took a personal turn in the late 1880s in the form of a "<a href="/wiki/War_of_Currents" title="War of Currents">War of Currents</a>" when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> started attacking <a href="/wiki/George_Westinghouse" title="George Westinghouse">George Westinghouse</a> and his development of the first US AC transformer systems, pointing out all the deaths caused by high voltage AC systems over the years and claiming any AC system was inherently dangerous.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Edison's propaganda campaign was short lived with his company switching over to AC in 1892.</p> 
     <p>AC became the dominant form of transmission of power with innovations in Europe and the US in <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> designs and the development of engineered <i>universal systems</i> allowing the large number of legacy systems to be connected to large AC grids.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas_Parke_Hughes_1930.2C_pages_120-121_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_Parke_Hughes_1930.2C_pages_120-121-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Raghu_Garud_2009.2C_page_249_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raghu_Garud_2009.2C_page_249-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the first half of the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_industry" title="Electric power industry">electric power industry</a> was <a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">vertically integrated</a>, meaning they one company did generation, transmission, distribution, and metering and billing. Starting in the 1970s and 1980s nations began the process of <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Privatisation" title="Privatisation" class="mw-redirect">privatisation</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">electricity markets</a>. The distribution system would remain regulated, but generation, retail, and sometimes transmission systems were transformed into competitive markets.</p>

     <p>Electric power begins at a generating station, where the potential difference can be as high as 13,800&nbsp;volts.<sup id="cite_ref-parcours_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parcours-6">[6]</a></sup> AC is usually used. Users of large amounts of DC power such as some <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">railway electrification systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchanges</a> and industrial processes such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> smelting usually either operate their own or have adjacent dedicated generating equipment, or use rectifiers to derive DC from the public AC supply. However, <a href="/wiki/HVDC" title="HVDC" class="mw-redirect">High-voltage DC</a> can be advantageous for isolating alternating-current systems or controlling the quantity of electricity transmitted. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">Hydro-Québec</a> has a direct-current line which goes from the <a href="/wiki/James_Bay" title="James Bay">James Bay</a> region to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>From the generating station it goes to the generating station’s switchyard where a step-up <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformer</a>&nbsp;increases the voltage to a level suitable for transmission, from 44,000 to 765,000 volts. Once in the transmission system, electricity from each generating station is combined with electricity produced elsewhere. Electricity is consumed as soon as it is produced. It is transmitted at a very high speed, close to the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_overview">Distribution overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>The transition from transmission to distribution happens in a power <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">substation</a>, which has the the following functions:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">Circuit breakers</a> and switches enable the substation to be disconnected from the transmission grid or for distribution lines to be disconnected.</li> 
      <li>Transformers step down transmission voltages, 35kV or more, down to primary distribution voltages. These are medium voltage circuits, usually 600-35,000 V.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>From the transformer, power goes to the <a href="/wiki/Busbar" title="Busbar">busbar</a> that can split the distribution power off in multiple directions. The bus distributes power to distribution lines, which fan out to customers.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Urban distribution is mainly underground, sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Common_utility_duct" title="Common utility duct" class="mw-redirect">common utility ducts</a>. Rural distribution is mostly above ground with <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">utility poles</a>, and suburban distribution is a mix.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> Closer to the customer, a distribution transformer steps the primary distribution power down to a low-voltage secondary circuit, usually 120 or 240V, depending on the region. The power comes to the customer via a <a href="/wiki/Service_drop" title="Service drop">service drop</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Electricity_meter" title="Electricity meter">electricity meter</a>. The final circuit in an urban system may be less than 50 feet, but may be over 300 feet for a rural customer.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_distribution">Primary distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Primary distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Primary distribution voltages are 22kV or 11 kV.<sup id="cite_ref-eolss_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eolss-10">[10]</a></sup> Only large consumers are fed directly from distribution voltages; most utility customers are connected to a transformer, which reduces the distribution voltage to the low voltage used by lighting and interior wiring systems.</p> 
     <p>Voltage varies according to its role in the supply and distribution system. According to international standards, there are initially two voltage groups: low voltage (LV): up to and including 1,000 V AC (or 1,500 V DC) and high voltage (HV): above 1 kV AC (or 1.5 kV DC).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_configurations">Network configurations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Network configurations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright">

     <p>Distribution networks are divided into two types, radial or network.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A radial system is arranged like a tree where each customer has one source of supply. A network system has multiple sources of supply operating in parallel. Spot networks are used for concentrated loads. Radial systems are commonly used in rural or suburban areas.</p> 
     <p>Radial systems usually include emergency connections where the system can be reconfigured in case of problems, such as a fault or required replacement. This can be done by opening and closing switches. It may be acceptable to close a loop for a short time.</p> 
     <p>Long feeders experience <a href="/wiki/Voltage_drop" title="Voltage drop">voltage drop</a> (<a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">power factor</a> distortion) requiring capacitors to be installed.</p> 
     <p>Reconfiguration, by exchanging the functional links between the elements of the system, represents one of the most important measures which can improve the operational performance of a distribution system. The problem of optimization through the reconfiguration of a power distribution system, in terms of its definition, is a historical single objective problem with constraints. Since 1975, when Merlin and Back<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> introduced the idea of distribution system reconfiguration for active power loss reduction, until nowadays, a lot of researchers have proposed diverse methods and algorithms to solve the reconfiguration problem as a single objective problem. Some authors have proposed Pareto optimality based approaches (including active power losses and reliability indices as objectives). For this purpose, different artificial intelligence based methods have been used: microgenetic,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> branch exchange,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> particle swarm optimization<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and non-dominated sorting genetic algorithm.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_services">Rural services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rural services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">Rural electrification</a> systems tend to use higher distribution voltages because of the longer distances covered by distribution lines (see <a href="/wiki/Rural_Electrification_Administration" title="Rural Electrification Administration" class="mw-redirect">Rural Electrification Administration</a>). 7.2, 12.47, 25, and 34.5&nbsp;kV distribution is common in the United States; 11&nbsp;kV and 33&nbsp;kV are common in the UK, Australia and New Zealand; 11&nbsp;kV and 22&nbsp;kV are common in South Africa. Other voltages are occasionally used. Distribution in rural areas may be only single-phase if it is not economical to install three-phase power for relatively few and small customers.</p> 
     <p>Rural services normally try to minimize the number of poles and wires. <a href="/wiki/Single-wire_earth_return" title="Single-wire earth return">Single-wire earth return</a> (SWER) is the least expensive, with one wire. It uses higher voltages (than urban distribution), which in turn permits use of galvanized steel wire. The strong steel wire allows for less expensive wide pole spacing. In rural areas a pole-mount transformer may serve only one customer.</p> 
     <p>Higher voltage split-phase or three phase service, at a higher infrastructure and a higher cost, provide increased equipment efficiency and lower energy cost for large agricultural facilities, petroleum pumping facilities, or water plants.</p> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan, Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Single_wire_earth_return" title="Single wire earth return" class="mw-redirect">single wire earth return</a> systems (SWER) are used to electrify remote rural areas.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_distribution">Secondary distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Secondary distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Electricity is delivered at a frequency of either 50 or 60&nbsp;Hz, depending on the region. It is delivered to domestic customers as <a href="/wiki/Single-phase_electric_power" title="Single-phase electric power">single-phase electric power</a>. Seen in an <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a>, the domestic power supply in North America would look like a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine wave</a>, oscillating between -170 volts and 170 volts, giving an effective voltage of 120 volts.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">Three-phase</a> power is more efficient in terms of power delivered per cable used, and is more suited to running large electric motors. Some large European appliances may be powered by <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">three-phase</a> power, such as electric stoves and clothes dryers.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">ground</a> connection is normally provided for the customer's system as well as for the equipment owned by the utility. The purpose of connecting the customer's system to ground is to limit the voltage that may develop if high voltage conductors fall down onto lower-voltage conductors which are usually mounted lower to the ground, or if a failure occurs within a distribution transformer. <a href="/wiki/Earthing_system" title="Earthing system">Earthing systems</a> can be TT, TN-S, TN-C-S or TN-C.</p>

     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="220-240_volt_systems">220-240 volt systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 220-240 volt systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Most of the world uses 50 Hz single-phase 220 or 230 V residential and light industrial service. In this system, the primary distribution network supplies a few substations per area, and the 230 V power from each substation is directly distributed. A live (hot) wire and neutral are connected to the building from one phase of three phase service. Single-phase distribution is used where motor loads are light. In Europe, electricity is normally distributed for industry and domestic use by the three-phase, four wire system. This gives a three-phase voltage of <span class="nowrap">400 volts</span> wye service and a single-phase voltage of <span class="nowrap">230 volts</span>. For industrial customers, 3-phase <span class="nowrap">690 / 400 volt</span> is also available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Large industrial customers have their own transformers with an input from 10 kV to 220 kV. In the UK a typical urban or suburban low-voltage substation would normally be rated between 315&nbsp;kVA and 1&nbsp;MVA and supply a whole neighborhood.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="110-120_volt_systems">110-120 volt systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_power_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 110-120 volt systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Most of the Americas use 60&nbsp;Hz AC, the 120/240 volt <a href="/wiki/Split_phase" title="Split phase" class="mw-redirect">split phase</a> system domestically and three phase for larger installations. Compared to European systems, North American ones have more step-down transformers near customers. This is because the higher domestic voltage used in Europe (230&nbsp;V vs 120&nbsp;V) may be carried over a greater distance with acceptable power loss.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg/220px-Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg.png" width="220" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg/330px-Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg/440px-Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2480" data-file-height="2600"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Power_Grid_of_Japan.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Japan's utility frequencies are 
        <span class="nowrap">50 Hz</span> and 
        <span class="nowrap">60 Hz</span>
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_sector_in_Japan" title="Electricity sector in Japan">electricity sector in Japan</a>, the standard frequencies for AC are 50 and 60&nbsp;Hz. In Japan parts of the country use 50&nbsp;Hz, while other parts use 60&nbsp;Hz.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> This is a relic of the 1800s. Some local providers in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> imported 50&nbsp;Hz German equipment, while the local power providers in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> brought in 60&nbsp;Hz generators from the United States. The grids grew until eventually the entire country was wired. Today the frequency is 50&nbsp;Hz in Eastern Japan (including&nbsp;Tokyo,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a>,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tohoku</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a>) and 60 Hertz in Western Japan (including&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Nagoya" title="Nagoya">Nagoya</a>,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>,&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Shikokuch%C5%AB%C5%8D" title="Shikokuchūō">Shikoku</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Most household appliances are made to work on either frequency. The problem of incompatibility came into the public eye when the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a> knocked out about a third of the east’s capacity, and power in the west couldn’t be fully shared with the east, since the country does not have a common frequency.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>There are four converter stations that move power across Japan’s AC frequency border. <a href="/wiki/Shin_Shinano" title="Shin Shinano">Shin Shinano</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Back-to-back_connection#Power_transmission" title="Back-to-back connection">back-to-back</a> HVDC facility in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> which forms one of four <a href="/wiki/Frequency_changer" title="Frequency changer">frequency changer</a> stations that link Japan's western and eastern power grids. The other three are at <a href="/wiki/Higashi-Shimizu_Frequency_Converter" title="Higashi-Shimizu Frequency Converter">Higashi-Shimizu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minami-Fukumitsu" title="Minami-Fukumitsu">Minami-Fukumitsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sakuma_Dam#HVDC_frequency_converter" title="Sakuma Dam">Sakuma Dam</a>. Together they can move up to 1.2 GW of power east or west.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
