     <p>A <b>feed-in tariff</b> (<b>FIT</b>, <b>standard offer contract</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-nrel.gov_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel.gov-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>advanced renewable tariff</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <b>renewable energy payments</b><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is a policy mechanism designed to accelerate investment in <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> technologies. It achieves this by offering long-term contracts to renewable energy producers, typically based on the cost of generation of each technology.<sup id="cite_ref-nrel.gov_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel.gov-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Couture.2C_T._2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Couture.2C_T._2010-4">[4]</a></sup> Rather than pay an equal amount for energy, however generated, technologies such as wind power, for instance, are awarded a lower per-kWh price, while technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_PV" title="Solar PV" class="mw-redirect">solar PV</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> are offered a higher price, reflecting costs that are higher at the moment.</p> 
     <p>In addition, feed-in tariffs often include "tariff degression", a mechanism according to which the price (or tariff) ratchets down over time. This is done in order to track<sup id="cite_ref-Couture.2C_T._2010_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Couture.2C_T._2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.25</sup> and encourage technological cost reductions.<sup id="cite_ref-nrel.gov_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel.gov-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:p.100</sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The goal of feed-in tariffs is to offer cost-based compensation to renewable energy producers, providing price certainty and long-term contracts that help finance renewable energy investments.<sup id="cite_ref-Couture.2C_T._2010_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Couture.2C_T._2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>FITs typically include three key provisions:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>guaranteed <a href="/wiki/Grid_access" title="Grid access" class="mw-redirect">grid access</a></li> 
      <li>long-term contracts</li> 
      <li>cost-based purchase prices</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Under a feed-in tariff, eligible <a href="/wiki/Renewable_electricity" title="Renewable electricity" class="mw-redirect">renewable electricity</a> generators, including homeowners, business owners, farmers and private investors, are paid a cost-based price for the renewable electricity they supply to the grid. This enables diverse technologies (wind, solar, biogas, etc.) to be developed and provides investors a reasonable return. This principle was explained in Germany's 2000 RES Act:</p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>The compensation rates...have been determined by means of scientific studies, subject to the provision that the rates identified should make it possible for an installation – when managed efficiently – to be operated cost-effectively, based on the use of <a href="/wiki/State-of-the-art" title="State-of-the-art" class="mw-redirect">state-of-the-art</a> technology and depending on the renewable energy sources naturally available in a given geographical environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_2000_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_2000-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>As a result, the tariff (or rate) may differ by technology, location (e.g. rooftop or ground-mounted for <a href="/wiki/Solar_PV" title="Solar PV" class="mw-redirect">solar PV</a> projects), size (residential or commercial scale) and region.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> The tariffs are typically designed to decline over time to track and encourage technological change.<sup id="cite_ref-Couture.2C_T._2010_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Couture.2C_T._2010-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>FITs typically offer a guaranteed purchase agreement for long (15–25 year) periods.<sup id="cite_ref-nrel.gov_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel.gov-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lipp.2C_J._2007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lipp.2C_J._2007-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Performance-based rates give incentives to producers to maximize the output and efficiency of their project.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, feed-in tariff policies had been enacted in over 50 countries, including Algeria, Australia, Austria, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, China, Cyprus, the Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Iran, Republic of Ireland, Israel, Italy, Kenya, the Republic of Korea, Lithuania, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Portugal, South Africa, Spain, Switzerland, Tanzania, Thailand, Turkey and the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-ren21_2010_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ren21_2010-13">[13]</a></sup> In early 2012 in Spain, the Rajoy administration suspended the Feed-in tariff for new projects.<sup id="cite_ref-ren21_2012_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ren21_2012-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2008, a detailed analysis by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> concluded that "well-adapted feed-in tariff regimes are generally the most efficient and effective support schemes for promoting renewable electricity".<sup id="cite_ref-ec.europa.eu_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ec.europa.eu-15">[15]</a></sup> This conclusion was supported by other analyses, including by the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Federation_for_Renewable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="European Federation for Renewable Energy (page does not exist)">European Federation for Renewable Energy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as well as by <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>A feed in tariff can differentiate on the basis of marginal cost. This is a theoretical alternative which is based on the concept of price differentiation (Finon). Under such a policy the tariff price ranges from some level slightly above the spot rate to the price required to obtain the optimal level of production determined by the government. Firms with lower marginal costs receive prices on the lower end of the spectrum that increase their revenue but not by as much as under the uniform feed in tariff. The more marginal producers face the higher tariff price. This version of the policy has two objectives. The first is to reduce the profitability of certain production cites. Many renewable sources are highly dependent on their location. For example, windmills are most profitable in windy locations, and solar plants are best at sunny locations. This means that generators tend to be concentrated at these most profitable sites. The differentiated tariff seeks to make less naturally productive sites more profitable and so spread out the generators which many consider to be an undesirable good in the area (Finon). Imagine cutting down all the forests to build wind farms; this would not be good for the environment. This however leads to a less cost effective production of renewable electricity as the most efficient sites are being under utilized. The other goal of tariffs differentiated by marginal cost is to reduce the cost of the program (Finon). Under the uniform tariff all producers received the same price which was at times in gross excess of the price needed to incentivize them to produce. The additional revenue translates into profit. Thus, the differentiated tariff attempts to give each producer what it requires to maintain production so that the optimal market quantity of renewable energy production can be reached (Finon).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Overall, and in light of incipient globalization, Feed-in Tariffs are posing increasing problems from the point of view of trade, as their implementation in one country can easily affect industries and policies of others, thus requiring an ideally global coordination of treatment and imposition of such policy instrument, which could be reached at the World Trade Organization.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compensation">Compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>There are three methods of compensation.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Feed-in tariff - compensation is above retail, and as the percentage of adopters increases, the FIT is reduced to the retail rate.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Net_metering" title="Net metering" class="mw-redirect">Net metering</a> - allows producers to consume electricity from the grid, e.g., when the wind stops. Credits typically roll over to future periods. Payments (to the utility or the consumer) depend on net consumption.</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Power_Purchase_Agreement" title="Power Purchase Agreement" class="mw-redirect">Power Purchase Agreement</a> - pays for the generation of electricity and is normally below the retail rate, although in the case of solar can be higher, because solar tends to be produced closer to peak demand.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The first form of feed-in tariff (under another name) was implemented in the US in 1978 under President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>, who signed the <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Act" title="National Energy Act">National Energy Act</a> (NEA). This law included five separate Acts, one of which was the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act" title="Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act">Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act</a> (PURPA). The purpose of the National Energy Act was to encourage <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> and develop new energy resources, including <a href="/wiki/Renewable" title="Renewable" class="mw-redirect">renewables</a> such as wind, solar and geothermal power.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Within PURPA was a provision that required utilities to purchase electricity generated from qualifying independent power producers at rates not to exceed their avoided cost.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup> Avoided costs were designed to reflect the cost that a utility would incur to provide that same electrical generation. Different interpretations of PURPA prevailed in the 1980s: some utilities and state utility commissions interpreted avoided costs narrowly to mean avoided fuel costs, while others chose to define "avoided costs" as the "avoided long-run marginal cost" of generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup> The long-run costs referred to the anticipated cost of electricity in the years ahead. This last approach was adopted by California in its Standard Offer Contract No. 4.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Another provision included in the PURPA law was that utilities were prevented from owning more than 50% of projects, to encourage new entrants.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>To comply with PURPA, some states began offering Standard Offer Contracts to producers. California's Public Utility Commission established a number of Standard Offer Contracts, including Standard Offer No.4 (SO4), which made use of fixed prices, based on the expected long-run cost of generation. The long-run estimates of electricity costs were based on the belief (widely held at the time) that oil and gas prices would continue to increase.<sup id="cite_ref-boell.org_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boell.org-25">[25]</a></sup> This led to an escalating schedule of fixed purchase prices, designed to reflect the long-run avoided costs of new electrical generation. By 1992, private power producers had installed approximately 1,700 MW of wind capacity in California, some of which is still in service today. The adoption of PURPA also led to significant renewable energy generation in states such as Florida, and Maine.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>This notwithstanding, PURPA retains negative connotations in the U.S. electricity industry. When oil and gas prices plummeted in the late 1980s, the Standard Offer Contracts that were signed to encourage new renewable energy development seemed high by comparison. As a result, PURPA contracts came to be seen as an expensive burden on electricity ratepayers.<sup id="cite_ref-boell.org_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boell.org-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Another source of opposition to PURPA stemmed from the fact that it was designed to encourage non-utility generation. This was interpreted as a threat by many large utilities, particularly monopolistic suppliers. As a result of its encouragement of non-utility generation, PURPA has also been interpreted as an important step toward increasing competition.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsh.2C_R._F._1999_pp._60-72-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In 1990, Germany adopted its "Stromeinspeisungsgesetz" (StrEG), or "Law on Feeding Electricity into the Grid".<sup id="cite_ref-Germany.2C_Stromeinspeisungsgesetz_1990_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany.2C_Stromeinspeisungsgesetz_1990-26">[26]</a></sup> The StrEG required utilities to purchase electricity generated from renewable energy suppliers at a percentage of the prevailing retail price of electricity. The percentage offered to solar and wind power was set at 90% of the residential electricity price, while other technologies such as hydro power and biomass sources were offered percentages ranging from 65–80%. A project cap of 5 MW was included.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany.2C_Stromeinspeisungsgesetz_1990_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany.2C_Stromeinspeisungsgesetz_1990-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>While Germany's StrEG was insufficient to encourage costlier technologies such as photovoltaics, it proved relatively effective at encouraging lower-cost technologies such as wind, leading to the deployment of 4,400 MW of new wind capacity between 1991 and 1999, representing approximately one third of the global capacity at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_2000_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_2000-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>An additional challenge that StrEG addressed was the right to interconnect to the grid. The StrEG guaranteed renewable electricity producers grid access.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_2000_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> Similar percentage-based feed-in laws were adopted in Spain,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> as well as in Denmark<sup id="cite_ref-Munksgaard.2C_J_2008_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munksgaard.2C_J_2008-28">[28]</a></sup> in the 1990s.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany.27s_Renewable_Energy_Sources_Act">Germany's Renewable Energy Sources Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Germany's Renewable Energy Sources Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Germany's Feed-in Law underwent a major restructuring in 2000, re-framed as the Act on Granting Priority to Renewable Energy Sources ("Erneuerbare Energien Gesetz", <a href="/wiki/German_Renewable_Energy_Act" title="German Renewable Energy Act">German Renewable Energy Act</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_2000_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> In its new form, it proved to be the world's most effective policy framework at accelerating the renewable deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsson.2C_S._2006_pp._256_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsson.2C_S._2006_pp._256-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Important changes included:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>Purchase prices were based on generation cost. This led to different prices for wind power, solar power, biomass/biogas and geothermal and for projects of different sizes.</li> 
      <li>Purchase guarantees were extended to 20 years.</li> 
      <li>Utilities were allowed to participate.</li> 
      <li>Rates were designed to decline annually based on expected cost reductions, known as "tariff degression".<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobsson.2C_S._2006_pp._256_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobsson.2C_S._2006_pp._256-29">[29]</a></sup></li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>Since it was the most successful, the German policy (amended in 2004 and 2008) often was the benchmark against which other feed-in tariff policies were considered.</p> 
     <p>Other countries followed the German approach. Long-term contracts are typically offered in a non-discriminatory manner to all renewable energy producers. Because purchase prices are based on costs, efficiently operated projects yield a reasonable rate of return.<sup id="cite_ref-Lipp.2C_J._2007_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lipp.2C_J._2007-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-feed-in-cooperation.org_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feed-in-cooperation.org-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>This principle was stated as:</p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>"The compensation rates...have been determined by means of scientific studies, subject to the proviso that the rates identified should make it possible for an installation – when managed efficiently – to be operated cost-effectively, based on the use of state-of-the-art technology and depending on the renewable energy sources naturally available in a given geographical environment."<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_2000_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_2000-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>— 2000 RES Act</cite>
      </div> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>Feed-in tariff policies typically target a 5–10% return.</p> 
     <p>Feed-in tariffs (REFIT) supported growth in solar power in Spain, Germany and wind power in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The success of photovoltaics in Germany resulted in an electricity price drop of up to 40% during peak output times,<sup id="cite_ref-Parkinson27032012_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkinson27032012-32">[32]</a></sup> with savings between €520 million and 840 million for consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-Parkinson13022012_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkinson13022012-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Parkinson27022012_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkinson27022012-34">[34]</a></sup> Savings for consumers have meant conversely reductions in the profit margin of big <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric power companies</a>, who reacted by lobbying the German government, which reduced subsidies in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Parkinson27022012_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkinson27022012-34">[34]</a></sup> Energy utilities lobbied for the abolition, or against the introduction, of feed-in tariffs in other parts of the world, including Australia and California.<sup id="cite_ref-Parkinson27032012_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkinson27032012-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Increase in the solar energy share in Germany also had the effect of closing gas- and coal-fired generation plants.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_electricity_rates">Effects on electricity rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects on electricity rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>FiTs have had both increased and decreased effects on electricity prices.<sup id="cite_ref-PURC_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PURC-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Increases have been attributed to the fact that renewable energy is typically more expensive than electricity generated from conventional sources.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Increases of approximately four Euros per month per household were recorded in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> However, renewable energy can reduce spot market prices via the <a href="/wiki/Merit_order" title="Merit order">merit order effect</a>, the practice of using higher-cost fossil fuel facilities only when demand exceeds the capacity of lower cost facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Sensfuss.2C_F._2009_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sensfuss.2C_F._2009-40">[40]</a></sup> This has led to electricity price reductions in Spain,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Denmark<sup id="cite_ref-Munksgaard.2C_J_2008_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munksgaard.2C_J_2008-28">[28]</a></sup> and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-Sensfuss.2C_F._2009_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sensfuss.2C_F._2009-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_parity">Grid parity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grid parity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Grid parity occurs when the cost of an alternative technology for electricity production matches the existing average for the area. Parity can vary both in time (i.e. during the course of the day and over the course of years) and in space (i.e. geographically). The price of electricity from the grid varies widely from high-cost areas such as Hawaii and California, to lower-cost areas such as Wyoming and Idaho.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In areas with time-of-day pricing, rates vary over the course of the day, rising during high-demand hours (e.g. 11 AM–8 PM) and declining during low-demand hours.</p> 
     <p>In some areas wind power, <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill gas</a> and biomass generation are already lower-cost than grid electricity. Parity has already been achieved in areas that use feed-in tariffs. For example, generation cost from landfill gas systems in Germany are currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> lower than the average electricity spot market price<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In remote areas, electricity from solar photovoltaics can be cheaper than building new distribution lines to connect to the transmission grid.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_alternatives_and_complements">Policy alternatives and complements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Policy alternatives and complements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Renewable_Portfolio_Standard" title="Renewable Portfolio Standard" class="mw-redirect">Renewable Portfolio Standards</a> (RPS) and subsidies create protected markets for renewable energy. RPS require utilities to obtain a minimum percentage of their energy from renewable sources. In some states, utilities can purchase <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Certificate_(United_States)" title="Renewable Energy Certificate (United States)">Renewable Energy Certificates</a> (USA), <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Certificate_System" title="Renewable Energy Certificate System">Renewable Energy Certificate System</a> (EU), <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Certificates_Registry" title="Renewable Energy Certificates Registry">Renewable Energy Certificates Registry</a> (AUS) to meet this requirement. These certificates are issued to renewable energy producers based on the amount of energy they feed into the grid. Selling the certificates is another way for the renewable producer to supplement its revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Certificate prices fluctuate based on overall energy demand and competition among renewable producers. If the amount of renewable energy produced exceeds the required amount, certificate prices may crash, as happened with carbon trading in Europe. This can damage the economic viability of the renewable producers.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Quota systems favor large, vertically integrated generators and multinational electric utilities, if only because certificates are generally denominated in units of one megawatt-hour. They are also more difficult to design and implement than an FIT.<sup id="cite_ref-wind-works.org_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wind-works.org-47">[47]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_country">By country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: By country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/PV_financial_incentives" title="PV financial incentives" class="mw-redirect">PV financial incentives</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Feed-in tariff laws were in place in 46 jurisdictions globally by 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Information about solar tariffs may be found in a consolidated form, however not all of the countries are listed in this source.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algeria">Algeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Algeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>To cover the additional costs of producing electricity from renewables and for the costs of diversification, producers of electricity from renewables receive a bonus for each kWh produced, marketed or consumed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For electricity generated from solar or radiant heat only, the bonus is 300% of the price per kWh of electricity produced by the market operator defined by Law 02-01 of 22 Dhu El Kaada 1422 corresponding to 5 February 2002 until the minimum contribution of solar energy represents 25% of all primary energy. For electricity generated from facilities using solar thermal systems solar-gas hybrid, the bonus is 200% of the price per kWh.</p> 
     <p>For contributions of solar energy below 25%, said bonus is paid in the following conditions:</p> 
     <table class="wikitable"> 
      <tbody>
       <tr> 
        <th>Solar Share</th> 
        <th>Bonus</th>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariffs_in_Australia" title="Feed-in tariffs in Australia">Feed-in tariffs in Australia</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Feed-in tariffs were introduced in 2008 in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, 2009 in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and 2010 in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> offers only local feed-in tariff schemes. A uniform federal scheme to supersede all State schemes was proposed by Tasmanian Greens Senator <a href="/wiki/Christine_Milne" title="Christine Milne">Christine Milne</a>, but not enacted.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> By mid-2011, Feed-in tariff in NSW and ACT had been closed to new generators, as the installed capacity cap had been reached. In NSW, both the Feed-in tariff and the cap were cut, due to the overly generous original settings.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The new conservative Victorian government replaced the original Feed-in tariff with a less generous transitional Feed-in tariff of 25 cents per kilowatt-hour for any power generated excess to the generator's usage, pending the outcome of an inquiry by the Victorian Competition and Efficiency Commission. This does not meet the normal definition and has been referred to as a "fake feed-in tariff". It is actually net metering with a payment for any kilowatt credit, instead of the normal roll over.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Green_Energy_Act_2009" title="Green Energy Act 2009">Green Energy Act 2009</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/WindShare" title="WindShare">WindShare</a>
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> introduced a feed-in tariff in 2006, revised in 2009<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and 2010, increasing from 42¢/kWh to 80.2¢/kWh for micro-scale (≤10&nbsp;kW) grid-tied photovoltaic projects,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group-55">[55]</a></sup> and decreasing to 64.2¢/kWh for applications received after 2 July 2010. Applications received prior to then had until 31 May 2011 to install the system to receive the higher rate.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Ontario's FiT program includes a tariff schedule for larger projects up to and including 10MW solar farms at a reduced rate. As of April 2010, several hundred projects have been approved, including 184 large scale projects, worth $8&nbsp;billion.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> By April 2012, 12,000 systems had been installed and the rate decreased to 54.9¢/kWh, for applications received after 1 September 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The price schedule as 2013 revised solar prices down to 28-38¢/kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>As of August 2011 a national solar tariff was issued at about US$0.15 per kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>China set a tariff for new onshore wind power plants in a move to help struggling project operators to realise profits. The National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC), the country's economic planning agency, announced four categories of onshore wind projects, which according to region will be able to apply for the tariffs. Areas with better wind resources will have lower tariffs, while those with lower outputs will be able to access more generous tariffs.</p> 
     <p>The tariffs are set at 0.51 yuan (US 0.075, GBP 0.05), 0.54 yuan, 0.58 yuan and 0.61 yuan. These represent a significant premium on the average rate of 0.34 yuan per kilowatt-hour paid to coal-fired electricity generators.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> introduced a tariff with law no. 180/2005 in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The tariff is guaranted for 15–30 years (depending on source). Supported sources are small hydropower (up to 10 MW), biomass, biogas, wind and photovoltaics. As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the highest tariff is 12.25 <a href="/wiki/CZK" title="CZK" class="mw-redirect">CZK</a>/kWh for small photovoltaic.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2010 over 1200 MW of photovoltaics were installed, but at the end of the year the FiT was eliminated for larger systems, and reduced by 50% for smaller systems. In 2011, no photovoltaic systems were installed.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>On the 20th of September 2014, The Ministry of Electricity announced the new feed-in tariff pricing for electricity generated by new and renewable energy sources for households and private sector companies. The energy tariff has been divided into five categories so that the price per kilowatt for households is EGP 0.84, but for 200 kilowatts of usage, the price rises to EGP 0.911. The third category, between 200 to 500 kilowatts, will be charged EGP 0.973, while the fourth, ranging from 500 kilowatts to 20 megawatts, will be charged EGP 0.973 per kilowatt per hour. The last category, which stretches between 20-50MW, will be charged EGP 1.02 per kilowatt per hour.Institutions that use over 50MW will be charged under a different system. The sale price for power generated from wind is calculated by the number of operating hours, and from 2500 hours up through 3000 hours, the rate is EGP 0.828.The rate will be set at EGP 0.684 for 3000–4000 hours.</p> 
     <p>The government will purchase the electricity generated by investors, taking inflation into account, while consumption will be paid in local currency and depreciation rates reviewed after two years. The Ministry of Finance will provide concessional subsidised bank financing for households and institutions using less than 200 KW at a rate of 4%, and 8% for 200-500KW. The government is preparing a law that would allow for state-owned lands to be made available for new energy production projects under a usufruct system in exchange for 2% of the energy produced. The electricity companies will be obligated to purchase and transport the energy. The new tariff system also includes a reduction in customs on new and renewable energy production supplies by 2% while the proportion of bank financing has been set at 40-60%. The government hopes for new and renewable energy to account for 20% Egypt’s total energy mix by 2020.</p> 
     <p>&lt;<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.dailynewsegypt.com/2014/09/20/electricity-ministry-sets-new-energy-feed-tariffs/">http://www.dailynewsegypt.com/2014/09/20/electricity-ministry-sets-new-energy-feed-tariffs/</a>&gt;</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The administrative procedure for ground-mounted PV systems was significantly modified in late 2009. The distinction between segments was essentially based on capacity, which determines the complexity of the administrative process. From 4 March to 30 September 2011, the tariff was attractive for installations only less than 100 kWp in size. Above 100 kWp, the FiT was 0,117 €/kWh, cutting into profits. A call for tenders for PV projects above 250 kWp was launched on 15 September 2011. The projects were to be analysed on multiple criteria, including the tariff rate requested by the applicant.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariffs_in_Germany" title="Feed-in tariffs in Germany">Feed-in tariffs in Germany</a>

     <p>First introduced in 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz" title="Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz" class="mw-redirect">Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz</a> (EEG) law is reviewed on a regular basis. Its predecessor was the 1991 "Stromeinspeisegesetz". As of May 2008, the cost of the program added about €1.01 (USD1.69) to each monthly residential electric bill.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In 2012 the costs have risen to €0.03592/kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The impact to consumers by 2013 was over 200 euros a year, and the phrase 'energy poverty' became common.</p> 
     <p>Tariff rates for PV electricity vary depending on system size and location. In 2009, tariffs were raised for electricity immediately consumed rather than supplied to the grid with increasing returns if more than 30% of overall production is consumed on-site. This is to incentivise demand-side management and help develop solutions to the intermittency of solar power.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Tariff duration is usually 20 calendar years plus the year of installation. Systems receive the tariff in effect at the time of installation for the entire period.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>India inaugurated its latest solar power program to date on 9 January 2010. The <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru_National_Solar_Mission" title="Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission">Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission</a> (JNNSM) was officially announced by Prime Minister of India on 12 January 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> This program aimed to install 20,000 MW of solar power by 2022. The first phase of this program targeted 1000 MW, by paying a tariff fixed by the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Regulatory_Commission" title="Central Electricity Regulatory Commission">(CERC)</a> of India. While in spirit this is a feed in tariff, several conditions affect project size and commissioning date. Th tariff for solar PV projects is fixed at Rs. 17.90 (USD 0.397)/kWh. Tariff for solar thermal projects is fixed Rs. 15.40 (USD 0.342/kWh). Tariff will be reviewed periodically by the CERC. In 2015, the feed in tariff is about Rs. 7.50 (USD 0.125)/kWh and is mostly applicable at utility level. The feed-in tariff for roof top PV plants is still not applicable.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Indonesian government, operating mainly through the <a href="/wiki/Perusahaan_Listrik_Negara" title="Perusahaan Listrik Negara">State Electricity Corporation</a> (<i>Perusahaan Listrik Negara, or PLN</i>), encouraged independent power producers (IPPs) to invest in the electric power sector.      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The scheme was put in place following extensive lobbying by industrial representative bodies such as the Irish BioEnergy Association and the Micro Energy Generation Association.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

No other Micro-scale Feed-In tariffs are available.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Homeowners with grid connected micro-generation systems are charged a €9.45 per billing cycle "low-usage surcharge" for importing less than 2kWh per day or being a net exporter of energy in a billing period.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>South Africa's National Energy Regulator (NERSA) announced 31 March 2009 a system of feed-in tariffs designed to produce 10 tw-h of electricity per year by 2013. The tariffs were substantially higher than those in NERSA's original proposal. The tariffs, differentiated by technology, were to be paid for 20 years.</p> 
     <p>NERSA said in its release that the tariffs were based on the cost of generation plus a reasonable profit. The tariffs for wind energy and concentrating solar power were among the most attractive worldwide.</p> 
     <p>The tariff for wind energy, 1.25 ZAR/kWh (€0.104/kWh) was greater than that offered in Germany and more than proposed in Ontario, Canada.</p> 
     <p>The tariff for concentrating solar, 2.10 ZAR/kWh, was less than that in Spain. NERSA's revised program followed extensive public consultation.</p> 
     <p>Stefan Gsänger, Secretary General of the World Wind Energy Association said, "South Africa is the first African country to introduce a feed-in tariff for wind energy. Many small and big investors will now be able to contribute to the take-off of the wind industry in the country. Such decentralised investment will enable South Africa to overcome its current energy crisis. It will also help many South African communities to invest in wind farms and generate electricity, new jobs and new income. We are especially pleased as this decision comes shortly after the first North American feed-in law has been proposed by the Government of the Canadian Province of Ontario".<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>However, the tariff was abandoned before it began in favor of a competitive bidding process launched on 3 August 2011. Under this bidding process, the South African government planned to procure 3,750MW of renewable energy: 1,850MW of onshore wind, 1,450MW of solar PV, 200MW of CSP, 75MW of small hydro, 25MW of landfill gas, 12.5MW of biogas, 12.5MW of biomass and 100MW of small projects. The bidding process comprised two steps:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Qualification phase. Projects are assessed based on structure of the project, legal, land acquisition and use, financial, environmental consent, technical, economic development and bid guarantee</li> 
      <li>Evaluation phase. Compliant bids are then evaluated based on: (1) price relative to a ceiling provided in bid documentation, accounting for 70% of the decision, and (2) economic development, accounting for 30% of the decision.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The first round of bids was due on 4 November 2011. PPA's were expected to be in place by June 2012. Projects should be commissioned by June 2014, except CSP projects expected by June 2015.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Switzerland introduced the so-called "Cost-covering remuneration for feed-in to the electricity grid (CRF)"<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> on 1 May 2008.</p> 
     <p>CRF applies to hydropower (up to 10 megawatts), photovoltaics, wind energy, geothermal energy, biomass and waste material from biomass and will be applicable for 20 and 25 years, depending on the technology. The implementation is done through the national grid operator SWISSGRID.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>While high by appearance, CRF has had little effect, as the total amount of "extra" cost to the system was capped. Since about 2009, no more projects could be financed. About 15'000 projects awaited allocation of monies. If all those projects were implemented, Switzerland could mothball all its nuclear power plants, which currently supply 40% of its power.</p> 
     <p>In 2011, after Fukushima, some local power companies, mostly owned by villages and cantons/provinces, selectively started offering their own tariff, thereby creating a mini-boom.</p> 
     <p>As of March 2012 the KEV-FIT for Solar PV had been lowered several times to CHF 0.30-0.40/kWh (USD 0.33-0.44/kWh) depending on size, but was higher than in Germany and most of the rest of the world.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In 2006, the Thai government enacted a tariff paid on top of utility avoided costs, differentiated by technology type and generator size and guaranteed for 7–10 years. Solar received the highest amount, 8 baht/kWh (about US cents 27/kWh). Large biomass projects received the lowest at 0.3 baht/kWh (at about 1 US cent per kWh). Additional per-kWh subsidies were provided for projects that offset diesel use in remote areas.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> As of 2010 March 1364 MW of private sector renewable energy was online with an additional 4104 MW in the pipeline with signed PPAs. Biomass made up the bulk of this capacity: 1292 MW (online) and 2119 MW (PPA only). Solar electricity was second but growing more rapidly, with 78 MW online and signed PPAs for an additional 1759 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Ukraine introduced the law 'On feed-in tariff' on 25 September 2008. The law guaranteed grid access for renewable energy producers (small hydro up to 10 MW, wind, biomass, photovoltaic and geothermal). The tariffs for renewable power producers are set by the national regulator.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> As of February 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the following tariffs per kWh were applied: biomass – UAH 1.3446 (EUR 0.13), wind – UAH 1.2277 (EUR 0.12), small hydro – UAH 0.8418 (EUR 0.08), solar - UAH 5.0509 (EUR 0.48). In case of significant fluctuations of the national currency against Euro the feed-in tariff adjusts.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariffs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Feed-in tariffs in the United Kingdom">Feed-in tariffs in the United Kingdom</a>
     </div> 
     <p>In October 2008 the United Kingdom announced that Britain would implement a scheme by 2010, in addition to its current renewable energy quota scheme (<a href="/wiki/Renewable_Obligation_Certificates" title="Renewable Obligation Certificates" class="mw-redirect">ROCS</a>). In July 2009 Britain's then-Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a>, presented details of the scheme, which began in early April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-renewableenergyworld.com_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renewableenergyworld.com-102">[102]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Less than a year into the scheme, in March 2011 the new <a href="/wiki/First_Cameron_ministry" title="First Cameron ministry">coalition government</a> announced that support for large-scale <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect">photovoltaic</a> installations (greater than 50&nbsp;kW) would be cut.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> This was in response to European speculators lining up to establish huge solar farms in the West Country that would have absorbed disproportionate amounts of the fund.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>On 9 June 2011, DECC<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> confirmed tariff cuts for solar PV systems above 50 KW after 1 August 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Many<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> were disappointed with DECC's decision.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> It was believed that the total subsidies for solar PV industry were unchanged, but that tariffs for large systems would be cut to benefit smaller systems. The fast track review was based on the long term plan to reach an annual installation of 1.9GW in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In October 2011 DECC announced dramatic cuts of around 55% to tariff rates, with additional reductions for community or group schemes. The cuts were to be effective from 12 December 2011, with a consultation exercise to end on 23 December 2011. This was successfully challenged in the high court by an application for judicial review, jointly made by environmental pressure group <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a> (FoE) and two solar companies - <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solarcentury&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solarcentury (page does not exist)">Solarcentury</a> and HomeSun. The judgment, made by Mr Justice Mitting after a two-day court hearing, was hailed as a major victory by green campaigners and the solar industry. Lawyers for the Department of Energy and Climate Change immediately moved to appeal the ruling. The appeal was unanimously rejected by the Supreme Court, allowing anyone who installed their systems before 3 March 2012 to receive the higher rate of 43.3 p/kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The 30.7 p/kWh rate was available for solar systems up to 5 MW, and consequently no larger systems were built.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Feed-In-Tariff Payments are Tax-Free in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-H2-ecO_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-ecO-112">[112]</a></sup></p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_the_United_States" title="Renewable energy in the United States">Renewable energy in the United States</a>
     </div> 
     <p>In April 2009, 11 <a href="/wiki/State_legislature_(United_States)" title="State legislature (United States)">state legislatures</a> were considering adopting a FiT as a complement to their renewable electricity mandates.<sup id="cite_ref-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group-55">[55]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="California">California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/California_Public_Utilities_Commission" title="California Public Utilities Commission">California Public Utilities Commission</a> (CPUC) approved a feed-in tariff on 31 January 2008 effective immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2010, Marin Energy Authority launched the first Community Choice Aggregate Feed-in Tariff program. The program was updated in November 2012, and now offers 20-year fixed-price contracts, with prices varying by energy source (peak, base-load, intermittent) and progress towards the current program cap of 10-MW.</p> 
     <p>Municipal utility companies enacted feed in tariff pilot programs in Palo Alto and Los Angeles: Palo Alto CLEAN (Clean Local Energy Accessible Now) is a program to purchase up to 4MW of electricity generated by solar electric systems located in CPAU's service territory. In 2012 the minimum project size was 100&nbsp;kW. Rates of purchase are between 12.360 ¢/kWh to 14.003 ¢/kWh depending on the length of the contract. The City began accepting applications on 2 April 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>On 17 April 2012, Los Angeles Department of Water and Power's Board of Water and Power Commissioners approved a 10MW FiT Demonstration Program.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>As of 1 January 2010 state laws allowed homeowners to sell excess power to the utility. Previously the homeowner would get no credit for over-production over the course of the year. In order to get the <a href="/wiki/California_Solar_Initiative" title="California Solar Initiative">California Solar Initiative</a> (CSI) rebate the customer was not allowed to install a system that deliberately over-produces thereby, encouraging efficiency measures to be installed after solar installation. This over-production credit was not available to certain municipal utility customers namely Los Angeles Water and Power.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In February 2009, city commissioners in <a href="/wiki/Gainesville,_Florida" title="Gainesville, Florida">Gainesville</a>, Florida, approved the nation's first solar feed-in tariff.<sup id="cite_ref-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transatlantic_Climate_Policy_Group-55">[55]</a></sup> The program was capped at 4 MW per year. As of 2011, Gainesville had increased solar generated electricity from 328&nbsp;kW to 7,391&nbsp;kW, approximately 1.2% of peak load energy (610&nbsp;MW).<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> The program was suspended in 2014 after more than 18 MW of capacity had been installed.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hawaii">Hawaii</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Hawaii">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In September 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Public_Utilities_Commission" title="Hawaii Public Utilities Commission">Hawaii Public Utilities Commission</a> required <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Electric_Company" title="Hawaiian Electric Company" class="mw-redirect">Hawaiian Electric Company</a> (HECO &amp; MECO &amp; HELCO) to pay above-market prices for renewable energy fed into the electric grid. The policy offers projects a set price and standard 20-year contract. The PUC planned to review the initial feed-in tariff two years after the program started and every three years thereafter.</p>

     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maine">Maine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Maine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In 2009 a "Feed-In" Tariff bill failed to pass.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> In June 2009 a pilot program was initiated however, and was available for projects up to 10MW in size.<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> On April 24, 2013, the Maine Utility and Energy Committee was to consider a new bill: LD1085 "An Act to Establish the Renewable Energy Feed-in Tariff".<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York">New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Power_Authority" title="Long Island Power Authority">Long Island Power Authority</a> (LIPA) adopted a feed-in tariff on 16 July 2012, for systems from 50&nbsp;kW (AC) to 20 MW (AC), and was limited to 50 MW (AC). As customers cannot use their own electricity, it is actually a 20-year fixed rate power purchase agreement and LIPA retains the SRECs. The 2012 NY legislature failed to pass legislation which would have opened a New York market for SRECs starting in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> Payment is 22.5¢/kWh,<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> less than what LIPA paid for peak generation at various times.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> At an estimated avoided cost of $0.075/kWh, the program added about $0.44/month to the average household electric bill.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oregon">Oregon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Oregon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>In June 2009, Oregon established a pilot solar volumetric incentive rate and payment program. Under this incentive program, systems are paid for the kilowatt-hours (kWh) generated over a 15-year period, at a rate set at the time a system is enrolled in the program. The Oregon Public Utility Commission (PUC) established rates and rules in May 2010. This program was offered by the three investor-owned utilities in Oregon and administered by the utilities. The PUC planned to periodically re-evaluate rates. Program costs were recoverable in utility rates and utility-owned systems were not eligible for the incentive.</p> 
     <p>The pilot program installation cap was limited to an aggregate cap of 25 megawatts (MW) of solar photovoltaics (PV), with a maximum system size cap of 500 kilowatts (kW). The aggregate program cap was to be spread equally over four years, with 6.25 MW of capacity being eligible to receive the incentive each year. The aggregate cap was divided, based on 2008 retail sales revenue. PGE had a cap of 14.9 MW, Pacific Power 9.8 MW, and Idaho Power 0.4 MW. Idaho Power's program was limited to residential installations. Rates differed by system size and geographic zone. Small- and medium-scale systems participated in a program modeled after net metering. Larger-scale systems were competitivelly bid. Participating PV systems must be grid-connected, metered and meet all applicable codes and regulations. Systems must be "permanently installed".</p> 
     <p>Systems sized 100&nbsp;kW or less could participate based on net metering. Generating capacity of 20 MW of the aggregate cap was reserved for the net metering portion, with 12 MW available for residential and 8 MW available for small commercial systems. These residential and small commercial systems were paid for the amount of electricity generated, up to the amount of electricity consumed. In essence, customers were paid for the amount of utility electric load consumption that is offset by onsite generation. Unlike typical feed-in tariffs, customers can consume the electricity generated on-site and receive a production incentive – or a volumetric incentive payment – for the amount of electricity generated and consumed. To remove a <a href="/wiki/Perverse_incentive" title="Perverse incentive">perverse incentive</a> to increase electricity consumption to receive a greater payment, the system had to be appropriately sized to meet average electricity consumption. Rates were determined by the PUC based on annual system cost and annual energy output, differentiated by geographic zones. The cost estimates were based on installation data from <a href="/wiki/Energy_Trust_of_Oregon" title="Energy Trust of Oregon">Energy Trust of Oregon</a>. The actual rates paid to the customer-generator were the volumetric incentive rate minus the retail rate. The volumetric incentive rates were to be re-evaluated every six months. The rates for the performance-based incentive program ranged from $0.25/kWh to $0.411/kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vermont">Vermont</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Vermont">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Vermont adopted feed-in tariffs on 27 May 2009 as part of the Vermont Energy Act of 2009. Generators must possess a capacity of no more than 2.2 MW, and participation is limited to 50 MW in 2012, a limit that increased by 5 to 10 MW/year to a total of 127.5 MW in 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> Payments were 24¢/kWh for solar, which was increased to 27.1¢/kWh in March 2012, and 11.8¢/kWh for wind over 100&nbsp;kW and 25.3¢/kWh for wind turbines up to 100&nbsp;kW. Other qualifying technologies included methane, hydro and biomass.<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> Vermont's SPEED program called for 20% renewable energy by 2017 and 75% by 2032. The program was fully subscribed in 2012. Payments are for 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feed-in_tariff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The territory operated a net metering program that paid the energy fed back to the grid at the retail rate. The rate varied monthly around 23 cents per kilowatt. The program credited the provider's account each month rather than making actual payments. At the end of the fiscal year (June) any excess was paid at a fixed 10 cents per KW of which 25% was retained for public schools. To participate in the program insurance and means for disconnecting the system accessible outside of the building and specific brands of equipment dictated by the government were required.</p>
