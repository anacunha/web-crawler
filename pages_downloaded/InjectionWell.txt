   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Injection well</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p>An <b>injection well</b> is a device that places fluid deep underground into porous rock formations, such as sandstone or limestone, or into or below the shallow soil layer. The fluid may be water, wastewater, brine (salt water), or water mixed with chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) defines an injection well as "a bored, drilled, or driven shaft, or a dug hole that is deeper than it is wide, or an improved sinkhole, or a subsurface fluid distribution system".<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup> Well construction depends on the injection fluid injected and depth of the injection zone. Deep wells that are designed to inject <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_wastes" title="Hazardous wastes" class="mw-redirect">hazardous wastes</a> or carbon dioxide deep below the Earth's surface have multiple layers of protective casing and cement, whereas shallow wells injecting non-hazardous fluids into or above drinking water sources are more simply constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Injection wells are used for many purposes.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_disposal">Waste disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Waste disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Waste_water" title="Waste water" class="mw-redirect">waste water</a> disposal, treated waste water is injected into the ground between <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">impermeable</a> layers of rocks to avoid polluting <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> supplies or adversely affecting quality of receiving waters. Injection wells are usually constructed of solid walled pipe to a deep elevation in order to prevent injectate from mixing with the surrounding environment.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Injection wells are widely considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be the best method for disposal of treated waste water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike outfalls or other direct disposal techniques, injection wells utilize the earth as a filter to further clean the treated wastewater before it reaches the receiving water. This method of waste water disposal also serves to spread the injectate over a wide area, further decreasing environmental impacts.</p> 
     <p>Critics of waste water injection wells<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> cite concerns relating to the injectate polluting receiving waters.<sup id="cite_ref-ProPublica-Poison-Beneath_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ProPublica-Poison-Beneath-2">[2]</a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Environmental_engineering" title="Environmental engineering">environmental engineering</a> professionals,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> however, consider waste water treatment followed by disposal through injection wells to be the most cost effective and environmentally responsible method of waste water treatment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The only known alternatives to injection wells are direct discharge of treated waste water to receiving waters or utilization of the treated water for irrigation. Direct discharge does not disperse the water over a wide area; the environmental impact is focused on a particular segment of a river and its downstream reaches or on a coastal waterbody. Extensive irrigation is often prohibitively expensive and requires ongoing maintenance and large electricity usage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Since the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Maui_County" title="Maui County" class="mw-redirect">Maui County</a>, Hawaii has been engaged in a struggle over the 3-5 million gallons per day of wastewater that it injects below the <a href="/wiki/Lahaina" title="Lahaina" class="mw-redirect">Lahaina</a> sewage treatment plant, over the claim that the water was emerging in seeps that were causing <a href="/wiki/Algae_bloom" title="Algae bloom" class="mw-redirect">algae blooms</a> and other environmental damage. After some twenty years, it was sued by environmental groups after multiple studies showed that more than half the injectate was appearing in nearby coastal waters. The judge in the suit rejected the County's arguments, potentially subjecting it to millions of dollars in federal fines. A 2001 consent decree required the county to obtain a water quality certification from the Hawaii Department Of Health, which it failed to do until 2010, after the suit was filed.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas_production">Oil and gas production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oil and gas production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">Enhanced oil recovery</a> and 
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">Hydraulic fracturing</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Another use of injection wells is in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a href="/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum" title="Extraction of petroleum">production</a>. Steam, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, water, and other substances can be <a href="/wiki/Water_injection_(oil_production)" title="Water injection (oil production)">injected into an oil-producing unit</a> in order to maintain <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir" title="Petroleum reservoir">reservoir</a> pressure, heat the oil or lower its viscosity, allowing it to flow to a producing well nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Class_II_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Class_II-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_site_remediation">Waste site remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Waste site remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Yet another use for injection wells is in environmental remediation, for cleanup of either soil or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> contamination. Injection wells can insert clean water into an <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>, thereby changing the direction and speed of groundwater flow, perhaps towards <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_well&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Extraction well (page does not exist)">extraction wells</a> downgradient, which could then more speedily and efficiently remove the contaminated groundwater. Injection wells can also be used in cleanup of soil contamination, for example by use of an ozonation system. Complex <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect">hydrocarbons</a> and other contaminants trapped in soil and otherwise inaccessible can be broken down by <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, a highly reactive gas, often with greater cost-effectiveness than could be had by digging out the affected area. Such systems are particularly useful in built-up urban environments where digging may be impractical due to overlying buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquifer_recharge">Aquifer recharge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aquifer recharge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Recently the option of refilling natural aquifers with injection or percolation has become more important, particularly in the driest region of the world, the <a href="/wiki/MENA" title="MENA">MENA</a> region (Middle East and North Africa).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Surface runoff can also be recharged into <a href="/wiki/Dry_well" title="Dry well">dry wells</a>, or simply barren wells that have been modified to functions as cisterns.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These hybrid <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> management systems, called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Recharge_well&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Recharge well (page does not exist)">recharge wells</a>, have the advantage of aquifer recharge and instantaneous supply of potable water at the same time. They can utilize existing infrastructure and require very little effort for the modification and operation. The activation can be as simple as inserting a polymer cover (foil) into the well shaft. Vertical pipes for conduction of the overflow to the bottom can enhance performance. The area around the well acts as funnel. If this area is maintained well the water will require little purification before it enters the cistern.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_requirements">Regulatory requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Regulatory requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In the United States, injection well activity is regulated by EPA and state governments under the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a> (SDWA).<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup> EPA has issued Underground Injection Control (UIC) regulations in order to protect drinking water sources.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Regs_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Regs-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>EPA regulations define six classes of injection wells. Class I wells are used for the injection of municipal and industrial wastes beneath underground sources of drinking water. Class II wells are used for the injection of fluids associated with oil and gas production, including waste from hydraulic fracturing. Class III wells are used for the injection of fluids used in mineral <a href="/wiki/Solution_mining" title="Solution mining" class="mw-redirect">solution mining</a> beneath underground sources of drinking water. Class IV wells, like Class I wells, are used for the injection of hazardous wastes but inject waste into or above underground sources of drinking water instead of below. Class V wells are those used for all non-hazardous injections that are not covered by Classes I through IV. Examples of Class V wells include stormwater drainage wells and <a href="/wiki/Septic_drain_field" title="Septic drain field">septic system leach fields</a>. Finally, Class VI wells are used for the injection of carbon dioxide for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestration</a>, or long term storage.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-1">[1]</a></sup> Currently, there are no Class VI wells in operation, but 6 to 10 wells are expected to be in use by 2016.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Injection-induced_earthquakes">Injection-induced earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Injection_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Injection-induced earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png/220px-Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png/330px-Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png/440px-Cumulative_induced_seismicity.png 2x" data-file-width="1040" data-file-height="782"></a>

        </div> Cumulative number of earthquakes in the central U.S. The red cluster at the center of the map shows an area near Oklahoma which experienced the largest increase in activity since 2009.

     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Induced_seismicity#Waste_disposal_wells" title="Induced seismicity">Induced seismicity § Waste disposal wells</a>
     </div> 
     <p>A July 2013 study by US Geological Survey scientist William Ellsworth links earthquakes to wastewater injection sites. In the four years from 2010-2013 the number of earthquakes of magnitude 3.0 or greater in the central and eastern United States increased dramatically. After decades of a steady earthquake rate (average of 21 events/year), activity increased starting in 2001 and peaked at 188 earthquakes in 2011. USGS scientists have found that at some locations the increase in seismicity coincides with the injection of wastewater in deep disposal wells. Injection-induced earthquakes are thought to be caused by pressure changes due to excess fluid injected deep below the surface and are being dubbed “man-made” earthquakes.<sup id="cite_ref-Man-Made_Earthquakes_Update_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Man-Made_Earthquakes_Update-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
