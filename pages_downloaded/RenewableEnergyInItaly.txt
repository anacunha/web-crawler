   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Renewable energy in Italy</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b><a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a></b> primary consumption in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> was 14.6 <a href="/wiki/Tonne_of_oil_equivalent" title="Tonne of oil equivalent">Mtoe</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-legambiente2015_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente2015-1">[1]</a></sup> This number has been growing steadily in the last decade and today accounts for a significant percentage of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Italy" title="Energy in Italy">national energy consumption</a>. In 2014, 38.2% of the national electric energy consumption came from renewable sources (in 2005 this value was 15.4%), covering 16.2% of the total energy consumption of the country (5.3% in 2005).<sup id="cite_ref-legambiente2015_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente2015-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>All of the 8.047 Italian municipalities (<a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">comune</a>) have deployed some source of renewable energy, with <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity_in_Italy" title="Hydroelectricity in Italy">hydroelectric power</a> being the leading renewable energy source in terms of production. <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Italy" title="Solar power in Italy">Solar energy</a> production alone accounted for almost 9% of the total electric consumption in the country in 2014, making Italy the country with the highest contribution from solar energy in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-legambiente2015_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente2015-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The urge to produce exclusively green energy in Italy came from the need to reduce the country's historical heavy dependence on <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels" class="mw-redirect">fossil fuels</a> and supply flows of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect">hydrocarbons</a> coming from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and to comply with the binding international agreements of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> (signed in 1997 and implemented in 2005).</p> 
     <p>During the twentieth century, Italy was at the forefront of technological development and the production of energy from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable sources</a>. In the field of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>, the first plant dates back to 1904, at <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, where in 1913 the first geothermal power plant was also built, and remained the only one in the world until 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> With regards to <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, the first plant in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> was built in Paderno d'Adda in <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a> between 1895 and 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> industry, the first power plant (that could produce steam at 450&nbsp;°C) was built in <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> in 1963, and in 1980 the first <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar power tower</a> that uses mirrors was built at <a href="/wiki/Adrano" title="Adrano">Adrano</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As for the production of <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, the first experimental projects (sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/National_Research_Council_(Italy)" title="National Research Council (Italy)">National Research Council</a> and in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Enel" title="Enel">Enel</a>) were started in the second half of the seventies as part of the restructuring of the entire system of production and supply of energy that followed the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973</a> and <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">1979</a> energy crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>During the eighties and the nineties <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> projects drew new life from three co-occurring factors: the rapid price increase of crude oil (caused by the tension and armed conflicts in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>); a new public awareness of environmentalism (fuelled by the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a> in 1986<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>); and the interruption of all construction of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plants" title="Nuclear power plants" class="mw-redirect">nuclear power plants</a> in Italy and the ban on <a href="/wiki/Enel" title="Enel">Enel</a> on the participating in the construction or management of nuclear power plants beyond national borders (following the <a href="/wiki/Italian_referendums,_1987" title="Italian referendums, 1987">referendum of 1987</a>).</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity">Electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Between 2005 and 2011 a surge in renewable energy investments and production took place in Italy, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Italy" title="Wind power in Italy">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Italy" title="Solar power in Italy">solar</a> energy during the latter years.. Total electricity produced from <a href="/wiki/Solar_PV" title="Solar PV" class="mw-redirect">solar PV</a> quintupled in 2011 compared to the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-gse1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gse1-8">[8]</a></sup> This was mainly due to a drop in costs and to high incentives introduced since 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Italian fossil fuel electricity generation sector underwent a profound crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Many Italian power plants burning fossil fuels were running at half capacity and others were in the process of being shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>By 2011 Renewable electricity accounted for the 24.5% of all electric energy produced in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-gse1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gse1-8">[8]</a></sup> This percentage grew rapidly and reached 38.5% in 2013, when a spike in hydroelectric production contributed with more than 9 TWh.<sup id="cite_ref-qua1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qua1-12">[12]</a></sup> The total gross capacity in 2011 was 41,352 MW and the total energy produced during the year was 84,190 GWh.<sup id="cite_ref-gse1_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gse1-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2013 the renewable electricity produced during the year was 106.5 TWh.<sup id="cite_ref-qua1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qua1-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>During the first quarter of 2014, renewables generated 39,8% of all electric energy produced,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and 49,1% during April.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Electricity production potential from renewables has been rising although hydroelectric figures vary considerably from one year to the next. Including biomass energy production of electricity from renewable sources reached a record of around 44% of total electricity production. In 2015 production from Wind, Photovoltaic and Geothermal sources exceeded Hydroelectric production for the first time.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_targets_and_progress">Current targets and progress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current targets and progress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Italy has a 17 percent target in its total energy use set by the European Union for 2020 and is close to meeting its goal having reached 16.2% of renewable energy consumption in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-legambiente2015_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente2015-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Italy has planned to subsidize electric cars.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Transport accounts for a large amount of fossil fuel use so a quick transition to sustainable transport such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_cars" title="Electric cars" class="mw-redirect">electric cars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tram#Electric_.28trolley_cars.29" title="Tram">trams</a> will be a key element of transition to renewable energy use.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Italy_National_Renewable_Energy_Action_Plan" title="Italy National Renewable Energy Action Plan">Italian National Renewable Energy Action Plan</a> has a target to reach the total share of renewable energies to 26%. 39% in the electricity sector, 17,09% in the heating/cooling sector and 14% in the transport sector by 2020.</p> 
     <p>On June 16, 2013, renewables covered 100% of the entire Italian electricity demand for 2 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
