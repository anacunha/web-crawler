     <p><b>Carnot's theorem</b>, developed in 1824 by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot</a>, also called <b>Carnot's rule</b>, is a principle that specifies limits on the maximum efficiency any <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> can obtain, which thus solely depends on the difference between the hot and cold temperature reservoirs.</p> 
     <p>Carnot's theorem states:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>All heat engines between two heat reservoirs are less efficient than a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">Carnot heat engine</a> operating between the same reservoirs.</li> 
      <li>Every Carnot heat engine between a pair of heat reservoirs is equally efficient, regardless of the working substance employed or the operation details.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The formula for this maximum efficiency is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_{\text{max}} = \eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/1/6/516f2b4734695a8660107ea0f12ae7b1.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <i>T<sub>C</sub></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature" class="mw-redirect">absolute temperature</a> of the cold reservoir, <i>T<sub>H</sub></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature" class="mw-redirect">absolute temperature</a> of the hot reservoir, and the efficiency <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/1/7/7174cbd6aeaaa56e37102b72386bb2b9.png"> is the ratio of the work done by the engine to the heat drawn out of the hot reservoir.</p> 
     <p>Based on modern thermodynamics, Carnot's theorem is a result of the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>. Historically, however, it was based on contemporary <a href="/wiki/Caloric_theory" title="Caloric theory">caloric theory</a> and preceded the establishment of the second law.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof">Proof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/300px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg" width="300" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/450px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/600px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg 2x" data-file-width="632" data-file-height="394"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg" class="internal" title="Agrandir"></a>
        </div> An impossible situation: A heat engine cannot drive a less efficient (reversible) heat engine without violating the second law of thermodynamics.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The proof of the Carnot theorem is a <a href="/wiki/Proof_by_contradiction" title="Proof by contradiction">proof by contradiction</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Reductio_ad_absurdum" title="Reductio ad absurdum">reductio ad absurdum</a>, as illustrated by the figure showing two heat engines operating between two reservoirs of different temperature. The heat engine with more efficiency (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/1/271b9c8fd686815a431801135e5d1005.png">) is driving a heat engine with less efficiency (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/5/f/95fdc76a137b1020fa9bce196b16fa22.png">), causing the latter to act as a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>. This pair of engines receives no outside energy, and operates solely on the energy released when heat is transferred from the hot and into the cold reservoir. However, if <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_M>\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/a/8da08b2aa68bfc700f20083b13b85593.png">, then the net heat flow would be backwards, i.e., into the hot reservoir:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q^\text{out}_\text{hot} = Q < \frac{\eta_M}{\eta_L}Q=Q^\text{in}_\text{hot}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/c/4/7c40cd826f6977cb56d5a2ebfb3cac31.png">.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>It is <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics#Controversies" title="Second law of thermodynamics">generally agreed</a> that this is impossible because it violates the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>.</p> 
     <p>We begin by verifying the values of work and heat flow depicted in the figure. First, we must point out an important caveat: the engine with less efficiency (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/5/f/95fdc76a137b1020fa9bce196b16fa22.png">) is being driven as a heat pump, and therefore must be a <i><a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible</a></i> engine. If the less efficient engine (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/5/f/95fdc76a137b1020fa9bce196b16fa22.png">) is not reversible, then the device could be built, but the expressions for work and heat flow shown in the figure would not be valid.</p> 
     <p>By restricting our discussion to cases where engine (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/5/f/95fdc76a137b1020fa9bce196b16fa22.png">) has less efficiency than engine (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/1/271b9c8fd686815a431801135e5d1005.png">), we are able to simplify notation by adopting the convention that all symbols, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/9/f09564c9ca56850d4cd6b3319e541aee.png"> and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/e/61e9c06ea9a85a5088a499df6458d276.png"> represent <a href="/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)#Terminology_for_signs" title="Sign (mathematics)">non-negative</a> quantities (since the direction of energy flow never changes sign in all cases where <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_L\leqslant\eta_M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/0/f/d0f771546a6ee27a15bb16082533e74d.png">). Conservation of energy demands that for each engine, the energy which enters, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" E_{in} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/c/3cc64c3923ae4dfe4eabba4e22cc7182.png">, must equal the energy which exits, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" E_{out} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/9/c/49c3055bdc2869df628167f6978e9b0e.png">:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E_{in}^{M} = Q = (1-\eta_M)Q + \eta_M Q = E_{out}^{M}  " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/5/4/e545f8664dd3365ad5daf4cb73d3b651.png">,
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E_{in}^{L} = \eta_M Q + \eta_M Q \left(\frac{1}{\eta_{L}}- 1 \right )=\frac{\eta_M}{\eta_L}Q = E_{out}^{L}  " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/a/0/2a0f0e0024584d94ea0c7c1294b8c6eb.png">,
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The figure is also consistent with the definition of <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine#Efficiency" title="Heat engine">efficiency</a> as <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta=W/Q_h" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/1/9/d199664b4def36f7c737afca748294d4.png"> for both engines:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_M= \frac{W_M}{Q^M_h}=\frac{\eta_M Q}{Q}=\eta_M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/5/b/25bdd70c503fdf9f8086500441283136.png">,
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt=" \eta_L=\frac{W_L}{Q^L_h}=\frac{\eta_M Q}{\frac{\eta_M}{\eta_L}Q}=\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/2/3/b237744b66348f17ddd8e3aab148568c.png">.
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>It may seem odd that a hypothetical heat pump with low efficiency is being used to violate the second law of thermodynamics, but the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_merit" title="Figure of merit">figure of merit</a> for refrigerator units is not efficiency, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W/Q_h" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/d/23d39c17d655c15cea5c8a9f4af91f59.png">, but the <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">coefficient of performance</a> (COP),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> which is <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q_c/W" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/3/6/336cffa0ff762d00f537aa5fbdc3d1e3.png">. A reversible heat engine with low thermodynamic efficiency, <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W/Q_h" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/d/23d39c17d655c15cea5c8a9f4af91f59.png"> delivers more heat to the hot reservoir for a given amount of work when it is being driven as a heat pump.</p> 
     <p>Having established that the heat flow values shown in the figure are correct, Carnot's theorem may be proven for irreversible and the reversible heat engines.<sup id="cite_ref-CarnotThoerem_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarnotThoerem-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reversible_engines">Reversible engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reversible engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>To see that every reversible engine operating between reservoirs <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/5/7/a5749ec33f2c95fe8c19d702d76d4968.png"> and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/6/f066e1184caa1b9991cbceb207ea6341.png"> must have the same efficiency, assume that two reversible heat engines have different values of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/1/7/7174cbd6aeaaa56e37102b72386bb2b9.png">, and let the more efficient engine (M) drive the less efficient engine (L) as a heat pump. As the figure shows, this will cause heat to flow from the cold to the hot reservoir without any external work or energy, which violates the second law of thermodynamics. Therefore both (reversible) heat engines have the same efficiency, and we conclude that:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <i>All reversible engines that operate between the same two heat reservoirs have the same efficiency.</i>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>This is an important result because it helps establish the <a href="/wiki/Clausius_theorem" title="Clausius theorem">Clausius theorem</a>, which implies that the change in <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> is unique for all reversible processes.,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Delta S = \int_a^b \frac {dQ_{rev}}T " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/0/2003e75625f537ad22ce1038d33d6e5d.png">,
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>over all paths (from <i><b>a</b></i> to <i><b>b</b></i> in <i>V-T</i> space). If this integral were not path independent, then entropy, <a href="/wiki/S" title="S">S</a>, would lose its status as a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state variable</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversible_engines">Irreversible engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Irreversible engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>If one of the engines is irreversible, it must be the (M) engine, placed so that it reverse drives the less efficient but reversible (L) engine. But if this irreversible engine is more efficient than the reversible engine, (i.e., if <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\eta_M >\eta_L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/a/8da08b2aa68bfc700f20083b13b85593.png">), then the second law of thermodynamics is violated. And, since the Carnot cycle represents a reversible engine, we have the first part of Carnot's theorem:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <i>No irreversible engine is more efficient than the Carnot engine operating between the same two reservoirs.</i>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_thermodynamic_temperature">Definition of thermodynamic temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Definition of thermodynamic temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability_to_fuel_cells_and_batteries">Applicability to fuel cells and batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applicability to fuel cells and batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Since <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> can generate useful power when all components of the system are at the same temperature (<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T=T_H=T_C" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/4/4/34426b3f270b4166496f52283582633f.png">), they are clearly not limited by Carnot's theorem, which states that no power can be generated when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H=T_C" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/5/8853fdf088a2be87da17638702fa30c6.png">. This is because Carnot's theorem applies to engines converting thermal energy to work, whereas fuel cells and batteries instead convert chemical energy to work.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> still provides restrictions on fuel cell and battery energy conversion.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
