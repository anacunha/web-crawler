     <p><b>Biomedical research</b> (or <b>experimental medicine</b>) is in general simply known as <b>medical research</b>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Basic_research" title="Basic research">basic research</a>, <a href="/wiki/Applied_research" title="Applied research">applied research</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Translational_research" title="Translational research">translational research</a> conducted to aid and support the development body of knowledge in the field of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>.</p> 
     <p>An important kind of medical research is <a href="/wiki/Clinical_research" title="Clinical research">clinical research</a>, which is distinguished by the involvement of patients. Other kinds of medical research include pre-clinical research, for example on animals, and basic medical research, for example in genetics.</p> 
     <p>Both clinical and pre-clinical research phases exist in the pharmaceutical industry's drug pipelines, where the clinical phase is denoted by the term <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trial</a>. However, only part of the whole of clinical or pre-clinical research is oriented towards a specific pharmaceutical purpose. The need for understanding, diagnostics, medical devices and non-pharmaceutical therapies means that medical research is much bigger than just trying to make new drugs.</p> 
     <p>The most basic medical research is a rapidly evolving area that owes much to basic biology and is given names such as Human Biosciences by universities.<sup id="cite_ref-www5.plymouth.ac.uk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www5.plymouth.ac.uk-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A new paradigm to biomedical research is being termed <a href="/wiki/Translational_research" title="Translational research">translational research</a>, which focuses on iterative feedback loops between the basic and clinical research domains to accelerate knowledge translation from the bedside to the bench, and back again. Medical research may involve doing research into <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_research" title="Clinical research">clinical research</a>, microbiology, physiology, oncology, <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> and research into many other <a href="/wiki/Non-communicable_disease" title="Non-communicable disease">non-communicable diseases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes" class="mw-redirect">diabetes</a> and cardiovascular diseases.</p> 
     <p>The increased longevity of humans over the past century can be significantly attributed to advances resulting from medical research. Among the major benefits of medical research have been <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a> for <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio" class="mw-redirect">polio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> treatment for <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes" class="mw-redirect">diabetes</a>, classes of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic" class="mw-redirect">antibiotics</a> for treating a host of maladies, medication for high <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a>, improved treatments for <a href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS" class="mw-redirect">AIDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statin" title="Statin">statins</a> and other treatments for <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a>, new surgical techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Microsurgery" title="Microsurgery">microsurgery</a>, and increasingly successful treatments for <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>. New, beneficial tests and treatments are expected as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>. Many challenges remain, however, including the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic_resistance" title="Antibiotic resistance" class="mw-redirect">antibiotic resistance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Obesity_epidemic" title="Obesity epidemic" class="mw-redirect">obesity epidemic</a>.</p> 
     <p>Most of the research in the field is pursued by <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_scientist" title="Biomedical scientist">biomedical scientists</a>, however significant contributions are made by other <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologists</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a>. Medical research, <a href="/wiki/Human_experimentation" title="Human experimentation" class="mw-redirect">done on humans</a>, has to strictly follow the <a href="/wiki/Medical_ethics" title="Medical ethics">medical ethics</a> as sanctioned in the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Helsinki" title="Declaration of Helsinki">Declaration of Helsinki</a> and elsewhere. In all cases, the <a href="/wiki/Research_ethics" title="Research ethics">research ethics</a> has to be respected.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_medical_research">Basic medical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic medical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Areas tackled in the most fundamental parts of medical research include cellular and molecular biology, medical genetics, immunology, neuroscience and psychology. Researchers, mainly in universities or government-funded research institutes, aim to establish an understanding of the cellular, molecular and physiological mechanisms underpinning human health and disease.<sup id="cite_ref-www5.plymouth.ac.uk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www5.plymouth.ac.uk-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Since many organisms share a common evolutionary history with humans and hence common features and systems, the basic end of medical research these days shades into basic biology.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preclinical_research">Preclinical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preclinical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Pre-clinical research covers research that prepares the ground for clinical research with patients. Typically the work requires no ethical approval (though some work with animals does), is supervised by people with PhDs rather than medical doctors, and is carried out in a university or company rather than a hospital or surgery.</p> 
     <p>The pre-clinical stage of pharmaceutical testing is covered at <a href="/wiki/Phases_of_clinical_research#Pre-clinical_studies" title="Phases of clinical research">Phases of clinical research#Pre-clinical studies</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_research">Clinical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Clinical research is carried out with patients. It is generally supervised by doctors in a medical setting such as a hospital and requires ethical approval.</p> 
     <p>The clinical phase of drug testing is covered here <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">Clinical trial</a>.</p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Research_funding" title="Research funding" class="mw-redirect">Research funding</a> in many countries derives from research bodies and private organizations which distribute money for equipment and salaries. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, funding bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/Medical_Research_Council_(UK)" title="Medical Research Council (UK)" class="mw-redirect">Medical Research Council</a> derive their assets from UK tax payers, and distribute this to institutions in a competitive manner. The <a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust</a> is the UK's largest non-governmental source of funds for biomedical research and provides over £600 million per year in grants to scientists and funds for research centres.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the United States, the most recent data from 2003<sup id="cite_ref-Moses05_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moses05-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> suggest that about 94 billion dollars were provided for biomedical research in the United States. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_company" title="Pharmaceutical company" class="mw-redirect">pharmaceutical companies</a> collectively contribute 26.4 billion dollars and 27.0 billion dollars, respectively, which constitute 28% and 29% of the total, respectively. Other significant contributors include <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> companies (17.9 billion dollars, 19% of total), <a href="/wiki/Medical_devices" title="Medical devices" class="mw-redirect">medical device</a> companies (9.2 billion dollars, 10% of total), other federal sources, and state and local governments. Foundations and charities, led by the <a href="/wiki/Bill_and_Melinda_Gates_Foundation" title="Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation" class="mw-redirect">Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation</a>, contributed about 3% of the funding.</p> 
     <p>In Australia, in 2000/01 (the most recent data available), about $1.7B was spent on biomedical research,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> with just under half ($800M, 47%) sourced from the Commonwealth government (all sources). About $540M came from business investments/funding and a further $220M from private or not-for-profit organisations (totalling 44%). The balance was from state and local governments. Since then there has been a significant in government funding through the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_and_Medical_Research_Council" title="National Health and Medical Research Council">National Health and Medical Research Council</a> (NHMRC), whose expenditure on research was nearly A$700 million in 2008–09.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The enactment of <a href="/wiki/Orphan_drug" title="Orphan drug">orphan drug</a> legislation in some countries has increased funding available to develop drugs meant to treat rare conditions, resulting in breakthroughs that previously were uneconomical to pursue.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government-funded_biomedical_research">Government-funded biomedical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Government-funded biomedical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Since the establishment of the National Institutes of Health (NIH) in the mid-1940s, the main source of U.S. Federal support of biomedical research, investment priorities and levels of funding have fluctuated. From 1995 to 2010, NIH support of biomedical research increased from 11 billion to 27 billion <sup id="cite_ref-National_Institutes_of_Health_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Institutes_of_Health-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the jump in federal spending, advancements measured by citations to publications and the number of drugs passed by the FDA along with other measures of scientific achievement remained stagnant over the same time span.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Financial projections indicate federal spending will remain constant in the near future.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_federal_funding_trends">US federal funding trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: US federal funding trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The National Institutes of Health (NIH) is the agency that is responsible for management of the lion's share of federal funding of biomedical research.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Institutes_of_Health_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Institutes_of_Health-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Over the past century there were two notable periods of NIH support. From 1995 to 1996 funding increased from $8.877 billion to $9.366 billion,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> years which represented the start of what is considered the "doubling period" of rapid NIH support.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Institutes_of_Health_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Institutes_of_Health-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The second notable period started in 1997 and ended in 2010, a period where the NIH moved to organize research spending for engagement with the scientific community.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1997 Applications were added and in 2000 Awards had been added.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> These changes were coupled with increasing support; funding rose from $13 billion in 1997 to approximately $27 billion in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 should also be noted in this history of NIH funding of biomedical research as it committed further funds in the midst of the great recession.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Estimating Long-Term Economic Returns of NIH Funding on Output in the Biosciences published in August, 2012 describes the latest trends of NIH organization. In recent years, the NIH has started to publish medical research trials Success Rates per dollar spent, an initiative which illustrates that research efficiency is viewed as a significant issue both by the public and policy makers.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privately_.28industry.29_funded_biomedical_research">Privately (industry) funded biomedical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Privately (industry) funded biomedical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Since 1980 the share of biomedical research funding from industry sources has grown from 32% to 62%,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> which has resulted in the development of numerous life-saving medical advances. The relationship between industry and government-funded research in the US has seen great movement over the years. The 1980 Bayh Dole Act was passed by Congress to foster a more constructive relationship between the collaboration of government and industry funded biomedical research. The Bayh Doyle Act gave private corporations the option of applying for government funded grants for biomedical research which in turn allowed the private corporations to license the technology.<sup id="cite_ref-kellogg.northwestern.edu_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kellogg.northwestern.edu-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Both government and industry research funding increased rapidly from between the years of 1994–2003; industry saw a compound average annual growth rate of 8.1% a year and slowed only slightly to a compound average annual growth rate of 5.8% from 2003 to 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The funding from industry for pharmaceutical research, a large part of all industry funded research, has slowed since 1994 due to multiple perceptions, lower approval rates from the FDA, increased costs with clinical trials due to more stringent regulation and longer anticipation for return on investment. A 2004 study showed that to bring a drug to market, drug development requires an average of 12–15 years and can reach costs up to $1.7 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-kellogg.northwestern.edu_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kellogg.northwestern.edu-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_influence_of_funding_on_research_and_the_management_of_conflicts_of_interests">The influence of funding on research and the management of conflicts of interests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The influence of funding on research and the management of conflicts of interests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>"Conflict of interest" in the field of medical research has been defined as "a set of conditions in which professional judgment concerning a primary interest (such as a patients welfare or the validity of research) tends to be unduly influenced by a secondary interest (such as financial gain)."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the early 1900s private companies such as the Carbolic Smoke Ball Company,<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Mrs. Winlow's Soothing Syrup<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> among other snake medicine remedies were solicited around the world and were the cause of many deaths due to misinformation. Information was not readily available to consumers nor was it required of the pharmaceutical producers to inform their customers of the ingredients that they were consuming. Samuel Hopkins Adams was an investigator to uncover the wide corruption and falsehoods that existed within the American pharmaceutical industry. He is quoted saying: "Gullible America will spend this year some seventy-five millions of dollars in the purchase of patent medicines. In consideration of this sum it will swallow huge quantities of alcohol, an appalling amount of opiates and narcotics, a wide assortment of varied drugs ranging from powerful and dangerous heart depressants to insidious liver stimulants; and far in excess of all other ingredients, undiluted fraud."<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Regulation on industry funded biomedical research has seen great changes since Samuel Hopkins Adams declaration. In 1906 congress passed the Pure Food and Drugs Act of 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1912 Congress passed the Shirley Amendment to prohibit the wide dissemination of false information on pharmaceuticals.<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Food and Drug Administration was formally created in 1930 under the McNarey Mapes Amendment to oversee the regulation of Food and Drugs in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1962 the Kefauver-Harris Amendments to the Food, Drug and Cosmetics Act made it so that before a drug was marketed in the United States the FDA must first approve that the drug was safe.<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Kefauver-Harris amendments also mandated that more stringent clinical trials must be performed before a drug is brought to the market.<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The Kefauver-Harris amendments were met with opposition from industry due to the requirement of lengthier clinical trial periods that would lessen the period of time in which the investor is able to see return on their money. In the pharmaceutical industry patents are typically granted for a 20-year period of time, and most patent applications are submitted during the early stages of the product development.<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Ariel Katz on average after a patent application is submitted it takes an additional 8 years before the FDA approves a drug for marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> As such this would leave a company with only 12 years to market the drug to see a return on their investments. After a sharp decline of new drugs entering the US market following the 1962 Kefauver-Harris amendments economist Sam Petlzman concluded that cost of loss of innovation was greater than the savings recognized by consumers no longer purchasing ineffective drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1984 the Hatch-Waxman Act or the Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984 was passed by congress.<sup id="cite_ref-fda.gov_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Hatch-Waxman Act was passed with the idea that giving brand manufacturers the ability to extend their patent by an additional 5 years would create greater incentives for innovation and private sector funding for investment.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The relationship that exists with industry funded biomedical research is that of which industry is the financier for academic institutions which in turn employ scientific investigators to conduct research. A fear that exists wherein a project is funded by industry is that firms might negate informing the public of negative effects to better promote their product.<sup id="cite_ref-mttlr.org_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mttlr.org-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> A list of studies show that public fear of the conflicts of interest that exist when biomedical research is funded by industry can be considered valid after a 2003 publication of "Scope and Impact of Financial Conflicts of Interest in Biomedical Research" in The Journal of American Association of Medicine. This publication included 37 different studies that met specific criteria to determine whether or not an academic institution or scientific investigator funded by industry had engaged in behavior that could be deduced to be a conflict of interest in the field of biomedical research. Survey results from one study concluded that 43% of scientific investigators employed by a participating academic institution had received research related gifts and discretionary funds from industry sponsors.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Another participating institution surveyed showed that 7.6% of investigators were financially tied to research sponsors, including paid speaking engagements (34%), consulting arrangements (33%), advisory board positions (32%) and equity (14%).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A 1994 study concluded that 58% out of 210 life science companies indicated that investigators were required to withhold information pertaining to their research as to extend the life of the interested companies' patents.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Rules and regulations regarding conflict of interest disclosures are being studied by experts in the biomedical research field to eliminate conflicts of interest that could possibly affect the outcomes of biomedical research.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The earliest narrative describing a medical trial is found in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Daniel" title="Book of Daniel">Book of Daniel</a>, which says that Babylonian king <a href="/wiki/Nebuchadnezzar" title="Nebuchadnezzar" class="mw-redirect">Nebuchadnezzar</a> ordered youths of royal blood to eat only red meat and wine for three years, while another group of youths ate only beans and water.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The experiment was intended to determine if a diet of vegetables and water was healthier than a diet of wine and red meat. At the experiment endpoint, the trial accomplished its prerogative: the youths who ate only beans and water were noticeably healthier.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Scientific curiosity to understand health outcomes from varying treatments has been present for centuries, but it was not until the mid-19th century when an organizational platform was created to support and regulate this curiosity. In 1945, Vannevar Bush said that biomedical scientific research was "the pacemaker of technological progress", an idea which contributed to the initiative to found the National Institutes of Health (NIH) in 1948, a historical benchmark that marked the beginning of a near century substantial investment in biomedical research.<sup id="cite_ref-scienceguide.nl_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scienceguide.nl-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The NIH provides more financial support for medical research that any other agency in the world to date and claims responsibility for numerous innovations that have improved global health.<sup id="cite_ref-scienceguide.nl_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scienceguide.nl-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The historical funding of biomedical research has undergone many changes over the past century. Innovations such as the polio vaccine, antibiotics and antipsychotic agents, developed in the early years of the NIH lead to social and political support of the agency. Political initiatives in the early 1990s lead to a doubling of NIH funding, spurring an era of great scientific progress.<sup id="cite_ref-fas.org_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas.org-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> There have been dramatic changes in the era since the turn of the 21st century to date; roughly around the start of the century, the cost of trials dramatically increased while the rate scientific discoveries did not keep pace.<sup id="cite_ref-fas.org_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas.org-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Biomedical research spending increased substantially faster than GDP growth over the past decade in the US, between the years of 2003 and 2007 spending increased 14% per year, while GDP growth increased 1% over the same period (both measures adjusted for inflation).<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Industry, not-for-profit entities, state and federal funding spending combined accounted for an increase in funding from $75.5 billion in 2003 to $101.1 billion in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to the immediacy of federal financing priorities and stagnant corporate spending during the recession, biomedical research spending decreased 2% in real terms in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite an overall increase of investment in biomedical research, there has been stagnation, and in some areas a marked decline in the number of drug and device approvals over the same time period.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Today, industry sponsored research accounts for 58% of expenditures, NIH for 27% of expenditures, state governments for 5% of expenditures, non NIH-federal sources for 5% of expenditures and not-for-profit entities accounted for 4% of support.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Federally funded biomedical research expenditures increased nominally, 0.7% (adjusted for inflation), from 2003 to 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Previous reports showed a stark contrast in federal investment, from 1994 to 2003, federal funding increased 100% (adjusted for inflation).<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The NIH manages the lions-share, over 85%, of federal biomedical research expenditures.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> NIH support for biomedical research decreased from $31.8 billion in 2003, to $29.0 billion in 2007, a 25% decline (in real terms adjusted for inflation), while non-NIH federal funding allowed for the maintenance of government financial support levels through the era (the 0.7% four-year increase). Spending from industry-initiated research increased 25% (adjusted for inflation) over the same time period of time, from 2003 to 2007, an increase from $40 billion in 2003, to $58.6 billion in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Industry sourced expenditures from 1994 to 2003 showed industry sponsored research funding increased 8.1%, a stark contrast to 25% increase in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Of industry sponsored research, pharmaceutical firm spending was the greatest contributor from all industry sponsored biomedical research spending, but only increased 15% (adjusted for inflation) from 2003 to 2007, while device and biotechnology firms accounted for the majority of the spending.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The stock performance, a measure that can be an indication of future firm growth or technological direction, has substantially increased for both predominantly medical device and biotechnology producers.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Contributing factors to this growth are thought to be less rigorous FDA approval requirements for devices as opposed to drugs, lower cost of trials, lower pricing and profitability of products and predictable influence of new technology due to a limited number of competitors.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Another visible shift during the era was a shift in focus to late stage research trials; formerly dispersed, since 1994 an increasingly large portion of industry-sponsored research was late phase trials rather than early-experimental phases now accounting for the majority of industry sponsored research.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> This shift is attributable to a lower risk investment and a shorter development to market schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The low risk preference is also reflected in the trend of large pharmaceutical firms acquiring smaller companies that hold patents to newly developed drug or device discoveries which have not yet passed federal regulation (large companies are mitigating their risk by purchasing technology created by smaller companies in early-phase high-risk studies).<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Medical research support from Universities increased from $22 billion in 2003 to $27.7 billion in 2007, a 7.8% increase (adjusted for inflation).<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2007 the most heavily funded institutions received 20% of HIN medical research funding, and the top 50 institutions received 58% of NIH medical research funding, the percent of funding allocated to the largest institutions is a trend which has increased only slightly over data from 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Relative to federal and private funding, health policy and service research accounted for a nominal amount of sponsored research; health policy and service research was funded $1.8 billion in 2003, which increased to $2.2 billion in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Stagnant rates of investment from the US government over the past decade, may be in part attributable to challenges that plague the field. To date only two-thirds of published drug trial findings have results that can be re-produced,<sup id="cite_ref-drugabuse.gov_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drugabuse.gov-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> which raises concerns from a US regulatory standpoint where great investment has been made in research ethics and standards, yet trial results remain inconsistent. Federal agencies have called upon greater regulation to address these problems; a spokesman from the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke, an agency of the NIH, stated that there is "widespread poor reporting of experimental design in articles and grant applications, that animal research should follow a core set of research parameters, and that a concerted effort by all stakeholders is needed to disseminate best reporting practices and put them into practice".<sup id="cite_ref-drugabuse.gov_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drugabuse.gov-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transparency_laws">Transparency laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transparency laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Two laws which are both still in effect, one passed in 2006 and the other in 2010, were instrumental in defining funding reporting standards for biomedical research, and defining for the first time reporting regulations that were previously not required. The 2006 Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act mandates that all entities receiving over $25,000 in federal funds must report annual spending reports, including disclosure of executive salaries.<sup id="cite_ref-hrsa.gov_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrsa.gov-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The 2010 amendment to the act mandates that progress reports be submitted along with financial reporting.<sup id="cite_ref-hrsa.gov_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrsa.gov-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Data from the federal mandate is managed and made publicly available on usaspening.gov.<sup id="cite_ref-hrsa.gov_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrsa.gov-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Aside from the main source, usaspending.gov, other reporting mechanisms exist: Data specifically on biomedical research funding from federal sources is made publicly available by the National Health Expenditure Accounts (NHEA), data on health services research, approximately 0.1% of federal funding on biomedical research, is available through the Coalition of Health Services Research, the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the Centers for Medicare &amp; Medicaid Services, and the Veterans Health Administration.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Biomedical_Research_2009_20-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Biomedical_Research_2009-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Currently there are not any funding reporting requirements for industry sponsored research, but there has been voluntary movement toward this goal.<sup id="cite_ref-policymed.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-policymed.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2014, major pharmaceutical stakeholders such as Roche and Johnson and Johnson have made financial information publicly available and Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA), the most prominent professional association for biomedical research companies, has recently begun to provide limited public funding reports.<sup id="cite_ref-policymed.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-policymed.com-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations_and_guidelines">Regulations and guidelines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regulations and guidelines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Medical research is very highly regulated. National regulatory authorities are appointed in almost every country worldwide to oversee and monitor medical research, such as for the development and distribution of new drugs. In the US the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)" class="mw-redirect">Food and Drug Administration</a> oversees new drug development, in Europe the <a href="/wiki/European_Medicines_Agency" title="European Medicines Agency">European Medicines Agency</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/EudraLex" title="EudraLex">EudraLex</a>), and in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health,_Labour_and_Welfare_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (Japan)" class="mw-redirect">Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (Japan)</a>. The <a href="/wiki/World_Medical_Association" title="World Medical Association">World Medical Association</a> develops the ethical standards for the medical profession, involved in medical research. The most fundamental of them is the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Helsinki" title="Declaration of Helsinki">Declaration of Helsinki</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_Harmonisation_of_Technical_Requirements_for_Registration_of_Pharmaceuticals_for_Human_Use" title="International Conference on Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Registration of Pharmaceuticals for Human Use" class="mw-redirect">International Conference on Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Registration of Pharmaceuticals for Human Use</a> (ICH) works on the creation of rules and guidelines for the development of new medication, such as the guidelines for <a href="/wiki/Good_clinical_practice" title="Good clinical practice">Good Clinical Practice</a> (GCP). All ideas of regulation are based on a country's ethical standards code. This is why treatment of a particular disease in one country may not be allowed, but is in another.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flaws_and_vulnerabilities">Flaws and vulnerabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Flaws and vulnerabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A major flaw and vulnerability in biomedical research appears to be the hypercompetition for the resources and positions that are required to conduct science. The competition seems to suppress the creativity, cooperation, risk-taking, and original thinking required to make fundamental discoveries. Other consequences of today's highly pressured environment for research appear to be a substantial number of research publications whose results cannot be replicated, and perverse incentives in research funding that encourage grantee institutions to grow without making sufficient investments in their own faculty and facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Other risky trends include a decline in the share of key research grants going to younger scientists, as well as a steady rise in the age at which investigators receive their first funding.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
