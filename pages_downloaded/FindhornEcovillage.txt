   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Findhorn Ecovillage</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>

    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p><b>Findhorn Ecovillage</b> is an experimental architectural community project based at The Park, in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, Scotland, near the village of <a href="/wiki/Findhorn" title="Findhorn">Findhorn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-local_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-local-1">[1]</a></sup> The project's main aim is to demonstrate a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> in environmental, social, and economic terms. Work began in the early 1980s under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> but now includes a wide diversity of organisations and activities.<sup id="cite_ref-Lovie_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lovie-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ecovillage_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecovillage-3">[3]</a></sup> Numerous different ecological techniques are in use, and the project has won a variety of awards, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Settlements_Programme" title="United Nations Human Settlements Programme">UN-Habitat</a> Best Practice Designation in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

     <p>A recent independent study<sup id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup> concludes that the residents have the lowest <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of any community measured so far in the industrialised world and is also half of the UK average.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although the project has attracted some controversy, the growing profile of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental issues</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> has led to a degree of mainstream acceptance of its ecological ethos.<sup id="cite_ref-Stern_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guggenheim_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guggenheim-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The October 1982 Conference ‘Building a Planetary Village’<sup id="cite_ref-Scott_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-10">[10]</a></sup> hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> marked the beginning of serious attempts by the <a href="/wiki/Intentional_community" title="Intentional community">intentional community</a>, which had existed at <a href="/wiki/Findhorn" title="Findhorn">Findhorn</a> since 1962<sup id="cite_ref-Riddell_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Riddell-11">[11]</a></sup> to demonstrate a human settlement that could be considered <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable" class="mw-redirect">sustainable</a> in environmental, social, and economic terms.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The term ‘<a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">ecovillage</a>’ later came to be used to describe such experiments and in 1995 the first international conference of ecovillages, <i>Ecovillages and Sustainable Communities for the 21st Century</i>, was held in Findhorn.<sup id="cite_ref-Bang_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bang-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dawson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawson-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>At first almost all of the activities this involved, such as eco-house construction, a 75&nbsp;kW <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> wind turbine and an ecological waste water treatment system were undertaken by the Findhorn Foundation itself, or its trading company New Findhorn Directions Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-walker_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup> However, from 1990 onwards a growing number of independent charities, businesses, small sister communities, independent practitioners and community bodies have grown up and significantly extended the size and diversity of ecological projects,<sup id="cite_ref-findhorn_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-findhorn-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Metcalf_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metcalf-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pewters_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pewters-18">[18]</a></sup> some of which are listed below. As of 2005 the Ecovillage has around 450 members centred around The Park (the main campus on the southern edge of Findhorn), but also based at numerous locations in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Forres" title="Forres">Forres</a> and elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>. The project supports approximately 300 jobs in the Findhorn/Forres area and provides a total aggregate economic impact in excess of £5 million per annum in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> of Scotland as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns2003_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2003-19">[19]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_eco-projects">Examples of eco-projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples of eco-projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">Ecovillages</a> usually rely on a wide variety of approaches to minimise their ecological footprint. Some of the most important used at Findhorn are listed here although it is important to bear in mind that in any given situation the single largest factor is likely to be the attitudes of the residents rather than the technologies themselves. The national scale is far better researched than the micro scale, and at this level it is easy to observe that countries with similar levels of wealth may have quite different footprints — see for example Chambers (2004).</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_building_codes">Ecological building codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecological building codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/220px-PA120016.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/330px-PA120016.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/440px-PA120016.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> An eco-house at Findhorn with turf roof and 
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a>
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The site has a comprehensive building code that all new buildings are required to meet. It goes far beyond current UK building standards<sup id="cite_ref-Talbott1993_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbott1993-20">[20]</a></sup> and includes features such as very efficient insulation (typically using products made from recycled paper), non-toxic organic paints, wood preservatives and boarding manufactured without the use of toxic glues or resins and roofing with natural clay tiles.</p> 
     <p>There are a wide variety of other options used including low-energy light bulbs, 'breathing wall' construction allowing a controlled exchange of air &amp; vapour water conservation features and locally sourced materials. Shared facilities such as laundry, kitchens, lounges etc. are encouraged, thus avoiding unnecessary duplication.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbott2001_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbott2001-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Most new buildings incorporate design features that invite passive solar radiation to reduce building heating needs, such as south-facing windows, conservatories and minimal wall openings on north walls. Sustainably harvested wood provides space heating for many homes both old and new, and an Ecovillage company supplies solar panels for domestic hot water heating to residential and commercial customers throughout the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-aessolar_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aessolar-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Community_Energy_Company" title="Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company" class="mw-redirect">Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company</a> recently awarded a grant for the installation of ground source heating for the proposed new Moray Arts Centre based at Findhorn.<sup id="cite_ref-HIE_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HIE-23">[23]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><i>Simply Build Green</i> — Talbott (1993) — is based on research undertaken at Findhorn Ecovillage and was the UK’s first technical guide to ecological housing.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eco-architecture">Eco-architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eco-architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The first new dwelling to be constructed at the Ecovillage was a small house in the round made from a recycled <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> barrel. The idea was later developed and several buildings on this theme were constructed. Other innovative styles include a <a href="/wiki/Straw-bale_construction" title="Straw-bale construction">strawbale house</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Earthship" title="Earthship">earthship</a> electricity sub-station. Over fifty new houses have now been constructed, some in exotic styles, others more prosaic in appearance. The rigorous building codes contrast with an apparent absence of visual guidelines and the result is a diversity of design rather than a consistent approach to aesthetics.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_Machine">Living Machine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Living Machine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:FindhornLM.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/220px-FindhornLM.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/330px-FindhornLM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/440px-FindhornLM.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:FindhornLM.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The Living Machine wastewater treatment plant at Findhorn.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>In 1995 <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Porritt" title="Jonathon Porritt">Jonathon Porritt</a> opened Europe's first <a href="/wiki/Living_Machine" title="Living Machine" class="mw-redirect">Living Machine</a> (also known as Eco-Restorers in The UK) at The Park campus. This is an ecologically engineered waste water treatment system which is designed to treat sewage from a population of up to 350 people and in common with a number of other such systems<sup id="cite_ref-ltl_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ltl-24">[24]</a></sup> also provides a research and educational facility to promote the technology. It was constructed with assistance from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The invention of Canadian scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Todd_(biologist)" title="John Todd (biologist)">John Todd</a>, they use tanks containing diverse communities of bacteria, algae, micro-organisms, numerous species of plants and trees, snails, fish and other living creatures to treat the water.<sup id="cite_ref-ltl_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ltl-24">[24]</a></sup> At the end of the series of tanks, the resulting water is pure enough to be returned to the local water table. Plans to use the water for irrigation have been considered but not implemented to date.<sup id="cite_ref-West_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_food_production">Organic food production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organic food production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>One of the most significant factors in the Ecovillage's low eco-footprint (see below) is its attitude to food production and consumption. Various smallholdings associated with the Ecovillage contribute to a <a href="/wiki/Community_supported_agriculture" title="Community supported agriculture" class="mw-redirect">community supported agriculture</a> or 'box' scheme which provides <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic produce</a> for the local area, some of it grown using <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> techniques. This horticulture '"provides more than 70% of the community's fresh food requirements"<sup id="cite_ref-food_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EarthShare_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EarthShare-27">[27]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Community_Stores" title="Phoenix Community Stores">Phoenix Community Stores</a>, based at the Park, is one of the largest retailers of organic produce in northern Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Ph.C5.93nix_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ph.C5.93nix-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_park">Wind park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wind park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Another reason for the low ecological impact of the settlement is the presence of four <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> wind turbines which can generate up to 750&nbsp;kW. These make the Park settlement net exporters of electricity produced from <a href="/wiki/Renewable" title="Renewable" class="mw-redirect">renewable</a> resources. The first V17 generator was installed in 1989 and three additional V29s were installed in March 2006. The original site was a <a href="/wiki/Trailer_park" title="Trailer park">caravan park</a> and as a result the Ecovillage has its own private electricity grid. Most of the generation is used on-site with any surplus exported to the <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_(UK)" title="National Grid (UK)" class="mw-redirect">National Grid</a>.</p>

     <p>Since 2002 a <a href="/wiki/Local_currency" title="Local currency">local currency</a> has been in operation. Launched by Ekopia, the community’s <a href="/wiki/Development_trust" title="Development trust">development trust</a>, it is accepted by almost all Ecovillage organisations. There are roughly £20,000 of notes in circulation and issuing them has enabled Ekopia to make low interest loans and donations to support various initiatives including an ecological guest facility, the wind park (above) and the local Youth Project.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The current issue, which is due to expire in 2011, is at par with sterling i.e. 1 Eko = £1, and notes are in one, five, ten and twenty denominations.<sup id="cite_ref-Eko_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eko-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CIFAL_Findhorn">CIFAL Findhorn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: CIFAL Findhorn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> has been running educational programmes in environmental education since the early 1980s, but a new development occurred in September 2006 with the formal launching of <a href="/wiki/CIFAL" title="CIFAL">CIFAL</a> Findhorn.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This is a new <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> training facility, which is a joint initiative between <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">The Moray Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Global_Ecovillage_Network" title="Global Ecovillage Network">Global Ecovillage Network</a>, the Findhorn Foundation and <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Institute_for_Training_and_Research" title="United Nations Institute for Training and Research">UNITAR</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_Footprint_Study">Ecological Footprint Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecological Footprint Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/220px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/330px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/440px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The wind turbines at Findhorn, which make the Ecovillage a net exporter of electricity.
       </div>

     </div> 
     <p>An <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> is an attempt to measure the total environmental impact of human activities in a given area. The results measure the amount of land and water that the population requires to provide the resources they utilise and to absorb their wastes. The calculations used converts this into a measure of land area, global hectares (gha). It is widely accepted<sup id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-33">[33]</a></sup> that the human impact on planet Earth now exceeds the available capacity of natural systems to sustain it, and that western countries typically have a resource usage that ‘would require three planets’ to maintain if every citizen on Earth had a similar impact. In October 2006 preliminary results of the ecological footprint study<sup id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup> for the Findhorn Ecovillage undertaken by The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Research_Centre" title="Sustainable Development Research Centre">Sustainable Development Research Centre</a> of the <a href="/wiki/UHI_Millennium_Institute" title="UHI Millennium Institute" class="mw-redirect">UHI Millennium Institute</a> in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Environment_Institute" title="Stockholm Environment Institute">Stockholm Environment Institute</a> confirmed that ecovillages can tread significantly more lightly on the Earth than more conventional communities.</p> 
     <p>The study found that residents of The Park and the nearby campus of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Hill" title="Cluny Hill">Cluny Hill</a> College have, on average, a footprint of 2.71 hectares per capita, a little over half the UK national average of 5.4 hectares. (By comparison the comparable figure for the United States is 9.5gha, whilst <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> is rated at the average 'global earthshare' of 1.9gha).<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-33">[33]</a></sup> Of particular significance are the results relating to food, ‘home and energy' use, and 'consumables and personal possessions' which have 35%, 27% and 44% of the national average respectively. Findhorn residents have an eco-footprint some 13% lower than those at the London eco-housing development, <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">BedZED</a>. The study is also intending to measure the additional impact caused by visitors to the Ecovillage but results are not yet available.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards.2C_UN_connections_and_critiques">Awards, UN connections and critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards, UN connections and critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>At a time when resource depletion and global warming are international concerns it is not perhaps surprising that these activities should receive various accolades.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>In September 1995 The Findhorn Ecological Village Project was awarded the 'We the Peoples 50 Communities' award given by the Friends of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> as part of the 50th Anniversary of the UN Celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>In October 1998 the Ecovillage Project, together with other 100 leadership Initiatives "which are inspiring innovative action on Earth", received Best Practice Designation from the United Nations Centre for Human Settlements — UNCHS (Habitat) and Dubai Municipality.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:PA120021.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/220px-PA120021.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/330px-PA120021.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/440px-PA120021.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944"></a>

     <ul> 
      <li>In September 2000 the conservation charity Trees for Life received the Millennium Marque Award in recognition of its work in helping to restore the Caledonian Forest in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Affric" title="Glen Affric">Glen Affric</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Trees_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trees-34">[34]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>The following year the <a href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP" class="mw-redirect">United Nations Environment Programme</a> noted that “most of the problems identified by <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> have their roots at the local level, therefore UNEP appreciates your significant contribution at the local level to models for holistic and sustainable living in harmony with the environment.”<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Various critiques exist in print, although these tend to concentrate on the eclectic spirituality of the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> rather than the environmental aspects of the Ecovillage. There is a statement of core values for the Ecovillage and its associated community called ‘Common Ground’<sup id="cite_ref-Common_Ground_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Common_Ground-35">[35]</a></sup> but other than affirming a commitment to “active spiritual practice” (which is diversely understood and interpreted<sup id="cite_ref-walker_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup>) it concentrates largely on preferred interpersonal behaviours rather than prescribing a spiritual philosophy. Objections were raised by a local pressure group against the expansion of the wind park<sup id="cite_ref-Elgin_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elgin-36">[36]</a></sup> but the concerns of local people<sup id="cite_ref-local_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-local-1">[1]</a></sup> about the Ecovillage appear to have reduced, partly due to the proactive stance of the ecovillagers themselves,<sup id="cite_ref-walker_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup> especially perhaps as a result of the gift of some amenity land to a local <a href="/wiki/Land_trust" title="Land trust">land trust</a> by Duneland Ltd., an Ecovillage company.<sup id="cite_ref-Duneland_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duneland-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bay_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bay-38">[38]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>While many houses are modest in size some are large, exceeding 200&nbsp;m² in built area for a single household. Diversity of lifestyle may well be part of any human settlement but the magnitude of such structures hints that the commitment to ‘living lightly on the Earth’ is not as whole-hearted as first appearances may suggest. Nonetheless, working examples of how a western lifestyle might be possible whilst living within the means of the Earth's renewable resources to support it are likely to be of interest at a time when the consensus of mainstream opinion is moving towards the view that ‘business as usual’ may no longer be an option.<sup id="cite_ref-Stern_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
