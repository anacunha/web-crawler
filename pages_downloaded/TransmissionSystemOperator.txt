     <p>A <b>transmission system operator</b> (<b>TSO</b>) is an entity entrusted with transporting energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a><sup id="cite_ref-entsog_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-entsog-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> or electrical power on a national or regional level, using fixed <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. The term is defined by the European Commission. The certification procedure for Transmission System Operators is listed in Article 10 of the Electricity and Gas Directives of 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Due to the cost of establishing a transmission infrastructure, such as <a href="/wiki/Power_transmission_line" title="Power transmission line" class="mw-redirect">main power lines</a> or gas main lines and associated connection points, a TSO is usually a <a href="/wiki/Natural_monopoly" title="Natural monopoly">natural monopoly</a>, and as such is often subjected to regulations.</p> 
     <p>In electrical power business, a TSO is an operator that <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmits electrical power</a> from generation plants over the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> to regional or local <a href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution" class="mw-redirect">electricity distribution</a> operators.</p> 
     <p>In natural gas business, a TSO receives gas from producers, transports it via pipeline through an area and delivers to gas distribution companies.</p> 
     <p>The United States has similar organizational categories: independent system operator (ISO) and <a href="/wiki/Regional_transmission_organization" title="Regional transmission organization">regional transmission organization</a> (RTO).</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_electrical_power_transmission">Role in electrical power transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_system_operator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role in electrical power transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Safety and reliability are a critical issue for transmission system operators, since any failure on their grid or their electrical generation sources might propagate to a very large number of customers, causing personal and property damages. Natural hazards and generation/consumption imbalances are a major cause of concern. To minimize the probability of grid instability and failure, regional or national transmission system operators are interconnected to each other.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_market_operations">Electricity market operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transmission_system_operator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electricity market operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The role of the System Operator in a wholesale <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">electricity market</a> is to manage the security of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">power system</a> in real time and co-ordinate the supply of and demand for electricity, in a manner that avoids fluctuations in frequency or interruptions of supply. The System Operator service is normally specified in rules or codes established as part of the electricity market.</p> 
     <p>The System Operator function may be owned by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission grid</a> company, or may be fully independent.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> They are often wholly or partly owned by state or national governments. In many cases they are independent of <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> companies (upstream) and <a href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution" class="mw-redirect">electricity distribution</a> companies (downstream). They are financed either by the states or countries or by charging a toll proportional to the energy they carry.</p> 
     <p>The System Operator is required to maintain a continuous (second-by-second) balance between electricity supply from power stations and demand from consumers, and also ensure the provision of reserves that will allow for sudden contingencies. The System Operator achieves this by determining the optimal combination of generating stations and reserve providers for each market trading period, instructing generators when and how much electricity to generate, and managing any contingent events that cause the balance between supply and demand to be disrupted. System Operations staff undertake this work using sophisticated energy modelling and communications systems.</p> 
     <p>In addition to its roles of real-time dispatch of generation and managing security, the System Operator also carries out investigations and planning to ensure that supply can meet demand and system security can be maintained during future trading periods. Examples of planning work may include co- ordinating generator and transmission outages, facilitating commissioning of new generating plant and procuring ancillary services to support power system operation.</p>
