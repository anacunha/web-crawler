     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_of_the_Tax_Credit">Goals of the Tax Credit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Roofing_Tax_Credit_for_Energy_Efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Goals of the Tax Credit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The 2009 roofing <a href="/wiki/Tax_credit" title="Tax credit">tax credit</a> is part of a twofold plan rolled out by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States Federal government</a>. The first and most relevant of goals was intended to stimulate the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> by incentivizing Americans to replace their roofs with more energy efficient roofs, thereby creating jobs by increasing the demand for labor in the <a href="/wiki/Roofing" title="Roofing" class="mw-redirect">roofing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> industries. The other goal of the 2009 Federal Roofing Tax Credit was to reduce carbon emissions to the environment by requiring <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> to install <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> products on their homes in order to receive the tax credit; the installation of Energy Star roofing products in homes would reduce the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> needed to maintain the desired temperature within the residence.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Covered_by_the_Roofing_Tax_Credit">Covered by the Roofing Tax Credit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Roofing_Tax_Credit_for_Energy_Efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Covered by the Roofing Tax Credit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In order to qualify for the tax credit, the new roofing materials was required to be metal with an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigmented coating</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> with cooling granules compliant with Energy Star requirements.The Environmental Protection Agency provided a list of some products which satisfy the tax credit's requirement, but consumers should also check with the manufacturer prior to purchase to ensure the materials qualify.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA1-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The government has also placed a restriction on the tax credit dictating that the new roof must be installed at the principal residence of the tax payer between January 1, 2009 and December 31, 2010. Principal residence is defined as a home owned by the party filing for the credit and used as his principal place of residence. Principal residences can include <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houseboat" title="Houseboat">houseboats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile homes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_apartment" title="Cooperative apartment" class="mw-redirect">cooperative apartments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Condominium" title="Condominium">condominiums</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Manufactured_housing" title="Manufactured housing">manufactured homes</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amount_of_the_Credit">Amount of the Credit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Roofing_Tax_Credit_for_Energy_Efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amount of the Credit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The Federal Roofing Tax Credit for Energy Efficiency is dependent on the cost of the materials used in the renovation. A consumer could only receive a tax credit of up to 30% of the material cost to maximum of $ 1,500. This credit is for funds spent on the Energy Star approved materials, not on installation or labor cost. For example, if the roofing materials cost $5,000 and labor expense was $5,000 resulting in a total cost of $10,000, a tax filer would still only receive a tax credit of $ 1,500, the maximum amount.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Energy_Star_Difference">The Energy Star Difference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Roofing_Tax_Credit_for_Energy_Efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Energy Star Difference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>It is an advisable idea to utilize materials that will reduce the heat of a homeowner's residence to reduce their <a href="/wiki/Domestic_energy_consumption" title="Domestic energy consumption">energy consumption</a> and their impact on the environment. However not all consumers will derive the same benefit from the use of a reflective heat-reducing roofs in reducing energy consumption.</p> 
     <p>Reflective heat reduction roofing or a <a href="/wiki/Cool_roof" title="Cool roof" class="mw-redirect">cool roof</a> is most beneficial in hot sunny climates. If a home's roof is partially or completely shaded, the homeowner may not benefit as much as someone with a roof that is in direct <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>. However even if a homeowner's roof is shaded, they will still receive the tax credit, receive benefit of heat reduction roofing, and enjoy a high quality environmentally friendly roof.</p>
