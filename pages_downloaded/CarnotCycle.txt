     <p>The <b>Carnot cycle</b> is a theoretical <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycle</a> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot</a> in 1824 and expanded upon by others in the 1830s and 1840s. It provides an upper limit on the efficiency that any classical thermodynamic engine can achieve during the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> into <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a>, or conversely, the efficiency of a <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> system in creating a temperature difference (e.g. refrigeration) by the application of work to the system. It is not an actual thermodynamic cycle but is a theoretical construct.</p> 
     <p>Every single thermodynamic system exists in a particular state. When a system is taken through a series of different states and finally returned to its initial state, a thermodynamic cycle is said to have occurred. In the process of going through this cycle, the system may perform work on its surroundings, thereby acting as a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>. A system undergoing a Carnot cycle is called a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">Carnot heat engine</a>, although such a "perfect" engine is only a theoretical construct and cannot be built in practice.<sup id="cite_ref-Giordano2009_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giordano2009-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages">Stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg/400px-Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg.png" width="400" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg/600px-Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg/800px-Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="800"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Carnot_cycle_p-V_diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Figure 1: A Carnot cycle illustrated on a 
        <a href="/wiki/PV_diagram" title="PV diagram" class="mw-redirect">PV diagram</a> to illustrate the work done.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The Carnot cycle when acting as a heat engine consists of the following steps:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li><b>Reversible <a href="/wiki/Isothermal" title="Isothermal" class="mw-redirect">isothermal</a> expansion of the gas at the "hot" temperature, <i>T</i><sub><i>1</i></sub> (isothermal heat addition or absorption).</b> During this step (1 to 2 on Figure 1, A to B in Figure 2) the gas is allowed to expand and it does work on the surroundings. The temperature of the gas does not change during the process, and thus the expansion is isothermal. The gas expansion is propelled by absorption of heat energy Q<sub>1</sub> and of entropy <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Delta S=Q_1/T_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/f/41f044c45e588d9d00422b0c4b0308b2.png"> from the high temperature reservoir.</li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">Isentropic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Reversible_adiabatic_process" title="Reversible adiabatic process" class="mw-redirect">reversible adiabatic</a>) expansion of the gas (isentropic work output).</b> For this step (2 to 3 on Figure 1, B to C in Figure 2) the mechanisms of the engine are assumed to be thermally insulated, thus they neither gain nor lose heat. The gas continues to expand, doing work on the surroundings, and losing an equivalent amount of internal energy. The gas expansion causes it to cool to the "cold" temperature, <i>T</i><sub><i>2</i></sub>. The entropy remains unchanged.</li> 
      <li><b>Reversible isothermal compression of the gas at the "cold" temperature, <i>T</i><sub><i>2</i></sub>. (isothermal heat rejection)</b> (3 to 4 on Figure 1, C to D on Figure 2) Now the surroundings do work on the gas, causing an amount of heat energy Q<sub>2</sub> and of entropy <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\Delta S=Q_2/T_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/1/7/8171c36bd2c124a8fdf91e6988becd9e.png"> to flow out of the gas to the low temperature reservoir. (This is the same amount of entropy absorbed in step 1, as can be seen from the <b><a href="/wiki/Clausius_inequality" title="Clausius inequality" class="mw-redirect">Clausius inequality</a></b>.)</li> 
      <li><b>Isentropic compression of the gas (isentropic work input).</b> (4 to 1 on Figure 1, D to A on Figure 2) Once again the mechanisms of the engine are assumed to be thermally insulated. During this step, the surroundings do work on the gas, increasing its internal energy and compressing it, causing the temperature to rise to <i>T</i><sub><i>1</i></sub>. The entropy remains unchanged. At this point the gas is in the same state as at the start of step 1.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_pressure-volume_graph">The pressure-volume graph</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The pressure-volume graph">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>When the Carnot cycle is plotted on a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_volume_diagram" title="Pressure volume diagram">pressure volume diagram</a>, the isothermal stages follow the isotherm lines for the working fluid, adiabatic stages move between isotherms and the area bounded by the complete cycle path represents the total work that can be done during one cycle.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_temperature-entropy_diagram">The temperature-entropy diagram</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The temperature-entropy diagram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:CarnotCycle1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CarnotCycle1.png/400px-CarnotCycle1.png" width="400" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CarnotCycle1.png/600px-CarnotCycle1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CarnotCycle1.png/800px-CarnotCycle1.png 2x" data-file-width="1300" data-file-height="975"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:CarnotCycle1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Figure 2: A Carnot cycle acting as a heat engine, illustrated on a temperature-entropy diagram. The cycle takes place between a hot reservoir at temperature T
        <sub>H</sub> and a cold reservoir at temperature T
        <sub>C</sub>. The vertical axis is temperature, the horizontal axis is entropy.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png/300px-Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png/450px-Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png/600px-Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png 2x" data-file-width="1300" data-file-height="975"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Ejemplo_Diagrama_T-S.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A generalized thermodynamic cycle taking place between a hot reservoir at temperature T
        <sub>H</sub> and a cold reservoir at temperature T
        <sub>C</sub>. By the 
        <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, the cycle cannot extend outside the temperature band from T
        <sub>C</sub> to T
        <sub>H</sub>. The area in red Q
        <sub>C</sub> is the amount of energy exchanged between the system and the cold reservoir. The area in white W is the amount of work energy exchanged by the system with its surroundings. The amount of heat exchanged with the hot reservoir is the sum of the two. If the system is behaving as an engine, the process moves clockwise around the loop, and moves counter-clockwise if it is behaving as a refrigerator. The efficiency to the cycle is the ratio of the white area (work) divided by the sum of the white and red areas (heat absorbed from the hot reservoir).
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The behaviour of a Carnot engine or refrigerator is best understood by using a <a href="/wiki/Temperature-entropy_diagram" title="Temperature-entropy diagram" class="mw-redirect">temperature-entropy diagram</a> (TS diagram), in which the thermodynamic state is specified by a point on a graph with <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> (S) as the horizontal axis and temperature (T) as the vertical axis. For a simple system with a fixed number of particles, any point on the graph will represent a particular state of the system. A thermodynamic process will consist of a curve connecting an initial state (A) and a final state (B). The area under the curve will be:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="Q=\int_A^B T\,dS
\quad\quad(1)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/2/3/52358dd202168d1253d22175b7a396a5.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>which is the amount of thermal energy transferred in the process. If the process moves to greater entropy, the area under the curve will be the amount of heat absorbed by the system in that process. If the process moves towards lesser entropy, it will be the amount of heat removed. For any cyclic process, there will be an upper portion of the cycle and a lower portion. For a clockwise cycle, the area under the upper portion will be the thermal energy absorbed during the cycle, while the area under the lower portion will be the thermal energy removed during the cycle. The area inside the cycle will then be the difference between the two, but since the internal energy of the system must have returned to its initial value, this difference must be the amount of work done by the system over the cycle. Referring to figure 1, mathematically, for a reversible process we may write the amount of work done over a cyclic process as:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="W = \oint PdV = \oint (dQ-dU)= \oint (TdS-dU)= \oint TdS-\oint dU = \oint TdS
\quad\quad\quad\quad(2)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/f/67fb48f4a3c0612ba8e8963f94483bc1.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Since <i>dU</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Exact_differential" title="Exact differential">exact differential</a>, its integral over any closed loop is zero and it follows that the area inside the loop on a T-S diagram is equal to the total work performed if the loop is traversed in a clockwise direction, and is equal to the total work done on the system as the loop is traversed in a counterclockwise direction.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Carnot_cycle">The Carnot cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Carnot cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Evaluation of the above integral is particularly simple for the Carnot cycle. The amount of energy transferred as work is</p>

     <p>This definition of efficiency makes sense for a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>, since it is the fraction of the heat energy extracted from the hot reservoir and converted to mechanical work. A <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> is usually the practical approximation.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Reversed_Carnot_cycle">The Reversed Carnot cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Reversed Carnot cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Carnot heat-engine cycle described is a totally reversible cycle. That is, all the processes that comprise it can be reversed, in which case it becomes the Carnot refrigeration cycle. This time, the cycle remains exactly the same except that the directions of any heat and work interactions are reversed. Heat is absorbed from the low-temperature reservoir, heat is rejected to a high-temperature reservoir, and a work input is required to accomplish all this. The P-V diagram of the reversed Carnot cycle is the same as for the Carnot cycle except that the directions of the processes are reversed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnot.27s_theorem">Carnot's theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carnot's theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)</a>
     </div> 
     <p>It can be seen from the above diagram, that for any cycle operating between temperatures <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_H" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/1/bc1a81bc2c1622b5907d5f3f3472e4b7.png"> and <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="T_C" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/0/2/c0281655c4162996a181bdd628c7cd39.png">, none can exceed the efficiency of a Carnot cycle.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Real_vs_Carnot.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Real_vs_Carnot.svg/500px-Real_vs_Carnot.svg.png" width="500" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Real_vs_Carnot.svg/750px-Real_vs_Carnot.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Real_vs_Carnot.svg/1000px-Real_vs_Carnot.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="330"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Real_vs_Carnot.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> A real engine (left) compared to the Carnot cycle (right). The entropy of a real material changes with temperature. This change is indicated by the curve on a T-S diagram. For this figure, the curve indicates a vapor-liquid equilibrium (
        <i>See <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a></i>). Irreversible systems and losses of heat (for example, due to friction) prevent the ideal from taking place at every step.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><b>Carnot's theorem</b> is a formal statement of this fact: <i>No engine operating between two heat reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between those same reservoirs.</i> Thus, Equation 3 gives the maximum efficiency possible for any engine using the corresponding temperatures. A corollary to Carnot's theorem states that: <i>All reversible engines operating between the same heat reservoirs are equally efficient.</i> Rearranging the right side of the equation gives what may be a more easily understood form of the equation. Namely that the theoretical maximum efficiency of a heat engine equals the difference in temperature between the hot and cold reservoir divided by the absolute temperature of the hot reservoir. To find the absolute temperature in kelvin, add 273.15 degrees to the Celsius temperature. Looking at this formula an interesting fact becomes apparent. Lowering the temperature of the cold reservoir will have more effect on the ceiling efficiency of a heat engine than raising the temperature of the hot reservoir by the same amount. In the real world, this may be difficult to achieve since the cold reservoir is often an existing ambient temperature.</p> 
     <p>In other words, maximum efficiency is achieved if and only if no new entropy is created in the cycle, which would be the case if e.g. friction leads to <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of work into heat. In that case the cycle is not reversible and the <a href="/wiki/Clausius_theorem" title="Clausius theorem">Clausius theorem</a> becomes an inequality rather than an equality. Otherwise, since entropy is a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state function</a>, the required dumping of heat into the environment to dispose of excess entropy leads to a (minimal) reduction in efficiency. So Equation 3 gives the efficiency of any <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible</a> <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>.</p> 
     <p>In mesoscopic heat engines, work per cycle of operation fluctuates due to thermal noise. For the case when work and heat fluctuations are counted, there is exact equality that relates average of exponents of work performed by any heat engine and the heat transfer from the hotter heat bath.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This relation transforms Carnot's inequality into an exact equality that applies to an arbitrary heat engine coupled to two heat reservoirs and operating at an arbitrary rate.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency_of_real_heat_engines">Efficiency of real heat engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Efficiency of real heat engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Heat_Engine#Efficiency" title="Heat Engine" class="mw-redirect">Heat engine efficiency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine#Other_criteria_of_heat_engine_performance" title="Heat engine">other performance criteria</a></i>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Carnot realized that in reality it is not possible to build a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_reversibility" title="Thermodynamic reversibility" class="mw-redirect">thermodynamically reversible</a> engine, so real heat engines are even less efficient than indicated by Equation 3. In addition, real engines that operate along this cycle are rare. Nevertheless, Equation 3 is extremely useful for determining the maximum efficiency that could ever be expected for a given set of thermal reservoirs.</p> 
     <p>Although <b>Carnot's cycle</b> is an idealisation, the expression of Carnot efficiency is still useful. Consider the <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">average</a> temperatures,</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\langle T_H\rangle = \frac{1}{\Delta S} \int_{Q_{in}} TdS" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/f/5/2f5c4f682a50cf460e564e04c3071352.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\langle T_C\rangle = \frac{1}{\Delta S} \int_{Q_{out}} TdS" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/f/d/afdd878dbc2d1e906148044f49ae7b8a.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>at which heat is input and output, respectively. Replace <i>T<sub>H</sub></i> and <i>T<sub>C</sub></i> in Equation (3) by 〈<i>T<sub>H</sub></i>〉 and 〈<i>T<sub>C</sub></i>〉 respectively.</p> 
     <p>For the Carnot cycle, or its equivalent, the average value 〈<i>T<sub>H</sub></i>〉 will equal the highest temperature available, namely <i>T<sub>H</sub></i>, and 〈<i>T<sub>C</sub></i>〉 the lowest, namely <i>T<sub>C</sub></i>. For other less efficient cycles, 〈<i>T<sub>H</sub></i>〉 will be lower than <i>T<sub>H</sub></i>, and 〈<i>T<sub>C</sub></i>〉 will be higher than <i>T<sub>C</sub></i>. This can help illustrate, for example, why a <a href="/wiki/Economizer" title="Economizer">reheater</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_heat_exchanger" title="Regenerative heat exchanger">regenerator</a> can improve the thermal efficiency of steam power plants—and why the thermal efficiency of combined-cycle power plants (which incorporate gas turbines operating at even higher temperatures) exceeds that of conventional steam plants. The first prototype of the diesel engine was based on the Carnot cycle.</p>
