     <p><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a> has been used in <a href="/wiki/Electric_wiring" title="Electric wiring" class="mw-redirect">electric wiring</a> since the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph" class="mw-redirect">telegraph</a> in the 1820s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> in 1876 created further demand for copper wire as an electrical conductor.<sup id="cite_ref-PH08_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH08-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper is the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">electrical conductor</a> in many categories of electrical wiring.<sup id="cite_ref-PH08_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH08-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper wire is used in <a href="/wiki/Power_generation" title="Power generation" class="mw-redirect">power generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_transmission" title="Power transmission">power transmission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_distribution" title="Power distribution" class="mw-redirect">power distribution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect">telecommunications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> circuitry, and countless types of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_equipment" title="Electrical equipment">electrical equipment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper and its alloys are also used to make <a href="/wiki/Electrical_contact" title="Electrical contact" class="mw-redirect">electrical contacts</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electrical_wiring" title="Electrical wiring">Electrical wiring</a> in buildings is the most important market for the copper industry.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Roughly half of all copper mined is used to manufacture electrical wire and cable conductors.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_copper">Properties of copper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties of copper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity" class="mw-redirect">Electrical conductivity</a> is a measure of how well a material transports an <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>. This is an essential property in electrical wiring systems. Copper has the highest electrical conductivity rating of all non-<a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>: the electrical resistivity of copper = 16.78 nΩ•m at 20 °C. Specially-pure <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-free_copper" title="Oxygen-free copper">Oxygen-Free Electronic (OFE) copper</a> is about 1% more conductive (i.e., achieves a minimum of 101% IACS).<sup id="cite_ref-ndt-ed_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ndt-ed-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NBS14_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBS14-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The theory of metals in their solid state<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> helps to explain the unusually high electrical conductivity of copper. In a copper <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>, the outermost 4s energy zone, or <a href="/wiki/Conduction_band" title="Conduction band" class="mw-redirect">conduction band</a>, is only half filled, so many <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> are able to carry <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. When an <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> is applied to a copper wire, the conduction of electrons accelerates towards the <a href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive" class="mw-redirect">electropositive</a> end, thereby creating a current. These electrons encounter resistance to their passage by colliding with impurity atoms, vacancies, lattice ions, and imperfections. The average distance travelled between collisions, defined as the “<a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">mean free path</a>,” is inversely proportional to the resistivity of the metal. What is unique about copper is its long mean free path (approximately 100 atomic spacings at room temperature). This mean free path increases rapidly as copper is chilled.<sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because of its superior conductivity, <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealed</a> copper became the international standard to which all other electrical conductors are compared. In 1913, the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> defined the conductivity of commercially pure copper in its International Annealed Copper Standard, as 100% IACS = 58.0&nbsp;MS/m at 20&nbsp;°C, decreasing by 0.393%/°C.<sup id="cite_ref-ndt-ed_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ndt-ed-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NBS14_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBS14-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Because commercial purity has improved over the last century, copper conductors used in building wire often slightly exceed the 100% IACS standard.<sup id="cite_ref-copper.org_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copper.org-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The main grade of copper used for electrical applications is electrolytic-tough pitch (ETP) copper (CW004A or <a href="/wiki/ASTM" title="ASTM" class="mw-redirect">ASTM</a> designation C11040). This copper is at least 99.90% pure and has an electrical conductivity of at least 101% IACS. ETP copper contains a small percentage of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (0.02 to 0.04%). If high conductivity copper needs to be <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welded</a> or <a href="/wiki/Braze" title="Braze" class="mw-redirect">brazed</a> or used in a reducing atmosphere, then <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-free_copper" title="Oxygen-free copper">oxygen-free copper</a> (CW008A or ASTM designation C10100) may be used.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Several electrically conductive metals are less dense than copper, but require larger cross sections to carry the same current and may not be usable when limited space is a major requirement.<sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-litz-wire1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-litz-wire1-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Aluminium</a> has 61% of the conductivity of copper.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The cross sectional area of an aluminium conductor must be 56% larger than copper for the same current carrying capability. The need to increase the thickness of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_wire" title="Aluminium wire" class="mw-redirect">aluminium wire</a> restricts its use in several applications,<sup id="cite_ref-litz-wire1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-litz-wire1-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> such as in small motors and automobiles. In some applications such as aerial <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric power transmission</a> cables, copper is rarely used<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Silver</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metal</a>, is the only metal with a higher electrical conductivity than copper. The electrical conductivity of silver is 106% of that of annealed copper on the IACS scale, and the electrical resistivity of silver = 15.9 nΩ•m at 20°C.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The high cost of silver combined with its low <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength" class="mw-redirect">tensile strength</a> limits its use to special applications, such as joint plating and sliding contact surfaces, and plating for the conductors in high-quality <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cables</a> used at frequencies above 30 MHz</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tensile_strength">Tensile strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tensile strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p><a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength" class="mw-redirect">Tensile strength</a> measures the force required to pull an object such as rope, wire, or a structural beam to the point where it breaks. The tensile strength of a material is the maximum amount of tensile stress it can take before breaking.</p> 
     <p>Copper’s higher tensile strength (200–250 N/mm<sup>2</sup> annealed) compared to aluminium (100 N/mm<sup>2</sup> for typical conductor alloys<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup>) is another reason why copper is used extensively in the building industry. Copper’s high strength resists stretching, neck-down, creep, nicks and breaks, and thereby also prevents failures and service interruptions.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper is much heavier than aluminum for conductors of equal current carrying capacity, so the high tensile strength is offset by its increased weight.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ductility">Ductility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ductility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">Ductility</a> is a material's ability to <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">deform</a> under <a href="/wiki/Tensile_stress" title="Tensile stress" class="mw-redirect">tensile stress</a>. This is often characterized by the material's ability to be stretched into a <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">wire</a>. Ductility is especially important in <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a> because materials that crack or break under stress cannot be hammered, rolled, or <a href="/wiki/Wire_drawing" title="Wire drawing">drawn</a> (drawing is a process that uses tensile forces to stretch metal).</p> 
     <p>Copper has a higher ductility than alternate metal conductors with the exception of gold and silver.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of copper’s high ductility, it is easy to draw down to diameters with very close tolerances.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strength_and_ductility_combination">Strength and ductility combination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Strength and ductility combination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Usually, the stronger a metal is, the less pliable it is. This is not the case with copper. A unique combination of high strength and high ductility makes copper ideal for wiring systems. At junction boxes and at terminations, for example, copper can be bent, twisted, and pulled without stretching or breaking.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creep_resistance">Creep resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Creep resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">Creep</a> is the gradual deformation of a material from constant expansions and contractions under “load, no-load” conditions. This process has adverse effects on electrical systems: terminations can become loose, causing connections to heat up or create dangerous arcing.</p> 
     <p>Copper has excellent creep characteristics which minimizes loosening at connections. For other metal conductors that creep, extra maintenance is required to check terminals periodically and ensure that screws remain tightened to prevent arcing and overheating.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrosion_resistance">Corrosion resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Corrosion resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">Corrosion</a> is the unwanted breakdown and weakening of a material due to chemical reactions. Copper generally resists corrosion from moisture, humidity, industrial pollution, and other atmospheric influences. However, any corrosion oxides, chlorides, and sulfides that do form on copper are somewhat conductive.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Under many application conditions copper is higher on the <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_series" title="Galvanic series">galvanic series</a> than other common structural metals, meaning that copper wire is less likely to be corroded in wet conditions. However, any more anodic metals in contact with copper will be corroded since will essentially be sacrificed to the copper.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion">Coefficient of thermal expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coefficient of thermal expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Metals and other solid materials expand upon heating and contract upon cooling. This is an undesirable occurrence in electrical systems. Copper has a low coefficient of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> for an electrical conducting material. Aluminium, an alternate common conductor, expands nearly one third more than copper under increasing temperatures. This higher degree of expansion, along with aluminium’s lower ductility, can cause electrical problems when bolted connections are improperly installed. By using proper hardware, such as spring pressure connections and cupped or split washers at the joint, it may be possible to create aluminium joints that compare in quality to copper joints.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_conductivity">Thermal conductivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Thermal conductivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">Thermal conductivity</a> is the ability of a material to conduct heat. In electrical systems, high thermal conductivity is important for dissipating waste heat, particularly at terminations and connections. Copper has a 60% higher thermal conductivity rating than aluminium,<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> so it is better able to reduce thermal hot spots in electrical wiring systems.<sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solderability">Solderability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Solderability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Soldering" title="Soldering">Soldering</a> is a process whereby two or more metals are joined together by a heating process. This is a desirable property in electrical systems. Copper is readily soldered to make durable connections when necessary.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ease_of_installation">Ease of installation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ease of installation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The inherent strength, hardness, and flexibility of copper building wire make it very easy to work with. Copper wiring can be installed simply and easily with no special tools, washers, pigtails, or joint compounds. Its flexibility makes it easy to join, while its hardness helps keep connections securely in place. It has good strength for pulling wire through tight places (“pull-through”), including conduits. It can be bent or twisted easily without breaking. It can be stripped and terminated during installation or service with far less danger of nicks or breaks. And it can be connected without the use of special lugs and fittings. The combination of all of these factors makes it easy for electricians to install copper wire.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CDAspam_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDAspam-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_copper_wire_and_cable">Types of copper wire and cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Types of copper wire and cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_and_stranded">Solid and stranded</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solid and stranded">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Wire#Forms_of_wire" title="Wire">Wire#Forms of wire</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg/220px-Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg/330px-Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg/440px-Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="661" data-file-height="664"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Stranded_lamp_wire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Stranded copper lamp cord, 16 gauge
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Solid wire consists of one strand of copper metal wire, bare or surrounded by an insulator. Single-strand copper conductors are typically used as magnet wire in motors and transformers. They are relatively rigid, do not bend easily, and are typically installed in permanent, infrequently handled, and low flex applications.</p> 
     <p>Stranded wire has a group of copper wires braided or twisted together. Stranded wire is more flexible and easier to install than a large single-strand wire of the same cross section. Stranding improves wire life in applications with vibration. A particular cross-section of a stranded conductor gives it essentially the same resistance characteristics as a single-strand conductor, but with added flexibility.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cable">Cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      Further information: 
      <a href="/wiki/Cable" title="Cable">Cable</a>
     </div> 
     <p>A copper cable consists of two or more copper wires running side by side and bonded, twisted or braided together to form a single assembly. Electrical cables may be made more flexible by stranding the wires.</p> 
     <p>Copper wires in a cable may be bare or they may be plated to reduce <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a> with a thin layer of another metal, most often <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> but sometimes <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>. Plating may lengthen wire life and makes <a href="/wiki/Soldering" title="Soldering">soldering</a> easier. <a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">Twisted pair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial</a> cables are designed to inhibit electromagnetic interference, prevent radiation of signals, and to provide <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission lines</a> with defined characteristics. Shielded cables are encased in foil or wire mesh.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_for_copper_wire_and_cable">Applications for copper wire and cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Applications for copper wire and cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Electrolytic-tough pitch (ETP) copper, a high-purity copper that contains <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloying</a> agent, represents the bulk of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">electrical conductor</a> applications because of its high <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity" class="mw-redirect">electrical conductivity</a> and improved <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealability</a>. ETP copper is used for <a href="/wiki/Power_transmission" title="Power transmission">power transmission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_distribution" title="Power distribution" class="mw-redirect">power distribution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect">telecommunications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Common applications include building wire, motor windings, <a href="/wiki/Cable" title="Cable">cables</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Busbar" title="Busbar">busbars</a>. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-free_copper" title="Oxygen-free copper">Oxygen-free coppers</a> are used to resist <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">hydrogen embrittlement</a> when extensive amounts of <a href="/wiki/Cold_work" title="Cold work" class="mw-redirect">cold work</a> is needed, and for applications requiring higher <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_cable" title="Telecommunications cable">telecommunications cable</a>). When hydrogen embrittlement is a concern and low electrical resistivity is not required, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> may be added to copper.<sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>For certain applications, copper alloy conductors are preferred instead of pure copper, especially when higher strengths or improved <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(mechanical)" title="Abrasion (mechanical)">abrasion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance properties are required. However, relative to pure copper, the higher strength and corrosion resistance benefits that are offered by copper alloys are offset by their lower electrical conductivities. Design engineers weigh the advantages and disadvantages of the various types of copper and copper alloy conductors when determining which type to specify for a specific electrical application. An example of a copper alloy conductor is <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> copper wire, which is used for <a href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad" class="mw-redirect">railroad</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrification" title="Electrification">electrification</a> in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In Britain the BPO (later <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Telecommunications" title="Post Office Telecommunications">Post Office Telecommunications</a>) used cadmium copper aerial lines with 1% cadmium for extra strength; for local lines 40&nbsp;lb/mile (1.3&nbsp;mm dia) and for toll lines 70&nbsp;lb/mile (1.7&nbsp;mm dia).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some of the major application markets for copper wire and cable are summarized below.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_wire">Building wire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Building wire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:MICCCable.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MICCCable.jpg/220px-MICCCable.jpg" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MICCCable.jpg/330px-MICCCable.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/MICCCable.jpg/440px-MICCCable.jpg 2x" data-file-width="990" data-file-height="984"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:MICCCable.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Mineral insulated copper clad cable (pyro).
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Building wire distributes electric power inside residential, commercial, or industrial buildings, mobile homes, recreational vehicles, boats, and substations at <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltages</a> up to 600 V. The thickness of the wire is based on <a href="/wiki/Amperage" title="Amperage" class="mw-redirect">amperage</a> requirements in conjunction with safe operating temperatures. Solid wire is used for smaller diameters; thicker diameters are stranded to provide flexibility. Conductor types include non-metallic/non-metallic corrosion-resistant cable (two or more insulated conductors with a nonmetallic outer sheath), armored or BX cable (cables are surrounded by a flexible metal enclosure), metal clad cable, service entrance cable, underground feeder cable, TC cable, fire resistant cable, and mineral insulated cable, including <a href="/wiki/Mineral-insulated_copper-clad_cable" title="Mineral-insulated copper-clad cable">mineral-insulated copper-clad cable</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper is commonly used for building wire because of its conductivity, strength, and reliability. Over the life of a building wire system, copper can also be the most economical conductor.</p> 
     <p>Copper used in building wire has a conductivity rating of 100% IACS<sup id="cite_ref-NBS14_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBS14-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> or better. Copper building wire requires less insulation and can be installed in smaller conduits than when lower-conductivity conductors are used. Also, comparatively, more copper wire can fit in a given conduit than conductors with lower conductivities. This greater “wire fill” is a special advantage when a system is rewired or expanded.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper building wire is compatible with brass and quality plated screws. The wire provides connections that will not corrode or creep. It is not, however, compatible with aluminium wire or connectors. If the two metals are joined, a galvanic reaction can occur. <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">Anodic</a> <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> during the reaction can disintegrate the aluminium. This is why most appliance and electrical equipment manufacturers use copper lead wires for connections to building wiring systems.<sup id="cite_ref-CDAspam_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDAspam-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Copper_cable_5G16.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Copper_cable_5G16.JPG/220px-Copper_cable_5G16.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Copper_cable_5G16.JPG/330px-Copper_cable_5G16.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Copper_cable_5G16.JPG/440px-Copper_cable_5G16.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2736" data-file-height="3648"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Copper_cable_5G16.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Power cable 5G16 (5 wires, green-yellow ground wire, 16mm
        <sup>2</sup>.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>"All-copper" building wiring is a term that refers to homes where the inside electrical service is carried exclusively over copper wiring. In all-copper homes, copper conductors are used in <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">circuit breaker</a> panels, <a href="/wiki/Branch_circuit" title="Branch circuit" class="mw-redirect">branch circuit</a> wiring (to outlets, switches, lighting fixtures and the like), and in dedicated branches serving heavy-load appliances (such as ranges, ovens, clothes dryers and air conditioners).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Attempts to replace copper with aluminium in building wire were curtailed in most countries when it was found that aluminium connections gradually loosened due to their inherent slow creep, combined with the high resistivity and heat generation of aluminium oxidation at joints. Spring-loaded contacts have largely alleviated this problem with aluminium conductors in building wire, but some building codes still forbid the use of aluminium.</p> 
     <p>For branch-circuit sizes, virtually all basic wiring for lights, outlets and switches is made from copper.<sup id="cite_ref-CDA_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDA-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The market for aluminium building wire today is mostly confined to larger gauge sizes used in supply circuits.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications_wire_.28for_telephone.2C_cable_TV.2C_Ethernet.29">Communications wire (for telephone, cable TV, Ethernet)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Communications wire (for telephone, cable TV, Ethernet)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Twisted_pair_cable">Twisted pair cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Twisted pair cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">Twisted pair</a> cabling is the most popular <a href="/wiki/Network_cable" title="Network cable" class="mw-redirect">network cable</a> and is often used in <a href="/wiki/Data_network" title="Data network" class="mw-redirect">data networks</a> for short and medium length connections (up to 100 meters or 328 feet).<sup id="cite_ref-LRG_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRG-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> This is due to its relatively lower costs compared to optical fiber and coaxial cable.</p> 
     <p>Unshielded twisted pair (UTP) cables are the primary cable type for telephone usage. In the late 20th century, UTPs emerged as the most common cable in computer networking cables, especially as <a href="/wiki/Patch_cable" title="Patch cable">patch cables</a> or temporary network connections.<sup id="cite_ref-electronicproducts1_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electronicproducts1-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> They are increasingly used in video applications, primarily in security cameras.</p> 
     <p>UTP <a href="/wiki/Plenum_cable" title="Plenum cable">plenum cables</a> that run above ceilings and inside walls use a solid copper core for each conductor, which enables the cable to hold its shape when bent. Patch cables, which connect computers to wall plates, use stranded copper wire because they are expected to be flexed during their lifetimes.<sup id="cite_ref-LRG_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRG-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>UTPs are the best balanced line wires available. However they are the easiest to tap into. When interference and security are concerns, shielded cable or <a href="/wiki/Fiber_optic_cable" title="Fiber optic cable" class="mw-redirect">fiber optic cable</a> is often considered.<sup id="cite_ref-LRG_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRG-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>UTP cables include: <a href="/wiki/Category_3_cable" title="Category 3 cable">Category 3 cable</a>, now the minimum requirement by the FCC (USA) for every telephone connection; <a href="/wiki/Category_5e_cable" title="Category 5e cable" class="mw-redirect">Category 5e cable</a>, 100-MHz enhanced pairs for running Gigabit Ethernet (1000Base-T); and <a href="/wiki/Category_6_cable" title="Category 6 cable">Category 6 cable</a>, where each pair runs 250&nbsp;MHz for improved 1000Base-T performance.<sup id="cite_ref-electronicproducts1_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-electronicproducts1-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:CAT5e_Cable.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/CAT5e_Cable.jpg/220px-CAT5e_Cable.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/CAT5e_Cable.jpg/330px-CAT5e_Cable.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/CAT5e_Cable.jpg/440px-CAT5e_Cable.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536"></a>

     <p>In copper twisted pair wire networks, <a href="/wiki/Copper_cable_certification" title="Copper cable certification">copper cable certification</a> is achieved through a thorough series of tests in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Industry_Association" title="Telecommunications Industry Association">Telecommunications Industry Association</a> (TIA) or <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) standards.</p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaxial_cable">Coaxial cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Coaxial cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">Coaxial cables</a> were extensively used in <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computer</a> systems and were the first type of major cable used for Local Area Networks (<a href="/wiki/LAN" title="LAN" class="mw-redirect">LAN</a>). Common applications for coaxial cable today include computer network (Internet) and instrumentation data connections, video and <a href="/wiki/CATV" title="CATV" class="mw-redirect">CATV</a> distribution, RF and microwave transmission, and feedlines connecting <a href="/wiki/Radio_transmitter" title="Radio transmitter" class="mw-redirect">radio transmitters</a> and receivers with their <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

        <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> transmission
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>While coaxial cables can go longer distances and have better protection from EMI than twisted pairs, coaxial cables are harder to work with and more difficult to run from offices to the wiring closet. For these reasons, it is now generally being replaced with less expensive UTP cables or by <a href="/wiki/Fiber_optic_cable" title="Fiber optic cable" class="mw-redirect">fiber optic cables</a> for more capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-LRG_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRG-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Today, many <a href="/wiki/CATV" title="CATV" class="mw-redirect">CATV</a> companies still use coaxial cables into homes. These cables, however, are increasingly connected to a fiber optic data communications system outside of the home. Most building management systems use proprietary copper cabling, as do paging/audio speaker systems. Security monitoring and entry systems still often depend on copper, although fiber cables are also used.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Structured_wiring">Structured wiring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Structured wiring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Most <a href="/wiki/Telephone_line" title="Telephone line">telephone lines</a> can share voice and data simultaneously. Pre-digital quad telephone wiring in homes is unable to handle communications needs for multiple phone lines, Internet service, video communications, data transmission, fax machines, and security services. <a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">Crosstalk</a>, static interference, inaudible signals, and interrupted service are common problems with outdated wiring. Computers connected to old-fashioned communications wiring often experience poor Internet performance.</p> 
     <p>“<a href="/wiki/Structured_cabling" title="Structured cabling">Structured wiring</a>” is the general term for 21st century <a href="/wiki/On-premises_wiring" title="On-premises wiring">On-premises wiring</a> for high-capacity telephone, video, data-transmission, security, control, and entertainment systems. Installations usually include a central distribution panel where all connections are made, as well as outlets with dedicated connections for phone, data, TV and audio jacks.</p> 
     <p>Structured wiring enables computers to communicate with each other error-free and at high speeds while resisting interference among various electrical sources, such as household appliances and external communications signals. Networked computers are able to share high-speed Internet connections simultaneously. Structured wiring can also connect computers with <a href="/wiki/Printer_(computing)" title="Printer (computing)">printers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">scanners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> machines, and even home security systems and home entertainment equipment.</p> 
     <p>Copper <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">Category 5</a> unshielded twisted pair (UTP) wires is the standard for carrying Internet, computer communications, and telephone signals. Category 5 has an rated bandwidth of 100&nbsp;MHz (megahertz), which is greater than a 56 Kb per second <a href="/wiki/Modem" title="Modem">modem</a>. Category 5 is increasingly being supplanted by a higher-speed version, known as Category 5e ("e" for enhanced). Category 6, which will likely accommodate at least twice the bandwidth of Category 5, will be able to carry at least 1 <a href="/wiki/Gigabit" title="Gigabit">gigabit</a> (billion bits) per second. This equates to about 50,000 pages of text per second.<sup id="cite_ref-CDAHOME_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDAHOME-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </div> Female socket connector for coaxial cable.
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Quad-shielded <a href="/wiki/RG-6" title="RG-6">RG-6</a> coaxial cable can carry a large number of TV channels at the same time. A star wiring pattern, where the wiring to each jack extends to a central distribution device, facilitates flexibility of services, problem identification, and better signal quality. This pattern has advantages to daisy chain loops. Installation tools, tips, and techniques for networked wiring systems using twisted pairs, coaxial cables, and connectors for each are available.<sup id="cite_ref-CDAHOME_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDAHOME-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Structured wiring competes with wireless systems in homes. While wireless systems certainly have convenience advantages, they also have drawbacks over copper-wired systems: the higher bandwidth of systems using Category 5e wiring typically support more than ten times the speeds of wireless systems for faster data applications and more channels for video applications. Alternatively, wireless systems are a security risk as they can transmit sensitive information to unintended users over similar receiver devices. Wireless systems are more susceptible to interference from other devices and systems, which can compromise performance.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Certain geographic areas and some buildings may be unsuitable for wireless installations, just as some buildings may present difficulties installing wires.</p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Power_distribution" title="Power distribution" class="mw-redirect">Power distribution</a> is the final stage in the delivery of electricity for an end use. A power distribution system carries electricity from the transmission system to consumers.</p> 
     <p>Power cables are used for the transmission and distribution of electric power, either outdoors or inside buildings. Details on the various types of power cables are available.<sup id="cite_ref-COBRE_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COBRE-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper is the preferred conductor material for underground transmission lines operating at high and extra-high voltages to 400 kV. The predominance of copper underground systems stems from its higher volumetric electrical and thermal conductivities compared to other conductors. These beneficial properties for copper conductors conserve space, minimize power loss, and maintain lower cable temperatures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Copper continues to dominate low-voltage lines in mines and underwater applications, as well as in electric railroads, hoists, and other outdoor services.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Aluminium, either alone or reinforced with steel, is the preferred conductor for overhead transmission lines due to its lighter weight and lower cost.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appliance_wire">Appliance wire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Appliance wire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Appliance wire for domestic applications and instruments is manufactured from bunch-stranded soft wire, which may be tinned for soldering or phase identification. Depending upon loads, insulation can be PVC, neoprene, ethylene propylene, polypropylene filler, or cotton.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automotive_wire_and_cable">Automotive wire and cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Automotive wire and cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

     <p>Automotive wire and cable requires insulation that is resistant to elevated temperatures, petroleum products, humidity, fire, and chemicals. PVC, neoprene, and polyethylene are the most common insulators. Potentials range from 12 V for electrical systems to between 300 V - 15,000 V for instruments, lighting, and ignition systems.<sup id="cite_ref-COBRE_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COBRE-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnet_wire_.28Winding_wire.29">Magnet wire (Winding wire)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Magnet wire (Winding wire)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Magnet_wire" title="Magnet wire">Magnet wire</a> is used in windings of <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inductor" title="Inductor">inductors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">generators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Headphone" title="Headphone" class="mw-redirect">headphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a> coils, hard drive head positioners, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnets</a>, and other devices.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The most suitable materials for magnet wire applications are unalloyed pure metals, particularly copper and aluminium. When factors such as chemical, physical, and mechanical property requirements are considered, copper is considered the first choice conductor for magnet wire.<sup id="cite_ref-PH95_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH95-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Most often, magnetic wire is composed of fully annealed, electrolytically refined copper to allow closer winding when making electromagnetic coils. The wire is coated with a range of <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> insulations, including <a href="/wiki/Varnish" title="Varnish">varnish</a>, rather than the thicker plastic or other types of insulation commonly used on electrical wire.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Aluminium magnet wire is sometimes used as an alternative for large transformers and motors. Because of its lower electrical conductivity, aluminium wire requires a 1.6-times larger cross sectional area than a copper wire to achieve comparable DC resistance.</p> 
     <p>High-purity <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-free_copper" title="Oxygen-free copper">oxygen-free copper</a> grades are used for high-temperature applications in reducing atmospheres or in motors or generators cooled by hydrogen gas.</p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">Electric motors</a> convert electrical energy into mechanical motion, usually through the interaction of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> and current-carrying conductors. Electric motors are found in numerous diverse applications, such as fans, blowers, pumps, machines, household appliances, power tools, and disk drives. The very largest electric motors with ratings in the thousands of kilowatts are used in such applications as the propulsion of large ships. The smallest motors move the dials in electric wristwatches.</p> 
     <p>Electric motors contain <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_coil" title="Electromagnetic coil">coils</a> to produce the required magnetic fields. For a given size of motor frame, high conductivity material allows the coils to be smaller to achieve the same level of loss due to coil resistance. Poorer conductors generate more waste heat when transferring electrical energy into kinetic energy.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Because of its high electrical conductivity, copper is commonly used in coil windings, bearings, collectors, brushes, and connectors of motors, including the highest quality motors. Copper’s greater conductivity versus other materials enhances the electrical energy efficiency of motors. For example, to reduce load losses in continuous-use induction-type motors above 1 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a>, manufacturers invariably use copper as the conducting material in windings. Aluminium is an alternate material in smaller horsepower motors, especially when motors are not used continuously.</p> 
     <p>One of the design elements of premium motors is the reduction of heat losses due to the electrical resistance of conductors. To improve the electrical energy efficiencies of induction-type motors, load loss can be reduced by increasing the cross section of copper coils. A high efficiency motor will usually have 20% more copper in the stator winding than its standard counterpart.</p> 
     <p>Early developments in motor efficiency focused on reducing electrical losses by increasing the packing weight of <a href="/wiki/Stator" title="Stator">stator</a> windings. This made sense since electrical losses typically account for more than half of all energy losses, and stator losses account for approximately two‐thirds of electrical losses.</p> 
     <p>There are, however, disadvantages in increasing the electrical efficiency of motors through larger windings. This increases motor size and cost, which may not be desirable in applications such as appliances and in automobiles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <p>A <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformer</a> is a device that transfers electrical energy from one circuit to another through its coils (windings). The properties needed for motor windings are similar to those needed for transformers, but with the additional requirement to withstand mechanical vibration and centrifugal forces at operating temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-EC_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EC-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Transformer windings are normally made from copper but aluminium is a suitable competitor where weight and first cost are decisive factors.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In North America, aluminium is the predominant choice of winding material for low-voltage, dry-type transformers larger than 15 kilovolt-amperes (kVA). In most other areas of the world, copper is the predominant winding material. Purchasing decisions are generally a function of loss valuations expressed in currency per kilowatt.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Copper used for the manufacture of transformer windings is in the form of wire for small products and strip for larger equipment. For small products, the wire must be strong enough to be wound without breakage, yet flexible enough to provide close-packed windings. Strip products must be of good surface quality so that insulating enamels do not break down under voltage. Good ductility is essential for the strip to be formed and packed while good strength is needed to withstand the high electro-mechanical stresses set up under occasional short-circuit conditions. Copper winding wires in transformers are compatible with all modern insulation materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Lacquer" title="Lacquer">lacquer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enameled_wire" title="Enameled wire" class="mw-redirect">enamel</a>. Lacquers permit the close spacing of windings to give best efficiency in the coils.<sup id="cite_ref-EC_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EC-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A major engineering reason to choose copper windings over aluminium is space considerations. This is because a copper-wound transformer can be made smaller than aluminium transformers. To obtain equal ratings in aluminium transformers, a 66% larger cross-sectional area is required than for copper conductors. However, the use of larger-sized conductors results in aluminium winding strength nearly equivalent to copper windings.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Connectivity is another important benefit of copper-wound transformers. Cleaning and brushing with a quality joint compound to prevent <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidation</a> is not necessary with copper.<sup id="cite_ref-VT_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VT-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnet_wire_in_generators">Magnet wire in generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Magnet wire in generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>The trend in modern generators is to operate at higher temperatures and higher electrical conductivities with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-free_copper" title="Oxygen-free copper">oxygen-free copper</a> for field bars and magnetic wire in place of formerly used deoxidized copper.<sup id="cite_ref-JG99_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JG99-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Splice_closures_for_copper_cable">Splice closures for copper cable</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Splice closures for copper cable">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A copper splice closure is defined as an enclosure, and the associated hardware, that is intended to restore the mechanical and environmental integrity of one or more copper cables entering the enclosure and providing some internal function for splicing, termination, or interconnection.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_closures">Types of closures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Types of closures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>As stated in <a href="/wiki/Telcordia" title="Telcordia" class="mw-redirect">Telcordia</a> industry requirements document <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://telecom-info.telcordia.com/site-cgi/ido/docs.cgi?ID=SEARCH&amp;DOCUMENT=GR-3151&amp;">GR-3151</a>, there are two principal configurations for closures: butt closures and in-line closures. Butt closures permit cables to enter the closure from one end only. This design may also be referred to as a dome closure. These closures can be used in a variety of applications, including branch splicing. In-line closures provide for the entry of cables at both ends of the closure. They can be used in a variety of applications, including branch splicing and cable access. In-line closures can also be used in a butt configuration by restricting cable access to one end of the closure.</p> 
     <p>A copper splice closure is defined by the functional design characteristics and, for the most part, is independent of specific deployment environments or applications. At this time, <a href="/wiki/Telcordia" title="Telcordia" class="mw-redirect">Telcordia</a> has identified two (2) types of copper closures:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li>Environmentally Sealed Closures (ESCs)</li> 
      <li>Free-Breathing Closures (FBCs)</li> 
     </ol> 
     <p>ESCs provide all of the features and functions expected of a typical splice closure in an enclosure that prevents the intrusion of liquid and vapor into the closure interior. This is accomplished through the use of an environmental sealing system such as rubber gaskets or hot-melt adhesives. Some ESCs use pressurized air to help keep moisture out of the closure.</p> 
     <p>FBCs provide all of the features and functions expected of a typical splice closure that prevents the intrusion of wind-driven rain, dust, and insects. Such a closure, however, permits the free exchange of air with the outside environment. Therefore, it is possible that condensation will form inside the closure. It is thus necessary to provide adequate drainage to prevent the accumulation of water inside the closure.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_future_trends">Some future trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper_wire_and_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Some future trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>Copper will continue to be the predominant material in most electrical wire applications, especially where space considerations are important.<sup id="cite_ref-PH08_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH08-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The automotive industry for decades has considered the use of smaller-diameter wires in certain applications. Many manufacturers are beginning to use copper alloys such as copper-magnesium (CuMg), which allow for smaller diameter wires with less weight and improved conductivity performance. Special alloys like copper-magnesium are beginning to see increased usage in automotive, aerospace, and defense applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisk_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisk-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Due to the need to increase the transmission of high-speed voice and data signals, the surface quality of copper wire is expected to continue to improve. Demands for better drawability and movement towards “zero” defects in copper conductors are expected to continue.</p> 
     <p>A minimum mechanical strength requirement for magnet wire may evolve in order to improve formability and prevent excessive stretching of wire during high speed coiling operations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>It does not seem likely that standards for copper wire purity will increase beyond the current minimum value of 101% IACS. Although 6-nines copper (99.9999% pure) has been produced in small quantities, it is extremely expensive and probably unnecessary for most commercial applications such as magnet, telecommunications, and building wire. The electrical conductivity of 6-nines copper and 4-nines copper (99.99% pure) is nearly the same at ambient temperature, although the higher-purity copper has a higher conductivity at cryogenic temperatures. Therefore, for non-cryogenic temperatures, 4-nines copper will probably remain the dominant material for most commercial wire applications.<sup id="cite_ref-PH08_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PH08-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
