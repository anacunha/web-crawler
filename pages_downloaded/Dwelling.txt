   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dwelling</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

     <p>In law, a <b>dwelling</b> (also <b>residence</b>, <b>abode</b>) is a self-contained unit of accommodation used by one or more <a href="/wiki/Households" title="Households" class="mw-redirect">households</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">home</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile home</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houseboat" title="Houseboat">houseboat</a> or other 'substantial' <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structure</a>. A dwelling typically includes nearby outbuildings, sheds etc. within the <a href="/wiki/Curtilage" title="Curtilage">curtilage</a> of the property, excluding any '<a href="/wiki/Open_fields_doctrine" title="Open fields doctrine" class="mw-redirect">open fields beyond</a>'. It has significance in relation to <a href="/wiki/Search_and_seizure" title="Search and seizure">search and seizure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conveyancing" title="Conveyancing">conveyancing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">burglary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning" class="mw-redirect">land use planning</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_definitions">Legal definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Legal definitions vary by <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Dwelling.27_.28England_and_Wales.29">'Dwelling' (England and Wales)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 'Dwelling' (England and Wales)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Under <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> a dwelling is defined as a self-contained 'substantial' unit of accommodation, such as a building, part of a building, caravan, houseboat or other mobile home. A tent is not normally considered to be substantial.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Dwelling.27_.28North_Carolina.29">'Dwelling' (North Carolina)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 'Dwelling' (North Carolina)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>According to N.C. Gen. Stat. § 160A-442, "Dwelling" means any building, structure, manufactured home or mobile home, or part thereof, used and occupied for human habitation or intended to be so used, and includes any outhouses and appurtenances belonging thereto or usually enjoyed therewith, except that it does not include any manufactured home or mobile home, which is used solely for a seasonal vacation purpose.</p> 
     <p>According to N.C. Gen. Stat. § 53-244.030, "Dwelling" means a residential structure that contains one to four units, whether or not that structure is attached to real property. The term includes an individual condominium unit, cooperative unit, manufactured home, mobile home, or trailer if it is used as a residence. Under the Oregon law, a "dwelling" is defined as a "building which regularly or intermittently is occupied by a person lodging therein at night, whether or not a person is actually present." United States v. Adams, 2009 U.S. App. LEXIS 25866 (9th Cir. Or. Nov. 25, 2009)</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Habitual_residence.27_.28international_law.29">'Habitual residence' (international law)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 'Habitual residence' (international law)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international conventions</a> a person can have only one <a href="/wiki/Habitual_residence" title="Habitual residence">habitual residence</a>, being the place where the individual ordinarily resides and routinely returns to after visiting other places for a reasonably significant period of time. It is used to determine the law which should be applied to determine a given legal dispute. The <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conference_on_Private_International_Law" title="Hague Conference on Private International Law">Hague Conference on Private International Law</a> has deliberately refrained from offering a definition so that the concept may be flexible and adaptable to practical requirements.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".27Inhabited_dwelling.27_.28Various_US_States.29">'Inhabited dwelling' (Various US States)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 'Inhabited dwelling' (Various US States)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>In California, Criminal Penal Code 246 which refers to shooting a firearm at an inhabited dwelling. This code specifies that a "dwelling" (more commonly referred to as a house) is "inhabited" if a person lives in it; it is irrelevant whether or not anyone is present. A house, building, or structure is not considered "inhabited" or "occupied" if the occupants have moved out or vacated and do not intend to return, even if personal property is/was left behind. Therefore, it would no longer be considered a dwelling for legal purposes, which from a defense standpoint, would negate a conviction under this code. For prosectors, it is advantageous to construe these terms loosely in order to secure as many convictions as possible for violation of this code. Examples of loose interpretation exist not only in California, but also in other states such as Colorado where similar code (Colorado Code § 18-1-901(3)(g)) applies in cases even when a shooting at an detached garage that does not traditionally constitute a dwelling or house. However, courts in both of these states and others have held that it does qualify as an occupied building for purposes of criminal conviction.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.27curtilage.27_.28boundary.29_of_a_dwelling">The 'curtilage' (boundary) of a dwelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dwelling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The 'curtilage' (boundary) of a dwelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Curtilage" title="Curtilage">curtilage</a>
     </div> 
     <p>In law, the <a href="/wiki/Curtilage" title="Curtilage">curtilage</a> of a dwelling is the land immediately surrounding it, including any closely associated buildings and structures. It delineates the boundary within which a home owner can have a <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_expectation_of_privacy" title="Reasonable expectation of privacy" class="mw-redirect">reasonable expectation of privacy</a> with particular relevance to <a href="/wiki/Search_and_seizure" title="Search and seizure">search and seizure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conveyancing" title="Conveyancing">conveyancing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">burglary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning" class="mw-redirect">land use planning</a>. In urban properties, the location of the curtilage may be evident from the position of fences, wall and similar; within larger properties it may be a matter of some legal debate as to where the private area ends and any '<a href="/wiki/Open_fields_doctrine" title="Open fields doctrine" class="mw-redirect">open fields beyond</a>'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
