   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Capacity factor</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

     <p>The net <b>capacity factor</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant" class="mw-redirect">power plant</a> is the ratio of its actual output over a period of time, to its potential output if it were possible for it to operate at full <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">nameplate capacity</a> continuously over the same period of time. To calculate the capacity factor, take the total amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> the plant produced during a period of time and divide by the amount of energy the plant would have produced at full capacity. Capacity factors vary greatly depending on the type of <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> that is used and the design of the plant. The capacity factor should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Availability_factor" title="Availability factor">availability factor</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_credit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Capacity credit (page does not exist)">capacity credit</a> (firm capacity) or with <a href="/wiki/Betz%27_law" title="Betz' law" class="mw-redirect">efficiency</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_calculations">Sample calculations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sample calculations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_power_plant">Nuclear power plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nuclear power plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png/220px-Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png/330px-Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png/440px-Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Nuclear_Power_Capacity_Factor.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Nuclear Power Capacity Factor
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>A nuclear power plant with a capacity of 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt" class="mw-redirect">megawatts</a> (MW) might produce 648,000 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt-hour" title="Megawatt-hour" class="mw-redirect">megawatt-hours</a> (MW·h) in a 30-day month. The number of megawatt-hours that would have been produced had the plant been operating at full capacity can be determined by multiplying the plant's maximum capacity by the number of hours in the time period. 1,000&nbsp;MW ×&nbsp;30&nbsp;days ×&nbsp;24&nbsp;hours/day is 720,000&nbsp;MW·h. The capacity factor is determined by dividing the actual output with the maximum possible output. In this case, the capacity factor is 0.9 (90%).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{648,000\ \mbox{MW·h}}{(30\ \mbox{days}) \times (24\ \mbox{hours/day}) \times (1000\ \mbox{MW})}=0.9={90\%}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/5/3/2534e9f03e09e1bc2519825fe4b893f9.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Palo_Verde_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Palo Verde Nuclear Generating Station">Palo Verde Nuclear Generating Station</a> is the largest nuclear plant in the US with a nameplate capacity of 3,942 MW between its three reactors. With an annual generation of 31,200,000 MWh in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> the capacity factor was 90.4%.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{31,200,000\ \mbox{MW·h}}{(365\ \mbox{days}) \times (24\ \mbox{hours/day}) \times (3942\ \mbox{MW})}=0.904={90.4\%}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/9/c/c9cb0f8b0f9ab55c12c0c64cb8b742bb.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>Each of Palo Verde’s three reactors are refueled every 18 months, with one refuel every spring and fall. In 2014, a refueling was completed in a record 28 days.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_farm">Wind farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wind farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The Danish wind farm <a href="/wiki/Horns_Rev_2" title="Horns Rev 2">Horns Rev 2</a>, the world's largest in 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> comprises 91 <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Wind_Power" title="Siemens Wind Power">Siemens</a> SWT-2.3-93 wind turbines each of 2.3 MW, with a nominal total capacity of 209 MW.</p> 
     <p>In 2012, the wind farm generated 956 &nbsp;GW·h of electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The capacity factor for this wind farm was 52.1%.</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{956,000\ \mbox{MW·h}}{(365\ \mbox{days}) \times (24\ \mbox{hours/day}) \times (209.3\ \mbox{MW})}=0.521 \approx{52\%}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/0/f/d0f2d7fcf107e8306f1dcdecb541ac22.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The record for an onshore wind farm is held by Burradale, which reached an annual capacity factor of 57.9% for 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Note that capacity factor is not constrained by the <a href="/wiki/Betz%27s_law" title="Betz's law">Betz limit</a> so capacities higher that 59.2% are possible. This is because capacity measures actual production relative to possible production by the turbine, as opposed to production vs. energy available in the wind.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectric_dam">Hydroelectric dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydroelectric dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Large rivers or large reservoirs in wet mountainous areas are ideal water supplies for <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, in Western Canada the Peace and Columbia rivers support capacity factors averaging 85% of a 10,000MW capacity in the mild, rainy oceanic climate.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2010, <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> is the largest power generating station in the world by nameplate capacity. In 2009, not yet fully complete, it had 26 main generator units @&nbsp;700&nbsp;MW and two auxiliary generator units @&nbsp;50&nbsp;MW for a total installed capacity of 18,300&nbsp;MW. Total generation in 2009 was 79.47&nbsp;TW·h, for a capacity factor of just under 50%:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{79,470,000\ \mbox{MW·h}}{(365\ \mbox{days}) \times (24\ \mbox{hours/day}) \times (18,300\ \mbox{MW})}=0.4957 \approx{50\%}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/9/b/a9b9da60cee6bfcaa9768ec8fdfd9562.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">Hoover Dam</a> has a nameplate capacity of 2080 MW<sup id="cite_ref-hoover_dam_faq_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoover_dam_faq-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and an annual generation averaging 4.2&nbsp;TW·h.<sup id="cite_ref-hoover_dam_faq_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoover_dam_faq-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> (The annual generation has varied between a high of 10.348&nbsp;TW·h in 1984, and a low of 2.648&nbsp;TW·h in 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-hoover_dam_faq_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoover_dam_faq-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup>) Taking the average figure for annual generation gives a capacity factor of:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="\frac{4,200,000\ \mbox{MW·h}}{(365\ \mbox{days}) \times (24\ \mbox{hours/day}) \times (2,080\ \mbox{MW})}=0.23 = 23\%" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/6/7/b67ff3c22e50ba77caaa3cc003eb8dc7.png">
      </dd>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_reduced_capacity_factor">Reasons for reduced capacity factor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reasons for reduced capacity factor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

     <p>There are several reasons why a plant would have a capacity factor lower than 100%. The first reason is that it was out of service or operating at reduced output for part of the time due to equipment failures or routine maintenance. This accounts for most of the unused capacity of base load power plants. Base load plants have the lowest costs per unit of electricity because they are designed for maximum efficiency and are operated continuously at high output. <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power" class="mw-redirect">Geothermal plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant" class="mw-redirect">coal-fired plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">bioenergy plants</a> that burn solid material are almost always operated as base load plants.</p> 
     <p>The second reason that a plant would have a capacity factor lower than 100% is that output is curtailed because the electricity is not needed or because the price of electricity is too low to make production economical. This accounts for most of the unused capacity of <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking power plants</a>. Peaking plants may operate for only a few hours per year or up to several hours per day. Their electricity is relatively expensive. It is uneconomical, even wasteful, to make a peaking power plant as efficient as a base load plant because they do not operate enough to pay for the extra equipment cost, and perhaps not enough to offset the <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> of the additional components.</p> 
     <p>A third reason is a variation on the second: the operators of a hydroelectric dam may <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage#Hydroelectric_dams" title="Grid energy storage">uprate its nameplate capacity</a> by adding more generator units. Since the supply of fuel (i.e. water) remains unchanged, the uprated dam obtains a higher peak output in exchange for a lower capacity factor. Because hydro plants are highly <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">dispatchable</a>, they are able to act as <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">load following power plants</a>. Having a higher peak capacity allows a dam's operators to sell more of the annual output of electricity during the hours of highest electricity demand (and thus the highest <a href="/wiki/Spot_price" title="Spot price" class="mw-redirect">spot price</a>). In practical terms, uprating a dam allows it to <a href="/wiki/Load_balancing_(electrical_power)" title="Load balancing (electrical power)">balance</a> a larger amount of <a href="/wiki/Variable_renewable_energy" title="Variable renewable energy">variable renewable energy</a> sources on the grid such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_plant" title="Solar power plant" class="mw-redirect">solar power plants</a>, and to compensate for unscheduled shutdowns of baseload power plants, or brief surges in demand for electricity.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Load_following_power_plants">Load following power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Load following power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">Load following power plants</a>, also called intermediate power plants, are in between these extremes in terms of capacity factor, efficiency and cost per unit of electricity. They produce most of their electricity during the day, when prices and demand are highest. However, the demand and price of electricity is far lower during the night and intermediate plants shutdown or reduce their output to low levels overnight.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity_factor_and_renewable_energy">Capacity factor and renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Capacity factor and renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:578px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:US_EIA_monthly_capacity_factors_for_renewables_2011-2013.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/89/US_EIA_monthly_capacity_factors_for_renewables_2011-2013.png" width="576" height="287" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="287"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">US EIA</a> monthly capacity factors for renewables, 2011-2013
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>When it comes to several <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a>, there is a fourth reason for unused capacity. The plant may be capable of producing electricity, but its "fuel" (<a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> or <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>) may not be available. A hydroelectric plant's production may also be affected by requirements to keep the water level from getting too high or low and to provide water for <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> downstream. However, solar, wind and hydroelectric plants do have high <a href="/wiki/Availability_factor" title="Availability factor">availability factors</a>, so when they have fuel available, they are almost always able to produce electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>When hydroelectric plants have water available, they are also useful for load following, because of their high <i>dispatchability</i>. A typical hydroelectric plant's operators can bring it from a stopped condition to full power in just a few minutes.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">Wind farms</a> are variable, due to the natural variability of the wind. For a wind farm, the capacity factor is mostly determined by the availability of wind. Transmission line capacity and electricity demand also affect the capacity factor. Typical capacity factors of current wind farms are between 25 and 45%, though current 110 meter towers can have up to 55% capacity factor, and future 140 meter towers are expected to have up to 65% capacity factor.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> is variable because of the daily rotation of the earth, seasonal changes, and because of cloud cover. According to,<sup id="cite_ref-Blees2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blees2008-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> the Sacramento Municipal Utility District observed a 15% capacity factor in 2005. However, according to the <a href="/wiki/SolarPACES" title="SolarPACES">SolarPACES</a> programme of the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> (IEA), solar power plants designed for solar-only generation are well matched to summer noon peak loads in areas with significant cooling demands, such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Spain" title="Solar power in Spain">Spain</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_plants_in_the_Mojave_Desert" title="Solar power plants in the Mojave Desert">south-western United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-mangey_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mangey-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> although in some locations solar PV does not reduce the need for generation of network upgrades given that air conditioner peak demand often occurs in the late afternoon or early evening when solar output is reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> SolarPACES states that by using thermal energy storage systems the operating periods of <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_power" title="Solar thermal power" class="mw-redirect">solar thermal power</a> (CSP) stations can be extended to become dispatchable (load following).<sup id="cite_ref-mangey_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mangey-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The IEA CSP Technology Roadmap (2010) suggests that "in the sunniest countries, CSP can be expected to become a competitive source of bulk power in peak and intermediate loads by 2020, and of base-load power by 2025 to 2030".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> A dispatchable source is more valuable than baseload power.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal" class="mw-redirect">Geothermal</a> has a higher capacity factor than many other power sources, and geothermal resources are available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. While the carrier medium for <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_electricity" title="Geothermal electricity">geothermal electricity</a> (water) must be properly managed, the source of geothermal energy, the Earth's heat, will be available for the foreseeable future.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Geothermal power can be looked at as a nuclear battery where the heat is produced via the decay of radioactive elements in the core and mantle of the earth.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capacity_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>According to the US Energy Information Administration (EIA), in 2009 the capacity factors were as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Nuclear–90.3%</li> 
      <li>Coal–63.8%</li> 
      <li>Natural Gas Plant–42.5%</li> 
      <li>Hydroelectric–39.8%</li> 
      <li>Renewables (Wind/Solar/Biomass)–33.9%</li> 
      <li>Oil–7.8%</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>However, these values often vary significantly by month.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Nuclear power 88.7% (2006 - 2012 average of US's plants).<sup id="cite_ref-ModernNucCapacity_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ModernNucCapacity-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Hydroelectricity, worldwide average 44%,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> range of 10% - 99% depending on water availability (with or without regulation via storage dam).</li> 
      <li>Wind farms 20-40%.<sup id="cite_ref-RERLWind_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RERLWind-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWEAMyth_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWEAMyth-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>CSP solar with storage and Natural Gas backup in Spain 63%.<sup id="cite_ref-CSPStorageCapacity_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSPStorageCapacity-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>CSP solar in California 33%.<sup id="cite_ref-CSPCapacity_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSPCapacity-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li>Photovoltaic solar in Germany 10%, Arizona 19%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-THSolarVsWind_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-THSolarVsWind-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arni_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arni-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
