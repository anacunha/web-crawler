     <p><b>Food microbiology</b> is the study of the <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> that inhabit, create, or <a href="/wiki/Food_contaminant" title="Food contaminant">contaminate</a> <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, including the study of microorganisms causing food spoilage.<sup id="cite_ref-FratamicoBayles_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FratamicoBayles-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> "Good" bacteria, however, such as <a href="/wiki/Probiotics" title="Probiotics" class="mw-redirect">probiotics</a>, are becoming increasingly important in <a href="/wiki/Food_science" title="Food science">food science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tannockpro3_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tannockpro3-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LjunghWadstrom_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LjunghWadstrom-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MayoB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayoB-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, microorganisms are essential for the production of foods such as <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> and, other <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">fermented foods</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_safety">Food safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Food safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">Food safety</a> is a major focus of food microbiology. Pathogenic <a href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium" class="mw-redirect">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">toxins</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> are all possible contaminants of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms" class="mw-redirect">microorganisms</a> and their products can also be used to combat these pathogenic microbes. <a href="/wiki/Probiotic" title="Probiotic">Probiotic</a> bacteria, including those that produce <a href="/wiki/Bacteriocin" title="Bacteriocin">bacteriocins</a>, can kill and inhibit <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Alternatively, purified <a href="/wiki/Bacteriocin" title="Bacteriocin">bacteriocins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Nisin" title="Nisin">nisin</a> can be added directly to food products. Finally, <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophages</a>, viruses that only infect <a href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium" class="mw-redirect">bacteria</a>, can be <a href="/wiki/Phage_therapy" title="Phage therapy">used to kill bacterial pathogens</a>. Thorough preparation of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, including proper <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>, eliminates most bacteria and viruses. However, <i>toxins produced</i> by contaminants may not be liable to change to non-toxic forms by heating or <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> the contaminated food.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation">Fermentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fermentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">
      See also: 
      <a href="/wiki/Yeast_in_winemaking" title="Yeast in winemaking">Yeast in winemaking</a>
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect">Fermentation</a> is one of the methods to preserve food and alter its quality. <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">Yeast</a>, especially <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, is used to leaven <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brew</a> <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and make <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>. Certain <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_bacteria" title="Lactic acid bacteria" class="mw-redirect">lactic acid bacteria</a>, are used to make <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_sauce" title="Hot sauce">hot sauce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickled_cucumber" title="Pickled cucumber">pickles</a>, fermented sausages and dishes such as <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>. A common effect of these fermentations is that the food product is less hospitable to other <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoilage" title="Spoilage" class="mw-redirect">spoilage</a>-causing microorganisms, thus extending the food's <a href="/wiki/Shelf-life" title="Shelf-life" class="mw-redirect">shelf-life</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> varieties also require <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">molds</a> to ripen and develop their characteristic <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavors</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_biopolymers">Microbial biopolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Microbial biopolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Several microbially produced polymers are used in the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BerndRehm_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BerndRehm-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alginate">Alginate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alginate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Alginates" title="Alginates" class="mw-redirect">Alginates</a> can be used as thickening agents.<sup id="cite_ref-2Remminghorst_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2Remminghorst-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although listed here under the category 'Microbial polysaccharides', commercial alginates are currently only produced by extraction from brown seaweeds such as <i>Laminaria hyperborea</i> or <i>L. japonica</i>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poly-.CE.B3-glutamic_acid">Poly-γ-glutamic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poly-γ-glutamic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Polyglutamic_acid" title="Polyglutamic acid">Poly-γ-glutamic acid</a> (γ-PGA) produced by various strains of <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus" title="Bacillus">Bacillus</a></i> has potential applications as a thickener in the food industry.<sup id="cite_ref-5shih_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5shih-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_authenticity">Food authenticity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food authenticity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>It is important to be able to detect microorganisms in food, in particular pathogenic microorganisms or <a href="/wiki/Detection_of_genetically_modified_organisms" title="Detection of genetically modified organisms">genetically modified microorganisms</a>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_testing">Food testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_microbiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>To ensure <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">safety of food products</a>, microbiological tests such as testing for <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> and spoilage organisms are required. This way the risk of contamination under normal use conditions can be examined and <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">food poisoning</a> outbreaks can be prevented. Testing of food products and ingredients is important along the whole <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> as possible flaws of products can occur at every stage of production.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Apart from detecting spoilage, microbiological tests can also determine germ content, identify yeasts and molds, and <a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">salmonella</a>. For salmonella, scientists are also developing rapid and portable technologies capable of identifying unique variants of Salmonella .<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">Polymerase Chain Reaction</a> (PCR) is a quick and inexpensive method to generate numbers of copies of a DNA fragment at a specific band ("PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)," 2008). For that reason, scientists are using PCR to detect different kinds of viruses or bacteria, such as HIV and anthrax based on their unique DNA patterns. Various kits are commercially available to help in food pathogen nucleic acids extraction,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> PCR detection, and differentiation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The detection of bacterial strands in food products is very important to everyone in the world, for it helps prevent the occurrence of food borne illness. Therefore, PCR is recognized as a DNA detector in order to amplify and trace the presence of pathogenic strands in different processed food.</p>
