     <p><b>High-density polyethylene</b> (<b>HDPE</b>) or <b>polyethylene high-density</b> (<b>PEHD</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. It is sometimes called "alkathene" or "polythene" when used for pipes.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> With a high strength-to-density ratio, HDPE is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottles" title="Plastic bottles" class="mw-redirect">plastic bottles</a>, corrosion-resistant piping, <a href="/wiki/Geomembrane" title="Geomembrane">geomembranes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic_lumber" title="Plastic lumber">plastic lumber</a>. HDPE is commonly recycled, and has the number "2" as its resin identification code (formerly known as <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">recycling symbol</a>).</p> 
     <p>In 2007, the global HDPE market reached a volume of more than 30 million tons.<sup id="cite_ref-ceresana_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ceresana-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-density_polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>HDPE is known for its large strength-to-density ratio.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The density of HDPE can range from 0.93 to 0.97 g/cm<sup>3</sup> or 970&nbsp;kg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the density of HDPE is only marginally higher than that of <a href="/wiki/Low-density_polyethylene" title="Low-density polyethylene">low-density polyethylene</a>, HDPE has little <a href="/wiki/Branching_(chemistry)" title="Branching (chemistry)" class="mw-redirect">branching</a>, giving it stronger <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength" class="mw-redirect">tensile strength</a> than LDPE. The difference in strength exceeds the difference in density, giving HDPE a higher <a href="/wiki/Specific_strength" title="Specific strength">specific strength</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also harder and more opaque and can withstand somewhat higher <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a> (120&nbsp;°C/ 248&nbsp;°F for short periods, 110&nbsp;°C /230&nbsp;°F continuously). High-density polyethylene, unlike <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>, cannot withstand normally required <a href="/wiki/Autoclave" title="Autoclave">autoclaving</a> conditions. The lack of branching is ensured by an appropriate choice of <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst" class="mw-redirect">catalyst</a> (<i>e.g.</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalyst" title="Ziegler-Natta catalyst" class="mw-redirect">Ziegler-Natta catalysts</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">reaction</a> conditions.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-density_polyethylene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>HDPE is resistant to many different <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> and has a wide variety of applications:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Swimming pool installation</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/3-D_printer" title="3-D printer" class="mw-redirect">3-D printer</a> filament</li> 
      <li>Arena Board (puck board)</li> 
      <li>Backpacking frames</li> 
      <li>Ballistic plates</li> 
      <li>Banners</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Bottle_cap" title="Bottle cap">Bottle caps</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_resistance" title="Chemical resistance">Chemical-resistant</a> piping</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Coax_cable" title="Coax cable" class="mw-redirect">Coax cable</a> inner insulator</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Food_storage_container" title="Food storage container">Food storage containers</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">Fuel tanks</a> for vehicles</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">Corrosion</a> protection for steel pipelines</li> 
      <li>Personal Hovercraft; albeit too heavy for good performance</li> 
      <li>Electrical and plumbing boxes</li> 
      <li>Far-IR <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Folding_chair" title="Folding chair">Folding chairs</a> and tables</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Geomembrane" title="Geomembrane">Geomembrane</a> for hydraulic applications (such as canals and bank reinforcements) and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical</a> containment</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">Geothermal</a> heat transfer piping systems</li> 
      <li>Heat-resistant firework <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(weapon)" title="Mortar (weapon)">mortars</a></li> 
      <li>* <a href="/wiki/Last" title="Last">Last</a> for shoes</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> distribution pipe systems</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">Fireworks</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Plastic_bag" title="Plastic bag">Plastic bags</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottles" title="Plastic bottles" class="mw-redirect">Plastic bottles</a> suitable both for recycling (such as milk jugs) or re-use</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Plastic_lumber" title="Plastic lumber">Plastic lumber</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">Plastic surgery</a> (skeletal and facial reconstruction)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Root_barrier" title="Root barrier">Root barrier</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Snowboard" title="Snowboard">Snowboard</a> rails and boxes</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Stone_paper" title="Stone paper">Stone paper</a></li> 
      <li>Storage sheds</li> 
      <li>Telecom ducts</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Tyvek" title="Tyvek">Tyvek</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Water_pipe" title="Water pipe">Water pipes</a> for domestic water supply and agricultural processes</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Wood_plastic_composite" title="Wood plastic composite" class="mw-redirect">Wood plastic composites</a> (utilizing <a href="/wiki/Plastic_recycling" title="Plastic recycling">recycled polymers</a>)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>HDPE is also used for <a href="/wiki/Landfill_liner" title="Landfill liner">cell liners</a> in <a href="/wiki/Resource_Conservation_and_Recovery_Act#Subtitle_D:_Non-hazardous_Solid_Wastes" title="Resource Conservation and Recovery Act">subtitle D</a> <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_landfill" title="Sanitary landfill" class="mw-redirect">sanitary landfills</a>, wherein large sheets of HDPE are either <a href="/wiki/Extrusion" title="Extrusion">extrusion</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wedge_welding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wedge welding (page does not exist)">wedge welded</a> to form a <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Homogeneous" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Homogeneous">homogeneous</a> chemical-resistant barrier, with the intention of preventing the <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> by the liquid constituents of <a href="/wiki/Solid_waste" title="Solid waste" class="mw-redirect">solid waste</a>.</p> 
     <p>HDPE is preferred by the <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a> trade for mortars over steel or <a href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC" class="mw-redirect">PVC</a> tubes, being more durable and safer. HDPE tends to rip or tear in a malfunction instead of shattering and becoming shrapnel like the other materials.</p> 
     <p>Milk jugs and other hollow goods manufactured through <a href="/wiki/Blow_molding" title="Blow molding">blow molding</a> are the most important application area for HDPE, accounting for one-third of worldwide production, or more than 8 million tons. In addition to being <a href="/wiki/Recycled" title="Recycled" class="mw-redirect">recycled</a> using conventional processes, HDPE can also be processed by <a href="/wiki/Recyclebots" title="Recyclebots" class="mw-redirect">recyclebots</a> into filament for <a href="/wiki/3-D_printers" title="3-D printers" class="mw-redirect">3-D printers</a> via <a href="/w/index.php?title=Distributed_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Distributed recycling (page does not exist)">distributed recycling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RPJ_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPJ-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> There is some evidence that this form of recycling is less energy intensive than conventional recycling, which can involve a large <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> for transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Above all, China, where beverage bottles made from HDPE were first imported in 2005, is a growing market for rigid HDPE packaging, as a result of its improving standard of living. In India and other highly populated, emerging nations, infrastructure expansion includes the deployment of pipes and cable insulation made from HDPE.<sup id="cite_ref-ceresana_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ceresana-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The material has benefited from discussions about possible health and environmental problems caused by PVC and Polycarbonate associated <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">Bisphenol A</a>, as well as its advantages over glass, metal, and cardboard.</p>
