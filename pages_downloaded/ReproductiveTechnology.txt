     <p><b>Reproductive technology</b> (<b>RT</b>) encompasses all current and anticipated uses of technology in human and animal <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted reproductive technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contraception" title="Contraception" class="mw-redirect">contraception</a> and others.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assisted_reproductive_technology">Assisted reproductive technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assisted reproductive technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">Assisted reproductive technology</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Assisted reproductive technology (ART) is the use of reproductive technology to treat infertility. This is today the only application of reproductive technology to increase reproduction that is used routinely. Examples include <i>in vitro</i> fertilization and its possible expansions.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Artificial_reproduction" title="Artificial reproduction">artificial reproduction</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloning</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Human_cloning" title="Human cloning">human cloning</a> for the special case of human beings)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_transfer" title="Cytoplasmic transfer">cytoplasmic transfer</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation" title="Cryopreservation">cryopreservation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">sperm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oocyte" title="Oocyte">oocytes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryos" title="Embryos" class="mw-redirect">embryos</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Embryo_transfer" title="Embryo transfer">embryo transfer</a></li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Fertility_medication" title="Fertility medication">fertility medication</a></li>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognostics">Prognostics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prognostics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Reproductive technology also includes methods to give an individual prognosis about future chances of pregnancy, facilitating an informed choice of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a>. In women, such methods include mapping of a woman's <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_reserve" title="Ovarian reserve">ovarian reserve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_follicle" title="Ovarian follicle">follicular</a> dynamics and associated <a href="/wiki/Female_reproductive_biomarkers" title="Female reproductive biomarkers" class="mw-redirect">biomarkers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In males, it includes <a href="/wiki/Semen_analysis" title="Semen analysis">semen analysis</a>.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contraception">Contraception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contraception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Contraception" title="Contraception" class="mw-redirect">Contraception</a> is a form of reproductive technology that enables people to control their fertility.</p>

     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Same-sex_procreation">Same-sex procreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Same-sex procreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <p>Same-sex procreation (where two females could have a daughter with equal genetic contributions from both, or where two males could have a son or daughter with equal genetic contributions from both) has become a possibility through the creation of either <a href="/wiki/Female_sperm" title="Female sperm">female sperm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Male_egg" title="Male egg">male eggs</a> from the cells of adult women and men. With female sperm and male eggs, lesbian and gay couples wishing to become parents would not have to rely on a third party donor of sperm or egg.</p> 
     <p>The first significant development occurred in 1991, in a patent application filed by U.Penn. scientists to fix male sperm by extracting some sperm, correcting a genetic defect in vitro, and injecting the sperm back into the male's testicles.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> While the vast majority of the patent application dealt with male sperm, one line suggested that the procedure would work with XX cells, i.e., cells from an adult woman to make female sperm.</p> 
     <p>In the two decades that followed, the idea of female sperm became more of a reality. In 1997, scientists partially confirmed such techniques by creating chicken female sperm in a similar manner.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> They did so by injecting blood stem cells from an adult female chicken into a male chicken's testicles. Some years later, other Japanese scientists created female offspring by combining the eggs of two adult mice.</p> 
     <p>In 2008, research was done specifically for methods on creating human female sperm using artificial or natural <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosomes</a> and testicular transplantation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> A UK-based group predicted they would be able to create human female sperm within five years.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Many issues of reproductive technology have given rise to <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">bioethical</a> issues, since technology often alters the assumptions that lie behind existing systems of sexual and reproductive <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>.</p> 
     <p>Also, ethical issues of <a href="/wiki/Human_enhancement" title="Human enhancement">human enhancement</a> arise when reproductive technology has evolved to be a potential technology for not only reproductively inhibited people but even for otherwise reproductively healthy people.</p> 
     <p><i>See individual subarticles for details</i></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Films and other fiction depicting contemporary emotional struggles of <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted reproductive technology</a> have had an upswing first in the latter part of the 2000s decade, although the techniques have been available for decades.<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Yet, the amount of people that can relate to it 
       <div role="note" class="hatnote">
        Further information: 
        <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology#Fictional_representation" title="Assisted reproductive technology">Assisted reproductive technology § Fictional representation</a>
       </div> </li> 
      <li>Science fiction has tackled the themes of creating life through other than the conventional methods since <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">Mary Shelley</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein" title="Frankenstein">Frankenstein</a></i>. In the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brave_New_World" title="Brave New World">Brave New World</a></i> (1932) was the first major fictional work to anticipate the possible social consequences of reproductive technology. Its largely negative view was reversed when the author revisited the same themes in his utopian final novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Island_(Huxley_novel)" title="Island (Huxley novel)">Island</a></i> (1962).</li> 
     </ul>
