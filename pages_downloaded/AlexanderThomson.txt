     <p><b>Alexander "Greek" Thomson</b> (9 April 1817 – 22 March 1875) was an eminent Scottish architect and <a href="/wiki/Architectural_theorist" title="Architectural theorist" class="mw-redirect">architectural theorist</a> who was a pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_building" title="Sustainable building" class="mw-redirect">sustainable building</a>. Although his work was published in the architectural press of his day, it was little appreciated <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/outwith" class="extiw" title="wikt:outwith">outwith</a> <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> during his lifetime. It has only been since the 1950s and 1960s that his critical reputation has revived—not least of all in connection with his probable influence on <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Henry-Russell_Hitchcock" title="Henry-Russell Hitchcock">Henry-Russell Hitchcock</a> wrote of Thomson in 1966: “Glasgow in the last 150 years has had two of the greatest architects of the Western world. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rennie_Mackintosh" title="Charles Rennie Mackintosh">C.R.Mackintosh</a> was not highly productive but his influence in central Europe was comparable to such American architects as <a href="/wiki/Louis_Sullivan" title="Louis Sullivan">Louis Sullivan</a> and Frank Lloyd Wright. An even greater and happily more productive architect, though one whose influence can only occasionally be traced in America in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> and in <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a> and not at all as far as I know in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, was Alexander Thomson.”<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Thomson was born in the village of <a href="/wiki/Balfron" title="Balfron">Balfron</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stirlingshire" title="Stirlingshire">Stirlingshire</a>. The son of John Thomson, a <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeper" title="Bookkeeper" class="mw-redirect">bookkeeper</a>, and Elizabeth Cooper Thomson, he was the ninth of twelve children. His father, who already had eight grown children from his previous marriage, died when Alexander was seven. The family consequently moved to the outskirts of Glasgow, but tragedy struck when the eldest daughter, Jane, and three of her brothers died between 1828 and 1830, the year that Alexander's mother died. The remaining children moved with one of the older brothers, William, a teacher, and his wife and child to Hangingshaw, just south of Glasgow. The Thomson boys all worked from a young age, but the children were also home schooled. It is believed that Alexander worked in a lawyer's office, possibly Wilson, James, and Kays, where his older brother, Ebenezer, was employed as a bookkeeper and where he later became a partner in the business.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Alexander was eventually apprenticed to Glasgow architect Robert Foote, ultimately gaining a place in the office of <a href="/wiki/John_Baird_I_(1798-1859)" title="John Baird I (1798-1859)">John Baird</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> as a <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">draughtsman</a>. In 1848 Thomson set up his own practice, Baird &amp; Thomson, with John Baird II, who became his brother-in-law, and this firm lasted nine years. In 1857, as "the rising architectural star of Glasgow,"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> he entered into practice with his brother <a href="/wiki/George_Thomson_(botanist)" title="George Thomson (botanist)">George</a> where he was to enjoy the most productive years of his life. He served as president of both the Glasgow Architectural Society and the Glasgow Institute of Architects. Thomson was an elder of the <a href="/wiki/United_Presbyterian_Church_of_Scotland" title="United Presbyterian Church of Scotland" class="mw-redirect">United Presbyterian Church of Scotland</a>, and his deep religious convictions informed his work. There is a strong suggestion that he closely identified <a href="/wiki/Solomon%E2%80%99s_temple" title="Solomon’s temple" class="mw-redirect">Solomon’s temple</a> with the raised <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> of the same form of his three major churches.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

     <p>He produced a diverse range of structures including villas, a castle, urbane terraces, commercial <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenements" title="Tenements" class="mw-redirect">tenements</a>, and three extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a>. Of these, Caledonia Road Free Church (1856–57 - now a ruin), Queen's Park United Presbyterian Church (1869 - destroyed in WWII), and <a href="/wiki/St_Vincent_Street_Church" title="St Vincent Street Church" class="mw-redirect">St Vincent Street Church</a> (1859), the last is the only intact survivor. Hitchcock once stated, <i>“[Thomson has built] three of the finest Romantic Classical churches in the world</i>”.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Thomson developed his own highly idiosyncratic style from <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levantine</a> sources and freely adapted them to the needs of the modern city.</p>

     <p>At the age of 34, Thomson designed his first and only castle, Craigrownie Castle, which stands at the tip of the Rosneath Peninsula in <a href="/wiki/Cove,_Dunbartonshire" title="Cove, Dunbartonshire" class="mw-redirect">Cove</a>, overlooking <a href="/wiki/Loch_Long" title="Loch Long">Loch Long</a>. The six-storey structure is Scots <a href="/wiki/Baronial" title="Baronial" class="mw-redirect">Baronial</a> in style, featuring a central tower with <a href="/wiki/Battlements" title="Battlements" class="mw-redirect">battlements</a>, steep <a href="/wiki/Gable" title="Gable">gables</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriel_window" title="Oriel window">oriel windows</a>, in addition to a <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapel</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Mews" title="Mews">mews</a> cottage.</p> 
     <p>Thomson's villa designs were realized at <a href="/wiki/Langside" title="Langside">Langside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollokshields" title="Pollokshields">Pollokshields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">Helensburgh</a>, Cove, the <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">Clyde</a> Estuary, and on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Isle of Bute</a>. His "mature villas are Grecian in style while resembling no other <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival" title="Greek Revival" class="mw-redirect">Greek Revival</a> houses,...[and they] are dominated by horizontal lines and rest on a strong podium."<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup> According to Gavin Stamp, "Thomson carefully designed his villas with symmetries within an overall asymmetry in a personal language in which the horizontal discipline of a continuous governing order—whether expressed or implied—was never abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup> Regarding similarities to Frank Lloyd Wright, Stamp states, "It has often been remarked that there are clear resemblances between the early houses of the <a href="/wiki/Prairie_School" title="Prairie School">Prairie School</a> and Thomson's horizontally massed design, with its low-pitched gables and spreading eaves -- together with a connecting garden." As Sir John Summerson noted, "There is something wildly 'American' about Thomson -- a 'New World' attitude. You can see it in the villas...a sort of primitivism, ultra-<a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscan</a>." <sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Later in his career he would abandon his eclecticism and adopt the purely <a href="/wiki/Ionic_order" title="Ionic order">Ionic</a> Greek style for which he is best known, as such he is perhaps the last in a continuous tradition of British <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival" title="Greek Revival" class="mw-redirect">Greek Revival</a> architects. In attacking the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" title="Gothic Revival" class="mw-redirect">Gothic</a>, he "insisted that '<a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> is really more scientifically constructed than <a href="/wiki/York_Minster" title="York Minster">York Minster</a>'...[alluding to] <a href="/wiki/Pugin" title="Pugin" class="mw-redirect">Pugin</a>'s comment that in their temples 'the Greeks erected their columns like the uprights of Stonehenge'."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other important works still standing include Moray Place, Great Western Terrace, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Halls" title="Egyptian Halls" class="mw-redirect">Egyptian Halls</a> in Union Street, Grosvenor Building, Buck's Head Building in Argyle Street, Grecian Buildings in <a href="/wiki/Sauchiehall_Street" title="Sauchiehall Street">Sauchiehall Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walmer_Crescent" title="Walmer Crescent">Walmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millbrae_Crescent" title="Millbrae Crescent">Millbrae Crescents</a>, and his villa, <a href="/wiki/Holmwood_House" title="Holmwood House">Holmwood House</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Cathcart" title="Cathcart">Cathcart</a>.</p>

     <p>Grave monuments designed by Thomson that are worthy of study include those to the Revd. A.O. Beattie and the Revd. G.M. Middleton, as well as that for John McIntyre in <a href="/wiki/Cathcart_Cemetery" title="Cathcart Cemetery">Cathcart Old Parish Cemetery</a>.</p> 
     <p>Thomson was a <a href="/wiki/Visionary" title="Visionary">visionary</a> who introduced into our vocabulary some of the essential elements of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_housing" title="Sustainable housing" class="mw-redirect">sustainable housing</a>. This argument hinges on an unrealized design Thomson prepared in 1868 for the Glasgow City Improvement Trust, an agency of the Town Council given the task of redeveloping a large area of slum housing centred on the medieval Old Town. The Trust invited Thomson and five other prominent architects to propose designs for the reconstruction of various parcels of land along the spine of Glasgow's <a href="/wiki/High_Street_(Glasgow)" title="High Street (Glasgow)" class="mw-redirect">High Street</a>. Thomson suggested that closely spaced parallel tenements be built within the central courtyard, the ends of which will be open to facilitate <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a>. He also proposed that alternate streets be glazed for better warmth and safety for the residents. Although Thomson's ideas failed to catch on at the time, new research and <a href="/wiki/CAD" title="CAD" class="mw-redirect">CAD</a> techniques have helped show how revolutionary was his proposal for improved workers' housing.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Thomson's published writings include the Haldane lectures on the history of architecture (1874) and the <i>Inquiry as to the Appropriateness of the Gothic Style for the Proposed building for the University of Glasgow</i> (1866) which attempted to refute <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">Ruskin</a> and Pugin’s claims for the superiority of Gothic.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>On 21 September 1847, Thomson married Jane Nicholson, granddaughter of the architect <a href="/wiki/Peter_Nicholson_(architect)" title="Peter Nicholson (architect)">Peter Nicholson</a>, in a <a href="/wiki/Double_wedding" title="Double wedding" class="mw-redirect">double wedding</a> ceremony with her sister, Jessie, who married John Baird II. They had twelve children in total and would later lose five of them in an <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a>.</p> 
     <p>One brother, <a href="/wiki/George_Thomson_(botanist)" title="George Thomson (botanist)">George Thomson</a> (1819–1878), became a <a href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist" class="mw-redirect">baptist</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> in <a href="/wiki/Limbe,_Cameroon" title="Limbe, Cameroon">Limbe, Cameroon</a> (then known as <i>"Victoria"</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-Desmond_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond-9">[9]</a></sup> where he combined his religious activities with a passion for <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Epiphytic" title="Epiphytic" class="mw-redirect">epiphytic</a> <a href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid" class="mw-redirect">orchid</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pachystoma" title="Pachystoma">Pachystoma</a></i> genus was named <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pachystoma_thomsonianum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pachystoma thomsonianum (page does not exist)">Pachystoma thomsonianum</a></i> in his honour.<sup id="cite_ref-HV70_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HV70-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>His nephew,<sup id="cite_ref-Desmond_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond-9">[9]</a></sup> Rev. <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cooper_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Cooper Thomson (page does not exist)">William Cooper Thomson</a> (1829-1878) was a missionary in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, after whose first wife the <a href="/wiki/Bleeding-heart_vine" title="Bleeding-heart vine" class="mw-redirect">bleeding-heart vine</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Clerodendrum_thomsoniae" title="Clerodendrum thomsoniae">Clerodendrum thomsoniae</a></i> was named.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Thomson died on 22 March 1875 at his home in Moray Place in <a href="/wiki/Strathbungo" title="Strathbungo">Strathbungo</a>, Glasgow, fittingly in one of his own creations. The architect was buried in the lair adjacent to that in which his five deceased children were laid to rest, in <a href="/wiki/Gorbals_Southern_Necropolis" title="Gorbals Southern Necropolis" class="mw-redirect">Gorbals Southern Necropolis</a>, on 26 March 1875, and he was joined there by his widow, Jane, in 1889.</p> 
     <p>His obituary appeared in "Building News" on 26 March 1875, written by his friend, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gildard" title="Thomas Gildard">Thomas Gildard</a>, who also wrote his biography.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The Glasgow Institute of Architects set up The Alexander Thomson Memorial immediately following his death. A marble bust of the architect by <a href="/wiki/John_Mossman" title="John Mossman">John Mossman</a> was presented to the Corporation Galleries, <a href="/wiki/Sauchiehall_Street" title="Sauchiehall Street">Sauchiehall Street</a>, and is now displayed in the <a href="/wiki/Kelvingrove_Art_Gallery_and_Museum" title="Kelvingrove Art Gallery and Museum">Kelvingrove Art Gallery and Museum</a>. The Alexander Thomson Travelling Studentship, of which the second winner was Charles Rennie Mackintosh, was established in his honor, "for the purpose of providing a travelling studentship for the furtherance of the study of ancient classic architecture, with special reference to the principles illustrated in Mr. Thomson’s works".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Thomson was the pre-eminent architect of his era in Glasgow, yet until recently, his buildings and his reputation have been largely neglected in the city graced by his works.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Holmwood_House" title="Holmwood House">Holmwood House</a> is generally considered to be Thomson's finest and most original residential subject. Under the ownership of the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Scotland" title="National Trust for Scotland">National Trust for Scotland</a>, Holmwood has been restored to its original condition and opened to the general public. During the renovation, nineteen panels of a classical <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> depicting scenes from Homer's <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> were discovered under layers of paint and wallpaper, rendering Thomson's nickname all the more apt.</p> 
     <p>In 1999, a <a href="/wiki/Retrospective" title="Retrospective">retrospective</a> entitled <i>Alexander Thomson: The Unknown Genius</i> was held at the <a href="/wiki/The_Lighthouse_(Glasgow)" title="The Lighthouse (Glasgow)" class="mw-redirect">The Lighthouse</a>, reminding Glaswegians of the need to preserve the remaining examples of this unique architect's contribution to their city.</p> 
     <p>The British emigre architect <a href="/wiki/George_Ashdown_Audsley" title="George Ashdown Audsley">George Ashdown Audsley</a> closely followed Thomson's ornamentation for several of his secular buildings. The most notable surviving example is his Bowling Green Offices (completed 1896) in New York City. The highly carved granite base of this tall office building is in the Thomson manner with brick <a href="/wiki/Chicago_school_(architecture)" title="Chicago school (architecture)">Chicago School</a> style floors above.</p>
