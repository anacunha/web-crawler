   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Mahoney tables</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
      Jump to: 
     <a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 
     <a href="#p-search">search</a> 
    </div> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <p>The <b>Mahoney tables</b> are a set of reference tables used in <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, used as a guide to climate-appropriate design. They are named after architect Carl Mahoney,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> who worked on them together with John Martin Evans,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_K%C3%B6nigsberger" title="Otto Königsberger">Otto Königsberger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> They were first published in 1971 by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Department_of_Economic_and_Social_Affairs" title="United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs">United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>The concept developed by Mahoney (1968) in Nigeria provided the basis of the</p> 
      <p>Mahoney Tables, later developed by Koenigsberger, Mahoney and Evans (1970), published by the United Nations in English, French and Spanish, with large sections included in the widely distributed publication by Koenigsberger et al. (1978). The Mahoney Tables (Evans, 1999; Evans, 2001) proposed a climate analysis sequence that starts with the basic and widely available monthly climatic data of temperature, humidity and rainfall, such as that found in HMSO (1958) and Pearce and Smith (1990), or data published by national meteorological services, for example SMN (1995). Today, the data for most major cities can be downloaded directly from the Internet</p> (from sites such as 
      <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.wunderground.com/global/AG.html">http://www.wunderground.com/global/AG.html</a>, 2006). 
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>— John Martin Evans, <i>The Comfort Triangles</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></cite>
      </div> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The tables use readily available <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> data and simple calculations to give design guidelines, in a manner similar to a <a href="/wiki/Spreadsheet" title="Spreadsheet">spreadsheet</a>, as opposed to detailed <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">thermal analysis or simulation</a>. There are six tables; four are used for entering climatic data, for comparison with the requirements for <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">thermal comfort</a>; and two for reading off appropriate design criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A rough outline of the table usage is:</p> 
     <ol> 
      <li><b>Air <a href="/wiki/Temperatures" title="Temperatures" class="mw-redirect">Temperatures</a></b>. The max, min, and mean temperatures for each month are entered into this table.</li> 
      <li><b><a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">Humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)" class="mw-redirect">Precipitation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">Wind</a></b>. The max, min, and mean figures for each month are entered into this table, and the conditions for each month classified into a <i>humidity group</i>.</li> 
      <li><b>Comparison of Comfort Conditions and Climate</b>. The desired max/min temperatures are entered, and compared to the climatic values from table 1. A note is made if the conditions create <i>heat stress</i> or <i>cold stress</i> (i.e. the building will be too hot or cold).</li> 
      <li><b>Indicators</b> (of humid or <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> conditions). Rules are provided for combining the stress (table 3) and humidity groups (table 2) to check a box classifying the humidity and aridity for each month. For each of six possible indicators, the number of months where that indicator was checked are added up, giving a yearly total.</li> 
      <li><b>Schematic Design Recommendations</b>. The yearly totals in table 4 correspond to rows in this table, listing schematic design recommendations, e.g. 'buildings oriented on east-west axis to reduce sun exposure', 'medium sized openings, 20%-40% of wall area'.</li> 
      <li><b>Design Development Recommendations</b>. Again the yearly totals from table 4 are used to read off recommendations, e.g. 'roofs should be <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass_(Building)" title="Thermal mass (Building)" class="mw-redirect">high-mass</a> and well insulated'.</li> 
     </ol>
