     <p><b>Propylene glycol</b>, also called <b>propane-1,2-diol</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>8</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. It is a viscous colorless liquid which is nearly odorless but possesses a faintly sweet taste. Chemically it is classed as a <a href="/wiki/Diol" title="Diol">diol</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible" class="mw-redirect">miscible</a> with a broad range of solvents, including <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>.</p> 
     <p>It is produced on a large scale and is primarily used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> but also sees use in food processing as the <a href="/wiki/E-number" title="E-number" class="mw-redirect">E-number</a> E1520.</p> 
     <p>The compound is sometimes called <b>α-propylene glycol</b> to distinguish it from the isomer <a href="/wiki/1,3-Propanediol" title="1,3-Propanediol">propane-1,3-diol</a> (β-propylene glycol).</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_properties">Structure and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Propylene glycol is a clear, colorless and <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic" class="mw-redirect">hygroscopic</a> liquid. Propylene glycol contains an asymmetrical carbon atom, so it exists in two <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomers</a>. The commercial product is a <a href="/wiki/Racemic" title="Racemic" class="mw-redirect">racemic</a> mixture. Pure optical isomers can be obtained by hydration of optically pure <a href="/wiki/Propylene_oxide" title="Propylene oxide">propylene oxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The freezing point of water is depressed when mixed with propylene glycol owing to the effects of dissolution of a solute in a solvent (<a href="/wiki/Freezing-point_depression" title="Freezing-point depression">freezing-point depression</a>); in general, glycols are non-corrosive, have very low volatility and very low toxicity, however, the closely related <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> (a key ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a>) is extremely toxic to humans and fatally toxic to many animals.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Industrially, propylene glycol is produced from <a href="/wiki/Propylene_oxide" title="Propylene oxide">propylene oxide</a><sup id="cite_ref-petro_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petro-4">[4]</a></sup> (for food-grade use), and global capacity in 1990 was 900,000 tonnes per year.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Different manufacturers use either non-catalytic high-temperature process at 200&nbsp;°C (392&nbsp;°F) to 220&nbsp;°C (428&nbsp;°F), or a catalytic method, which proceeds at 150&nbsp;°C (302&nbsp;°F) to 180&nbsp;°C (356&nbsp;°F) in the presence of ion exchange resin or a small amount of sulfuric acid or alkali.</p> 
     <p>Final products contain 20% propylene glycol, 1.5% of <a href="/wiki/Dipropylene_glycol" title="Dipropylene glycol">dipropylene glycol</a> and small amounts of other <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene_glycol" title="Polypropylene glycol">polypropylene glycols</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Further purification produces finished industrial grade or USP/JP/EP/BP grade propylene glycol that is typically 99.5% or greater. Propylene glycol can also be converted from <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> byproduct. This starting material is usually reserved for industrial use because of the noticeable odor and taste that accompanies the final product.</p> 
     <p>(<i>S</i>)-Propanediol may be synthesized from <a href="/wiki/Mannitol" title="Mannitol"><small>D</small>-mannitol</a>, through the following scheme:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Forty-five percent of propylene glycol produced is used as chemical <a href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock" class="mw-redirect">feedstock</a> for the production of unsaturated <a href="/wiki/Polyester_resin" title="Polyester resin">polyester resins</a>. In this regard, propylene glycol reacts with a mixture of unsaturated <a href="/wiki/Maleic_anhydride" title="Maleic anhydride">maleic anhydride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isophthalic_acid" title="Isophthalic acid">isophthalic acid</a> to give a <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymer</a>. This partially unsaturated polymer undergoes further <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">crosslinking</a> to yield <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermoset_plastics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thermoset plastics (page does not exist)">thermoset plastics</a>. Related to this application, propylene glycol reacts with propylene oxide to give oligomers and polymers that are used to produce <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethanes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Propylene glycol is used as a <a href="/wiki/Humectant" title="Humectant">humectant</a> (E1520), solvent,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and preservative in food and for tobacco products. It is also one of the major ingredients (10-92%), along with <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">vegetable glycerin</a>, of the "<a href="/wiki/E-liquid" title="E-liquid" class="mw-redirect">e-liquid</a>" and cartridges used in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_cigarettes" title="Electronic cigarettes" class="mw-redirect">electronic cigarettes</a> (as well as liquid nicotine),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> where it is aerosolized in the atomizer.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Propylene glycol is also used in various edible items such as coffee-based drinks, liquid sweeteners, ice cream, whipped dairy products and soda.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vaporizer_(inhalation_device)" title="Vaporizer (inhalation device)">Vaporizers</a> used for delivery of pharmaceuticals or personal-care products often include propylene glycol among the ingredients.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Propylene glycol is used as a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" title="Pharmaceuticals" class="mw-redirect">pharmaceuticals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Oral_administration" title="Oral administration">oral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Injectable" title="Injectable" class="mw-redirect">injectable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Topical" title="Topical" class="mw-redirect">topical</a> formulations, such as for <a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">diazepam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lorazepam" title="Lorazepam">lorazepam</a> which are <a href="/wiki/Insoluble" title="Insoluble" class="mw-redirect">insoluble</a> in water.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Like <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>, propylene glycol is able to lower the freezing point of water, and so it is used as aircraft <a href="/wiki/De-icing" title="De-icing" class="mw-redirect">de-icing</a> fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Water-propylene glycol mixtures dyed pink to indicate the mixture is relatively nontoxic are sold under the name of <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">RV</a> or marine antifreeze. Propylene glycol is frequently used as a substitute for ethylene glycol in low toxicity, environmentally friendly automotive <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a>. It is also used to winterize the plumbing systems in vacant structures.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Eutectic_composition" title="Eutectic composition" class="mw-redirect">eutectic composition</a>/temperature is 60:40 propylene glycol:water/-60&nbsp;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The −50&nbsp;°F/−45&nbsp;°C commercial product is, however, water rich; a typical formulation is 40:60.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Propylene glycol is used in veterinary medicine as an oral treatment for <a href="/wiki/Hyperketonaemia" title="Hyperketonaemia" class="mw-redirect">hyperketonaemia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>. Glucose, which can be used in non-ruminants for this purpose, is not effective due to its consumption by the resident microbes of the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>. Propylene glycol is partially <a href="/wiki/Metabolized" title="Metabolized" class="mw-redirect">metabolized</a> in the rumen to <a href="/wiki/Propionate" title="Propionate">propionate</a> which can be used as an energy source. The remainder is absorbed into the bloodstream and used by the liver for <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">gluconeogenesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_in_humans">Safety in humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety in humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oral_administration">Oral administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oral administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The acute oral toxicity of propylene glycol is very low, and large quantities are required to cause perceptible health damage in humans; propylene glycol is metabolized in the human body into <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvic acid</a> (a normal part of the glucose-metabolism process, readily converted to energy), <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> (handled by ethanol-metabolism), <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> (a normal acid generally abundant during digestion),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Propionaldehyde" title="Propionaldehyde">propionaldehyde</a> (a potentially hazardous substance).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Serious toxicity generally occurs at plasma concentrations over 4 g/L in, which requires extremely high intake over a relatively short period of time, or when used as a vehicle for drugs or vitamins given intravenously or orally.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It would be nearly impossible to reach toxic levels by consuming foods or supplements, which contain at most 1 g/kg of PG, except for alcoholic beverages which are allowed 5 percent = 50g/kg.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Cases of propylene glycol poisoning are usually related to either inappropriate intravenous administration or accidental ingestion of large quantities by children.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>A recent case of propylene glycol poisoning from excess whiskey ingestion has been reported, in which a young man required ICU admission for respiratory depression and a significant osmolal gap metabolic acidosis.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The potential for long-term oral toxicity is also low. In one study, in 1972, 12 rats were provided with feed containing as much as 5% PG over a period of 104 weeks, and they showed no apparent ill effects; no data on offspring were offered.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Because of its low chronic oral toxicity, propylene glycol was classified by the U. S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> as "<a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>" (GRAS) for use as a direct <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additive</a>, including frozen foods such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frozen_dessert" title="Frozen dessert">frozen desserts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The GRAS designation is specific to its use in food, and does not apply to other uses.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skin.2C_eye_and_inhalation_contact">Skin, eye and inhalation contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Skin, eye and inhalation contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Prolonged contact with propylene glycol is essentially non-irritating to the skin.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Undiluted propylene glycol is minimally irritating to the eye, producing slight transient <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">conjunctivitis</a>; the eye recovers after the exposure is removed. Exposure to mists may cause eye irritation, as well as <a href="/wiki/Upper_respiratory_tract" title="Upper respiratory tract" class="mw-redirect">upper respiratory tract</a> irritation. Inhalation of propylene glycol vapors appears to present no significant hazard in ordinary applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Robertson47_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson47-35">[35]</a></sup> However, limited human experience indicates that inhalation of propylene glycol mists could be irritating to some individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It is therefore recommended that propylene glycol not be used in applications where inhalation exposure or human eye contact with the spray mists of these materials is likely, such as <a href="/wiki/Simulated_fog" title="Simulated fog" class="mw-redirect">simulated fogs</a> for theatrical productions, or antifreeze solutions for emergency <a href="/wiki/Eye_wash" title="Eye wash" class="mw-redirect">eye wash</a> stations.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>According to a 2010 study by <a href="/wiki/Karlstad_University" title="Karlstad University">Karlstad University</a>, the concentrations of PGEs (counted as the sum of propylene glycol and glycol ethers) in indoor air, particularly bedroom air, has been linked to increased risk of developing numerous respiratory and immune disorders in children, including <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hay_fever" title="Hay fever" class="mw-redirect">hay fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema" class="mw-redirect">eczema</a>, and allergies, with increased risk ranging from 50% to 180%. This concentration has been linked to use of water-based paints and water-based system cleansers. However, the study authors write that glycol ethers and not propylene glycol are the likely culprit.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Propylene glycol does not cause <a href="/wiki/Sensitization" title="Sensitization">sensitization</a>, and it shows no evidence of being a carcinogen or of being <a href="/wiki/Genotoxicity" title="Genotoxicity">genotoxic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intravenous_administration">Intravenous administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Intravenous administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Adverse responses to <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_administration" title="Intravenous administration" class="mw-redirect">intravenous administration</a> of drugs that use propylene glycol as an <a href="/wiki/Excipient" title="Excipient">excipient</a> have been seen in a number of people, particularly with large dosages. Responses may include "hypotension, <a href="/wiki/Bradycardia" title="Bradycardia">bradycardia</a>... <a href="/wiki/QRS" title="QRS" class="mw-redirect">QRS</a> and <a href="/wiki/T_wave" title="T wave">T</a> abnormalities on the ECG, <a href="/wiki/Arrhythmia" title="Arrhythmia" class="mw-redirect">arrhythmia</a>, cardiac arrest, serum <a href="/wiki/Hyperosmolality" title="Hyperosmolality" class="mw-redirect">hyperosmolality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acidosis" title="Lactic acidosis">lactic acidosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haemolysis" title="Haemolysis" class="mw-redirect">haemolysis</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A high percentage (12% to 42%) of directly-injected propylene glycol is eliminated or secreted in urine unaltered depending on dosage, with the remainder appearing in its <a href="/wiki/Glucuronide" title="Glucuronide">glucuronide</a>-form. The speed of renal filtration decreases as dosage increases,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> which may be due to propylene glycol's mild anesthetic / CNS-depressant -properties as an alcohol.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In one case, intravenous administration of propylene glycol-suspended nitroglycerin to an elderly man may have induced coma and acidosis.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Propylene glycol is an approved food additive for dog food under the category of animal feed and is <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a> for dogs,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> with an <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<font size="-1"><sub>50</sub></font></a> of 9&nbsp;mL/kg. The LD<sub>50</sub> is higher for most laboratory animals (20&nbsp;mL/kg).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> However, it is prohibited for use in food for cats due to links to <a href="/wiki/Heinz_body" title="Heinz body">Heinz body</a> <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allergic_reaction">Allergic reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Allergic reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Individuals who cannot tolerate propylene glycol experience inflamed dry skin in the facial area, or small red dots on the body. Investigators believe that the incidence of allergic contact dermatitis to propylene glycol may be greater than 2% in patients with eczema or fungal infections, which are very common in countries with lesser sun exposure, and lower than normal vitamin D balances. Therefore propylene glycol allergy is more common in those countries.<sup id="cite_ref-AMA-DEA1994_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMA-DEA1994-50">[50]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">Environmental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Propylene_glycol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Propylene glycol is known to exert high levels of <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_oxygen_demand" title="Biochemical oxygen demand">biochemical oxygen demand</a> (BOD) during degradation in surface waters. This process can adversely affect aquatic life by consuming oxygen needed by aquatic organisms for survival. Large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_oxygen" title="Dissolved oxygen" class="mw-redirect">dissolved oxygen</a> (DO) in the water column are consumed when microbial populations decompose propylene glycol<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p> 
     <p>Sufficient dissolved oxygen levels in surface waters are critical for the survival of fish, macroinvertebrates, and other aquatic organisms. If oxygen concentrations drop below a minimum level, organisms emigrate, if able and possible, to areas with higher oxygen levels or eventually die. This effect can drastically reduce the amount of usable aquatic habitat. Reductions in DO levels can reduce or eliminate bottom-feeder populations, create conditions that favor a change in a community’s species profile, or alter critical <a href="/wiki/Food-web" title="Food-web" class="mw-redirect">food-web</a> interactions.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
