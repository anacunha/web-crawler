     <p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, <b>thermal energy</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> present in a system due to its <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The average translational kinetic energy possessed by free particles in a system of free particles in thermodynamic equilibrium (as measured in the frame of reference of the center of mass of that system) may also be referred to as the thermal energy per particle.<sup id="cite_ref-hyperphysics_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hyperphysics-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In thermodynamics it is often most convenient and precise to think of heat as the transfer of energy, just as work is also a transfer of energy. Heat and work therefore depend on the path of transfer and are not state functions, whereas internal energy is a state function.</p> 
     <p>Microscopically, the thermal energy may include both the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> and potential energy of a system's constituent particles, which may be atoms, molecules, electrons, or particles. It originates from the individually random, or disordered, motion of particles in a large ensemble. In ideal monatomic gases, thermal energy is entirely kinetic energy. In other substances, in cases where some of the thermal energy is stored in atomic vibration or by increased separation of particles having mutual forces of attraction, the thermal energy is equally partitioned between potential energy and kinetic energy. Thermal energy is thus <a href="/wiki/Equipartition_theorem" title="Equipartition theorem">equally partitioned</a> between all available <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry)" title="Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)">degrees of freedom</a> of the particles. As noted, these degrees of freedom may include pure translational motion in gases, rotational motion, vibrational motion and associated potential energies. In general, due to quantum mechanical reasons, the availability of any such degrees of freedom is a function of the energy in the system, and therefore depends on the temperature (see <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> for discussion of this phenomenon).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Macroscopically, the thermal energy of a system at a given temperature is proportional to its heat capacity. However, since the heat capacity differs according to whether or not constant volume or constant pressure is specified, or phase changes permitted, the heat capacity cannot be used to define thermal energy unless it is done in such a way as to ensure that only heat gain or loss (not work) makes any changes in the internal energy of the system. Usually, this means specifying the "constant volume heat capacity" of the system so that no work is done. Also the heat capacity of a system for such purposes must not include heat absorbed by any chemical reaction or process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differentiation_from_heat">Differentiation from heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Differentiation from heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a> is the thermal energy transferred across a boundary of one region of matter to another.<sup id="cite_ref-speyer_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leland_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leland-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> As a process variable, heat is a characteristic of a process, not a property of the system; it is not <i>contained</i> within the boundary of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-speyer_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand, thermal energy is a property of a system, and exists on both sides of a boundary. Classically (see <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>), thermal energy is the statistical mean of the microscopic fluctuations of the kinetic energy of the systems' particles, and it is the source and the effect of the transfer of heat across a system boundary.</p> 
     <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Zeroth_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Zeroth law of thermodynamics">zeroth law of thermodynamics</a>, heat is exchanged between thermodynamic systems in thermal contact only if their temperatures are different.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> If heat traverses the boundary in direction <i>into</i> the system, the internal energy change is considered to be a positive quantity, while <i>exiting</i> the system, it is negative. Heat flows from the hotter to the colder system, decreasing the thermal energy of the hotter system, and increasing the thermal energy of the colder system. Then, when the two systems have reached <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a>, they have the same temperature, and the net exchange of thermal energy vanishes and heat flow ceases. Even after they reach thermal equilibrium, thermal energy continues to be exchanged between systems, but the <i>net</i> exchange of thermal energy is zero, and therefore there is no heat.</p> 
     <p>After the transfer, the energy transferred by heat is called by other terms, such as thermal energy or latent energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Incrop_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Incrop-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although heat often ends up as thermal energy after transfer, it may cause changes other than a change in temperature. For example, the energy may be absorbed or released in <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transitions</a>, such as melting or evaporation, which are the gain or loss of a form of potential energy called <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>.</p> 
     <p>Thermal energy may be increased in a system by other means than heat, for example when mechanical or electrical work is performed on the system. Heat flow may cause work to be performed on a system by compressing a system's volume, for example. A heat engine uses the movement of thermal energy (heat flow) to do mechanical work. No qualitative difference exists between the thermal energy added by other means. There is also no need in classical thermodynamics to characterize the thermal energy in terms of atomic or molecular behavior. A change in thermal energy induced in a system is the product of the change in entropy and the temperature of the system.</p> 
     <p>Rather than being itself the thermal energy involved in a transfer, heat is sometimes also understood as the process of that transfer, i.e. <i>heat</i> functions as a verb.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Thermal energy is the portion of the thermodynamic or internal energy of a system that is responsible for the temperature of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-speyer_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The thermal energy of a system scales with its size and is therefore an <a href="/wiki/Extensive_property" title="Extensive property" class="mw-redirect">extensive property</a>. It is not a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state function</a> of the system unless the system has been constructed so that all changes in internal energy are due to changes in thermal energy, as a result of heat transfer (not work). Otherwise thermal energy is dependent on the way or method by which the system attained its temperature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thermal energy can be transformed into and out of other types of energy, and is not generally a <a href="/wiki/Conserved_quantity" title="Conserved quantity">conserved quantity</a>.</p> 
     <p>From a macroscopic thermodynamic description, the thermal energy of a system is given by the product of its constant volume specific <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a>, <i>C(T)</i>, and its <a href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature" class="mw-redirect">absolute temperature</a>, <i>T</i>:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_{thermal} = C(T) \cdot T." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/5/3c561a8b7285e675cf52acf7d63fbf83.png">
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>The heat capacity is a function of temperature itself, and is typically measured and specified for certain standard conditions and a specific <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount of substance</a> (molar heat capacity) or <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> units (specific heat capacity). At constant volume (<i>V</i>), <i>C</i><sub>V</sub> it is the temperature coefficient of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-fuchs_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In practice, given a narrow temperature range, for example the operational range of a heat engine, the heat capacity of a system is often constant, and thus thermal energy changes are conveniently measured as temperature fluctuations in the system.</p> 
     <p>In the microscopical description of <a href="/wiki/Statistical_physics" title="Statistical physics">statistical physics</a>, the thermal energy is identified with the mechanical kinetic energy of the constituent particles or other forms of kinetic energy associated with quantum-mechanical <a href="/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics)" title="Microstate (statistical mechanics)">microstates</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>The distinguishing difference between the terms <i>kinetic energy</i> and <i>thermal energy</i> is that thermal energy is the <i>mean</i> energy of disordered, i.e. random, motion of the particles or the oscillations in the system. The conversion of energy of ordered motion to thermal energy results from collisions.<sup id="cite_ref-blundell_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blundell-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>All kinetic energy is partitioned into the degrees of freedom of the system. The average energy of a single particle with <i>f</i> quadratic degrees of freedom in a thermal bath of temperature <i>T</i> is a statistical mean energy given by the <a href="/wiki/Equipartition_theorem" title="Equipartition theorem">equipartition theorem</a> as</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E_{thermal} = f \cdot \tfrac 1 2 kT \,\!" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/c/4ecebd887f3fd86832312bd59f734d3d.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <i>k</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a>. The total thermal energy of a sample of matter or a thermodynamic system is consequently the average sum of the kinetic energies of all particles in the system. Thus, for a system of <i>N</i> particles its thermal energy is<sup id="cite_ref-schroeder_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schroeder-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_{thermal} = N \cdot f \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} kT." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/4/7/147b1d90f3da1b391e68b96b231be6ad.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>For gaseous systems, the factor <i>f</i>, the number of degrees of freedom, commonly has the value 3 in the case of the monatomic gas, 5 for many diatomic gases, and 7 for larger molecules at ambient temperatures. In general however, it is a function of the temperature of the system as internal modes of motion, vibration, or rotation become available in higher energy regimes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p><i>U<sub>thermal</sub></i> is not the total energy of a system. Physical systems also contains static <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a>) that arises from interactions between particles, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_potential_energy" title="Nuclear potential energy" class="mw-redirect">nuclear energy</a> associated with atomic nuclei of particles, and even the <a href="/wiki/Rest_mass_energy" title="Rest mass energy" class="mw-redirect">rest mass energy</a> due to the equivalence of energy and mass.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_energy_of_the_ideal_gas">Thermal energy of the ideal gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermal energy of the ideal gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Thermal energy is most easily defined in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>, which is well approximated by a <a href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic" class="mw-redirect">monatomic</a> gas at low pressure. The ideal gas is a gas of particles considered as point objects of perfect spherical symmetry that interact only by elastic collisions and fill a volume such that their mean free path between collisions is much larger than their diameter.</p> 
     <p>The mechanical kinetic energy of a single particle is</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="E_{kinetic} = \tfrac 1 2 m v^2 \,\!" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/c/7/9c71059dbaa9f2defc6e6da0fbc904e4.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where <i>m</i> is the particle's mass and <i>v</i> is its velocity. The thermal energy of the gas sample consisting of <i>N</i> atoms is given by the sum of these energies, assuming no losses to the container or the environment:</p> 
     <dl> 
      <dd>
       <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="U_{thermal} = \tfrac 1 2 N m \overline{v^2} = \tfrac{3}{2} N k T," src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/c/04cda6fe2db68be35df1747b16964984.png">
      </dd> 
     </dl> 
     <p>where the line over the velocity term indicates that the average value is calculated over the entire ensemble. The total thermal energy of the sample is proportional to the macroscopic temperature by a constant factor accounting for the three translational degrees of freedom of each particle and the Boltzmann constant. The Boltzmann constant converts units between the microscopic model and the macroscopic temperature. This formalism is the basic assumption that directly yields the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a> and it shows that for the ideal gas, the internal energy <i>U</i> consists only of its thermal energy:</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>In an 1847 lecture entitled <i>On Matter, Living Force, and Heat</i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> characterized various terms that are closely related to thermal energy and heat. He identified the terms <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> as forms of heat each effecting distinct physical phenomena, namely the potential and kinetic energy of particles, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> He describes latent energy as the energy of interaction in a given configuration of particles, i.e. a form of <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a>, and the sensible heat as an energy affecting temperature measured by the thermometer due to the thermal energy, which he called the <i>living force</i>.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microscopic_observations">Microscopic observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microscopic observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>For a long time, molecular rotations could not be directly observed experimentally. Only measurement techniques with atomic resolution, such as <a href="/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscopy" title="Scanning tunneling microscopy" class="mw-redirect">scanning tunneling microscopy</a> made it possible to detect the rotation of a single molecule.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_origin_of_heat_energy_on_Earth">The origin of heat energy on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The origin of heat energy on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth's</a> proximity to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is the reason that almost everything near Earth's surface is warm with a temperature substantially above absolute zero.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation" class="mw-redirect">Solar radiation</a> constantly replenishes heat energy that Earth loses into space and a relatively stable state of near equilibrium is achieved. Because of the wide variety of heat diffusion mechanisms (one of which is black-body radiation which occurs at the speed of light), objects on Earth rarely vary too far from the global mean surface and air temperature of 287 to 288&nbsp;K (14 to 15&nbsp;°C). The more an object's or system's temperature varies from this average, the more rapidly it tends to come back into equilibrium with the ambient environment.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_energy_of_individual_particles">Thermal energy of individual particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thermal energy of individual particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The term <i>thermal energy</i> is also often used as a property of single particles to designate the kinetic energy of the particles. An example is the description of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron" class="mw-redirect">thermal neutrons</a> having a certain thermal energy, which means that the kinetic energy of the particle is equivalent to the temperature of its surrounding</p>
