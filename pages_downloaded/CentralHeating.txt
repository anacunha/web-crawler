     <p>A <b>central heating system</b> provides warmth to the whole interior of a building (or portion of a building) from one point to multiple <a href="/wiki/Room_(architecture)" title="Room (architecture)" class="mw-redirect">rooms</a>. When combined with other systems in order to control the building <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, the whole system may be an <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> (<a href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating" class="mw-redirect">heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_Conditioning" title="Air Conditioning" class="mw-redirect">air conditioning</a>) system.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Central heating differs from <a href="/wiki/Space_heater" title="Space heater">space heating</a> in that the heat generation occurs in one place, such as a <a href="/wiki/Furnace_room" title="Furnace room">furnace room</a> in a house or a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_room" title="Mechanical room">mechanical room</a> in a large building (though not necessarily at the "central" geometric point). The heat is distributed throughout the building, typically by <a href="/wiki/Forced-air" title="Forced-air">forced-air</a> through ductwork, by water circulating through pipes, or by steam fed through pipes.</p> 
     <p>The most common method of heat generation involves the combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a>. Increasingly, buildings utilize solar-powered heat sources, in which case the distribution system normally uses water circulation.</p> 
     <p>In much of the <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climate</a> zone, most new housing has come with central heating installed since the Second World War, at least. Such areas normally use <a href="/wiki/Gas_heater" title="Gas heater">gas heaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">oil-fired</a> system, often using <a href="/wiki/Forced-air" title="Forced-air">forced-air</a> systems. Steam-heating systems, fired by coal, oil or gas, are also used, primarily for larger buildings. <a href="/wiki/Electric_heating" title="Electric heating">Electrical heating systems</a> occur less commonly and are practical only with low-cost electricity or when <a href="/wiki/Ground_source_heat_pump" title="Ground source heat pump" class="mw-redirect">ground source heat pumps</a> are used. Considering the combined system of central generating plant and electric resistance heating, the overall efficiency will be less than for direct use of fossil fuel for space heating.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Rome_And_Greece">Ancient Rome And Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Rome And Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_(Palencia).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg/220px-Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg/330px-Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg/440px-Villa_Romana_La_Olmeda_030_Pedrosa_De_La_Vega_-_Salda%C3%B1a_%28Palencia%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1936" data-file-height="1296"></a>

     <p>The ancient Greeks originally developed central heating. The <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Ephesus" title="Temple of Ephesus" class="mw-redirect">Temple of Ephesus</a> was heated by <a href="/wiki/Flues" title="Flues" class="mw-redirect">Flues</a> planted in the ground and circulating the heat which was generated by fire. Some buildings in the Roman Empire used central heating systems, conducting air heated by <a href="/wiki/Furnaces" title="Furnaces" class="mw-redirect">furnaces</a> through empty spaces under the floors and out of <a href="/wiki/Pipe_(material)" title="Pipe (material)" class="mw-redirect">pipes</a> in the walls—a system known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">hypocaust</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Roman hypocaust continued to be used on a smaller scale during <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">late Antiquity</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad" class="mw-redirect">Umayyad</a> <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">caliphate</a>, while later Muslim builders employed a simpler system of <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_heating" title="Underfloor heating">underfloor pipes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>After the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, overwhelmingly across Europe, heating reverted to more primitive fireplaces for almost a thousand years.</p> 
     <p>In the early medieval Alpine upland, a simpler central heating system where heat travelled through underfloor channels from the furnace room replaced the Roman hypocaust at some places. In <a href="/wiki/Reichenau_Abbey" title="Reichenau Abbey" class="mw-redirect">Reichenau Abbey</a> a network of interconnected underfloor channels heated the 300 m² large assembly room of the monks during the winter months. The degree of efficiency of the system has been calculated at 90%.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>In the 13th century, the <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" title="Cistercian" class="mw-redirect">Cistercian</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> revived central heating in <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christian Europe</a> using river diversions combined with indoor wood-fired furnaces. The well-preserved <a href="/wiki/Real_Monasterio_de_Nuestra_Senora_de_Rueda" title="Real Monasterio de Nuestra Senora de Rueda" class="mw-redirect">Royal Monastery of Our Lady of the Wheel</a> (founded 1202) on the <a href="/wiki/Ebro_River" title="Ebro River" class="mw-redirect">Ebro River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> provides an excellent example of such an application.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_central_heating_systems">Modern central heating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern central heating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The three main methods of central heating were developed in the late 18th to mid-19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_air">Hot air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hot air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:170px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG/168px-Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG" width="168" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG/252px-Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG/336px-Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="698"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Sylvester_stove_cutaway.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> Sylvester's warm-air stove, 1819
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/William_Strutt_(inventor)" title="William Strutt (inventor)">William Strutt</a> designed a new mill building in <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> with a central hot air furnace in 1793, although the idea had been already proposed by <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a> almost a hundred years earlier. Strutt's design consisted of a large stove that heated air brought from the outside by a large underground passage. The air was ventilated through the building by large central ducts.</p> 
     <p>In 1807, he collaborated with another eminent engineer, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sylvester" title="Charles Sylvester">Charles Sylvester</a>, on the construction of a new building to house Derby's Royal Infirmary. Sylvester was instrumental in applying Strutt's novel heating system for the new hospital. He published his ideas in <i>The Philosophy of Domestic Economy; as exemplified in the mode of Warming, Ventilating, Washing, Drying, &amp; Cooking, ... in the Derbyshire General Infirmary</i> in 1819. Sylvester documented the new ways of heating hospitals that were included in the design, and the healthier features such as self-cleaning and air-refreshing toilets.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The infirmary's novel heating system allowed the patients to breathe fresh heated air whilst old air was channeled up to a glass and iron dome at the centre.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Their designs proved very influential. They were widely copied in the new mills of the <a href="/wiki/The_Midlands,_England" title="The Midlands, England" class="mw-redirect">Midlands</a> and were constantly improved, reaching maturity with the work of de Chabannes on the ventilation of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> in the 1810s. This system remained the standard for heating small buildings for the rest of the century.</p>

     <p>The English writer <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Plat" title="Hugh Plat">Hugh Plat</a> proposed a steam based central heating system for a greenhouse in 1594, although this was an isolated occurrence and was not followed up until the 18th century. Colonel Coke devised a system of pipes that would carry steam around the house from a central boiler, but it was <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> the Scottish inventor who was the first to build a working system in his house.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>A central boiler supplied high-pressure steam that then distributed the heat within the building through a system of pipes embedded in the columns. He implemented the system on a much larger scale at a textile factory in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robertson_Buchanan" title="Robertson Buchanan">Robertson Buchanan</a> wrote the definitive description of these installations in his treatises published in 1807 and 1815. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Tredgold" title="Thomas Tredgold">Thomas Tredgold</a>'s work <i>Principles of Warming and Ventilating Public Buildings</i>, delineated the method of the application of hot steam heating to smaller, non-industrial buildings. This method had superseded the hot air systems by the late 19th century.</p>

     <p>Early hot water systems were used in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> for central heating of the <a href="/wiki/Summer_Palace_of_Peter_the_Great" title="Summer Palace of Peter the Great">Summer Palace</a> (1710–1714) of <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>. Slightly later, in 1716, came the first use of water in Sweden to distribute heating in buildings. <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A5rten_Triewald" title="Mårten Triewald">Mårten Triewald</a>, a Swedish engineer, used this method for a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> at <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>. Jean Simon Bonnemain (1743–1830), a French architect,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> introduced the technique to industry on a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>, at Château du Pêcq, near <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</p> 
     <p>However, these scattered attempts were isolated and mainly confined in their application to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a>. Tredgold originally dismissed its use as impractical, but changed his mind in 1836, when the technology went into a phase of rapid development.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Early systems had used low pressure water systems, which required very large pipes. One of the first modern hot water central heating systems to remedy this deficiency were installed by <a href="/wiki/Angier_March_Perkins" title="Angier March Perkins">Angier March Perkins</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in the 1830s. At that time central heating was coming into fashion in Britain, with steam or hot air systems generally being used.</p>

     <p>Perkins' 1832 apparatus distributed water at 200 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> (392&nbsp;°F) through small diameter pipes at high pressure. A crucial invention to make the system viable was the thread screwed joint, that allowed the joint between the pipes to bear a similar pressure to the pipe itself. He also separated the boiler from the heat source to reduce the risk of explosion. The first unit was installed in the home of <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_the_Bank_of_England" title="Governor of the Bank of England">Governor of the Bank of England</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Horsley_Palmer" title="John Horsley Palmer">John Horsley Palmer</a> so that he could grow <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>'s cold climate.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>His systems were installed in factories and churches across the country, many of them remaining in usable condition for over 150 years. His system was also adapted for use by bakers in the heating of their ovens and in the making of paper from wood pulp.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Franz_San_Galli" title="Franz San Galli">Franz San Galli</a>, a Prussian-born Russian businessman living in <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg" class="mw-redirect">St. Petersburg</a>, invented the <a href="/wiki/Radiator" title="Radiator">radiator</a> between 1855 and 1857, which was a major step in the final shaping of modern central heating.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The Victorian <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> radiator became widespread by the end of the 19th century.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_sources">Energy sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energy sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The energy source selected for a central heating system varies by region. The primary energy source is selected on the basis of cost, convenience, efficiency and reliability. The energy cost of heating is one of the main costs of operating a building in a cold climate. Some central heating plants can switch fuels for reasons of economy and convenience; for example, a home owner may install a wood-fired furnace with electrical backup for occasional unattended operation.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">Solid fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Wood_fuel" title="Wood fuel">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> can be stockpiled at the point of use, but are inconvenient to handle and difficult to automatically control. Wood fuel is still used where the supply is plentiful and the occupants of the building don't mind the work involved in hauling in fuel, removing ashes, and tending the fire. Pellet fuel systems can automatically stoke the fire, but still need manual removal of ash. Coal was once an important residential heating fuel but today is rarely found.</p> 
     <p>Liquid fuels are petroleum product such as <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>. These are still widely applied where other heat sources are unavailable. Fuel oil can be automatically fired in a central heating system and requires no ash removal and little maintenance of the combustion system. However, the variable price of oil on world markets leads to erratic and high prices compared to some other energy sources. Institutional heating systems (office buildings or schools, for example) can use low-grade, inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Bunker_fuel" title="Bunker fuel" class="mw-redirect">bunker fuel</a> to run their heating plants, but capital cost is high compared to more easily managed liquid fuels.</p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> is a widespread heating fuel in North America and northern Europe. Gas burners are automatically controlled and require no ash removal and little maintenance. However, not all areas have access to a natural gas distribution system. Liquified petroleum gas or propane can be stored at the point of use and periodically replenished by a truck-mounted mobile tank.</p> 
     <p>Some areas have low cost electric power, making electric heating economically practical. Electric heating can either be purely resistance-type heating or make use of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a> system to take advantage of low-grade heat in the air or ground.</p> 
     <p>A <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> system uses centrally located boilers or water heaters and circulates heat energy to individual customers by circulating hot water or steam. This has the advantage of a central highly efficient energy converter than can use the best available pollution controls, and that is professionally operated. The district heating system can use heat sources impractical to deploy to individual homes, such as heavy oil, wood byproducts, or (hypothetically) nuclear fission. The distribution network is more costly to build than for gas or electric heating, and so is only found in densely populated areas or compact communities.</p> 
     <p>Not all central heating systems require purchased energy. A few buildings are served by local geothermal heat, using hot water or steam from a local well to provide building heat. Such areas are uncommon. A <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar" class="mw-redirect">passive solar</a> system requires no purchased fuel but needs to be carefully designed for the site.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_heating">Water heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Water heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>Circulating hot water can be used for central heating. Sometimes these systems are called <a href="/wiki/Hydronics" title="Hydronics">hydronic heating systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <p>Common components of a central heating system using water-circulation include:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>A supply of fuel, electric power or <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> supply lines</li> 
      <li>A <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">Boiler</a> (or a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> for district heating) which heats water in the system</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">Pump</a> to circulate the water</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Radiator_(heating)" title="Radiator (heating)">Radiators</a> which are wall-mounted panels through which the heated water passes in order to release heat into rooms.</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The circulating water systems use a closed loop; the same water is heated and then reheated. A sealed system provides a form of central heating in which the water used for heating circulates independently of the building's normal water supply.</p> 
     <p>An <a href="/wiki/Expansion_tank" title="Expansion tank">expansion tank</a> contains compressed gas, separated from the sealed-system water by a diaphragm. This allows for normal variations of pressure in the system. A <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">safety valve</a> allows water to escape from the system when pressure becomes too high, and a valve can open to replenish water from the normal water supply if the pressure drops too low. Sealed systems offer an alternative to open-vent systems, in which steam can escape from the system, and gets replaced from the building's water supply via a feed and central storage system.</p> 
     <p>Heating systems in the United Kingdom and in other parts of Europe commonly combine the needs of space heating with domestic hot-water heating. These systems occur less commonly in the USA. In this case, the heated water in a sealed system flows through a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> in a hot-water tank or <a href="/wiki/Hot_water_cylinder" title="Hot water cylinder" class="mw-redirect">hot-water cylinder</a> where it heats water from the regular potable water supply for use at hot-water <a href="/wiki/Tap_(valve)" title="Tap (valve)">taps</a> or <a href="/wiki/Major_appliance" title="Major appliance">appliances</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Washing_machine" title="Washing machine">washing machines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dishwasher" title="Dishwasher">dishwashers</a>.</p> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Expansion_tank.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Expansion_tank.jpg/220px-Expansion_tank.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Expansion_tank.jpg/330px-Expansion_tank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Expansion_tank.jpg/440px-Expansion_tank.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1950" data-file-height="2600"></a>

     </div> 
     <p>Hydronic radiant floor heating systems use a boiler or district heating to heat water and a pump to circulate the hot water in plastic pipes installed in a concrete slab. The pipes, embedded in the floor, carry heated water that conducts warmth to the surface of the floor, where it broadcasts heat energy to the room above. Hydronic heating systems are also used with antifreeze solutions in ice and snow melt systems for walkways, parking lots and streets. They are more commonly used in commercial and whole house radiant floor heat projects, whereas electric radiant heat systems are more commonly used in smaller "spot warming" applications.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_heating">Steam heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Steam heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>A steam heating system takes advantage of the high <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> which is given off when steam condenses to liquid water. In a steam heating system, each room is equipped with a so-called "radiator" which is connected to a source of low-pressure steam (a boiler). Steam entering the radiator condenses and gives up its latent heat, returning to liquid water. The radiator in turn heats the air of the room, and provides some direct <a href="/wiki/Radiant_heat" title="Radiant heat" class="mw-redirect">radiant heat</a>. The condensate water returns to the boiler either by gravity or with the assistance of a pump. Some systems use only a single pipe for combined steam and condensate return. Since trapped air prevents proper circulation, such systems have vent valves to allow air to be purged. In domestic and small commercial buildings, the steam is generated at relatively low pressure, less than 15 psig (200 kPa).</p> 
     <p>Steam heating systems are rarely installed in new single-family residential construction owing to the cost of the piping installation. Pipes must be carefully sloped to prevent trapped condensate blockage. Compared to other methods of heating, it is more difficult to control the output of a steam system. However, steam can be sent, for example, between buildings on a campus to allow use of an efficient central boiler and low cost fuel. Tall buildings take advantage of the low density of steam to avoid the excessive pressure required to circulate hot water from a basement mounted boiler. In industrial systems, process steam used for power generation or other purposes can also be tapped for space heating. Steam for heating systems may also be obtained from heat recovery boilers using otherwise wasted heat from industrial processes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_heaters">Electric heaters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Electric heaters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Electric heating or resistance heating converts electricity directly to heat. Electric heat is often more expensive than heat produced by combustion appliances like natural gas, propane, and oil. Electric resistance heat can be provided by baseboard heaters, space heaters, radiant heaters, furnaces, wall heaters, or thermal storage systems.</p> 
     <p>Electric heaters are usually part of a fan oil which is part of a central air conditioner. They circulate heat by blowing air across the heating element which is supplied to the furnace through return air ducts. Blowers in electric furnaces move air over one to five resistance coils or elements which are usually rated at five kilowatts. The heating elements activate one at a time to avoid overloading the electrical system. Overheating is prevented by a safety switch called a limit controller or limit switch. This limit controller may shut the furnace off if the blower fails or if something is blocking the air flow. The heated air is then sent back through the home through supply ducts.</p> 
     <p>In larger commercial applications, central heating is provided through an <a href="/wiki/Air_handler" title="Air handler">air handler</a> which incorporates similar components as a furnace but on a larger scale.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_pumps">Heat pumps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Heat pumps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>In mild climates a <a href="/wiki/Air_source_heat_pumps" title="Air source heat pumps">air source heat pump</a> can be used to air condition the building during hot weather, and to warm the building using heat extracted from outdoor air in cold weather. Air-source heat pumps are generally uneconomic for outdoor temperatures much below freezing. In colder climates, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">geothermal heat pumps</a> can be used to extract heat from the ground. For economy, these systems are designed for average low winter temperatures and use supplemental heating for extreme low temperature conditions. The advantage of the heat pump is that it reduces the purchased energy required for building heating; often geothermal source systems also supply domestic hot water. Even in places where fossil fuels provide most electricity, a geothermal system may offset <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> production since most of the heat is supplied from the surrounding environment, with only 15–30% as electrical consumption.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_aspects">Environmental aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>From an energy-efficiency standpoint considerable heat gets lost or goes to waste if only a single room needs heating, since central heating has distribution losses and (in the case of forced-air systems particularly) may heat some unoccupied rooms without need. In such buildings which require isolated heating, one may wish to consider non-central systems such as individual room heaters, fireplaces or other devices. Alternatively, architects can design new buildings which can virtually eliminate the need for heating, such as those built to the <a href="/wiki/Passive_House" title="Passive House" class="mw-redirect">Passive House</a> standard.</p> 
     <p>However, if a building does need full heating, combustion central heating offers a more <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> solution than electric-air central heating or than other direct <a href="/wiki/Electric_heating" title="Electric heating">electric heating</a> devices. This stems from the fact that most electricity originates remotely using <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels" class="mw-redirect">fossil fuels</a>, with up to two-thirds of the energy in the fuel lost (unless utilized for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission losses</a>. In Sweden proposals exist to phase out direct electric heating for this reason (see <a href="/wiki/Oil_phase-out_in_Sweden" title="Oil phase-out in Sweden" class="mw-redirect">oil phase-out in Sweden</a>). Nuclear and hydroelectric sources reduce this factor.</p> 
     <p>In contrast, hot-water central heating systems can use water heated in or close to the building using high-efficiency <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">condensing boilers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>, or <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>. Wet <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_heating" title="Underfloor heating">underfloor heating</a> has proven ideal. This offers the option of relatively easy conversion in the future to use developing technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pumps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_combisystem" title="Solar combisystem">solar combisystems</a>, thereby also providing <a href="/wiki/Future-proof" title="Future-proof" class="mw-redirect">future-proofing</a>.</p> 
     <p>Typical efficiencies for central heating (measured at the customer's purchase of energy) are: 65-97% for gas fired heating; 80-89% for oil-fired, and 45-60% for coal-fired heating.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
