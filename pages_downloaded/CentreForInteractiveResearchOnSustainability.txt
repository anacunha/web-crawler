     <p>The <b>Centre for Interactive Research on Sustainability</b> (<b>CIRS</b>) is located at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia's</a> (UBC) Point Grey Campus in Vancouver. The building is dedicated to research collaboration and outreach on urban sustainability. It was officially opened in November 2011.</p> 
     <p>The CIRS vision is to be the most innovative high performance building in North America and an internationally recognized leader in accelerating the adopt of sustainable building and urban practices.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some key features:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>600 tons of carbon dioxide sequestered in the structure <sup id="cite_ref-youtube_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Campus energy consumption reduced by 275 megawatt-hours per year <sup id="cite_ref-youtube_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Water 100% supplied by rainwater <sup id="cite_ref-youtube_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Campus carbon dioxide emissions reduced by 150 tons each year<sup id="cite_ref-youtube_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>It has been certified <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> Platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Green_Building_Council" title="Canada Green Building Council">Canada Green Building Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CaGBC_page_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CaGBC_page-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Develop applied technical solutions in building materials and design for commercialization by partners.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Combine expert knowledge and public values and preferences in exploring pathways to a sustainable society.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Collaborate with private, public and non-governmental organization sector partners to create superior policy and business decisions for sustainable urban development.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Educate, train and demonstrate sustainable technologies and services and the practices of sustainable urban development.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals">Goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Be a world leader in three critical areas of sustainability research: technology, communication, participation and behaviour change, and policy and investment.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Make real progress on sustainability in the region and transform the UBC campus into a testing ground for alternative energy sources, and technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
      <li>Be an incubator for sustainability technologies and capitalize on the enormous export potential to enter the trillion-dollar global urban infrastructure market.<sup id="cite_ref-mvg_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mvg-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
     </ul> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The CIRC was conceived in 2000 by Professor John Robinson and designed by Peter Busby who excelled in green building designs.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> Together, they organized and talked to potential partners which led to Alberto Cayuela, formally an employee of Stantec and now associate director responsible for CIRS, joining the team as program manager.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Once funding and concept was decided, the location was chosen. Originally, it was to be built on the UBC Point Grey campus, but, because CIRS was an inter-instituational project (UBC leads the project in conjunction with Simon Fraser University, BC Institute of Technology, and Emily Carr University of Art + Design) it was felt that the Great Northern Way campus would be a better position.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> In line with the idea of an inter-institutional project, the new position would expand its scope and breadth.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>In 2008, the decision to move CIRS back to UBC Point Grey campus was made due to delays to the project timeline brought on by the complexity of building on the Great Northern Campus.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> Although, now only one institution (UBC) owned CIRS, other institutions continued to participate through other means.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Construction began in 2009 with excavation and foundation work for the first six months with the building being complete in August 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_Benefits">Building Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Building Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_Systems">Structural Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structural Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The CIRS is constructed of wood and allows for various benefits such as reducing ecological impact, sequestering carbon, helping the mitigation of an ecological problem, bringing warmth to the building interiors, and also maximizing daylight and views.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_Materials">Building Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Building Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The primary exterior material is white brick, clear glazing, wood, and neutral colored concrete. The benefits are the reduction of 'red list' materials, improvement of indoor air quality, reduction of material waste, and reduction of carbon emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_Systems">Energy Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The CIRS achieves a net-positive energy performance and reduces UBC's overall energy consumption by over 1 million kilowatt hours per year. The energy system benefits the project by providing a comparison between energy modelling processes, allows for more opportunities for research and system testing, inspire a new approach to energy supply/consumption, reduce heat demand and carbon emissions of neighboring buildings, and provide opportunities for continued learning.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainwater_System">Rainwater System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rainwater System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>CIRS is entirely water self-sufficient by harvesting rainwater and stored in a 100-cubic-meter cistern underneath the building. Around 1,226,000 litres of rainwater is harvested throughout the year, and average demand is 2000 litres per day. The benefits of a rainwater potable water system is that it creates awareness, educates the public on water supply/consumption, reduces potable water demand, promotes stormwater management, and provides an onsite water source for fire suppression.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reclaimed_Water">Reclaimed Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reclaimed Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>All the reclaimed water originates either from the CIRS building or sewer system which is treated onsite using a Solar Aquatics wastewater treatment system and reused in the building for toilet flushing and to irrigate the living roof, landscaped areas and the living wall. The main benefit of this treatment system is awareness and engaging the public in viewing sustainability as a possibility. <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">Lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>Narrow floor plates allowed for daylight penetration and light to enter the building from both sides. The benefits is that it improves the inhabitant health and comfort, creates a friendly space, reduces energy consumption and cost, and provides a surface for photovoltaic cells.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">Ventilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ventilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>The CIRS uses a mixed mode system utilizing mostly passive natural ventilation. There are two mechanical air handling units that supply fresh filtered air: one to the large auditorium and the other to the rest of the building. In addition, there are manual operable windows that allows for airflow and temperature control. This system reduces energy consumption, connects the inhabitants with nature, and improve their health.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Recognition">Public Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_for_Interactive_Research_on_Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>The CIRS has been publicly recognized by many as "North America's Greenest Building" <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and one of the greenest buildings in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Most recently in 2013, the CIRS won the Green Building Award awarded by the Wood WORKS! BC Wood Design Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
