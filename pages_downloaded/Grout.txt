     <p><b>Grout</b> is a particularly fluid form of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a><sup id="cite_ref-maconline_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maconline-1">[1]</a></sup> used to fill gaps. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> to embed <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebars</a> in <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> walls, connect sections of pre-cast <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, fill voids, and seal joints such as those between <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiles</a>. Grout is generally a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, often color tint, and sometimes fine gravel (if it is being used to fill large spaces such as the cores of concrete blocks).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike other structural pastes such as plaster or <a href="/wiki/Joint_compound" title="Joint compound">joint compound</a>, correctly-mixed and -applied grout forms a waterproof seal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Although both are applied as a thick emulsion and harden over time, grout is distinguished from its close relative <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a><sup id="cite_ref-beall1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beall1-2">[2]</a></sup> by its viscosity; grout is thin so it flows readily into gaps, while mortar is thick enough to support not only its own weight, but also that of masonry placed on top of it.<sup id="cite_ref-maconline_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maconline-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Grout varieties include <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiling</a> grout, <a href="/wiki/Flooring" title="Flooring">flooring</a> grout, <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> grout, <a href="/wiki/Non-shrink_grout" title="Non-shrink grout">non-shrink grout</a>, structural grout and <a href="/wiki/Thixotropy" title="Thixotropy">thixotropic</a> grout.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Tiling grout is often used to fill the spaces between tiles or mosaics, and to secure tile to its base. Although ungrouted mosaics do exist, most have grout between the <a href="/wiki/Tesserae" title="Tesserae" class="mw-redirect">tesserae</a>. Tiling grout is also cement-based, and comes in sanded as well as unsanded varieties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The sanded variety contains finely ground silica sand; unsanded is finer and produces a non-gritty final surface. They are often enhanced with polymers and/ or <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Structural grout is often used in reinforced masonry to fill voids in masonry housing reinforcing steel, securing the steel in place and bonding it to the masonry.<sup id="cite_ref-beall1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beall1-2">[2]</a></sup> Non-shrink grout is used beneath metal bearing plates to ensure a consistent bearing surface between the plate and its substrate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p><a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> is the most common cementing agent in grout, but <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">urethane</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a>-based formulas are also popular.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
     <p>Portland cement-based grouts come in different varieties depending on the particle size of the ground <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> used to make the cement, with a standard size of around 15 <a href="/wiki/Micron" title="Micron" class="mw-redirect">microns</a>, microfine at around 6–10 microns, and ultrafine below 5 microns. Finer particle sizes let the grout penetrate more deeply into a fissure.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Because these grouts depend on the presence of sand for their basic strength, they are often somewhat gritty when finally cured and hardened.</p> 
     <p>Tools associated with groutwork include:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>grout saw</b> or <b>grout scraper</b> a manual tool for removal of old and discolored grout. The blade is usually composed of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a>.</li> 
      <li><b>grout float</b> a <a href="/wiki/Trowel" title="Trowel">trowel</a>-like tool for smoothing the surface of a grout line, typically made of rubber or soft plastic.</li> 
      <li><b>grout sealer</b> a water-based or solvent-based sealant applied over dried grout that resists water, oil, and acid-based contaminants.</li> 
      <li><b>Dremel grout attachment</b> an attachment guide used in a <a href="/wiki/Die_grinder" title="Die grinder">die grinder</a> for faster removal of old grout than a standard grout saw.</li> 
      <li><b>pointing <a href="/wiki/Trowel" title="Trowel">Trowel</a></b> Used for applying grout in flagstone, and other stone works.</li> 
     </ul>
